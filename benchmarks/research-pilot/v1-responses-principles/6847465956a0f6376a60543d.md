# Einstein's Teacup: Stir It and They RUN to the Middle
### A ready-to-shoot YouTube Short outline (solo high-schooler, one smartphone, household props)

*Counter-intuitive angle: spinning water should fling floating bits to the rim like a merry-go-round — but instead the pepper stampedes to the dead center, the same hidden flow Einstein used to explain why rivers carve their bends.*

**Topic:** Why stirred water gathers floating particles at the center-bottom — slower-moving water near the bottom drives a hidden inward-rolling loop that herds the bits into a central pile (Einstein's tea-leaf effect). It is NOT plain "spinning throws things outward."

---

## Global camera & phone settings (set once)
Vertical **9:16** • **grid ON** • **flash OFF** • **1080p at 30fps** • use the **main/wide lens, NOT ultra-wide** (ultra-wide bends the round rim into an egg shape). For every glass shot: **tap the center of the glass to focus, then long-press to lock focus + brightness** so the phone doesn't re-hunt when your hand moves through frame.

**Overhead rig (used for all top-down glass shots — Beats 1, 3, 4, and the Beat 6 freeze):** Build a bridge — two equal-height stacks of books about a glass-width apart, a flat board/tray/stiff cutting board laid across the gap as a "ceiling," and the **phone taped to the underside of that board**, lens over the gap, screen facing up so you can still see it. Slide the white plate + glass into the gap under the lens. Mark the glass's spot with a small tape "L" so refills land back in frame. (No rigid board? Tape the phone flat under a shelf edge and put the glass on a box beneath it.)

## Shot-by-shot timeline (0:00–0:48)

### Beat 1 — Hook (0:00–0:03)
- **Narration:** "Spin the water fast — and the pepper runs to the middle."
- **On-screen action:** Overhead rig, straight top-down. In frame: clear glass ~3/4 full of water over a white plate, rim filling the frame. 0.0–1.0s your hand is already stirring FAST — pepper smears into a spinning ring that looks flung outward. 1.0–1.5s lift the spoon straight up and out of frame in one clean motion. 1.5–3.0s the pepper sweeps inward and gathers into a tight dark heap DEAD CENTER; land the last word as the pile forms. Tap record a beat before you stir so you don't clip the pile forming.
- **Text overlay:** "They should fly OUT ->" — upper third, centered, appears ~0.3s, holds through 0:03.
- **Framing:** Extreme close-up, top-down; rig frees both hands. Glass rim edge-to-edge with a thin ring of white plate showing.
- **Lighting:** Mid-day, window to the SIDE of the glass (not overhead, not behind the phone) so pepper casts tiny shadows that read the heap as a 3-D pile. Tilt the whole rig a few degrees to slide any bright reflection off the center.

### Beat 2 — Setup / Build (0:03–0:11)
- **Narration:** "Your gut says spinning flings everything outward — like a merry-go-round throwing you off the edge. So how'd it pile up in the center?"
- **On-screen action:** Cut to you talking to the phone. Spin your finger in a fast circle, then fling your hand outward to act out the "expected" throw; point back down toward the glass on "center." Keep energy fast.
- **Text overlay:** "EXPECTED: outward" — lower third, appears at 0:03.
- **Framing:** Medium head-and-shoulders, eye-level. Prop the phone against a stack of books / leaned on a mug at face height (or hold selfie-style). Solo: use a 3–5s self-timer or hit record and step in; leave headroom so the outward-fling gesture stays in shot.
- **Lighting:** Face the window (never film with the window behind you — you become a silhouette). At night, face your desk lamp/ceiling light and hold a sheet of white paper under your chin to bounce light up. Keep this same light side for Beat 5 so the two talking clips match.

### Beat 3 — Surprising Demonstration (0:11–0:22)
- **Narration:** "Fresh water, fresh pepper. I stir hard… then lift the spoon and just wait. There — watch them sweep in and stack dead center."
- **On-screen action:** Back to the overhead rig, identical framing to Beat 1 so the cut feels seamless. Refill clean water off-camera (start the clip clean). Sprinkle a modest pinch of ground black pepper, stir hard in a circle ~2s, lift the spoon straight up and out of frame in one motion, then hold your hands out of shot and let it settle 3–4 real seconds while the current herds the specks into one central pile. Do NOT over-stir (a deep vortex dimple ruins the read).
- **Text overlay:** "ACTUAL: dead center" — appears the instant the pile forms.
- **Framing:** Extreme close-up, top-down; phone taped so it stays dead still.
- **Lighting:** Same side window. A matte (non-glossy) white plate underneath cuts reflections bouncing back up; bring your stirring hand in from the side the light is NOT on so no shadow falls on the center.

### Beat 4 — Reveal (0:22–0:30)
- **Narration:** "That's not spinning winning. It's the water near the bottom moving slower than the water up top."
- **On-screen action:** Keep the top-down pile on screen. With a fingertip just above the glass, "draw" a flat fast circle at the top, then tap low on the glass side to mark the slow bottom water; point at the central heap on the last word.
- **Text overlay:** "bottom water = slower" — lower third.
- **Framing:** Overhead top-down close-up of the settled pile; keep your finger from casting a shadow over the center (come in from the non-lit side).
- **Lighting:** Same side light; keep the center glare-free — it's the exact spot the viewer needs to see.

### Beat 5 — Plain-Language Why (0:30–0:42)
- **Narration:** "Friction drags the bottom water, so its outward push is weaker down there. That weak spot lets a hidden loop of water crawl along the floor and herd the pepper inward." *(Friction = the rubbing drag between the water and the glass bottom.)*
- **On-screen action:** Cut to you. Use both hands to mime a rolling doughnut-shaped loop: fingers sweep out across the top, down the side, then IN along the bottom toward each other. Mid-beat, pick up the glass and tilt it toward the lens for ~1s so the pile at the center-bottom is visible from a low angle.
- **Text overlay:** "the water rolls INWARD along the bottom" — lower third, appears at 0:30.
- **Framing:** Medium eye-level selfie (propped like Beat 2), plus a ~1s close-up insert of the glass tilt. Solo: propped phone + timer, or hold the phone in one hand and the glass in the other for the tilt.
- **Lighting:** Face the window/lamp on the same side as Beat 2 so the talking clips match.

### Beat 6 — Payoff / Loop-Back (0:42–0:48)
- **Narration:** "Einstein used this same swirl to explain why rivers carve their bends. Now rewatch — the pepper never actually flew out."
- **On-screen action:** Point at camera on "Einstein," then point back down at the center pile. On "rewatch," swipe your hand upward to mimic the loop-scroll and freeze on the top-down center-pile shot to invite an immediate replay.
- **Text overlay:** "Watch again — it goes IN the whole time" — centered.
- **Framing:** Start on the medium eye-level selfie for the pointing, then cut to the overhead top-down center-pile shot and hold/freeze on it. The final freeze is just the last frame of your Beat-3 overhead clip reused.
- **Lighting:** Selfie matches the other talking beats; the freeze reuses Beat 3's lighting.

*Retention note: the riskiest moment is the 3–4s "wait" in Beat 3 after the spoon lifts — the "ACTUAL: dead center" overlay promises an imminent payoff and the narration keeps talking through the pause so the frame never feels dead.*

## Props (household items)
- **1 clear, straight-sided drinking glass** (plain pint glass). Substitutes: label-peeled pasta-sauce jar or clear mason jar. Avoid faceted "crystal" — ridges scatter light and hide the pile.
- **Tap water, ~3/4 glass**, room temperature. Keep a second glass of clean water + a small jug by the sink to rinse and refill fast between the Beat-1 and Beat-3 takes.
- **Ground black pepper — a modest pinch per take** (darkest, cheapest, converges fastest). Substitutes: loose tea leaves or crushed dry cereal. Light sprinkle only, so the heap reads as a dot, not a smear.
- **1 metal spoon** (tea- or tablespoon). Substitute: chopstick or the skinny end of a butter knife (narrower stirrer = cleaner swirl, less vortex dimple).
- **1 white plate OR sheet of white printer paper** under the glass so the dark pepper pops. Substitute: doubled white paper towel or a light cutting board.
- **Stack of books (or a shoebox) + a flat board/tray** — your free overhead rig. Substitute: a sturdy shelf edge to tape the phone under.
- **Roll of tape** (masking/painter's/clear) — lock the phone down and mark the glass's spot.
- *Optional:* a dark, non-shiny tabletop or dark towel behind you for the talking shots so your face isn't lost against clutter.

**Safety:** you're working with water directly under your phone. Fill to 3/4 (not the brim), keep the rig wiped dry, don't stir hard enough to slop, and always refill over the sink — never over the rig. One splash on the lens ruins the take and risks the phone.

## Judgment calls & alternatives (where the plan deliberately leaves you a choice)

1. **Hook line — three tested options.** The outline uses the punchiest statement form, but two alternates work equally well and all pair with the "They should fly OUT ->" overlay:
   - Primary: "Spin the water fast — and the pepper runs to the middle."
   - Alt A: "Physics says these should fly out. Watch them pile up instead."
   - Alt B (question form, strong open loop): "Why does spinning water shove everything to the exact center?"
   Pick what feels natural in your voice; the question form often maximizes the curiosity gap.

2. **Runtime flex.** If 48s feels long or Beat 2's energy sags, Beat 2 can trim to 5s without breaking the arc (pulls you to ~45s).

3. **The one wording that was corrected for accuracy.** Beat 6 originally said Einstein used "this *exact* effect" for rivers; it was softened to "this *same swirl*" because a teacup and a river bend share the *mechanism* (a hidden inward loop moving particles/sediment), not every detail. Keep the softened version — this was the only substantive disagreement in the review, resolved in favor of accuracy.

4. **The accuracy floor you must not break.** Never let narration collapse into "centrifugal force flings it, then something pulls it back." The correct, kid-simple story is: bottom water is slower -> its outward push is weaker down there -> that weakness lets a hidden loop roll inward along the floor -> the loop herds the pepper to the center. Beats 4–5 say this correctly; don't ad-lib it back into a myth.

5. **Two physical gotchas most likely to ruin a take.**
   - Shoot the pile forming AFTER the spoon stops, not during — convergence reads best in the 1.5–4s after you lift the spoon. Over-stirring makes a vortex dimple that muddies the read.
   - Glare is the silent killer. If a bright reflection sits on the water where the pile forms, the payoff is invisible. Side-light + a few degrees of rig tilt + a matte white plate is the fix; verify it in the pre-post checklist.

6. **Why this concept earns the effort.** The freshness isn't the trick itself (stirred-tea clips exist) — it's the framing: "spinning should throw things OUT, but it herds them IN, and this is the very thing Einstein used to explain river bends." Keep that reframed 'why' as your closing beat; it separates this from the saturated version."
