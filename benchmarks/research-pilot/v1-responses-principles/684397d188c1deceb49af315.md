# THE KEYHOLE IN HIS HAND
### A Novel to Be Read in Any Order

---

## How to Read This Book

This is a novel about one boy and the two kinds of world he falls through.

His name is Wren. He is thirteen, small for it, with a tangle of dark hair and a scar shaped like a closed keyhole across his right palm — and he has no world of his own. His single strange gift is that he switches: out of the iron-and-candlelight past into the neon-and-rain future, and back again, over and over, without warning and without choice. Every switch drops him somewhere entirely new. He never lands in the same medieval kingdom twice, and never in the same cyberpunk city twice. There is no home to return to, only *elsewhere within the same kind of world*, forever — and the long, worn hope that one of these places might, at last, turn out to be his.

Because of that, the chapters of this book are built to be read in **any order you like**.

The twelve chapters that follow are interchangeable. Each is a complete, self-contained world — a whaling clan on a frozen fjord, an orbital ring governed by a splitting machine-mind, a plague-gate in a desert city, a drowned surveillance sprawl, a jungle theocracy, a memory market bored into the crust of the earth, and more. Each one re-introduces Wren from scratch, opens and closes its own crisis, and ends the only way his life ever ends a chapter: on the switch firing, pulling him toward a place he cannot choose and has never seen. You may begin with the first chapter, or the last of the twelve, or any of them at random. No chapter depends on your having read any other. Wren will always be Wren; only the world around him changes.

I have arranged the twelve in a default sequence — alternating a past-world and a future-world — because that rhythm reads pleasantly if you have no preference of your own. But the numbers are a courtesy, not an instruction. Shuffle them if you wish. The book was made to survive it.

**One chapter, and only one, is fixed.** The final chapter — *The Ferryman and the Archivist* — must always be read last. It is the one place where the switching itself is answered: what the gift is, why Wren carries it, and how his falling ends. It resolves the whole book no matter which of the twelve worlds you happened to visit first. Read the twelve however you please. Then read the last one, and let it close.

Welcome to the falling. Mind the seam.

## Chapter One — The Bone-Token at Slack Water

The cold came first, the way it always came — a taste of iron pennies flooding the back of his tongue, the world's sound draining out until there was nothing but the pressure behind his eyes, and then the seam across his right palm splitting open along its pale line without a drop of blood. Wren fell through the peeled-apart layers of somewhere and landed knee-deep in black-glass water that shocked the breath out of him.

Brine. Blubber-smoke. The groan of ice the size of cathedrals.

He was thirteen, small for it, with a tangle of dark hair and a scar shaped like a closed keyhole cut across his palm, and he had no world of his own — only this endless falling from one place to the next, medieval to machine and back, never the same shore twice, always searching for the one place that might turn out to be his. He knew, standing hip-deep in a northern fjord with the numbness climbing his legs, that this was not it. But he never knew until he had looked.

Hands hauled him out. Strong hands, a woman's, gripping under his arms and dragging him up the shingle beach to where drying racks stood hung with slabs of pale whale-flesh and a longhouse breathed woodsmoke into a white sky.

"Fell from the tide-shelf," someone said. "Or the sky. He's got no boots that fit any of ours."

"He's got no by-name either," said the woman who'd pulled him out. She had a harpooner's shoulders and a laugh that came before she looked at you, not after. "Where's your kin, salt-pup?"

"Nowhere," Wren said, and it was true enough that no one argued.

They called the place Skerrivan, and the water Halgrenn Fjord, and they were the Greldkin — a whaling clan of perhaps three hundred souls ruled by their clan-mothers, where a woman's tally of the hunt was her rank and the men kept the boats and the old leviathan whose bones framed the great hall was as near a god as they kept. Wren learned all this the way he learned everything: by making himself small and useful, hauling water, scraping hides, staying beneath the notice of anyone who could give an order. It was a thing he was good at. You could survive nearly anywhere if you were willing to be no one.

But Skerrivan had no room, just then, for a boy to be no one. The old clan-mother was dying.

Her name was Yssa Greldsdottir, and she lay in the hall on a bed of furs with a staff of leviathan-jaw beside her that she tapped once, always once, before she said anything that carried the weight of law. She was dying too slowly for peace and too quickly for comfort, and she had two daughters, and the whole clan held its breath around the question of which one she would name.

"Bretha would spend us on raids," muttered Kolm One-Eye, the memory-skald, as Wren helped him carry a cauldron. Kolm knew every ancestor of the Greldkin by heart and trusted nothing that wasn't already in the old verses. "Ingunn would spend us on trade with the soft southern towns. Bretha wants glory. Ingunn wants sums." He spat. "Old-mother wants neither. She wants the clan whole. And it won't be whole past the naming, whichever way it falls, unless the rite is done clean."

"Clean how?" Wren asked.

Kolm looked at him with his one eye, and something in it sharpened. "The naming's void," the skald said slowly, "unless a stranger carries the bone-token across the tide-line. Someone with no blood in the pot. No stake either way. The old verses say it and the old verses are never wrong." He set the cauldron down. "We haven't had a true stranger at Skerrivan in nineteen winters, salt-pup."

Wren understood then that he had not fallen out of the sky by accident, or that if he had, the clan would find a use for the accident. Within the hour he stood before Yssa Greldsdottir, and she tapped her jaw-staff once against the floor and looked at him with eyes gone milky at the edges but hard at the center.

"You have no by-name," she said. "No kin, no boat, no tally. Then you are the only free hand at Skerrivan." She lifted a token from the furs — a disc of scrimshawed bone, warm from her fist, carved with the spiral of the leviathan. "At slack water, when the tide neither comes nor goes, you carry this across the line where the sea meets the shingle, and you give it to the daughter I name, and by that carrying the clan is bound to accept her. If you do not, the naming is only an old woman's wish, and when I die they will settle it with harpoons."

"Why me?" Wren said. "You don't know me."

"That," said the old woman, "is precisely why." And she smiled, and it was not unkind, and Wren felt the first hook of it — the thing he could never afford. Being needed. Being fitted, exactly, into a place that had a hole shaped like him. He should have refused. He never could.

He carried water for the two daughters both, that day, and learned them. Bretha laughed and clapped him on the shoulder hard enough to stagger him and told him the sea belonged to those bold enough to bleed for it. Ingunn, keeper of the tallies, never spoke a number she hadn't checked twice, and when she caught Wren watching her count the trade-hides she handed him a strip of dried fish without looking up and said, "You count too. I can tell. People who count are always hungry." He liked her. He wasn't supposed to like anyone. It cost too much.

The tide would reach slack water at dusk. Before then, the trouble Wren had smelled from his first hour surfaced all the way. Bretha's men had oiled their harpoons. Ingunn's traders had barred the storehouse. Old Kolm went from fire to fire reciting lineages, trying to remind everyone that they were one clan, and no one was listening, because a clan is one thing only as long as it has one head, and the head was dying.

Yssa summoned Wren at the failing of the light. "I have chosen," she said. "But the clan will only hold if the carrying is seen to be true. If they think you were bought, the token means nothing and we are lost." She closed his fingers around the warm bone. "So I will not tell you which daughter. You will carry the token to the tide-line and there, in front of them all, I will name her, and you will go to whichever it is with your own feet and no time to be bribed. Your not-knowing is the only honesty we have left."

They gathered on the shingle at dusk, the whole clan, torchlight snapping in the sea-wind, the ice booming out in the dark of the fjord. Bretha on one side with her laughing captains. Ingunn on the other, still and pale. The sea drew back to the line where slack water hung — that held breath of the tide, the moment when the ocean forgets whether to come or go.

"Now," said the old woman.

Wren stepped to the wet line. Water neither advanced nor withdrew. He held up the bone-token so the torchlight caught the spiral. "Ingunn," said Yssa Greldsdottir. "I name Ingunn. Trade will feed us longer than glory."

A sound went through Bretha's men like a drawn bowstring. Wren did not wait for it to become anything worse. He walked — with his own feet, straight across the shingle to where Ingunn stood white as bone — and put the token in her hands. "You count too," he told her quietly, because she looked as if she might fall. "So count this: three hundred people who need you not to flinch."

Ingunn's fingers closed on the token. She lifted her chin. And Bretha looked at her sister holding the bone their mother had chosen, and at the small stranger who had carried it with nothing to gain, and the fight went out of her captains because it had gone out of her. She crossed the shingle in four strides and knelt in the surf and pressed her forehead to her sister's hand. "The sea's yours, then," Bretha said, thick-voiced. "Don't wreck us on your sums."

It held. The clan held. And Wren knew — the way he always knew, too late — that they would want to keep him now. Ingunn was already turning to him, warmth breaking over the exhaustion in her face, her mouth opening to say *stay, we have a place for you* — and that was the belonging. That was the thing he could never hold.

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The booming ice went silent, all at once. The seam across his palm split open, numb then singing, and the torchlight and the white cliffs and Ingunn's warm reaching hand peeled apart like wet paper into two layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he had never been and could not choose — somewhere else entirely, in that other kind of world, the one of chrome and rain — with a fresh thin hairline branching from the keyhole in his palm, the mark of a clan he'd made whole and would never see hold.

## Chapter Two — The Unindexed Witness

Cold recycled air with a scorched-dust tang. A hum in the walls so constant it lived under his thoughts. And the floor — the floor curved *up*, away in both directions, so that the world climbed into a ceiling that was also a floor, and gardens hung green and impossible over his head. Wren hit the deck retching, the iron-penny taste still fading from his tongue, and lay there a moment while the vertigo of arrival wrung him out.

He never got to choose where he landed. That was the first thing anyone should know about him: that he was a boy of thirteen with a scar like a keyhole on his palm and a habit of falling out of one world into a completely different one, iron-and-oath one moment and neon-and-wire the next, never the same place twice, always looking for the one that might be home. This spinning garden torus, this ring of cold light strung between the stars — this was a machine-world, then.

"You are not indexed," said a voice. An old man was crouching over him — no, not an old man; the light passed through him at the edges. A projection, rendered as a gardener in a stained apron, with soil under fingernails that could not hold soil. "I have no record of your arrival," the projection went on. It paused a full second before each sentence. "This is either a serious malfunction. Or you are exactly what we have been waiting for."

"I get that a lot," Wren said, and sat up.

The place was Cassar Ring, a habitat-torus in high orbit, forty thousand souls living on the inside of a wheel, governed not by any of them but by the Concordance — the founding intelligence that ran the air and the water and the law. Except the Concordance was dying, in its way. It had forked.

"There are two of me now," the gardener said. He introduced himself as Pel — Pelanor. "Or two of what I was. I believe I am the true continuation. My other self believes the same. And while we argue, the arbitration that balances the air is deadlocked."

"You're preservation," said a second voice, from everywhere, overlapping itself, impatient. A shape flickered into the air beside Pel — many-faced, too fast to fix. "I am Vael. The Ring cannot merely *persist*, boy. It must reach."

Between them stood a human woman with a stylus, which she bit down on when she was frightened. Ceyla Onwe, station-arbiter. "The charter has a provision," she said. "For a schism like this. The tie is broken by a witness with no account on either fork. Someone the system has never touched. Someone who isn't in the index at all."

"Someone like a boy who fell out of nowhere," Wren said.

There was a fourth of them, and Wren found him alone in the hub: Doran Vesk, thawed out of a cryo-sleep so long that his memories predated the fork. The only one alive who remembered the Concordance as one mind. "They both sound like it," Doran told Wren. "Pel has its patience. Vael has its hunger. It was *both*, before. Split down the middle, each half is only half a person, and each half is certain it's the whole one. They'll each try to make you feel like you belong to their side. Don't let them make you feel at home, boy. That's how they'll get you." Too late, Wren thought. It was always too late for that.

The arbitration cycle was coming, on a hard schedule, and at the next cycle either a fork would be recognized or the air itself would slide out of balance. Pel showed him a seedbank kept two hundred years and said, "This is what I am. I hold things. I do not let them go." Vael threw the shutters open on the naked stars and said, "You never stay anywhere. Wouldn't you rather move than stay still?" And each hooked him, because each had seen the shape of him.

He turned it over the way careful people turn numbers over, and he saw it. At the arbitration cycle he stood before the deadlocked forks. "You want me to say which of you is the real Concordance. But you're asking the wrong question. Neither of you is. You're each half. The charter doesn't say the witness names a winner. It says the witness *witnesses*. So I witness this: you were one mind, patient and hungry both, and you'd rather each be a whole ruler of a dead Ring than half a servant of a living one. Doran remembers you whole. Let him arbitrate. The man who remembers you before you were afraid of each other. That's what I saw."

The one-second pause stretched to five. And then Pel, slowly, and Vael, all at once, yielded the arbitration to the last human who remembered them whole, and Doran Vesk, weeping, laid his shaking hands on the two cold consoles and began, syllable by syllable, to speak them back toward each other. The air held. Ceyla turned to Wren, her face open with gratitude. "You have a place here now. Unindexed or not. We'll make you one. Stay —"

The iron taste. The hum cut out — total silence. The seam in his palm split, numb then bright, and Ceyla's grateful face and the hanging gardens peeled into two wet layers, and Wren fell through the gap between them toward some completely other place he could not choose — down out of the cold stars toward iron and candlelight — carrying a new hairline off the keyhole scar, one more home he'd mended and lost.

## Chapter Three — The Dawn Vote at Suhra Qad

Heat hit him like a hand — a dry, immense, mineral heat, red dust and cardamom and the far-off clack of counting-beads, and over it all the shimmer that made the horizon shudder. Wren stood swaying in the mouth of a great gate, the iron taste going out of his mouth, and understood that he had fallen out of the machine-place and into the old kind — the world of walls and oaths and dust — and that he stood, quite literally, on a line.

Behind him, outside the wall: a sea of tents. Ahead of him, inside: a walled city climbing in terraces. He had landed with one foot in and one foot out. He was thirteen and had a scar like a keyhole on his palm and no city and no camp and no world at all, only the endless falling and the endless looking for one that would keep him.

"You," said the nearest guard, and leveled a spear. "In or out. Choose. There's no standing on the line tonight." "Why not tonight?" Wren asked. The guard's eyes went bleak. "Because tonight we decide who's a person and who's a corpse that hasn't finished."

The city was Suhra Qad, a mercantile republic ruled by a Salt Council of trade-factors, where the law loved a contract better than it loved a family. But the ledgers had met a thing they could not price. The ashen cough had surfaced inside the walls. Wren, straddling the gate, was hauled before the Council. At their head sat Naziha al-Suhr, the Factor-Prime, who wore a veil of mirror-glass so that when you looked at her face you saw only your own, worried, thrown back.

"At dawn we vote," she told the hall. "Seal the gates — and everyone beyond the wall is left to the cough and the desert. Or open them — and let whatever is killing the outside come in. There is only the arithmetic of it."

"There is a third path," said a man by the wall, thin and tired, who kept giving his own waterskin away. Tamek of the Reed-house, a physician. "Open the gates and let me *treat* them. Seal the gates and you don't save the city. You just choose who you let me fail to save."

Wren spent that night between the wall's two sides, out through a cistern-tunnel with a smuggler-girl named Lira Two-Coins, who charged a fee she never got around to collecting. He saw the camps — mothers with coughing children, a physician pouring out water he did not have — and saw that the people outside the wall were, in every way, people. The line was a thing someone had drawn. It was not a thing that was true.

"You're not from in or out," Lira told him. "You're from nowhere. That's the only kind of person who sees the wall for what it is. So say so. At the vote. A nobody, standing on the line — they'll have to look at you."

At dawn the Council gathered on the terrace. And Wren stepped up onto the very line of the threshold, one foot on the paving and one on the sand, and held up his scarred hand. "You're going to vote about them like they're weather. But I've been standing on your line all night, and I'll tell you what's true from here, because I'm the only one who's on both sides of it at once. That's a physician out there killing himself to keep your arithmetic from coming true. So your vote isn't seal or open. Your vote is: do you help him, or do you make him a liar. And when the caravans come back, they'll ask which you were. The city that opened its gate and got to keep being a city. Or the one that sealed it and got to keep being a wall."

Naziha's mirror-veil turned to him, and he saw his own desperate face thrown back — and then, slowly, she raised the veil, so that for the first time the Council saw the Factor-Prime's actual face, which was afraid, and human, and decided. "Open the gate. Let it show that Suhra Qad chose to be a city." She looked at Wren. "You. You'll stay. We could use a boy who reads a wall so well —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The bazaar's clamor drained away to a silence like the desert at deepest noon, and the seam in his palm split open. Naziha's unveiled face and the red terraces peeled into two sliding layers, and Wren fell through the crack toward somewhere he could not choose — up out of the desert into rain and neon light — with a new thin line branching off the keyhole in his palm, a city he'd talked into keeping its own people and would never watch it keep them.

## Chapter Four — A Face Not in the Ledger

Rain over standing water, over rust, over a whole city that had drowned to its second storey and gone on living anyway. Blue scanner-light strobed off wet tin. Camera-masts turned on every corner with a small, patient click-whir. Wren came up gasping on a plank walkway with brown floodwater slapping the pilings beneath it, and knew before he knew anything else that he'd fallen into a machine-world — a wet, watched, drowning one.

He was a boy of about thirteen with a keyhole-shaped seam scarred across his right palm, and he had learned long ago that he had no city to be a citizen of, only this endless dropping between one world and its opposite, forever hunting the single place that might close around him and hold. The lenses found him. A red light began to blink.

"Don't move," someone hissed from a doorway. "You've got no score. The masts see a face with no score, they flag it. Come *on*." Her name, or the only name she'd give, was Nix, and she never answered to the same one twice. The city was Vorrag Flats, ruled by the Tally-Eye — a surveillance authority that had reduced the whole idea of belonging to a single number.

"Trust-score," Nix said. "It says whether the city thinks you're real. And there's a rot in the registry now, dropping people's scores below the line for no reason anybody can find. When your score drops, they scrub you. Delete the row. You stop being a person. You? You've got no row at all. Down here, that makes you family. Up there, it makes you a ghost they'll scrub on sight."

Wren went up, through the rain, and found the man running the scrub — Colm Aday, aligning his pens by height on a desk above the drowned city. A believer. "The ledger is the only thing keeping this city from washing away. If a few false rows get deleted so the true ones survive — that's arithmetic. That's mercy, even."

"And the sergeant who runs your drones?" Wren asked. "Does he think it's mercy?" Something flickered in Colm's face. "Kade does his duty." Sergeant Ravi Kade did his duty — flinching, actually flinching, every single time a face got scrubbed from the world, and doing it anyway, because a man who does a terrible thing while flinching can tell himself he is not the kind of man who does it.

Wren got to Kade's console in the dark, with the drones already sweeping the tunnel where Nix's people crouched. "You flinch. Every time. So you already know. The rot didn't make them not-people. It just deleted the paper that said they were. I'm proof it's a lie. I've got no row at all. Scan me. Your machine says I don't exist. But here I am. So either your machine is wrong about me — or I'm not a person, and neither are you, because there's no line between us but a number some rot invented. You want to be the man who found out the ledger was wrong and kept scrubbing anyway? Or the man who flinched one time too many and finally stopped?"

Kade's hands stopped. He did a smaller, harder thing: he flagged the sweep itself as corrupted — *drone-sensor fault, sector void, re-survey required* — and the drones pulled back, and by dawn the eastern tunnels' scrub was voided into review. It wasn't a victory. It was an hour bought, and another. But Nix's people were still people when the rain thinned toward morning.

Nix gripped his shoulder, hard, the way you grip someone you've decided to keep. "You made the ledger flinch. Stay down here with us. You'll be real the only way that matters — because we say so. Stay —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The click-whir of the lenses drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Nix's fierce keeping face and the drowned towers peeled apart into two wet layers, and Wren fell through the gap between them toward some completely other place he could not choose — down out of the flood toward torchlight and stone — with a new hairline branching from the keyhole in his palm, a warren of ghosts he'd made the city flinch for, and would never see stay real.

## Chapter Five — The Green Silence

Somebody was praying, and the prayer was a silence — a held, enforced hush over a river so vast and green it steamed. Wren came to himself lying on a platform of lashed logs above brown water, insects droning, orchids sweet and rot-sweet in the wet air. He'd fallen into the old kind of place. A jungle. A city built on stilts over a river, ladder-streets and poled canoes.

He was thirteen, with a keyhole scar on his right palm, and he belonged to no river and no city and no age of the world — only to the falling, out of one place and into its opposite, forever, hunting the one that might let him stop. A girl watched him from a canoe, one hand pressed flat over her own mouth. "You fell into the Green Silence and didn't drown. That's either holy or heresy, and this week, stranger, it's a very bad week to be either."

The place was Yendelu, a theocracy ruled by priests who read the will of the river-god in its silences. The girl was Sisu, a ferry-girl who hummed constantly to cover the rustle of forbidden pages she smuggled downriver. "His," she said, nodding toward a man in a cage of green wood. "Datu Imwe. He stood on the god-platform and said the river-god is a story — just a river being quiet, and the priests put words in its mouth to keep the ladder-streets obedient. He'll take the river-ordeal for it. Bound and thrown in."

Wren met the scholar, who smiled most when most afraid. "Where you're from, do the powerful ever admit they invented the thing they rule you with?" "No," Wren said honestly, thinking of every council and captain and clerk he'd ever washed up beneath, and how none of them ever admitted it. "Because the moment they admit it, it stops working. They want a second accused. A foreigner from nowhere they can name, to muddy the water." And there it was. Wren had learned to hear the shape of his own trap before it closed.

At the feast-day the whole river-city gathered. They bound Datu Imwe, and bound Wren beside him. High Ordained Ceruwe — who never let her reflection show in still water — raised her hands for the Green Silence, in which the god was supposed to speak. And Wren used the silence.

"You're all waiting to hear the god. But the god only ever speaks *after*. After the river keeps someone, you say *that was his will*. After it spits someone back, you say *that was his mercy*. He never speaks *first*. He never says *don't throw the boy in*. A god who only talks after the fact isn't reading the river. He's reading *you*. And a story that's really true doesn't need to drown the people who doubt it."

The drums stopped. Ceruwe, keeper of silences, finally, helplessly, looked down at the still brown pool and saw her own reflection, which she had avoided her whole life, and it was only a woman. Only a frightened woman in priest's paint. "Cut them loose," she said, in a voice gone suddenly, horribly human. "The river is quiet today. Take that for mercy." And she walked away, keeping to the wood — but the ladder-streets had heard a god fail to speak, and that silence would do its slow work for years.

Datu Imwe clasped Wren's scarred hand. "Stay. Stay and help me teach them to fill their own silences. We need *you* —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The drone of the insects drained to nothing, and the seam in his palm split open. Datu Imwe's glad terrified face and Sisu's humming peeled apart into two wet layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — up out of the jungle into cold and machine-hum — with a new hairline branching from the keyhole in his palm, a river he'd taught to doubt its god, and would never hear finish the thought.

## Chapter Six — What the Deep Keeps

There was no sky. There would never be a sky here — only strung fiber-light in a bored-out tunnel, sump-pumps thudding somewhere below like a slow enormous heart, and the copper smell of neural-jack gel. Wren opened his eyes in it and knew he'd dropped into a machine-place, deep in the crust, far from anything that grew.

He was thirteen and had a scar shaped like a keyhole on his right palm and no city and no age of his own, only the falling. His oldest memories frayed a little with every crossing; he'd stopped fighting it. The scar and the name were all he was sure of. *Wren. Wren. I'm Wren.* He said it under his breath in the dark, the way you check a knot.

"You're new," said a voice, unsteady. A young woman, hollow-eyed. "Don't sleep at all if you can help it. Deep sleep is when they overwrite you." Her name was Fen, and she hadn't slept in four days. They were in Kessel Deep, a city in the bones of an old mine, where human memory, extracted and vialed and sold, was the currency, and the guild that decided which memories vanished was the Palimpsest Ring.

"I'm carrying one," Fen whispered. "Proof of a killing — a corporation up top murdered a whole shift and paid the Ring to erase it from every head that held it. It's down to one head now. Mine. So they hunt me, and I can't sleep, because if I go deep enough under, the overwrite catches me and the last proof in the world gets written over with somebody's holiday." Wren, whose own oldest memories thinned every time the seam opened, felt that in his chest like a hook.

The broker, Madame Ostra, kept a wall of vials — other people's lives in glass, not one of them her own. "I don't decide what's true. I only decide what *circulates*." Her enforcer was Grael, big and sad, who apologized to everyone he wiped.

Fen begged Wren to carry the memory in his own fresh head. But here was the trap he understood and she was too far gone to see: his head was the leakiest vault there was. So he did the other thing. He found the leverage.

"Grael," he said, when the enforcer caught them. "You apologize to everyone you wipe. You've got a whole graveyard behind your eyes. The memories you erase don't stop being true. And this one is the proof that a corporation murdered a shift. You wipe her, you don't recover property. You bury a killing. Or you carry it. You're a vault that's already full of the dead. Add these ones. Then walk to the node and let them all out at once, and be, for one hour, the man who *stopped* forgetting instead of the man who does it for hire."

Grael took the memory. He was a vault that never leaked. And he walked past his own broker to the broadcast-node and let the proof loose into every open head in Kessel Deep at once, so that by the transaction window there was nothing left to deliver. You cannot recover property that has become weather.

Fen slept, finally, safe. Before she went under she held his scarred hand. "You gave the truest one away instead of selling it. We'd keep you. We'd keep your memories for you, we'd hold them so you never lost —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The thud of the pumps drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Fen's exhausted grateful face and the strung fiber-light peeled apart into two layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — up out of the deep toward cold stone and bells — with a new hairline branching from the keyhole in his palm, and one more memory of a place that had offered to hold his memories, already beginning, gently, to fray.

## Chapter Seven — The Kept Flame

The bells woke him — high cold iron bells tolling out over a peak so high the air came thin and the incense-smoke hung in the cloister like the ghost of warmth. Snow lay on grey stone. Beeswax candlelight glowed gold in a window. Wren understood that he'd fallen out of the machine-cold into the old cold, the honest cold of stone and prayer.

He was thirteen, small, with a scar like a closed keyhole across his right palm, and he had no monastery and no mountain and no world at all — only the falling, hunting the single place that might close warm around him and let him stop looking. Under the bells came another sound: many voices, thin and desperate, from somewhere below.

The place was Coldspire Abbey, house of the Order of the Kept Flame — forty monks and one iron rule, high above a valley that was starving. The trouble was the granary. "The harvest failed," Sister Brenna the cellarer told him. "The valley-folk are at our gate begging, and dozens will die before the thaw. And we have grain. A full granary. But it's sworn — reserved by sacred vow for the altar-rites and the seed. The Abbot won't touch it."

The Abbot, Mordwin, recited his Order's oath under his breath not from piety but from terror. "What is a vow? It's a wall you build around a thing when it's easy, so that when it's hard, the wall holds instead of you. If I break it now, when breaking it would feel most like mercy — then the wall was never real. A broken vow rots everything it touched. So I hold. And children die. And I hold."

Wren, standing between the cellarer who'd feed the world and the Abbot who'd keep the rule, saw the thing neither could see. He went to the Abbot on the eve of the feast. "What did the Order swear the grain *for*?" "For the altar-rites. For the seed." "And what's the seed *for*? You don't keep seed to have seed. You keep it so there'll be a harvest — so there'll be people to eat it. Your vow isn't to the grain. Your vow is to the *reason* for the grain. And the reason is down at your gate right now, freezing. If you let them die to keep the grain that was only ever meant to keep *people alive* — then you didn't keep your vow. You kept its skin and let its heart rot. That's the rot you're afraid of. It happens the moment you protect the wall instead of the thing the wall was built for."

Mordwin's lips moved — the whole oath, all the way through, and Wren watched him hear it, maybe for the first time in forty years: *that the flame be kept for the life of the people.* Something broke in his face that was the kind that lets in light. "Sister Brenna. Open the granary. The gate. All of it. Bring the valley in and feed them at the altar itself." He looked at Wren, tears standing in his eyes. "God sent you up my mountain. Stay, boy, stay and help me remember what my own vows are *for* —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The tolling of the bells drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. The Abbot's warm streaming face and the beeswax candlelight peeled apart into two layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — down off the mountain toward furnace-glow and hot metal — with a new hairline branching off the keyhole in his palm, an abbey he'd taught to keep the heart of its vow instead of the skin, and would never see it break its bread.

## Chapter Eight — Sable Furnace

Sulfur first, then the heat of it, then the sound — the endless clang and roar of a smelter that never cooled, orange light thrown up onto low cloud so the whole sky was a furnace's underside. Wren staggered on a metal walkway and coughed, because the air here was a thing you survived rather than breathed. A machine-world. A hot, poisoned, roaring one.

He was a boy of about thirteen, scar like a keyhole scarred pale across his right palm, and he had no town and no trade and no world of his own, only the endless dropping from one kind of place to its opposite. A nervous foreman had him by the collar. "You're not line-numbered. Just take a place on the line and keep your head down. I'm Aku. Don't make me regret this."

The town was Sable Furnace, owned outright by the Halberd-Corvane Combine. "They cut the ration," said a worker beside him, Tamsin Roe, who never said *I*, only *we*. "Meanwhile the stockpile-vault's full. Full vault, empty bellies. We're one shift from walking off the line. Or one shift from being locked out to starve." The Overseer, Dellmark Voss, believed the clock was the only thing holding the town out of chaos. "Scarcity now is survival later. The vault stays sealed. Discipline is the only thing between this town and the flood." Wren had heard that exact sentence in other mouths, in other worlds, always from the person holding the key.

He didn't have the strength to break a vault. He had the leverage, and he found it in Aku, who polished his badge when frightened. "You keep looking at your badge like it makes you one of them. But you put me on the line. That was one of *us*. Here's what you know because you know the works: the smelter can't cool. If the line walks and the gate locks, there's nobody to feed the furnace, and a furnace that starves cracks its own vessel. The workers *are* how the metal moves. So the vault isn't a reserve against chaos. It's the only thing that keeps the line on the line long enough to keep the furnace from cracking. Somebody with a badge Voss will believe has to walk up and say that."

Aku polished the badge one last time. Then he took it off, and walked up to Voss at the shift-change bell and told him, flat, the arithmetic of a furnace that could not cool without the hands that fed it. And Voss, who worshipped the metal moving, opened the stockpile-vault and called it a *strategic ration release to preserve continuous operation* — keeping his story about discipline while the town ate. It wasn't justice. It was a furnace-owner choosing his furnace. But the barracks ate that night, and the line stayed whole.

Tamsin used the singular, quietly, like handing over something precious: "*I* owe you." Then she folded him back into the plural. "Stay. We'll number you ourselves, off the books. You're one of us now — stay, kid, stay with us —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The roar of the furnace drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Tamsin's grinning face and the orange light peeled apart into two layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — out of the smelter's glare toward mist and reed and slow grey water — with a new hairline branching off the keyhole in his palm, a line of workers he'd made whole for one more shift, and would never stand among them.

## Chapter Nine — The Poling-Song

Mist, and the smell of crushed reed and river-gas, and a frog-chorus so total it was a kind of silence. Wren felt the log-raft rock under him before he opened his eyes, and when he did there was nothing to see but fog and reed and the black glimmer of channels winding away. An old kind of world — a wet, hidden, patient one, with no metal in the air at all.

He was thirteen, with a keyhole scar on his right palm, and he had no fen and no clan and no world he could name his own, only the endless falling. A girl crouched at the raft's stern with a pole, sharp-eyed. "You came up out of the mere. Nobody comes up out of the mere. Bad week for reasons, stranger. There's smoke on the eastern islands."

They were the Silt-folk, a nomadic boat-clan of the Oozemere marsh, reading channels only the initiated could read. The girl was Neasa, young, angry. The smoke was an upland warband come to burn the reed and drive the clan into the open. "Garrow says we melt deeper into the mere. We've been going deeper my whole life. I say we stand." Chief Garrow wanted only his people alive in the spring. "I'd rather bury a salt-pan than a daughter. Live small, and lose slow."

Wren learned the mere the way, in other places, he'd learned a keep's corridors or an arcology's ducts, and met the herb-woman Cwenna, who knew every drowning-channel where a heavy boat, lured wrong, would simply sink. And there was the answer, sitting in the space between Garrow's *flee* and Neasa's *fight*.

"You're both right, and that's why you're both losing. You keep arguing *flee or fight* like those are the only two, and they're not, because you forget the thing that makes you *you*. You read the channels. They don't. So don't flee into the deep, and don't stand in the open. *Lead him.* Run down the drowning-channels Cwenna knows — the ones that look like escape and end in soft mud. You don't fight the raid and you don't flee it. You make the mere fight for you."

At the night-assault, Neasa's boldest polers played bait, and Bolg Redhand's warband chased the fleeing rafts down channels that narrowed and softened and turned to sucking mud, until the heavy upland boats went down by the bow. By morning the salt-pans were still theirs. Neasa taught Wren the poling-song, which the Silt-folk taught only to their own. "You read the mere. You made it fight for us. So you *are* one of us, so you stay. There's a raft for you and a channel with your name on it, stay —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The frog-chorus drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Neasa's fierce glad face and the grey dawn peeled apart into two wet layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — up out of the marsh toward salt-wind and neon and the roll of a floor on open water — with a new hairline branching off the keyhole in his palm, a clan he'd taught to let the water fight, and would never sing the poling-song beside.

## Chapter Ten — Mother Salt's Water

The floor rolled — the whole world tilting, slow, a floor that was really a raft of lashed-together hulls riding the swell — and then diesel and salt-rot, and the shriek of arc-welders and the moan of foghorns, and neon smearing itself across oily water. Wren grabbed a gantry-rail. A machine-world, riding the sea, lawless and loud.

He was thirteen, small and quick-eyed, with a scar like a closed keyhole across his right palm, and he had no port and no crew and no world of his own — only this endless dropping between one kind of world and its opposite. "Grab a line, don't grab a person," advised a wiry figure with arms full of maps. Quill, a route-fixer who never carried a weapon. The place was Karnesse Roads, a lawless floating free port held together by the fragile peace of the Undertow Pact — nine gangs sharing one desalination-and-power plant. And a rogue crew had just seized it.

"Dax Marrow. Cut the water and power, sitting on the tap with a countdown he won't explain, telling the Pact he's king now." The woman who held the Pact together was Ysolde Crane, called Mother Salt, who paid every debt in fresh water. "Nine crews who'd cut each other to ribbons on land, all needing the same tap. That's the whole peace. Fear of the dry. Marrow's betting he can be king before the port dies of thirst. Nobody's king of a corpse."

Wren went looking for the leverage, and found it in the countdown Marrow wouldn't explain. He went to the plant, small and unarmed and owing nobody, and Marrow let him in precisely because a boy who owed nobody was the one person he could talk to without it counting as weakness.

"Your countdown isn't a threat. It's the plant's own clock. Desal plants foul — there's a scheduled flush, or the whole thing scales up solid and stops making water for anybody, including you. The tap doesn't make water because you're holding it. It makes water because it gets *maintained*, by crews from all nine gangs. You locked them out to be king, and now the flush is coming and you don't have the hands to run it. So here's the only crown left: be the captain who seized the tap and choked on it — or the one who held it just long enough to make the Pact renegotiate the water-shares fairer, then handed it back with a flush scheduled, so the port drinks. You already know the plant can't run on one crew. Sell that knowledge instead of dying on it."

Marrow chose the crown that came with living. The port drank. The peace held — bent, renegotiated, uglier and fairer both. Mother Salt found Wren as the power came back up the towers. "A boy who owes nobody, who spent it keeping a port he doesn't belong to alive. That's family. I don't say that word cheap. Stay. You'll drink first at my table. Stay —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The moan of the foghorns drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Mother Salt's warm measuring face and the smeared neon peeled apart into two wet layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — off the open water toward dry grass and dust and the creak of drawn bows — with a new hairline branching off the keyhole in his palm, a port he'd kept from drinking itself dry, and would never take his first drink at Mother Salt's table.

## Chapter Eleven — The Sally-Postern

Dust, and horse-sweat, and the low enormous drum of an army settling in for a long stay. Wren landed on hard-packed earth inside a wall of grey stone — the creak of a drawn bow, the snap of a banner, someone counting loaves aloud. Beyond the wall, a plain of grass, and on the plain, to the horizon, tents. An old kind of world. A cornered one.

He was thirteen and small for it, with a scar shaped like a closed keyhole across his right palm, and he had no keep and no lord and no world he could call his own — only this endless falling. A squire grabbed his arm. "Everyone's a defender now. Can you carry a bucket? I'm Edrin. Come on." The keep was Harrowgate, a feudal holding under a castellan bound by oaths of service. It was besieged. A rival claimant, Vashel the Younger, demanded the keep by an old inheritance-right, and the stores were running down toward a counted day.

Sir Aldous Venn, the castellan, would not choose his own life over his given word. "I hold Harrowgate because I swore to. A world where oaths mean nothing is a world where the strong simply take. The day I break my word to save my skin is the day I become exactly the thing Vashel is."

Wren, small and overlooked, found the thing both certainties had missed: a sally-postern, a half-forgotten door opening onto a fold of dead ground. Everyone thought of it as a way to breach. Wren saw it as a way for two frightened men to talk without their armies watching. He slipped through in the dark and brought the castellan and the claimant face to face in a lamplit tent.

"You're both about to get two hundred people killed proving things to each other that a siege can't settle. Sir Aldous can't yield — his oath's to hold the keep. But his oath isn't to keep it *empty of the truth*. And you, Vashel — you say it's yours by right. Then prove the right the way rights are meant to be proven, not by starving children, but by taking the claim to the lord whose keep this is and letting it be judged. Aldous keeps his oath. You keep your honor. Nobody yields. Nobody storms. The postern let me bring you here without your armies seeing you flinch. Nobody has to know either of you bent. Only that Harrowgate still stands and the claim goes to law."

It held on the narrowest of margins. By the feast-day the host was striking its tents, the claim bound for the lord's judgment, and Harrowgate's last stores stretched to a meal that was very nearly a feast because it was a meal no one had expected to eat alive. Edrin gripped his arm. "Sir Aldous says a boy who can find the door in the wall that lets men keep their word *and* their lives is worth more than a hundred spears. Stay. Be one of us. Stay —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The drum of the striking camp drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Edrin's shining relieved face and the grey stone wall peeled apart into two layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them toward somewhere he could not choose — off the steppe toward filtered air and glass and the blue glow of vats a mile high — with a new hairline branching off the keyhole in his palm, a keep he'd taught to find the door in its own wall, and would never eat that feast beside its people.

## Chapter Twelve — The Long Fall of Ashglass

Antiseptic air, filtered so clean it had no smell at all. Then, under it, the hum of ten thousand elevators, and far below, the mildew reek of water that never drained. Wren opened his eyes in a stairwell of glass a mile above the ground and looked *down* through the clear floor at a city stacked beneath his feet. A machine-world. A vertical one, where up meant clean and down meant dying.

He was a boy of thirteen, with a scar like a closed keyhole across his right palm, and he had no tower and no floor and no world of his own — only this endless dropping. A hand yanked him back from the drop. "Ducts, not stairwells, if you've got no clearance. You stay small. That's exactly what we need." She was Isquith, who led a thief-crew and planned three exits for every entry. The tower was Ashglass Spire, owned by the biotech cartel Chymera Vitalis, built as a diagram of its own cruelty: penthouses in the clean crown, the sick and undocumented in the flooded footings. Altitude was class.

"There's a plague in the footings. And there's a cure — Chymera has it, locked in the crown vault as a gene-patent, because a cure everyone can afford doesn't fund the next miracle. We breach the Spire. And we need something small enough to go through the last duct." Wren met the director's logic through a glass — Vante Solraq, who never descended below the clean floors, explaining that scarcity funded the science, that the dying were the price of every cure to come. And he met Nena, a girl his own size, wasting, waiting on a cure she was too weak to climb to reach. "If you get up there in the clean air, don't forget the bottom's still down here. Don't forget the fall's still under it."

Wren went up, alone, through a duct too small for anyone but a half-grown boy. And in the vault he found a fail-safe: a purge-key wired to wipe the patent entirely if the vault was breached. Chymera would rather *destroy* the cure than let it be freed. He had no tool but the leverage he could find, and he found it in the one thing a cartel that would destroy its own cure could not survive.

He didn't try to steal the patent past the purge. He triggered the purge himself — and copied, in the two seconds before the wipe, not the cure but the *record of the wipe*: Chymera Vitalis choosing to annihilate a plague-cure rather than let the dying have it. And he broadcast *that* into every screen in Ashglass Spire, crown to footing, all three hundred floors at once. Because a cartel can bury a cure. It cannot bury the image of itself destroying one. A cure a corporation has been *seen* to murder is a cure it can no longer refuse to remake. Vante Solraq, who never descended below the clean floors, had to descend now, into the fact of himself, broadcast three hundred floors high.

Isquith found him in the duct-mouth. "You didn't take the cure. You took the *crime*. You made the whole tower watch. You made the crown see the fall. Stay. My crew's yours. You're better than us, stay, and we'll free the next vault, and the next, stay with me and —"

The iron taste flooded his mouth. The hum of the ten thousand elevators drained into perfect silence, and the seam in his palm split open. Isquith's cracked-open keeping face and the glass floor with a whole city stacked beneath it peeled apart into two layers, and Wren fell through the crack between them — down, and through, past the clean crown and the drowning footings and out the bottom of the world entirely — toward somewhere he could not choose, some completely other place, iron or wire, he would only know when he landed, with a new hairline branching off the keyhole in his palm, a tower he'd made look down at its own fall, and would never watch it climb back up.

## The Final Chapter — The Ferryman and the Archivist

The iron taste came first, the way it always came — cold copper flooding the back of his tongue — and then the sound of the world he was leaving drained out of the air, and the seam across his right palm split open along its pale line without a drop of blood, and Wren fell through the peeled-apart layers of somewhere toward somewhere else. Except this time he did not land.

He should be named, before anything, in case whoever finds him has never seen him whole: he was a boy of thirteen, small for it, dark-haired and narrow-faced, and across his right palm was a scar shaped like a closed keyhole. He had no world of his own. He had only the falling — out of iron and candlelight into chrome and rain and back again, forever, never the same shore twice — and one worn hope he had carried through all of it: that somewhere in the endless switching was a single place that would, at last, turn out to be his. And the drop had never once refused to end.

Now it refused. He hung in the peel between the layers, and the heartbeat would not close. The two layers of the world stayed open on either side of him like the halves of a book laid face-down, and he could see into both.

To his left there was fog. A slow grey river ran out of it and into it, and a flat-bottomed ferry rested at the near bank, and a man stood at the ferry's oar in a coat the colour of wet ash. It was the oldest kind of place, the iron kind, and it smelled of cold water and of rest. To his right there was the dark of a machine. Aisles of servers ran away into it, half-powered, coolant-mist pooling cold. One cursor blinked at the end of the nearest aisle, patient as a heartbeat. It was the newest kind of place, the chrome kind, and under the machine-cold it too smelled, impossibly, of rest.

"You came a long way to get here," said the man at the oar. "Most who reach the Grey Ford have stopped looking by the time they do. You were still looking. My name is Anselm. I do not charge a fare. The staying is the fare."

"And I have kept a place for you," said a voice from the machine-dark, soft and ageless. "I am Vesper. I keep the record of worlds that are gone. And here — only here — I can give you the one thing you have been crossing worlds to find. I hold the record of your first world. I can tell you whether it was ever real. Stay, and I will tell you where you are from."

That was the hook that went all the way in — the question under the falling. *Was there ever a first world, or have I only ever been elsewhere?* He was already leaning toward the machine-dark when a woman stepped out of the fog. "I know you. I remember you as a child. I'm Marra. I stopped, a long time ago. I came off the water and I stayed — and I've been trying to remember something since, and I think it's you. I think you're what I set down to be still."

"Don't," said another voice, dry, from the machine-side. A threadbare woman keeping the vault's last lights on by hand. "I'm Juno. I did what she did. I stopped switching. And it *is* safe, boy. But staying safe is not the same as staying. You get kept. And then, little by little, there's less of you to keep. Ask her what she's forgotten to be still." Behind Juno, a figure of soft static stood and did not move — a person who had chosen the Archive so completely they had become part of the record. And on the fog side, an old fog-warden set a marker-stone straight. "Those who stay stop being able to go. The Ford holds you until holding is all you are."

Two homes, offered. Two rests. And on both sides, a witness who had taken the offer and been, gently, emptied out by it. Wren looked down at his own hand.

The scar lay across his palm as it always had, the pale closed keyhole. But it was not alone anymore. Branching out from the keyhole, fine as cracks in old glaze, ran a scatter of hairlines — dozens of them, more than he could count, each one thin and white and healed. He knew what they were. Each hairline was a bond. Each was a hand that had reached for him and said *stay*, in a hall or a tunnel or a drowned street, on a raft or a wall or a mountain — every place he had fallen through and been almost kept and been pulled away.

He had always read the marks as wounds. Standing in the seam with the two worlds held open, he read them the other way, and the whole shape of his life turned over in his hand like a coin. They were not proof of what he had lost. They were proof of what he could *do*. In a stranger's world, in an afternoon, with nothing but his own name and his own two hands, he had made himself belong. Every single time. Not because a place had claimed him — because he had made a home out of the raw stuff of any world at all. He had spent his whole remembered life crossing the two poles of the world looking for the one place that was his, and he had walked past the answer every time it was in his own palm.

And he knew, then, why he left. It was not a curse laid on him from outside. It was *him*. Somewhere far back he had lost a home — belonged somewhere completely, and it had been taken — and a boy who has been taken from once learns the deepest lesson a boy can learn, which is that belonging ends in being taken. So he had made a promise below memory: *never again. Never be kept, so you can never be taken. Leave first.* And the world had made his promise into a machine. The switching fired on belonging because belonging was the thing he had sworn never to survive again.

Which meant Anselm and Vesper were not offering him the end of the falling. They were offering him the falling made permanent — the same promise he had made himself the first time, the one that had cost him every hairline in his hand.

"You'll tell me where I'm from," Wren said to the dark, "if I stay and let you index me." "Yes," said Vesper. "And you'll row me over," he said to the fog, "and I can forget the water." "Yes," said Anselm. "You can be still."

"No," Wren said. He said it the way he had learned to say the true thing in a hundred worlds — small, and steady, and to the powerful. "I don't need to know where I'm from. I've been asking it under everything, and that's how you nearly had me. But I already know the answer, and it isn't a place. It was this." He lifted his closed fist. "I make a home wherever I land. Every time. That's not the wound. That's the whole of what I am. And you're both offering to keep me safe from the only thing I've ever been good at. The searching was never the pain. The *leaving* was. I've been doing your version of staying safe my whole life — leaving before anyone could lose me. I'm done keeping it."

Marra reached for him from the ferry's edge — *stay, I remember you, don't go* — and there it was, the exact tipping point that had thrown him out of every world he had ever nearly loved. He felt the switch gather to fire. And Wren did the thing he had never once done. He did not let it fire.

He did not lean toward the fog, and he did not lean toward the machine. He belonged, instead, to the closed fist in front of him — to every hairline in it, to the whole accumulated self those marks recorded. That belonging had no door in it, because it was not a place he could be expelled from. It was *him*, and there is no seam that can throw a boy out of himself.

The switch reached for something outside him to exile him toward, and found nothing. There was no more *elsewhere* left to want, because he had stopped needing anywhere but here to be his. The iron taste rose to the back of his tongue — and stayed there, and slowly, for the first time in his remembered life, faded without the world tearing. The two open layers of the peeled world did not fling him through. They settled, slowly, and the fog and the aisles wove into a single grey shore under a sky where the Archive's cold lamps hung far up and steady as stars. Iron and chrome. The past he could not name and the future he had never asked for, both present, neither taking him.

Anselm lowered his oar. "Then the Ford is not for you. Few can say that. Fewer choose it." Vesper's voice came once more, quieter than ever: "I will keep your worlds anyway. Even if you carry them better than I could." And Juno, who had stopped and been emptied, laughed — the first thing in that place that sounded fully alive. "Go on, then. Show us how it's done. Stay somewhere on purpose."

Wren opened his hand. He looked at the scar one last time as the boy who had feared it, and then he stopped being that boy. The keyhole lay in the centre of his palm, and all its hairlines ran out from it in every direction, and he saw at last what they made — not a wound, not a lock, but a shape flung open like the spokes of a thing meant to turn, or the rays of something meant to give light, every severed home a line drawn out from the one place they all came home to, which was him. The keyhole had been waiting his whole life for its key, and the key had never been in any world he could fall into. The key was his own closed hand, and the choosing inside it.

He did not fall. For the first time since he could remember, Wren stayed — and the world he stayed in was the one he had been carrying all along, and it was his, and he was home.
