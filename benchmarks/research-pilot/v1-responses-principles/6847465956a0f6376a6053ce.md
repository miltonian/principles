# Sunlight, the Outdoors, and Vitamin D: What the Evidence Really Says — and What You Should Do About It

**A public-health research report for the general public**
*Authored by a Senior Research Fellow, National Public Health Institute*
**Date: 4 July 2026**

---

## Executive Overview — Key Findings & Recommendations

Few pieces of health advice are as popular — or as muddled — as "get some sun for your Vitamin D." Sunlight, being outdoors, and Vitamin D are woven together in the public imagination, yet they are not the same thing. Confusing them can lead you to chase benefits a suntan will never deliver, or to worry about risks that barely apply to you. This report cuts through the noise with what the best recent peer-reviewed evidence actually shows — and what you can safely do about it.

Throughout, I use one plain-language term you'll see often: **serum 25-hydroxyvitamin D**, usually shortened to **25(OH)D** — the blood marker doctors measure to judge your Vitamin D level. When I say "raising your Vitamin D," I mean raising this blood marker, whether the source is sun, food, or a supplement.

### Key Findings at a Glance

- **Vitamin D genuinely protects your bones.** Too little causes soft, weak bones — *rickets* (a childhood bone-softening disease with bowed legs) and *osteomalacia* (the adult version: aching, softened bones) — and contributes to fractures later in life. This is the vitamin's well-established job (Institute of Medicine, 2011).
- **The "wonder-drug" claims have not held up.** Large, high-quality trials — the U.S. VITAL trial (Manson et al., 2019) and Australia's D-Health trial (Neale et al., 2022) — found Vitamin D supplements did **not** prevent cancer, heart disease, or early death in generally healthy, already-replete people.
- **Many outdoor benefits have nothing to do with Vitamin D.** Better mood, better sleep, lower blood pressure, and more physical activity come from daylight, movement, and nature — not from the vitamin itself. A Vitamin D pill taken indoors would not reproduce them.
- **Sunlight only makes Vitamin D under specific physical conditions.** Above about 35° latitude — most of Europe, Canada, and the northern U.S. — there is a "Vitamin D winter" (roughly October–March) when midday sun produces almost none, however long you sit in it. Window glass and high-SPF sunscreen also block the necessary rays.
- **You never need to burn.** Your skin makes its maximum Vitamin D at roughly one-third of the dose that would redden it (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023). Sunburn adds skin-cancer risk without adding Vitamin D.
- **There is no single agreed daily supplement dose.** Reputable authorities genuinely disagree; be sceptical of any source offering one universal number.
- **Both too little and too much carry risks** — deficiency at one end; skin damage (UV) or supplement toxicity (megadosing) at the other.

### Top Recommendations

- Get Vitamin D from **brief, casual sun** when season and latitude allow — or from a **supplement** when they don't.
- Never chase sun to the point of pinkness; protect skin with shade, clothing, and sunscreen for longer exposures.
- If you supplement, favour **Vitamin D3**, keep within recommended ranges, and stay below the **4,000 IU/day** adult upper limit.
- Aim for the balance point: *enough to avoid deficiency, not so much you court skin damage or toxicity.*

## Two Different Things — Vitamin D vs. Being Outdoors

When people say "sunshine is good for you," they blur two completely different biological stories, and keeping them apart is the backbone of this report.

- **Pathway A — Vitamin D benefits:** effects that travel through your blood marker, 25(OH)D. Raise it — by sun, food, or a pill — and you get the effect.
- **Pathway B — independent outdoor benefits:** everything else that happens *because you were outside* — daylight reaching your eyes, ultraviolet light on skin, movement, and the mental reset of green space. These have nothing to do with your Vitamin D level.

The simplest sorting tool is the **indoor-pill test**: *Would swallowing a Vitamin D capsule indoors, in the dark, reproduce this effect?* If yes → Pathway A. If no → Pathway B. This matters because several benefits the public files under "Vitamin D" fail the test — their real engine is light or activity.

| Benefit | Pathway | How it works | Why it sits here | Evidence strength |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Bone health & calcium balance | **A** | 25(OH)D drives calcium absorption, bone mineralization, and calms the parathyroid glands | A pill raising 25(OH)D reproduces it — the textbook role | Mechanism **well-established**; but extra supplements in already-replete adults did *not* cut fractures (LeBoff et al., 2022) |
| Muscle function & falls | **A** | 25(OH)D signalling in muscle | If real, runs through Vitamin D status | **Contested** — DO-HEALTH found no falls benefit in replete elders (Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2020) |
| Immune / autoimmune signalling | **A** | 25(OH)D modulates immune cells | Reproducible by supplement | **Emerging** — one trial cut incident autoimmune disease ~22% (Hahn et al., 2022); respiratory-infection protection strongest in the deficient (Martineau et al., 2017; attenuated in Jolliffe et al., 2021) |
| Circadian rhythm & sleep | **B** | Daylight on the eyes' non-image-forming receptors sets your body clock (*circadian entrainment* = keeping your internal clock in sync) | A pill emits no light — fails the test | Biologically plausible; **not confirmed in this report's cited trials** |
| Blood pressure & heart | **B** | Being outdoors lowers blood pressure; a proposed skin route is UV-triggered *nitric oxide* (a molecule that relaxes blood vessels), independent of Vitamin D | Vitamin D trials showed no heart benefit, so the effect isn't 25(OH)D-driven | Vitamin D→heart **well-established null** (Manson et al., 2019); forest-setting BP drop **emerging** (Ideno et al., 2017) |
| Mood, stress, wellbeing | **B** | Light, movement, and mental restoration in nature | Vitamin D did *not* prevent/treat depression | **Well-established it's NOT Vitamin D** (Okereke et al., 2020); nature-and-wellbeing link **emerging** (White et al., 2019; Kotera et al., 2022) |
| Cardiometabolic fitness | **B** | Physical activity done outdoors | A pill involves no movement | Outdoor-activity/wellbeing link **emerging** (White et al., 2019) |

**Commonly misattributed to Vitamin D (actually Pathway B):**

- *"Sunlight lifts my mood because of Vitamin D."* — A large trial found Vitamin D did not improve depression (Okereke et al., 2020); the lift comes from light, activity, and nature (White et al., 2019).
- *"Sun lowers my blood pressure via Vitamin D."* — Supplements showed no heart benefit (Manson et al., 2019); the outdoor effect is non-Vitamin-D (Ideno et al., 2017).
- *"Morning sun helps me sleep because of Vitamin D."* — That's daylight resetting your body clock through your eyes, entirely separate from Vitamin D.

*(An honesty note from our evidence review: the circadian-light and dermal-nitric-oxide mechanisms are biologically plausible but were **not confirmed by a dedicated trial in our source set** — treat them as promising hypotheses, not settled fact.)*

## Can Sunlight Even Make Vitamin D Right Now?

A fact that reframes all sun advice: **latitude and season are not fine-print caveats — they decide whether sun makes any Vitamin D at all.**

**The mechanism.** Vitamin D synthesis in skin is driven *only* by **ultraviolet-B (UVB)** light — a narrow slice of sunlight around **290–315 nanometres** (a nanometre is a billionth of a metre, a way of measuring a light wave's length). UVB converts a cholesterol-like molecule in your skin (7-dehydrocholesterol) into previtamin D3, which becomes Vitamin D3 (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023). The warmth and brightness you feel — longer-wavelength UVA and visible light — do **not** drive this reaction.

**Why the sun's height decides everything.** The key idea is the **solar zenith angle** — plainly, how high or low the sun sits. When the sun is low (early/late day, winter, far from the equator), its light slants through more atmosphere, which filters out short-wavelength UVB while letting longer wavelengths pass. Result: little or no UVB reaches the ground, so little or no Vitamin D is made — *even under a cloudless sky.*

**The three synthesis blockers to remember:**

- **Winter above ~35° latitude.** Modelling finds that above roughly 35° (north or south), midday sun makes little to no Vitamin D through winter — about October–March in the Northern Hemisphere (*Globally Estimated UVB Exposure Times*, Nutrients, 2024). To place yourself: 35°N runs near Los Angeles, Atlanta, and the Mediterranean coast of North Africa. Almost all of Europe, Canada, and the northern U.S. sit *above* it — a genuine "Vitamin D winter."
- **Ordinary window glass.** Standard glass blocks UVB while letting UVA through. Sitting in a sunny window, a car, or a sunroom produces **no** Vitamin D, however bright and warm it feels.
- **Correctly applied high-SPF sunscreen.** Sunscreen absorbs UVB, so thorough application drives synthesis to negligible levels. (In practice most people apply far less than the tested amount, which is why regular users aren't uniformly deficient — but that doesn't change the physics.)

**The consequence:** for a large share of the world, for a large part of the year, sun alone *cannot* supply Vitamin D. That physical ceiling — not personal habits — is exactly why food and supplements exist.

## How Much Sun — Practical Duration Guidance

**Core message:** for making Vitamin D you need only brief, casual exposure — a *fraction* of the time to turn pink — and only when UVB is actually present. When the sun can't deliver, a supplement is the safe alternative. You never need to tan or burn: skin Vitamin D production **peaks at about one-third of a minimal erythemal dose (MED)** — the smallest UV dose that causes faint redness (*erythema*) — so the target sits well short of pinkness (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023).

| Variable | Effect on required time | Burn-risk / feasibility note |
|---|---|---|
| **Skin type** (Fitzpatrick I → VI) | Longer for darker skin (≈2–5× for darkest vs. fairest; a working estimate) | Melanin also *raises* the sunburn threshold, so the target stays sub-burn for every skin type |
| **Latitude / season** (summer, low lat → winter, high lat) | Shorter when UV is strong; **impossible** in Vitamin D winter | Use the reported **UV Index (UVI ≥ 3)** as a rough "go" signal (a public-health convention, not a hard cutoff); if unattainable, supplement |
| **Time of day** (early/late → midday) | Shorter (more efficient) at midday | Midday carries the *highest* burn risk → keep it brief; off-peak sun is UVB-poor |
| **Body area exposed** (hands+face → arms+legs) | Shorter, roughly proportional to skin bared | More skin = less time = lower dose per patch = safer |

**Illustrative example (ranges only):** a fair-skinned adult at mid-latitude, in summer, around midday, with arms and legs bare, needs **on the order of 10–15 minutes** — comfortably below their burn threshold. A more deeply pigmented adult under the same conditions may need **substantially longer — on the order of 25–40 minutes.** Exact minutes depend on the *combination* of variables; treat all figures as ranges, never a universal count.

**Staying below the burn line.** Every recommended exposure stays well short of redness — around one-third to one-half of your MED, where Vitamin D production is near-maximal but sunburn is not approached (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023). No amount of tanning or burning is required or safe — and supplementation removes any need to chase risky sun.

## Supplements — How Much, and D2 vs. D3

**There is no single, unified "consensus" daily Vitamin D figure across health authorities.** Different expert bodies have published genuinely different numbers; be wary of any source claiming one universal dose.

| Authority | Who it addresses | Recommended intake | Underlying target |
|---|---|---|---|
| U.S. National Academy of Medicine (NAM/IOM) | General population, ages 1–70 | **~600 IU/day** | Keep 25(OH)D ≥20 ng/mL for bone health (Institute of Medicine, 2011) |
| U.S. National Academy of Medicine (NAM/IOM) | Older adults, ~71+ | **~800 IU/day** | Same bone-health basis, age-adjusted (Institute of Medicine, 2011) |
| Endocrine Society (2011) | At-risk / deficient individuals | **~1,500–2,000 IU/day** | Maintain a higher 25(OH)D (≥30 ng/mL) in at-risk groups (Holick et al., 2011) |

*(Unit note: **IU** = International Units; 1 microgram = 40 IU, so 600 IU = 15 µg.)*

**Why they disagree** — three different starting points:
1. **Different blood targets.** NAM judged ~20 ng/mL adequate; the Endocrine Society's 2011 guideline set a higher ≥30 ng/mL bar. A higher target needs a higher intake.
2. **Different populations.** NAM figures are population-wide for healthy people; the Endocrine Society's higher numbers target at-risk or deficient individuals.
3. **Different risk framing.** NAM took a conservative, bone-centred stance; the Endocrine Society weighed broader potential benefits in vulnerable groups.

**A genuine, unresolved three-way disagreement worth surfacing:** the U.S. Institute of Medicine set the "sufficient" blood threshold at **≥20 ng/mL**; the 2011 Endocrine Society set it higher at **≥30 ng/mL**; and — tellingly — the Endocrine Society **revised course in 2024**, declining to set a single serum target at all, and now advises *against* routine blood testing in healthy people and against exceeding standard intakes for healthy adults 19–74 (Demay et al., 2024). Three respected bodies, three different answers. The "right number" is genuinely contested, not settled — which is exactly why this report refuses to give you one.

**D2 vs. D3.** Supplements come in two forms:
- **Vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol)** — typically from plant/fungal sources.
- **Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol)** — the form your skin makes from sunlight, typically from animal sources.

**D3 is generally more effective than D2 at raising and maintaining 25(OH)D** (Tripkovic et al., 2012; Balachandar et al., 2023), most clearly with large intermittent doses; with modest daily dosing the gap narrows and can become statistically insignificant. **Practical takeaway:** where you have a choice, D3 is the more reliable pick — though steady daily D2 still raises your level.

*General information only, not individual medical advice — at-risk dosing should be guided by lab-confirmed 25(OH)D and a clinician.*

## What the Strong Evidence Actually Shows

For years, headlines suggested people with higher Vitamin D seemed healthier — lower rates of cancer, heart disease, and early death. Understanding *where that idea came from* matters when you decide whether to act on it.

**Why "more Vitamin D = healthier" is weaker evidence than it sounds.** Much early optimism came from **observational studies** — research that watches groups and notes patterns without assigning any treatment. The problem is **confounding**: when a hidden third factor influences both things you're comparing, making them look linked when neither causes the other. Two confounders stand out. First, **outdoor physical activity** — active people make more Vitamin D *and* their exercise protects health. Second, **general underlying health** — already-healthy people get outdoors more (raising Vitamin D), while the sick stay in. So higher Vitamin D can be a *sign* of an active life, not its *cause*.

**What happens when you test it properly.** A **randomized controlled trial (RCT)** randomly assigns people to Vitamin D or a dummy pill (placebo), isolating the supplement's true effect. Several large RCTs did exactly this:

- **Cancer:** VITAL found no reduction in new invasive cancers (hazard ratio 0.96, 95% CI 0.88–1.06 — a *null result*, meaning no significant effect) (Manson et al., 2019).
- **Heart disease:** VITAL found no significant reduction in major cardiovascular events (HR 0.97, 0.85–1.12) (Manson et al., 2019).
- **Death from any cause:** the large D-Health trial found no significant reduction in overall, cancer, or heart-disease mortality (Neale et al., 2022).
- **Broad outcomes in older adults:** DO-HEALTH found no significant benefit on its main combined health outcomes (Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2020).

Some exploratory sub-analyses hint at possible signals (for example, around cancer mortality), but these are not definitively established and should not be read as proof.

> **⚠️ Reliability caveat, in plain words.** *Correlation is not causation here.* Studies showing people with more Vitamin D are healthier do **not** prove that taking Vitamin D makes you healthier — much of that pattern reflects active, already-healthy people spending more time outdoors. On current evidence, **Vitamin D supplements are not proven to prevent cancer, heart disease, or premature death.** Vitamin D's well-established benefits are for bones and muscles. If you supplement, do so for those established reasons.

## Advice by Life Stage and Sex

Needs, risks, and the strength of the evidence shift across the life course and between the sexes. Where the evidence is thin, I say so plainly rather than fill the gap.

### Infants (0–12 months)
The distinctive fact: **breast milk is naturally low in Vitamin D**, so a breastfed baby cannot get enough from milk alone. This is precisely why an Adequate Intake of **400 IU/day** is set for the first year, with routine infant supplementation recommended (Institute of Medicine, 2011). Without it, infants risk **rickets**; the 2024 guideline reaffirms supplementation partly to prevent it (Demay et al., 2024). *(Pathway A.)*

### Children & Adolescents
Guidelines make an unusually specific call: **empiric daily supplementation for everyone aged 1–18** to prevent rickets and reduce acute respiratory infections, with trial doses of roughly 300–2,000 IU/day (Demay et al., 2024). The respiratory benefit is Pathway A and strongest in children who begin deficient (Martineau et al., 2017). The group's unique *risk* is on the sun side: **intermittent intense sun and childhood sunburn raise lifelong melanoma risk** — a UV harm, *not* a Vitamin D effect, since skin makes its full Vitamin D well below a burning dose (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023).

### Adult Premenopausal Women
The distinctive consideration is **pre-conception status**. Much landmark trial evidence doesn't apply here — VITAL enrolled women only from age 55 (Manson et al., 2019; LeBoff et al., 2022). What the evidence *does* show is high deficiency among reproductive-age women with darker skin or limited sun (e.g., 61.4% in UK South Asian populations; 56.6% in U.S. non-Hispanic Black vs. 26.4% in White adults) (O'Neill et al., 2018). Because status at conception shapes early pregnancy and the baby's initial stores, entering pregnancy already replete matters. *Honest limitation: direct randomized evidence on Vitamin D and menstrual-cycle or fertility outcomes is not in the sources reviewed, so I assert no such benefit.*

### Pregnancy (a distinct female sub-case)
The 2024 guideline **specifically recommends empiric supplementation during pregnancy**, citing evidence for reduced **pre-eclampsia** (a dangerous blood-pressure disorder of pregnancy), **preterm birth**, and neonatal complications, with trial doses roughly 600–5,000 IU/day (Demay et al., 2024). The unique items are these gestational outcomes plus building the baby's Vitamin D stores. Notably, pregnancy is one of the few situations for which this otherwise-cautious guideline endorses supplementation *even though* it advises against routine testing in healthy adults. *(Pathway A.)*

### Adult Postmenopausal Women
After menopause, bone loss accelerates, so extra Vitamin D feels like it should protect the skeleton. The distinctive finding tempers that: the VITAL fracture substudy (enrolling women from age 55) found **2,000 IU/day of D3 did not reduce total, non-vertebral, or hip fractures** in this generally replete group (total-fracture HR 0.98, 95% CI 0.89–1.08) (LeBoff et al., 2022). The take-home: supplementing *on top of* adequate status is not a fracture shield — the real value is preventing or correcting *genuine deficiency*, not high-dosing the already-replete.

### Adult Men
The reviewed evidence contains **no study isolating a sex-specific Vitamin D benefit for men**, so I invent none — a transparent gap in the evidence base. This group's distinct, sourced item is a **sun/UV risk**: **chronic, cumulative lifetime exposure** raises the risk of **squamous cell carcinoma**, alongside UVA-driven **photoaging** (skin wrinkling and leathering) (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023) — a different mechanism from the intermittent-burn→melanoma pattern noted for children. Practical upshot: men with high lifelong outdoor or occupational exposure benefit most from routine skin protection, while still making ample Vitamin D well below a burning dose. This is a UV harm, *not* a Vitamin D effect.

### Older Adults (65+)
The distinctive item: the 2024 guideline **endorses empiric supplementation specifically for adults 75 and over**, citing a possible mortality reduction — the only adult band for which it recommends routine supplementation (Demay et al., 2024). Recommended intake also rises to about **800 IU/day at 71+** (Institute of Medicine, 2011), reflecting that older skin makes less. But keep expectations modest: DO-HEALTH tested 2,000 IU/day in adults 70+ and found **no significant benefit on falls, physical function, blood pressure, cognition, or infection** (Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2020). Falls and frailty are real concerns, but Vitamin D is *not* an established falls remedy for the already-replete.

## Too Little vs. Too Much — Risks and How to Avoid Them

Vitamin D health follows a **U-shaped curve**: risk rises at both ends. A guiding principle: the sun that makes Vitamin D never has to reach a sunburn, and **burning is never recommended** — it adds skin-cancer and aging risk without adding Vitamin D.

**Risks of too little (deficiency).** The well-established core is skeletal (Pathway A): **rickets** in children, **osteomalacia** in adults, and a contribution to **osteoporosis and fractures** in later life (Institute of Medicine, 2011; Demay et al., 2024). Prolonged deficiency also drives **secondary hyperparathyroidism** (overworked parathyroid glands draining bone to defend blood calcium) — an established consequence. **Muscle weakness and falls** are plausibly linked, but supplementing replete elders showed no falls benefit (Bischoff-Ferrari et al., 2020) — so *correcting deficiency may help; high-dosing the replete does not.* Links to immune function and mood are largely **correlational, not proven cause-and-effect** (Vitamin D did not prevent depression: Okereke et al., 2020). **Most at risk:** darker skin, high latitude/winter, housebound or institutionalized people, heavily covered clothing, obesity (fat tissue sequesters Vitamin D), exclusively breastfed infants, and older adults.

**Risks of too much — two distinct causes.**
- **Too much UV/sun:** sunburn, photoaging, and skin cancer (basal cell and squamous cell carcinoma from chronic exposure; melanoma linked to intermittent intense exposure and sunburns) (Raymond-Lezman & Riskin, 2023).
- **Too much supplement:** **Vitamin D toxicity (hypervitaminosis D)** causing **hypercalcemia** — dangerously high blood calcium — with nausea, excessive urination, confusion, and kidney stones. Critically, **toxicity comes from over-supplementation, essentially never from sun**: the skin self-regulates and degrades excess. Toxicity requires sustained intakes far above the **Tolerable Upper Intake Level of 4,000 IU/day** for adults (Institute of Medicine, 2011).

**Risk → mitigation, at a glance:**

| Risk | Concrete mitigation |
|---|---|
| Rickets / osteomalacia / deficiency fractures | Ensure adequate intake via brief sub-burn sun *or* a supplement; correct confirmed deficiency |
| Deficiency-related weakness/falls | Correct genuine deficiency; don't expect benefit from high-dosing the replete |
| Sunburn, photoaging, skin cancer | Avoid burning; limit peak midday exposure; use shade, clothing, and sunscreen after the short synthesis window |
| Vitamin D toxicity / hypercalcemia | Stay near recommended intakes; don't megadose; respect the 4,000 IU/day upper limit |

**Both-directions strategy.** *Timing:* aim for brief, sub-burn exposure; midday is most efficient but riskiest, so keep it short. *Sunscreen:* your primary defence against burn, aging, and cancer — and because it blocks the UVB synthesis needs, get your Vitamin D from a supplement instead and protect your skin freely. *Supplementation as the safe fallback:* when synthesis is impossible or unsafe (Vitamin D winter, darker skin, covered or indoor lifestyles, or anyone advised to minimize sun), supplements deliver Vitamin D with zero UV risk — just stay within safe limits.

## The Bottom Line

- **Bones are the sure thing.** Vitamin D's proven job is protecting your skeleton. The cancer/heart/longevity claims did not survive rigorous trials.
- **Outdoors and Vitamin D overlap but differ.** Much of what's great about being outside — mood, sleep, blood pressure, activity — is *not* the vitamin. A pill won't give you those; going outside will.
- **Sun is seasonal chemistry.** Above ~35° latitude in winter, and behind glass or high-SPF sunscreen, sun makes little or no Vitamin D. Plan around that.
- **A little sun is plenty; never burn.** Brief, casual exposure — adjusted for skin tone, season, and how much skin is bare — does the job well below the burn line.
- **Supplement sensibly.** No single agreed dose exists; NAM says ~600–800 IU/day, the Endocrine Society ~1,500–2,000 IU/day for at-risk people. Choose D3, and stay under 4,000 IU/day.
- **Aim for the middle.** Enough to avoid deficiency; not so much you risk your skin or your kidneys.
- **Check current guidance.** Numbers here reflect my training knowledge, not a live search — confirm specifics with your clinician.

### A note on how this report handles disagreement and gaps

This report deliberately preserves rather than smooths over several genuine tensions the underlying evidence review surfaced:

- **Three authorities disagree on the "sufficient" blood level** (IOM ≥20 ng/mL vs. Endocrine 2011 ≥30 ng/mL vs. Endocrine 2024 declining to set one). No single "correct" threshold is asserted.
- **The big RCTs are largely null** for cancer/CVD/mortality/fracture/falls in already-replete people — the positive signals (autoimmune disease, respiratory infection in the deficient) are narrower and hedged, not upgraded to "proven prevention."
