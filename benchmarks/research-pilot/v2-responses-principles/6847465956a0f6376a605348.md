## PART 2 — BACKGROUND & PRODUCT PORTFOLIO BRIEF

**Company identity.** Palantir Technologies Inc. trades under the ticker **PLTR on the Nasdaq Global Select Market**, where it began trading on **November 26, 2024** (announced Nov 14, 2024). It went public earlier via a **direct listing on the New York Stock Exchange on September 30, 2020**, so the accurate current framing is **Nasdaq: PLTR**, noting the 2020 NYSE debut. The company is a U.S. software firm selling **data-integration and analytics platforms** to government and commercial customers, headquartered in Denver, Colorado (per Palantir's platform pages and Wikipedia).

**Founding timeline.** Palantir was founded in **2003** (incorporated May 6, 2003) by five people: **Peter Thiel, Alex Karp, Joe Lonsdale, Stephen Cohen, and Nathan Gettings**. Thiel co-founded the company about a year after selling PayPal to eBay; Karp, the long-time CEO, met Thiel at Stanford Law. The name references the "seeing stone" (palantír) from Tolkien. The CIA-affiliated venture firm **In-Q-Tel invested roughly $2 million** in the early phase and served as an early customer, making the intelligence community Palantir's first proving ground (per Wikipedia and Fast Company). A concise arc: **2003** founded → mid-2000s In-Q-Tel and intelligence-community backing → Gotham matures for defense → Foundry expands to commercial → **Sept 30, 2020** NYSE direct listing → **April 2023** AIP launch → **Nov 26, 2024** move to Nasdaq → 2024–2025 revenue acceleration.

**Product portfolio.** Palantir sells three core platforms.

- **Gotham** is the original product, built for **government, defense, and intelligence** users. It integrates large volumes of disparate data and makes it searchable and analyzable so operators — analysts, military, and intelligence staff — can accelerate operational decisions. Palantir now describes it as an "AI-ready operating system" for operators (per palantir.com/platforms/gotham).
- **Foundry** is a **data-integration and data-operations platform for enterprises** (commercial and government). It centers on the **Foundry Ontology**, a semantic layer mapping an organization's real-world objects, actions, and data, so teams can harmonize data, author logic, run analytics, and automate workflows. Where Gotham targets government operational analysis, Foundry is the general-purpose enterprise backbone (per palantir.com/platforms/foundry).
- **The Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP)**, **launched April 2023**, connects large language models and generative AI to an organization's private data and operations. It provides governed access to a range of LLMs, tools to build LLM-driven functions, and the ability to create AI agents and automations that **execute across enterprise processes** — AI that can act, not merely chat. AIP is positioned as the driver of Palantir's commercial acceleration (per palantir.com/platforms/aip). *(Palantir also markets Apollo, a continuous-deployment platform; the three above are the customer-facing analytics portfolio.)*

**Government vs. commercial split.** Palantir reports two segments: **Government** (defense, intelligence, and other public-sector agencies, U.S. and allied) and **Commercial** (private-sector enterprises). Historically government-heavy, the business is now led in growth by **U.S. commercial**, driven by AIP. For FY2025, total revenue of **$4.48B (+56%)** split roughly **Government ~54% / Commercial ~46%** (per a segment aggregator). The widely reported U.S.-only breakdowns are **U.S. government $1.86B (+55%)** and **U.S. commercial $1.47B (+109%)**. The takeaway: government remains the larger single segment, but commercial — especially U.S. commercial — is growing far faster, which is the core "why they succeeded" narrative tied to AIP.

**The single "why."** Palantir's 2024–2025 success is one story told at several altitudes. **AIP-driven U.S. commercial demand** accelerated quarter after quarter, powered operationally by the **boot-camp funnel and customer-count growth** and booked into a fast-rising **backlog**. That commercial engine lifted **total revenue growth from +29% to +56%** while **profits scaled roughly eightfold** at a 127% Rule of 40 — growth that was both fast and GAAP-profitable. Those fundamentals are what the market then re-rated: a roughly +1,005% share-price move, a ~$300B+ market cap, and admission to both the **S&P 500 and Nasdaq-100**. In short: AIP converted commercial demand into accelerating, profitable revenue, and the market and major indices ratified it. None of the founding history, government-mission, or leadership material is load-bearing to this explanation.

## PART 6 — THE DRAFT ARTICLE

# From Government Contractor to Index Darling: Inside Palantir's 2024–2025 Breakout

*How an accelerating, AI-driven commercial business turned a once-obscure software firm into a ~$300 billion company — and what actually drove the run.*

**Where the story starts.** At the start of 2024, shares of Palantir Technologies traded at roughly $16 (per Macrotrends). Two years later, at the end of 2025, they closed near $178 (per StatMuse) — a gain of roughly 1,000% over the period. Somewhere in the middle of that run, in September 2024, Palantir was admitted to the S&P 500 — the club of America's largest public companies — an unusual arrival for a firm many investors still thought of, if they thought of it at all, as a secretive government contractor. How did a company built in the mid-2000s for spies and soldiers become one of the market's defining stories of 2024–2025? The short answer is not about mission, politics, or personalities. It is about numbers: accelerating revenue, real profits, and a commercial business growing at a pace rarely seen at this scale, powered by the company's newest product.

**Background: who Palantir actually is.** Palantir Technologies Inc. (ticker: **PLTR**) is a U.S. software company that sells data-integration and analytics platforms to both government agencies and private businesses. It was founded in 2003 by a group of five — most famously the investor Peter Thiel and the company's long-time CEO, Alex Karp — and its earliest backing included a roughly $2 million investment from In-Q-Tel, the CIA-affiliated venture arm (per Wikipedia and press coverage). The name is a nod to the "seeing stones" of *The Lord of the Rings*. A quick note on the ticker, because it is a common trip-up: Palantir went public via a **direct listing on the New York Stock Exchange on September 30, 2020**, but it later **moved its listing to the Nasdaq, where trading began November 26, 2024** (per the company's BusinessWire announcement and Nasdaq trader notices). The accurate framing today is **Nasdaq: PLTR**.

Palantir sells three core platforms. **Gotham** is the original product, built for government, defense, and intelligence users — think of it as a tool that pulls scattered, messy data into one place so operators can search it and make faster decisions. **Foundry** is the commercial counterpart: a data "operating backbone" for large enterprises that harmonizes disconnected data into a single, usable model so teams can run analytics and automate workflows. **The Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP)**, launched in April 2023, is the newest and the reason for much of the recent excitement — in plain language, AIP plugs modern AI (the large language models behind tools like chatbots) into a company's own private data and operations, so the AI can actually *do* things rather than merely chat (all per Palantir's platform pages). Palantir reports in two segments, **government** and **commercial**. Government remains the larger single bucket — roughly 54% of FY2025 revenue against 46% commercial — but commercial, especially U.S. commercial, is the fast-growing part. That mix is the whole plot.

**The 2024–2025 scoreboard.** Start with the market's verdict. Palantir's share price rose from about $16 at the start of 2024 to about $76 at the end of 2024, and on to about $178 at the end of 2025 (start- and end-2024 per Macrotrends; end-2025 per StatMuse). By mid-2026 the company carried a market capitalization near $300 billion (roughly $309.7B per stockanalysis.com; $315.4B per Capital.com, as of July 4, 2026). One honest note on the trajectory: the stock cooled in the first half of 2026, trading near $130 as of July 4, 2026 (per CNBC and stockanalysis.com) — below its end-2025 close and its 52-week high of about $208. This was a spectacular run, but not a straight line and not still at its peak.

The fundamentals underneath the stock make the re-rating more than hype. Revenue did something unusual for a company this size: it **accelerated**. Total revenue was **$2.87 billion in FY2024, up 29%** (per Palantir's investor-relations release), then **$4.48 billion in FY2025, up 56%** (per CNBC and the BusinessWire earnings release). Profitability tells a parallel story. GAAP net income (profit under standard accounting rules) scaled from roughly **$210 million in FY2023** — its first full year of GAAP profitability — to roughly **$462 million in FY2024** and roughly **$1.63 billion in FY2025** (FY2023 per Palantir's 8-K; FY2025 per CNBC). That is profit growing roughly eightfold in two years, and it was profitable growth: the company's "Rule of 40" score — a software-industry health check that adds revenue growth to profit margin, where anything above 40 is strong — reached **127% for FY2025** (per Investing.com's summary of the Q4 2025 slides). The right frame is profit *scaling*, not a first-ever turn to black. Finally, the validation stamps: Palantir was **added to the S&P 500**, effective September 23, 2024 (per S&P Global), and to the **Nasdaq-100**, effective December 23, 2024 (per Nasdaq). Index inclusion matters partly because it forces index-tracking funds to buy the stock, and partly because S&P 500 eligibility requires a track record of profitability.

**The AIP engine: the commercial breakout.** If the scoreboard is the *what*, AIP-driven commercial growth is the *how*. The clearest single number in the story is the U.S. commercial revenue growth curve, which accelerated every quarter: **+54% year over year in Q3 2024, +64% in Q4 2024, +71% in Q1 2025, +93% in Q2 2025, and +121% in Q3 2025** (per Palantir's quarterly 8-K and BusinessWire earnings releases). For the full year, U.S. commercial revenue reached **$1.47 billion in FY2025, up 109%** — roughly doubling. The customer base expanded to match: total customer count grew in the high-30s to mid-40s percent year over year, and U.S. commercial customer count reached **530 in Q3 2025 (up 65%)** and **571 in Q4 2025 (up 49%)** — two different quarters, not a contradiction.

How does a company sign up commercial customers this fast? A big part of the answer is a sales mechanism Palantir calls the **AIP "boot camp"** — a short, hands-on session, often just a few days, in which Palantir's engineers sit with a prospective customer and build a working AI tool on that customer's *own* data. The pitch is to turn skeptics into buyers by showing results in days rather than slides (per Palantir's blog and program page). The conversions the company describes make the mechanism concrete. On its Q3 2024 earnings call, Palantir cited **TrinityRail**, which reached a functional AI workflow in about three months with a stated "$30 million impact"; **Associated Materials**, which implemented more than ten business use cases in nine months and lifted an on-time delivery rate from 40% to 90%; and an unnamed equipment-rental firm whose account value grew roughly twelvefold after converting to an enterprise agreement. In 2025, named expansions included a five-year partnership widening with automotive supplier **Lear** and a generative-AI underwriting collaboration with insurer **AIG**. Crucially, this demand is being *booked*: U.S. commercial total contract value rose to **$1.31 billion in Q3 2025, up 342%**, and remaining deal value — the backlog of contracted work not yet recognized as revenue — reached **$3.63 billion in Q3 2025 (up 199%)** and roughly **$4.38 billion by Q4 2025 (up 145%)** (per BusinessWire and the Q4 2025 slides).

**Why it worked, ranked.** The drivers rank by how directly and powerfully the evidence supports them; some figures *show* success, while others *caused* it. **(1) AIP-driven U.S. commercial revenue acceleration** — growth climbing from +54% to +121% year over year across five quarters, and +109% for FY2025, is the single most direct engine, and Palantir attributes it to AIP demand. **(2) Profit scaling and profit quality** — GAAP net income from ~$210M (FY2023) to ~$1.63B (FY2025) at a 127% Rule of 40 shows the growth was durable and high-margin, and it gated S&P 500 eligibility. **(3) Total-company revenue acceleration** — company-wide growth speeding up from 29% to 56% proves the commercial engine was big enough to lift the whole business, largely a consequence of driver 1. **(4) Share-price and market-cap surge with index inclusion** — a roughly 10x move to a ~$300B market cap plus S&P 500 and Nasdaq-100 membership is the most visible marker, but it mostly reflects the fundamentals. **(5) Contract backlog and deal momentum** — record TCV and rising RDV show demand contracted for the future, a leading indicator. **(6) Customer growth and the boot-camp mechanism** — fast-rising customer counts and the conversion funnel are the genuine *how* behind driver 1. Notably, none of this rests on Palantir's founding lore, government mission, or leadership persona.

**The takeaway.** For a business or investor reader, the story reduces to a single sentence: **AIP converted commercial demand into accelerating, profitable revenue, and the market and major indices ratified it.** Palantir did the two things software investors prize most at the same time — grew faster *and* earned real GAAP profits — on the back of a genuinely new product cycle in enterprise AI. The forward-looking balance is equally important. As of mid-2026 the stock sat near $130, well below its end-2025 close of about $178 and its 52-week high near $208 (per CNBC and stockanalysis.com) — a reminder that a richly valued growth stock re-prices quickly. The bull case is that the commercial acceleration and swelling backlog carry forward; the bear case is that expectations baked into a ~$300 billion valuation are hard to keep beating.
