# THE LONG WAY HOME
### *The Boy Between Worlds*

*A novel of a boy the two ages could not stop trading — an age of iron and mud and banners, and an age of neon and rain and wire — and of the one door he was always, secretly, being drawn toward.*

---

## A Note to the Reader

You are about to meet a boy who never gets to choose where he goes next — and, in a small way, neither will he choose for you.

Here is how this book works. Every chapter but the last is a doorway standing on its own. You may walk through them in any order you please: shuffle them, open to whichever title calls to you, follow a whim, dip in for a single world and set the book down, or read straight through from front to back. There is no wrong path. Each chapter is a complete adventure set in one world — an age of iron and mud and banners, or an age of neon and rain and wire — and it asks nothing of you that it hasn't already handed you itself.

That is the promise, and it is the boy's own life made into a way of reading. Every chapter meets him fresh, tells you plainly who he is, and shows you how the two worlds keep trading him back and forth: one breath in a keep of cold stone, then a taste of copper, a high ringing behind the teeth, and he is somewhere of glass and static instead. He never has time to prepare. He lands disoriented but whole — remembering himself, wanting only one thing. And so do you arrive, each time: dropped clean into a place you have never been, needing no earlier chapter to make sense of the one in front of you. Nothing you skip will leave you stranded. Nothing you read first can spoil what comes later. There is no hidden "correct" order to find, because there isn't one to miss.

There is exactly one exception, and it is the only rule this book asks you to keep.

The final chapter — "The Final Landing" — is fixed. It sits last, always, no matter which way you wandered to reach it, and it cannot be moved, swapped, or read out of turn. Save it for the end. It is the single chapter written to come after all the others: the place where the trading stops and the story closes for good. And because it was built to gather up whatever the earlier worlds gave you — however many you read, in whatever order — it will land as a true and satisfying ending for you, and for every reader who found their own way here. One conclusion, waiting faithfully at the end of every possible path.

So begin wherever you like. Trust that the boy will find you, world after world, and tell you again who he is and what he is looking for. Wander as long as you please. Only, when you feel the story ready to close, turn last to The Final Landing — and let it bring him home.

# The Ebbmark Holdfasts

The water was already at my shins when the world finished arriving, and for one long breath I couldn't remember my own name.

That was the worst part, every time. Not the cold that came up through my bones like I'd been dead an hour, not the taste of old pennies flooding my mouth — the forgetting. I stood on a stone causeway with the sea sucking at my legs and I was nobody, a shape full of salt water, until my thumb found the little brass button sewn into the seam of my coat. Worn smooth. Warm when nothing else was. I pressed it the way you'd press a bruise to make sure it was still yours to feel.

*Kes*, it said back. *You're Kes. Thirteen. You're the boy the two worlds keep trading back and forth.*

One breath I'd be somewhere of wire and neon and rain that fell sideways off towers; then the copper would come and the high ringing behind my teeth, and I'd be gone, dropped into somewhere of iron and mud and cold instead — always the far side from where I'd stood, always a place I had never seen and would never see twice. I didn't choose it. I couldn't stop it. I could only land, and figure out fast what kind of trouble I'd landed in.

"So," I said under my breath, and my voice came out cracked. "Water, then."

I was on a causeway between islands. That was the first thing the map behind my eyes assembled — I always did that first, before I trusted anything, sweep a place for its exits and its bosses and whatever was worth stealing. Low humps of black rock rose out of a grey sea, and on each hump crouched a hall of tarred timber, roofs weighted down with fishing net and stones so the storms couldn't peel them. Smoke leaned flat off the chimneys. Somewhere a bell was ringing, slow and wet, and I understood without being told that the bell meant *the tide is turning* and that I was standing exactly where a person should not be standing when it turned.

The causeway was already an inch deep and rising.

I ran for the nearest island, boots throwing up sheets of water, and made the shingle just as the sea closed over the stones behind me. Up the beach, a knot of people had gathered around a rock that stood out past the low-tide line — a flat black table of stone with iron rings hammered into it. And chained to the rings, wrists raw, was a girl about my age.

I stopped. My whole chest went tight in the stupid, immediate way it always does. That's my worst habit, if you want to know: I attach too fast. I see one person drowning in front of me and everything else in the world goes quiet. I knew, even then, standing on that cold beach, that the copper would come and tear me out and leave her behind — and I still couldn't make my feet turn around.

"What is this," I said, to no one, to everyone.

A big man in a sealskin cloak answered without looking at me. He had a face like weathered wood and a sea-lord's heavy silver at his throat. "A gift to the deep," he said, "so it stills its temper and lets us cross to the far holdfast dry-shod. She's a thrall. She costs me nothing."

The girl on the rock looked at me. She wasn't crying. She'd got past crying to the flat, furious calm of someone doing arithmetic. "Tide's under an hour," she said to me, plainly, like she was reporting weather. "It comes to my chin first. Then over."

"That's — no," I said. Eloquent. My mouth does that when I'm scared; it reaches for a joke or it reaches for nothing. "You can't. That's not how tides work. They don't take *bribes.*"

The sea-lord's men laughed, which was worse than if they'd hit me.

Here is the thing about a place like this. I'd read it in one sweep and I already knew its shape: islands shrinking a hand's-width every generation, the sea climbing and climbing, and men down to fighting each other over the last dry acres. When the ground itself is disappearing under you, you'll believe anything that promises to slow it down. You'll chain a girl to a rock. Fear makes people do arithmetic with other people's lives.

I couldn't out-argue their fear. I'd learned that much across more landings than I could count. But I could out-*think* the tide, because a tide is just a clock, and I am very good at clocks.

"That bell," I said. "The wet one. It rings the safe hours?"

The girl's eyes sharpened. "Rings when the causeways drown. Three tolls means the deep channel's open."

"How long between the first toll and the third?"

"...Time to say a long prayer."

Not much. But something. I went down the beach like I was just a foolish boy come to gawp, and I crouched by the offering-rock as if to pity her, and under my breath, fast, I said: "The chains are iron rings on wet stone. Iron on stone rusts. Which ring's the reddest?"

She didn't miss a beat. Improviser knows improviser. "Left. Bottom bolt. It weeps rust every low tide."

I had a bone needle in my coat — I always seem to have *something*, a bent bit of this, a scrap of that; the world strips my weapons every jump but it can never quite strip my hands — and a length of tarred cord I'd lifted off a net-pile on the way down without deciding to. A rusted bolt in wet stone is a bolt that wants to leave. You don't pull it. You work it, back and forth, letting the rust do the breaking, and you wait for the sea to help.

"When the water comes," I murmured, "it'll lift the rock's weight off the bolt for half a second at each swell. That's when it gives. You have to time it to the swell, not to your strength."

"You're mad," she said. But she was already feeling for the bolt with her raw fingers.

We waited. That was the awful part — we had to *let the sea come*, let it climb her legs, her waist, because the same water that would drown her was the only lever big enough to free her. The sea-lord and his men drifted back up the beach to their dry hall, satisfied, not even watching, because to them the ending was already written.

The bell tolled once. Far off, a causeway went under.

The girl worked the bolt in time with the swells, and I lay flat in the freezing shallows with the cord looped through the loosening ring, hauling on the *out*-drag of each wave, adding my small pull to the sea's enormous one. Water in my mouth. Water in my eyes. My hands went from cold to nothing at all.

The bell tolled twice.

"It's moving," she gasped. "It's — "

The bolt came free in a curl of orange rust and a rope of my own breath, and the chain slithered off the rock, and I got an arm under her and we half-swam, half-fell toward the shingle as the offering-rock vanished under the grey. We crawled up the beach on our hands and knees like two half-drowned dogs, and lay there heaving, and above us the third toll rang out across the water — the deep channel open, the safe hour begun, the thing the sea-lord had chained her up to buy arriving all on its own, for free, the way it always would have.

"It was going to open anyway," she said, staring at the sky. "The tide. It doesn't take bribes. You were right."

"I'm right about clocks," I said. "Not much else."

She turned her head and looked at me, really looked, and I felt it start — the tightness in my chest, the wanting to promise her something. And that was exactly when the copper came.

It flooded up under my tongue, bright and sudden, and the ringing began to climb behind my teeth, high and thin, and I knew, with the sick certainty I'd never once been wrong about, that I had a breath left in this world. Maybe two.

"Listen," I said fast, sitting up, gripping her wrist where the iron had marked it. "The rock frees anyone the same way. Rust and swells. Teach them. Teach everyone. Nobody chains anybody to that stone again, you hear me? *Nobody.*"

"Who are you?" she said. "Where are you even from?"

"I'm going to go home," I told her, which was the only true answer I had and no answer at all. "I keep — trying to get home." My thumb was already on the brass button, and the world was already thinning at its edges like fog burning off. "Tell me your name. Quick. So I keep it."

"Sela," she said.

The ringing went to a scream. The cold came up through me like the tide had, all the way to the roots of my teeth, and the grey beach and the girl named Sela folded away into copper and nothing —

— and I understood, in the last clear instant, the thing that world had come to teach me. That I could carry someone off the sinking ground. I could pull them up out of the water and onto the shingle and buy them their safe hour. But I couldn't save the ground. The ground was always, always going under. All you got to keep was the person, and then not even them.

*So: gone, then.* And somewhere ahead of me, a new world I had never seen was already waiting to be cold in.

# Arrears

The rain came at me sideways, and I woke up already falling.

I hit a walkway grate on my hands and knees, and for one blank second I didn't know my own name. That was the worst part, every time — the falling, the cold that goes all the way into the marrow, and that thin scraped moment where I was nobody at all. Then my thumb found the little brass button in my pocket, worn smooth, the one thing that always comes with me, and my name came back like a light snapping on. Kes. Thirteen. The boy the two worlds keep trading back and forth — one breath I'm somewhere of iron and mud, the next my mouth floods copper, a high ringing climbs behind my teeth, and I'm gone, dropped into some age of neon and wire I've never seen. No say in it. No map. Just the button that refuses to point north, and the one thing I always want, wherever I land: home.

So: neon, then.

I got a look at the world. It went straight up. Towers of grimy light stacked so high the rain seemed to come from a ceiling instead of a sky, every level bolted onto the one below it like the city had been built by people who'd run out of ground and just kept going anyway. Green and pink signage bled across wet chrome and pooled in the walkway grates. Somewhere far down there was water — real water, black and shining, lapping at buildings that had clearly once had streets and now had canals. The whole place breathed damp. It smelled of ozone and hot plastic and something sweeter underneath, like flowers left too long in a vase.

And everything hummed. That was the thing I noticed after the exits and the drop-offs and the fact that I had no shoes worth the name — the hum. It came up through the grate into my knees, a low electric buzz, and it seemed to come from the people too. A woman shouldered past me with a soft mechanical whine in her chest, in and out, in and out, like a bellows.

A drone the size of a dinner plate dropped out of the rain and put a cold blue light on my face.

"Unregistered," it said, in a voice like a smiling knife. "Identify or clear the concourse."

I did the thing I always do when a new world holds a blade to me. I bowed. Old habit, wrong century — but a bow buys you a second, and a second is sometimes all you get. "Just passing through," I said. "I'm nobody. Truly. Ask anyone."

"Unregistered persons on the Arrears concourse are subject to—"

"He's mine," said a girl. "Slow your circuits."

She came out of the light-and-rain like she'd been cut from it. Older than me, maybe sixteen, in a coat that had been patched so many times the patches had patches. Her eyes were ordinary but there was a faint line of light under the skin of her throat, blue, pulsing, and when she talked I heard it under her voice — that same soft bellows-whine everyone here seemed to carry. In and out. In and out.

"He's my runner," she told the drone. "New hire. Registration's queued." She flashed something, a chip, a card, I couldn't tell — the drone's light flickered, considered, and lost interest. It lifted back into the rain.

The girl looked at me. "You are the worst-dressed person I have seen in a month," she said, "and I have seen the underlevels. What are you, off a cargo hauler? You've got that look. Lost." She said the word like a diagnosis.

"Something like that," I said. "Where is this?"

"Arrears." She said it flat, like it wasn't worth explaining, then must have seen my face because she softened a hair. "The city. This part of it. You breathe the flood-damp long enough you need help doing it — nobody's lungs were built for this air. So." She tapped her own throat, where the light was. "You rent the help."

"Rent," I repeated.

"Everything here you rent. Your lungs, your eyes if they went, your legs, your heart if you're rich enough to wear out the one you were born with. Miss a payment—" She snapped her fingers. "—and the corp shuts off what you're leasing. Just like that. Your arm goes dead. Your pump quits." Her hand drifted back to her throat, and I saw her check the light there the way I check my button. To be sure it was still on. "I'm three behind," she said, quieter, like she hadn't meant to. Then, sharp again: "You want to eat or not?"

I was starving. I usually am, the first hour in a new world. Falling takes something out of you.

Her name was Vane. She ran packages nobody wanted traced — that was the whole job, she explained over a bowl of noodles at a stall wedged under a dripping overhang, the broth so hot it burned the copper taste out of my mouth at last. "Corps need things moved they can't be seen moving," she said. "So they hire people like me. Deniable. If I get caught, I was never there, they never sent me, and my pump gets shut off from a hundred floors up before I can say a word in my own defense." She slurped. "Great career. Recommend it."

"Why do it?"

She looked at me like I was very young, which I was. "Because tonight's job pays out my lungs. Buys them. Outright. Not a lease — mine. Own the air in my own chest for the first time in my life." Her eyes went somewhere far off. "Three years I've been in arrears. One run and I'm free."

Here is the flaw in me I can never seem to fix: I attach too fast. I know it. I know the copper's going to come for me sooner or later, in an hour or a day, and rip me out of wherever I've made myself at home, and leave whoever I've decided to save standing there alone. It's happened enough times that I ought to have learned to keep my hands in my pockets and my heart shut. I never do. She said one run and I'm free and something in me just — reached for her. Reached for the whole idea of somebody buying back their own body.

"I'll help," I said.

"You can't."

"I'm good at getting into places," I said. "And out of them. Try me."

She should have said no. Instead she looked at the button I was turning over and over in my fingers — I hadn't even noticed I was doing it — and something in my face must have convinced her, or she was just tired, or three years of arrears makes a person say yes to any pair of hands. "Fine," she said. "But you do what I say. And when it goes wrong — it always goes a little wrong — you don't freeze."

I don't freeze. I improvise. It's the other thing I'm good at. Give me a bent piece of wire or a length of cable or a bone needle — I've had all three, in different centuries — and I'll pick you a lock or set you a snare. Tools change. The trick of using whatever's in reach doesn't.

The run went like this.

The package was a case the size of my two fists, sealed, humming faintly like everything else in that city. We took it down. Down and down, through lifts that stank of coolant, past levels where the neon got sicker and the water got closer, until we were walking on gangways bolted just above the black flood, and the towers rose over us like a canyon made of light. Brown-out down here, the signage stuttering, half the walkways dark. Vane moved fast and quiet and I kept two steps back and mapped as I went — exits, sightlines, which gaps in the railing dropped into water and which dropped into machinery. You learn a place by its ways out.

"Handoff's at the old pump station," Vane said. "In and out. Client takes the case, pays my account, my lungs go green, we go home." She almost smiled. "Simple."

It was not simple.

The pump station was a cavern of dead machines, the flood licking at their bases, one blue emergency light turning everything the color of a bruise. The client was waiting — a man in a coat too clean for down here, two others behind him with the flat stillness of people who hurt people for a living. And I knew, the way I always know within a breath of trouble, that this was wrong. Because the man wasn't looking at the case.

He was looking at Vane's throat.

"Change of terms," he said. "The case, and the pump. That model's worth more than your payout. We'll take both."

Take both. I watched Vane understand it. Not just no payout — no lungs. They'd cut the leased pump out of her throat and leave her three floors from breathable air with nothing to breathe with. The case had never been the job. She'd been the job. Deniable girl, deniable death, and a piece of hardware they could resell.

"Run," Vane said, but there were two men between her and the only lift.

Here's what I had. A pump station full of dead machines. A case that hummed. A flood two feet below the gangway. And a lifetime of walking into strange worlds with nothing and making something out of it.

"Hey," I said, loud, stepping into the blue light with the case held up over the water. "You want this?"

The clean man's eyes snapped to it. Everyone's did. That's the thing about a thing everyone wants — hold it over a long enough drop and you own the room for exactly as long as your arm holds out.

"You drop that," the man said, very carefully, "and your account—"

"I don't have an account," I said, and grinned at him, and I could hear the shake in my own voice underneath the grin because I was terrified, I'm always terrified, I just crack the joke anyway. "I'm nobody. I told the drone. Nobody, off a cargo hauler. You can't shut off a thing I don't have."

That stopped him. He'd built his whole world on the leash — miss a payment, lose an arm — and here was a boy the leash didn't reach. For one second he didn't know what to do with me.

One second was all I needed.

I threw the case — not at him, past him, out over the black water — and every head turned to follow it, and I hit the manual release on the nearest dead pump, the big red lever I'd clocked the second we walked in, because old machines all have a lever and levers are the same in every century. The pump woke with a roar. A wall of pressured water blew sideways off the gangway, and the flood surged, and the whole rotten walkway lurched, and in the noise and the spray and the men grabbing for railings I had Vane's wrist and we were gone, up the lift, up and up, the doors closing on the sound of the case hitting water far below.

We didn't stop until the air got clean.

Vane leaned against a wall a hundred floors up, hand at her throat, the blue light there still pulsing — still hers, for now, uncut. She was laughing and it kept catching in her chest with the whine of the pump, laugh and whine, laugh and whine. "You threw the case," she said. "Do you know what that case was worth? You threw a year of my life in the water."

"They were going to take your lungs," I said. "I threw the thing they wanted so they'd want it more than they wanted you. That's all a distraction is. You give them a bigger yes to chase."

She wiped her eyes. Then she got very still, and looked at me, and said, "But you didn't get free either. That case — if you'd handed it over clean, they might've paid, might've—"

"No," I said. "They were never going to pay. Some places, the deal's a lie before you even shake on it. You can't own yourself where somebody else already wrote you down as inventory." I thought about it as I said it, which is how the true things usually arrive with me — I don't know I've learned them till I hear myself say them out loud. "You never owned your lungs, Vane. Not really. Not while somebody a hundred floors up could reach down and switch them off. That's not owning. That's just a longer lease."

She didn't like that. Then she did, slow, the way you accept cold water. "So what do I do?"

"Get somewhere they can't reach the switch," I said. "I don't know where that is. I've never found it either." And that was the truest thing of all, and it cost me to say it, because it was the shape of my whole life — always looking for the one place nobody could reach in and take me out of. "But it's not here. It's not any place where owning yourself has a monthly price."

I meant to say more. I always mean to.

The copper hit my tongue like a coin under the teeth.

I had time to see her face change — she saw something happen in mine, the way people always do at the end, the going-away look — and I had time to press my thumb to the button one last time and say her name so I'd carry it, Vane, Vane, the girl who wanted to buy back her own breath. Then the high ringing came up behind my teeth, and the cold came for me, that bone-deep falling cold, and the neon canyon and the flood and the girl with the light in her throat all folded up and were gone, spent, a world I would never stand in again.

The last thing I thought, going down into the dark between one place and the next, wasn't a thought so much as an ache. That somewhere out past all these rented rooms and switched-off lungs, there had to be a place that was mine — where nobody upstairs held the lever, where owning yourself wasn't the most expensive thing there was. I didn't know its name yet. I only knew I was still looking for it.

And then the falling took me, and the world went white, and I braced for iron.

# The Windmarrow Confederacy

I came to face-down in grass, which is a mercy, because grass is soft, and I have arrived in worse.

For the length of one heartbeat I didn't know who was breathing into that turf. My mouth was full of the taste of old coins and my whole body rang with a cold that had nothing to do with the warm day — the deep, marrow-deep chill that comes with every crossing, like some part of me stays behind in the last place and has to catch up. I couldn't find my name. So I did what I always do: I pressed my thumb to the small brass button in the seam of my coat, worn smooth as a river stone, the one thing that comes with me everywhere and never points north no matter how you turn it.

*Kes. You're Kes.* Thirteen years old, and the boy the centuries keep passing back and forth like a coin they can't decide who owns. One life I'm somewhere of towers and rain and humming wire; then the copper rises and the high ring climbs behind my teeth, and I'm flung to the far side of everything, into an age of iron — and it's never the same iron twice. Always a new world. Always a place I have to learn from nothing.

I rolled over and looked up at a sky so wide it made me dizzy. No towers. No walls. No wall *anywhere*, in any direction, just green rolling out to a blue rim of hills.

"So," I said. "Grass, then."

A boy on a small shaggy horse was watching me from ten feet off. He couldn't have been much older than me, but he sat that horse like they'd been poured together. Behind him, a whole camp of round felt tents breathed pale smoke into the morning, and the air smelled of that smoke and of horses and of something sour-sweet I'd learn was fermented mare's milk. Nobody had built anything here that couldn't be folded up and carried away by evening. That told me more than a speech would have. These were people who kept their whole world portable — who had decided, a long time ago, that a home you couldn't pick up and take with you wasn't worth having.

"You fell out of nowhere," the boy said. Not hostile. Curious, the way you'd be curious about weather.

"That's the general shape of my life, yeah."

I got to my feet, swaying, and swept the camp the way I always do — who holds the power, where the exits are, what's worth taking. The power part was strange here. There was a big tent in the center, grander than the rest, but nobody bowed to it. People came and went from it like it was a meeting-hall, not a throne-room. I filed that away.

The boy's name was Torg, and his people were the horse-clans of the high plateau, and they were, right then, coming apart at the seams.

I got the shape of it over a bowl of something warm that I drank without asking what it was. For as long as anyone remembered, the clans had governed themselves by an assembly — every clan sent elders, every voice counted, decisions made in the open under that wide sky. It was slow and it was loud and it worked. But now a man had risen — a war-captain named Kadru who'd won a string of raids against the wall-building valley kingdoms to the south — and Kadru had a new idea. One voice, he said, was faster than a hundred. One crown could strike where a hundred elders would still be arguing. Give him the crown, and he'd give them the valley.

"And people are listening," Torg said, low, glancing toward the grand tent. "My aunt sits the assembly. Kadru's people have been at her for a week. Threats, mostly. If she stands up tomorrow and speaks against him, and she's the only one who does, they'll break her. Cast her clan out. Winter's coming. A clan alone on the plateau in winter is a clan that dies."

There it was. The oldest fight there is, dressed in felt and horsehair: whether a people should stay a chorus or become a fist. I'd seen it in world after world, and it never got any less dangerous.

"So she needs someone else to stand up first," I said. "Or with her. So she's not alone."

"Nobody will. They're all scared of the same winter."

I should have kept out of it. I *know* I should have. I'm going to be torn out of every world I land in the moment I let myself care, and every time I care anyway, and it costs me. But Torg was looking at me with the particular hope of someone who hasn't got a plan and is desperate for one to walk out of the grass, and I've never in my life been able to walk away from that.

"Tell me how the assembly works," I said. "Exactly. Who speaks first, who speaks last, how a vote's counted. The rules. There are always rules, and rules always have a loose seam."

We talked half the night. And here's what I found, mapping it the way I map everything: Kadru wasn't winning the argument. He was winning the *silence*. Nobody believed him — they were just each afraid to be the first and only one to say so, because the first and only one gets crushed. It's the loneliest kind of fear. Everybody's waiting for everybody else.

You don't beat that with a better speech. You beat it by making the first voice not be alone.

"When the assembly opens," I said, "the speaker calls for any who oppose to stand. And normally your aunt stands, alone, and Kadru's people mark her. Right?"

"Right."

"What if she doesn't stand first? What if she asks a question first, instead of taking a side? A question anybody can agree with, that costs nothing to agree with — so that when she finally does stand, half the tent's already on their feet with her without noticing they got there."

Torg frowned. "What question?"

"'Who here would follow Kadru into the valley?'" I said. "Loud, and proud, and first. His own people will leap up — they want to look strong. Let them. Let the whole tent see exactly how *few* of them there are. Then she asks the second question. 'And who here has ever once been failed by the assembly of all our voices?' And nobody stands, because it's true, it's never failed them, that's why it's lasted. And *then* she stands, and she's not the lone fool against the crown — she's just the one who said out loud what the empty floor already showed."

Torg was very still. "You're going to get her killed. Or me."

"I'm going to get her *counted*," I said. "That's different. Barely."

I'll be honest: I lay awake the rest of that night sure I'd overreached, sure my cleverness was just a trap with extra steps. It usually feels like that at the darkest hour. But when the assembly gathered the next morning under the enormous sky, and the felt walls were rolled up so the wind moved through, and Kadru stood there broad and certain with his war-captains fanned behind him — Torg's aunt, a small iron-haired woman with a voice like a struck bell, asked her first question exactly as I'd shaped it.

*Who would follow Kadru into the valley?*

His men surged up, grinning, fists in the air. Perhaps thirty of them. And the tent — the whole vast rest of the tent — sat and looked at those thirty standing men, and you could feel it happen, the arithmetic doing itself in every head at once. *Thirty. Only thirty. I thought it was everyone. It isn't everyone. It was never everyone.*

She asked the second question. Nobody stood; the point landed like a stone in still water. And when she rose at last to speak for the assembly, she rose into a silence that was suddenly on her side, and a dozen elders rose with her, and then a hundred, and Kadru's thirty looked very small and very loud in the middle of all that standing quiet. He didn't get his crown. You don't take a fist to a people that's just remembered it's a chorus.

Afterward Torg found me at the edge of camp, packing nothing, because I had nothing to pack.

"Stay," he said. "You could ride. You could be clan."

And I felt the pull of it — I always do, some kind voice in every world saying *settle, stay, this can be your place* — and I felt myself distrust it, the way I always do, because how can any place be mine when I know a taste of copper can rip me out of it between one word and the next?

"I can't," I said. "I'm trying to get home. There's — someone. Somewhere. I have to keep trying." The words came out thin. They always do; I can never quite say who or where, only that the wanting is the truest thing in me.

But I looked at those tents that could fold up and travel, at a whole people who carried home on horseback and never once mistook the ground for the thing that mattered, and something in my chest eased a fraction.

"Maybe home isn't a place that stays put," I said slowly, mostly to myself. "Maybe it's the people who move with you."

Torg opened his mouth to answer — and I tasted the penny-bright flood under my tongue, and the ring went up behind my teeth, high and rising, and the cold came marrow-deep. My thumb found the button. *So: gone, then,* I thought, and pressed my other hand once, hard, to Torg's shoulder so I'd carry the shape of him a little way into the dark.

The wide sky folded shut. And ahead, unseen, a world I'd never walked was waiting for me to be cold in it.

# The Copy Assizes

The first thing I always lose is my name, and the first thing I do is get it back.

I came awake on a cold floor with the taste of copper draining off my tongue, and for a breath there was nothing where I ought to be — no name, no home, just the falling still ringing in my bones. Then my hand closed on the little brass button worn smooth in my pocket, the one thing that rides through every crossing with me, and it all snapped into place: Kes, thirteen, the boy the centuries keep passing back and forth. One minute I'm somewhere of banners and rushlight; then my mouth floods copper and a high thin ringing climbs behind my teeth and I'm gone, dropped into some world of wire and light I've never seen and can't get back out of on my own. I don't choose it. I never have. All I keep is what's in my head and the button in my fist and the one want that follows me everywhere — home.

This place had no rain. That was the first strange thing.

Every wired world I've ever fallen into had rain, or ash, or something coming down out of a dirty sky. This one was clean. Too clean — recycled air with a faint chemical sweetness to it, and a low even hum, and no sky at all, just a curved ceiling of soft white light that went on and on. When I got to a window I understood. We were on a ring. A great wheel of a city turning slowly in black nothing, and below the window the world curved up on both sides until it met itself overhead, buildings hanging upside down far above me, their lights on. An orbital place. A city built in a circle around a nothing, spinning to make a floor.

"You can't be in here," a voice said. "This is a sealed chamber. This is a legal proceeding."

I turned. A tall room, pale and quiet, more like a temple than a court, though I'd worked out it was a court by the way everyone was arranged — the watchers on benches, the officials in grey, and up front, in the good light, the argument. That's how I read a new place. Not the faces first. The power first: who's standing where, who's afraid of whom, who holds the thing everyone else wants. Here the thing everyone wanted was a chair. A single chair, empty, in the center, and two women standing on either side of it, and every eye in the room going back and forth between them.

They were the same woman.

Not sisters. Not alike. The same — the same face, the same nervous way of holding one wrist with the other hand, the same small scar over one eyebrow. Same woman, standing in two places, looking across the empty chair at herself with an expression I can only call horror.

"He's a stray off the transit deck," said the grey official who'd caught me. "I'll have him removed—"

"Let him stay." One of the two women — the two same women — looked at me suddenly, hungrily. "An outside witness. Someone with no stake. That's exactly what this room hasn't got." She had a light behind her eyes, faint, gold, flickering when she blinked, and I'd learn later that everyone here had it — the little lamp of a mind that had been written down as data, backed up, kept. "Boy. You've never seen either of us before?"

"Never seen anyone here before," I said honestly. "I'm not from — here."

"Then you're the only clean pair of eyes in the building." She turned to the officials. "I invoke witness. He watches."

And because it was a place that ran on rules the way the iron-worlds run on oaths, they let the rules carry me. I got a bench. I sat. I turned the button over in my fingers and I watched, and slowly, from the arguments, I understood the impossible thing.

The woman had been murdered. Somebody had killed her in her rooms on the ring, three days back. And in this city, dying wasn't the end of you — not if you had the coin. Your mind got copied, always, kept safe as data in case the worst happened, and when it did they poured you into a new body and you went on, the same person, the law said, the very same. So they'd gone to wake her up. To give her back her life and let her name her killer.

Except there were two backups. Two copies of her, both current, both hers, and when they woke both — by accident, by fault, nobody would own to it — they got two women. Identical. Each certain she was the real one. Each with every memory up to that last ordinary evening. And neither of them remembered dying. The copy is taken before the knife. So there was a murder, and a victim who'd survived it twice over, and not one of the three people who were her could say who'd held the blade — because the woman who'd been stabbed hadn't backed up her final hour, and these two had backed up before it ever happened.

Two women. One life. One chair, which was the name for the life — her rooms, her work, her account, her self, all of it, and only room in the law for one to sit in it. The other would be filed. Shelved. Stored as data, awake nowhere, which everyone in that pale room spoke of in the same lowered voice they'd use for a grave.

"The receipts are identical," an official was saying, weary, like he'd said it a hundred times. "Timestamps identical. Neither copy is the elder. Neither is corrupt. By every record we hold, both women are, equally and fully, the deceased. The law has no instrument to divide them."

"Then flip a coin," said one of the two — the one on the left, the one who'd invoked me. Her voice cracked. "Just — decide. I can't stand in this room and watch myself get shelved, and I can't stand in it and shelve myself, I can't, do you understand what you're asking—"

"I'll take the shelf," said the one on the right, quietly, and the whole room turned. "If it comes to that. I'll take the shelf, if she'll—" She stopped. Her hands were shaking. "No. No, I won't. God help me, I want to live too. I want it as much as she does. Of course I do. I'm her."

And they looked at each other across the empty chair, the two same women, both wanting the one life so badly it was tearing them, and I thought: there is no test in this room that can find a difference, because there isn't one. Not in the receipts. Not in the memories. Not anywhere they've thought to look.

So I did what I do. I looked where nobody else was looking.

"May I ask them something?" I said. My voice went thin in that big pale room. "The witness. Can the witness ask?"

They let me. Rules again.

I walked down to the chair. I didn't look at their faces — I'd have gone cross-eyed, they were so much the same. I looked at their hands. The left one still gripping her own wrist. The right one's fingers white where she clutched the hem of her sleeve. Frightened, both. Human, both.

"You each remember your last evening," I said. "The same evening. Tell me — not what you did. Tell me what you'd do now. Right now, in this chair, if it were yours and hers was the shelf. What would you do about her?"

The one on the left didn't hesitate. "Fight it," she said fiercely. "Appeal. Petition. I'd spend the account fighting to get her un-shelved, I'd never stop, I—" She heard herself and faltered, because of course she would; she was arguing to be the one who lived.

The one on the right was quiet a long moment. Then she said, "I'd let her out. If I woke in the chair and she was on the shelf." Her voice was very steady now, steadier than it had been all day. "I'd spend the first thing I owned buying her a body of her own. Even if the law said she was me and I didn't have to. Even if it cost me the chair in the end." She looked at her double, and there were tears on her face but her voice didn't shake. "Because I know what the shelf would feel like. I'd never let anyone feel that. Not even — not even the part of me I'd have to compete with to live."

The room was silent.

And I understood something I hadn't had words for until it came out of my own mouth. "You're not the same," I said. "You were. Three days ago you were exactly the same, down to the scar. But you're not the deceased anymore, either of you — she's gone, the one who lived that last hour and died in it, she didn't back up, she's just gone. You're both what came after. And the second you woke up as two, you started being two." I pointed, not at their faces, at the choice each had just made. "That's the difference. Not in the receipts. The real you was never the one with the records. It's the one standing here now, choosing what to do next. And you two just chose different things."

I turned to the officials. "You can't tell them apart by looking back. Stop looking back. Look at what they'll do. One of them, offered the whole life, will fight to keep it. The other, offered the whole life, will give half of it away to spare herself the shelf." I spread my hands. "I don't know which of those two people should get the chair. That's not for a stray off the transit deck to say. But they are not the same person, and now you can prove it, because they just proved it themselves."

It didn't solve it clean. Nothing solves clean. But it broke the thing open — the law had been stuck because it could only measure the past, and I'd shown them a place to measure that wasn't the past at all. They adjourned to argue about it. The one on the right — the one who'd give half her life away — caught my sleeve as they cleared the room.

"How did you know?" she said. "How did you know to ask the future instead of the past?"

I thought about the falling. About waking up on cold floors with no name, over and over, and getting myself back every time not by remembering who I'd been but by deciding, right then, thumb on the button, to be him again. "Because I lose everything I've done, all the time," I said. "Everywhere I go. And the only thing that's ever told me who I am isn't the record. It's the next thing I choose to do."

She started to answer.

The copper hit my tongue.

I barely had time — thumb to the button, her face already changing because she could see mine changing, that going-away look people get at the very end. The ringing came up sharp behind my teeth. The cold reached in. And the pale ring-city and the empty chair and the two women who were two people now folded away and were gone, spent, a place I'd never stand in again.

Falling, I held onto the last true thing like it was warm: that whoever I really was, it wasn't written down anywhere behind me. It was out ahead, in the next thing I'd choose when I landed, wherever I landed, however far from home. I braced for the world to catch me, and I chose, already, to be Kes when it did.

# The Hushed City of Ember Gate

The first thing was the smell, and the smell was so bad it dragged me up out of the forgetting almost before the forgetting could take hold.

Pyre-smoke. Vinegar. Under it, sweetish and terrible, something I didn't let myself name. I was on my knees on cobblestones with a mouth full of old-coin copper and a cold in my bones like the grave, and for one blank breath I was nobody at all — just a boy-shaped hole in the world, breathing that reek. Then my thumb found the little brass button in my coat-seam, worn smooth, warm when I wasn't, and it gave me back to myself.

*Kes. Thirteen. The boy the worlds trade back and forth.* One life I'm somewhere of neon and cold rain and endless hum; then the copper comes and the ring behind my teeth, and I'm hurled to the far shore of time into an age of stone and iron and tallow — never the same age twice, always a fresh world with fresh trouble, and never any say in it from me.

I got up. A bell was tolling. It didn't stop tolling the whole time I was there.

"So," I whispered. "Stone, then."

I swept the street the way I always do, first thing, before I trust anything — but there was almost nothing to sweep. The street was empty. Shuttered houses leaned over a gutter, and on door after door someone had daubed a rough cross in chalk. High walls closed off the far end, and rising over them, nine great gate-towers, all of them barred. A walled city with its gates shut against something. And you didn't shut nine gates against an army; an army you fought. You shut them against a thing you couldn't fight. A sickness.

I'd landed inside a plague, in a city that had sealed itself so tight the air had nowhere to go.

A figure came around the corner and I nearly bolted — a tall shape in a long waxed coat and a mask with a curved beak, stuffed with dried herbs against the bad air. A plague-doctor. He looked at me through glass eyeholes and said, muffled, "You're not marked. Get off the street, boy, before the wardens take you for a looter. Curfew's from the last bell." Then he was gone, unhurried, a big slow bird stalking the empty stones.

I found a doorway to breathe in, and that's where I heard the crying.

It came from a house at the end of the row — one of the chalk-crossed ones, its door not just marked but nailed. A sealed house. I'd learn what that meant fast enough: when the sickness came into a house, the wardens didn't take the sick out. They boarded the whole family *in*, well and ill together, and left them to it, and painted the cross, and moved on. Quarantine. Contain the thing by burying people alive with it.

And at the shuttered window of that house, pressed to the crack, was a girl's face. Healthy. Bright-eyed. Terrified.

My chest did the thing. The stupid, immediate thing. I crossed the street before I'd decided to.

"Hey," I said, low, at the crack in the shutter. "Hey. Are you sick?"

"No." Her voice was fierce and small. "My little brother is. My mother's tending him. But I'm *not* — I felt his forehead a day ago and now I'm walled up to die with him and I'm *not sick.*" She pressed her palm to the shutter. "They won't listen. The warden said a healthy face today is a dead one in three days, always, and he's not even wrong most times, but I'm — I could still *run.* I could still get out and away and I'd live. If someone would just — "

"What's your name?" I said. I don't know why that first. Maybe because I've learned that if I'm going to lose someone — and I always lose them, the copper always comes — I at least want to keep the name.

"Wren."

"Wren. I'm Kes. I'm going to get you out."

I shouldn't have said it. Promising is the cruelest thing I do, because I can't keep promises; a world can take me mid-sentence. But I said it, and I meant it, and I set about it the only way I know — by mapping the trap for its loose seam.

The house was nailed, not stone-walled. The wardens patrolled but there weren't enough of them; the city was dying too fast to guard every door. The bell told the hours; curfew came at the last toll before dark, and after that the wardens went in and the streets belonged to fear. There was a back way — there's always a back way — a narrow yard behind the row, a low wall, an alley to the lesser gates.

"Here's the shape of it," I told her through the crack, fast and quiet. "The nails are in dry old wood. I've got a needle and I've got my hands, and dry old wood around an iron nail splits if you work it. Not the door — too loud, too watched. The back shutter, over the yard. When the curfew bell rings and the wardens go inside to count the new dead, that's your quiet hour. I'll have the shutter half-worked by then. You slip the yard wall, take the alley left, and you're at the smallest gate before anyone's counting you."

"My mother won't leave my brother," Wren whispered. "She made me swear I'd go without her. She made me *swear.*" Her voice cracked. "I hate her. I love her. Go without her, Kes. She wants me to live."

"Then you live," I said. "You live, Wren. That's the whole job."

I worked that back shutter through the long grey afternoon while the bell tolled and tolled and the pyre-smoke drifted and the sealed city held its breath. I split the wood around three nails, patient, quiet, the way you free a rusted bolt or pick a lock or do any of the small patient things that are the only real magic I've ever had. The light went bronze, then thin, then blue. Almost. Almost. One more nail and the shutter would swing and Wren would be through the yard and gone, and for once, *for once*, I'd get someone all the way out.

The curfew bell began to ring.

And under my tongue, bright and sudden and merciless, the copper came.

"No," I said out loud. "No, not now, I'm — one more, I just need one more — "

The ring climbed behind my teeth, that high thin scream I'd never once been wrong about, and the cold came marrow-deep, and the shutter had one nail left in it, one, and I hadn't reached it. My hands were already blurring at their edges. The world was thinning like fog off water.

"Wren," I said, and my voice was going. "The last nail's the top left. Push the shutter *up* and out, it'll give, I split it, it'll give — push up and out — the yard wall, the alley *left* — go without her — Wren, *go* — "

Her face was at the crack, wide-eyed, watching me come apart into copper and cold. "Kes — what's happening to you — Kes — "

"I'm sorry," I said, which is the truest thing I ever say and never enough. "I'm so sorry. Push up and out. Live."

And the sealed city and the tolling bell and the girl named Wren folded away into brightness and nothing, and the cold took me all the way down, and I didn't get to see the shutter swing. I'll never know. That's the part I carry. I got her to the last nail and not through it, learned her name and lost her in the same grey afternoon, and I don't know if the shutter gave.

I think it did. I have to think it did. I split that wood clean. She had strong hands.

*So: gone, then.* Somewhere ahead a new world waited, cold and unseen, and I fell toward it with a name I hadn't managed to save held tight as the button in my palm.

# Verdance

I have landed in a lot of terrible places, and this was the first one that hurt because it was beautiful.

I came out of the falling flat on my back in grass — real grass, soft and warm — and for the length of one breath I was nobody, just a cold empty space where a name should be. Then my fingers found the brass button in my pocket, worn round at the edges, the one thing that crosses over with me every time, and it all came back at once: Kes, thirteen, the boy the ages keep handing back and forth. One moment I'm somewhere of tallow smoke and cold stone; then my tongue goes to copper, a high ringing starts up behind my teeth, and I'm torn loose and dropped into a world of light and machines I've never walked before, with no way home but the next crossing I don't control. I don't pick where I go. I never have. All I carry is what's in my head, the button in my hand, and one stubborn want — to get back to the place that was mine.

I sat up, and my breath caught, because I'd landed in a garden the size of a kingdom.

Terraces climbed the air on every side, green and gold, hung with vines and heavy fruit, and among them wound clean paths and clear pools and slow airships shaped like whales drifting under a sun so bright and soft it didn't hurt to look at. There was no rain here. No ash, no grime, no black flood. The air smelled of green things growing and warm bread, and everywhere people moved unhurried, laughing, their hands full of the day's harvest. Wind spun tall white blades on the towers, catching the light. It was, I understood slowly, the most hopeful place I'd ever fallen into. A world that had been sick and had made itself well. A garden that had grown up out of some old ruin and put flowers where the neon used to be.

"You came up through the transit lift," said a girl, "and you're not on the roster, and you've got the strangest face I've ever seen." She said it kindly. She said everything kindly, I'd learn — it was that kind of place. "Are you a visitor? We so rarely get visitors."

Her name was Sable and she kept the records — the archives, she called it, the memory of the commune, going all the way back to the founding. She was maybe fifteen, ink-stained even here in the future, because some things don't change across a thousand years and one of them is that the person who loves the archive always ends up marked by it. She took me in without a second thought. That was the place all over. They fed me, they gave me clean clothes that fit, they asked me nothing sharp. Everyone I met wanted me to be happy.

And I wanted to stay.

That's the thing I have to tell you, because it's the whole shape of what happened. I fall into a lot of worlds that want to kill me, and I fight my way through them, and part of me is always glad, in a bitter way, when the copper comes to take me out. This place I didn't want to leave. I looked at the terraces and the slow whales in the sky and the people who weren't afraid, and something in me that is always cold and always falling just — sat down in the grass and thought, here. Maybe here. Maybe this is a place a person could stop.

But I can't turn off the other thing in me. The noticing. I walk into a new world and before I've decided to, I'm mapping it — who holds the power, what people are afraid of, what's worth stealing and what's a lie. Sword-world or wire-world, I do it without meaning to. And three days into paradise, the map started coming out wrong.

Little things. The fruit came up the lifts full and the lifts went back down empty — but they went down full too, sometimes, at night, loaded with crates nobody talked about. The pools were always clean, always full, in a place that never seemed to rain. And when I asked Sable where the water came from, her kind face did something I'd never seen it do. It closed.

"From the founding," she said. "The founders solved water. That's why we can live like this. It's in the archive." And she changed the subject, gently, the way you'd steer a child from a hot stove.

So I went to the archive.

I'm good at getting into places. It's my one useful talent across all the centuries — give me a locked door and something bent to poke it with and enough nerve, and I'll get through. The archive was locked with light instead of iron, but Sable had shown me how it opened because she trusted me, because everyone here trusted everyone, and that trust was the softest lock of all. I went in at night, when the terraces glowed and the whales hung dark against the stars, and I found the founding.

And the founding had a seam in it.

I can't read their machines. But I can read a thing that's been changed — I've spent my whole strange life learning to tell the true from the added-on, and the founding-story of the commune had a place in it where the light stuttered, where the record had been smoothed over, like a wall replastered to hide a door. I couldn't open it. But Sable could, and in the morning I made her look at it.

"That's nothing," she said. "That's just—" And then she really looked, the way an archivist looks, the way I look at a thing that's been tampered with, and I watched her whole world tilt under her. "That's an edit," she whispered. "Someone edited the founding."

We opened the seam together. It took most of a day and it cost her something to do it — I could see it costing her, the way it costs to pull up a floorboard when you're afraid of what's under it. And under it was the truth the garden was built on.

The water didn't come from the founders solving water. It came from somewhere else — down, out, over a horizon nobody in the commune ever crossed, from a dry cracked place where other people lived, real people, and pumped and dug and thirsted so that the pools up here could stay full and the fruit could stay heavy and the whales could drift under a soft sun. The commune's whole shining life ran on their backs, and the founders had known, and had reached into the memory of the world and cut that part out. Not just hidden it. Erased it. So that everyone born here after would grow up loving a paradise and never once feel the weight of what it stood on.

Sable sat on the floor of the archive for a long time with the truth glowing in front of her.

"I could put it back," she said finally, in a very small voice. "The edit. I know how. I could smooth it over again and no one would know and everything would go on being—" She stopped. "Beautiful. It would go on being beautiful."

"It would," I said.

"They're happy," she said, and it was almost a plea. "Everyone here is happy. If I tell them — I don't know what happens. Maybe it all falls apart. Maybe they hate me. Maybe nothing changes except that we all have to carry it." She looked up at me with wet eyes. "You're a visitor. You'll leave. Tell me to leave it and I'll leave it."

And I wanted to. God help me, I wanted to tell her to plaster the wall back over, because I wanted this place to be what it looked like, because I was tired, so tired, of every world turning out to have a knife in it. I wanted, for once, one place that was simply good, that a person could belong to and rest.

"I can't tell you that," I said. My voice came out rough. "Because I know something about this. I go looking, everywhere I land — for a place that could be mine. Somewhere I could stop and belong. And I think I finally understand what makes a place home and what makes it just a beautiful room you're renting from someone else's suffering." I put my hand flat on the cold light of the archive floor. "A place built on a lie can never be home, Sable. Not really. Not to anyone who knows. It's just a very nice place to be a stranger in. The people down there in the dry country — they've got nothing, but the truth of their lives is their own. And everyone up here has everything, and doesn't even own the story of how they got it. That's not belonging. That's the most expensive kind of homelessness there is."

She cried for a while. I let her. Then she wiped her face and stood up and said, "I don't know how to tell them."

"You don't have to know how," I said. "You just have to not plaster it back over. Leave the door open. Let them find it the way you did. It's their story too. They've got a right to carry it, even if it's heavy. Especially if it's heavy."

She reached out and touched the button I was turning in my fingers — I hadn't noticed I'd started again. "Is that your home?" she asked. "That little thing?"

"It's the piece of it I've got left," I said. "It doesn't point anywhere. But it's mine, and the story of it is mine, all the way down, no seams. That's the whole difference." I almost laughed. "Took me a garden full of lies to figure that out."

She was going to say something. I saw her draw breath for it.

The copper came first, sharp under my teeth.

I got my thumb to the button. I got her name — Sable, the girl who chose the heavy truth over the beautiful lie. Her face changed because mine did, that going-away look, and the ringing rose, and the cold came reaching in to pull me out of the warmth, and the terraces and the pools and the slow whales in the golden air folded shut and were gone, spent, a place I would never stand in again no matter how much I'd wanted to.

Falling, I held to the one thing I'd carried up out of paradise: that home isn't the prettiest place you land. It's the one whose whole true story belongs to you, dry country and all. I hadn't found mine yet. But now I knew a little more of what it would have to be — and that was worth more than a garden, even a garden that lied as gently as that one had. I braced for iron, and I let the light take me.

# The Inkreach Colleges

I woke in lamplight with a mouth full of copper and, for one blank breath, no idea whose eyes were doing the looking.

That's the toll, every time — the cold that goes into the marrow and the moment where my own name slips off the shelf and I'm nobody. I found the small brass button worn into the seam of my coat, pressed my thumb to it, felt it warm when the rest of me was ice, and it handed me back. *Kes. Thirteen. The boy the ages keep trading between them.* One life I'm somewhere of rain-slick towers and humming light; then the penny-taste rises and the high ring behind my teeth, and I'm flung across everything into an age of iron and ink — a different one each time, always new, always a fresh puzzle, never any choosing on my part.

The lamplight, it turned out, was a lot of lamps. I sat up on a bench in a domed hall where a hundred hanging lamps threw a hundred soft pools of light across walls covered in interlocking star-patterned tile, and the whole place smelled of rosewater and drying ink. Somewhere near, a soft rhythmic clicking — I'd learn it was the little brass rings of an instrument for reading the sky. Somewhere else, the scratch of a great many pens.

"So," I breathed. "Ink, then."

I swept the room the way I always do, before I trust it. And this was the strangest sweep I'd done in a while, because the thing that held power in this place wasn't a sword or a throne. It was the shelves. Rank on rank of bound manuscripts rising into shadow, and men and women moving among them with the careful reverence you'd give a king. I'd landed in a city of scholars — a desert oasis, I'd gather, one of a whole federation of them — where the real coin wasn't gold. It was what you knew, and what you could prove.

A young man nearly tripped over me. He was maybe twenty, ink to the elbows, wild-eyed in a way that had nothing to do with finding a strange boy on a bench and everything to do with something he was clutching to his chest — a sheaf of papers covered in diagrams and rows of figures.

"You can't be in here," he said, not unkindly, just distracted. "The reading hall's closed. They've closed everything." He glanced over his shoulder. "Are you a runner? Did the Assembly of Readers send you? No. No, you're nobody's. Good. Nobody's is what I need."

"That's me," I agreed. "Extremely nobody's. What's wrong?"

His name was Adib, and what was wrong was this: he had proved something true, and the truth was going to get it burned.

I got the shape of it while he paced. He'd spent three years measuring the way a shadow crosses a wall through the seasons, and his figures showed — he tried to explain the mathematics and I understood maybe a third of it — that a claim the ruling patron-prince had built his whole authority on, a claim about the order of the heavens and the prince's special place beneath it, was simply wrong. Not wickedly. Just wrong, the way an old map is wrong. But the prince funded the colleges. The prince's face was on the coin. A boy with figures that made the prince's heavens false was a boy whose figures would be quietly gathered up and quietly fed to a quiet fire, and himself struck from every ledger as if he'd never studied here at all.

"They came for the copies this morning," Adib said. "This is the last one. If it burns, it's as though I never thought it. As though the truth un-happens." He laughed, a little wildly. "They can't argue the figures. So they'll erase the man."

Something went cold and hard in me that had nothing to do with the crossing. I've been erased more times than I can count — every world strips my name off me for that one breath, every jump un-writes a little more of where I came from. I know exactly what it is to have yourself quietly rubbed out. It made me furious on his behalf in a way I didn't have to fake.

"They can burn the paper," I said. "Can they un-say a thing that's been said out loud, in front of witnesses, in public? Where everyone can carry it away in their own head?"

Adib stopped pacing. "There's a public reading tomorrow. The Great Recitation — the colleges open the halls, anyone may bring a proof to be spoken aloud before the whole assembly. But the prince's readers vet what's spoken. They'll never let me to the floor. They have my name on a list."

"They have *your* name on a list," I said. "They don't have mine. I'm nobody's, remember?" I was already grinning, and the grinning is a bad sign in me, it means I'm scared and improvising and about to do something I can't take back. "How long to teach a nobody to read your figures aloud? Not to understand them. Just to say them true, loud, and clear, in front of a hundred people who'll each walk out carrying the memory?"

He stared at me. "You'd be thrown out of the city. Beaten, maybe."

"I get thrown out of everywhere," I said. "It's practically my trade."

We had one night. He taught me the reading like you'd teach someone a song in a language they don't speak — sound by sound, the strange words for the sky's machinery, the order of the figures, where to pause so a listener's mind could catch up. I have a good memory for the shape of a thing even when the meaning stays foggy, and by grey dawn I could say his proof through without a stumble.

The Great Recitation filled the domed hall to its lamplit ceiling. Scholars, students, the curious, the prince's grey-robed readers standing along the walls with their list of approved names. And when a pause came, I stepped out onto the floor — a nobody, on nobody's list — and before anyone could decide what I was, I opened my mouth and started to speak Adib's figures into the enormous listening quiet.

I nearly ruined it. Of course I did. Halfway through, a grey-robed reader started toward me with a face like a slammed door, and my nerve went sideways the way it does, and instead of speaking faster I said, brightly, to the whole hall — "You'll want to remember this bit, it's the part they've decided you're not clever enough to hear" — which was exactly the wrong joke at exactly the wrong moment, and Adib, somewhere in the crowd, made a noise like a man watching a vase fall.

But here's the thing about the wrong joke. It made a hundred scholars — a hundred people whose entire pride was in being clever enough to hear anything — *bristle*, and lean in, and listen harder, precisely to prove they were clever enough. The grey reader reaching for me suddenly had a hundred affronted faces turned his way. He hesitated. And in that hesitation I said the rest of the proof, all of it, clear and true, sound by sound, and I watched it land in a hundred heads at once, where no fire could reach.

They dragged me out, after. They did throw me out; the joke was on the nose about that. But the proof was said. A hundred people carried it home in their skulls, and you cannot board up a hundred skulls, and by nightfall the thing Adib had thought could not un-happen.

He found me at the lesser gate as the guards were shoving me through it.

"Why?" he said. "You didn't even understand it."

"Didn't have to," I said, spitting dust. "I understood *you*. I understood getting erased." I pressed my thumb to the button in my coat without meaning to, the old habit, proof I was still myself. "Some things you carry stay yours only if you flat-out refuse to let anyone rub them out. That's all I know, really. That's the only thing I've learned that keeps."

He wanted me to stay — they always want me to stay, and I always want to and never can. I was about to say something about home, about the someone and the somewhere I'm forever trying to get back to, when the copper flooded up under my tongue and the high ring climbed behind my teeth and the cold came into my marrow.

"So: gone, then," I said, and Adib's mouth fell open, and the lamplit hall of the world folded away into brightness and nothing — and I fell toward a new world I'd never seen, refusing, all the way down, to let it rub me out.

# The Cinderstack

There was ash falling before I even had a name to catch it with.

I came out of the crossing on a hard metal ledge, and for one scraped breath I was nobody — no name, no self, just the cold of the falling and grey stuff drifting down onto my face. Then my hand found the little brass button worn smooth in my pocket, the thing that comes with me no matter where I'm thrown, and I was Kes again, thirteen, the boy the two worlds keep trading back and forth. One breath I'm in some age of mud and banners; then copper floods my tongue, a high ringing goes up behind my teeth, and I'm gone — dropped into a world of machines and light I've never seen, and can't leave except when it decides to spit me out. No say in it. Never have had. Just what's in my head, the button in my fist, and the one want I can't put down: home.

I sat up into a snowfall that wasn't snow.

Ash. Fine and grey and endless, sifting down out of a sky the color of a bruise, and it caught in my hair and my clothes and coated every surface soft as felt. A volcano, somewhere, forever — that's what it felt like, a world that had been burning quietly for so long the burning had become the weather. And this world, like a couple I've fallen into, went straight up: a vertical city, tower on tower, bolted into the flank of something enormous, the ash thickest at the bottom where I was and thinning as the towers climbed toward a far high place where — I could just make it out — the sky went clean and blue.

Up there, the air was good. Down here, I coughed on my second breath.

And there was a rule to the place. I felt it before I understood it, the way you feel the shape of power in any new world — who stands where, who's afraid of whom. Down here in the ash, everyone I could see had metal in them. A woman with a bright steel arm hauling cargo. A man whose legs whirred as he walked, both of them below the knee gleaming and jointed. Cheap patched augments, scavenged parts, the kind of body-help I'd seen wear people down in other wired worlds — except here it wasn't a debt. Here it was a mark. Because when I finally got up a level, out of the worst of the ash, the people there had less metal, and looked at the metal-armed ones below with a curled lip, and higher still, near the clean air, I'd learn, the ones who ran things were proud of having no augments at all. Pure. Born-and-stayed flesh. In every other machine-world I'd fallen into, the rich wore their chrome like jewels. Here it was backwards. Here the clean-fleshed rich looked down on the augmented poor, and the higher you were allowed to climb, the less of a machine you were allowed to be.

Which meant a girl with a steel arm was, by the rules of this ash-choked city, exactly as low as you could go.

Her name was Torch and she was trying to climb.

I met her because she nearly knocked me off a gantry, moving fast, glancing back. She had a steel arm, the left, from the shoulder — good work under the grime, but unmistakable, the seam where flesh met alloy plain as a brand. And she had that look I know from the inside: the look of someone with somewhere they have to get to and everything in the way.

"Out of the road," she said, "unless you want a warden's drone reading your face. You're not marked, are you? You're clean." She said clean like it was a slur and a warning both. "You could go up. You could—" She stopped herself. "Never mind. Move."

"Where are you going?" I asked. I attach too fast. It's my worst habit and I have long since stopped fighting it. Something about her — the desperation held so tight it had gone quiet — reached into me and I was already hers before I'd thought about it.

She almost didn't answer. Then it came out of her in a rush, the way a thing comes out when you've been carrying it alone too long. "Summit. The clean levels. My mother's up there — she went up years ago, before the arm, before I got marked, she passes for clean and she's dying and I have to reach her before—" Her voice broke on it. "And they won't let this arm past the checkpoints. An augment doesn't climb. That's the whole law of the world. So I have to climb anyway, up the old lifts, the dead ways, before she—" She shook her head hard, scattering ash. "You can't help. Nobody can. Get clear."

I didn't get clear. Of course I didn't.

"I can help," I said. "I'm good at dead ways. I'm good at getting into places I'm not allowed to be." Which was true. It's the truest thing about me. Every world I land in, I'm not allowed to be there, and I've spent my whole life learning the ways around the checkpoints. "You need somebody clean-faced to get past the drones. Look at me. I'm nobody. I'm not marked. Point me at the wardens and let me be your key."

She looked at me a long moment, and the wanting in her — the need to reach her mother — won out over the sense that said trust no one.

The climb was the hardest thing I've done in any century.

We went up through the bones of the old city, the dead lift-shafts and maintenance ladders the wardens didn't watch because no augmented person was supposed to be able to reach them. The ash thinned as we climbed but the cold got worse and the air got thinner in a different way, and every checkpoint we hit I went first — clean-faced, nobody, bowing my head to the blue light of the drones the way I've learned to bow to every blade in every world, buying us a second, a passage, a lie. I'd tell them Torch was cargo. I'd tell them she was maintenance. I improvised with whatever the level gave me — a stolen work-tag here, a cart to hide her arm under there. And it worked, level after level, up toward the clean high blue.

Until the last checkpoint, where a warden looked too long, and reached for Torch's sleeve, and found steel under it.

What happened next cost me. I have to tell it plain, because it's the part that stays with me.

There was a lift — a real one, the last one, going up to the clean air where her mother was, and it was closing, and there was a warden between Torch and the doors and a drone dropping toward her marked arm. And I did the only thing there was to do, which was be the bigger problem. I threw myself at the warden, all thirteen years of me, shouting, making myself the thing worth grabbing — an unmarked stray who shouldn't exist, a puzzle, a scandal — and in the tangle of it I shoved Torch at the closing doors and she made it, she made it through, steel arm and all, up toward her dying mother in the clean blue air.

And the warden's drone caught me across the ribs with something that wasn't a blade but hurt worse, a jolt that went into me like cold fire and knotted every muscle I had, and I went down on the ash-slick metal with the lift doors shut and Torch gone up and safe and me left behind in the grey. It hurt. It kept hurting. Not the kind of hurt that heals clean and is forgotten — the kind that gets into the deep of you and marks a place that won't be smoothed over. I lay there and I thought, distantly, through the pain: she made it. She made it. And I'd never see the top of that lift, never see whether she reached her mother in time. I'd given everything to get her up, and I didn't even get to know how it ended.

That's the price, sometimes. You spend yourself getting someone to the door and the door shuts and you're on the wrong side of it, alone, and you pay for it in a place inside that doesn't mend.

I curled around the button in my fist on the cold floor, ash sifting over me, and I said my own name to hold onto it — Kes, Kes — and I said hers too, so I'd carry it. Torch. The girl who climbed with a marked arm toward the clean air, who made it because I made myself the bigger problem. Whatever happened up there, she got the chance. I gave her that. It cost me something I wouldn't get back, and I decided, lying in the grey, that it had been worth the cost — that some prices you pay gladly even knowing you'll carry the mark of them into every world after.

The copper came while I was still curled on the floor.

There was no beauty to the going, this time. Just the ringing rising over the pain, and the cold reaching down into the deeper cold, and the ash-city and its backwards law and the closed lift folding shut and going out, spent, a place I'd never stand in again and never learn the end of. I pressed the button. I held her name.

Falling, I understood that I'd carry this one differently than the losses that come clean — that helping her had left a scar in the true sense, a place worn raw that would ache in the next world and the one after. And I found, going down into the dark, that I wouldn't have traded it. A person who climbs out of the ash for someone they love ought to have somebody clear the checkpoints for them, even a stray, even a boy who'll be gone before the story ends. I braced for iron, and the ache came with me, and I let it.

# The Saltgold Traverse

The heat hit me before the world had finished assembling, and the heat and the cold fought each other across my skin, and in the middle of that war I forgot my own name.

That's the crossing for you. However hot the new world, the jump leaves its own cold in you, deep in the bone, and for one blank breath you're nobody at all — just a body full of copper-taste and shivering under a hammering sun. My thumb went to the small brass button sewn in my coat-seam, worn smooth as a pebble, and it gave me back. *Kes. Thirteen. The boy two ages keep trading.* One life I'm somewhere of neon and cold rain and endless machine-hum; then the penny-flood rises under my tongue, the ring climbs behind my teeth, and I'm flung to the far side of time into an age of iron and dust — always a new one, always a puzzle, never any choosing.

I was standing in the shade of a wall built, impossibly, of salt. Great slabs of it cut like grey brick, stacked and mortared, glittering faintly. Beyond the shade, a market roared: caravan bells over the murmur of a thousand voices, camels groaning, the flash of indigo cloth, and everywhere the dull gleam of gold — gold dust weighed on tiny scales, gold coming in on the backs of animals from somewhere south, gold flowing toward a palace whose towers I could see over the rooftops.

"So," I breathed. "Gold, then. And salt."

I swept it, first thing, the way I always do — who holds the power, where the power hides its knife. And this place had two faces; I could feel it right away. On the surface: a great and pious empire, its monarch famous for devotion, calls to prayer ringing out over the salt-white streets. But underneath — I've a nose for undertows — something older moved. Men in hunter's leathers with charms of bone and iron at their throats, going where soldiers wouldn't stop them. A secret society, coiled inside the shining one. Every gold-rich kingdom I ever landed in kept a secret like that, tucked behind its scripture: the older power that the wealth actually rested on.

I found the trouble the way I always find it — it found me. A girl was weeping against the salt wall, alone, in fine clothes gone dusty, and my chest did its stupid immediate thing, and I crossed to her before I'd decided to.

Her name was Ayo, and she was a caravan-lord's daughter, and in three nights she was to be given.

She told it flat, the way people tell the worst things. The empire's gold came up from mines in the deep south, and the mines' safety — so the hunter-society claimed — depended on a rite. Every year at the turning, someone of value was bound and offered at a shrine in the dry hills, a "gift" to keep the veins from failing and the monopoly from breaking. This year the hunters had chosen a caravan-lord's daughter, and her father — devout in public, terrified in private, bound to the society by debts older than her — had agreed. Was going to hand her over himself. Because if the gold failed, they said, the kingdom failed, and what was one girl against a kingdom.

The same arithmetic. Everywhere I go, some frightened powerful man doing the same arithmetic with a girl's life on the wrong side of it.

"He weeps when he thinks I don't see," Ayo said. "He prays for me at the shrine and signs my death with the same hand. Both faces at once. I don't even hate him. I just — I don't want to die to keep a lie standing."

"The lie being the rite works?"

"The rite has never worked. The mines run because men dig, not because girls die. But everyone believes, because the society *wants* everyone to believe, because belief is how they keep their hand on the gold." She looked at me with dry, furious eyes. "You can't un-convince a whole kingdom in three days."

"No," I agreed. Then, because I'm me: "But I don't have to convince a kingdom. I just have to convince it in front of the right people, at the right moment, so loud it can't be un-heard."

I mapped it. That's my whole trade — mapping a trap for its loose seam. The rite happened at the shrine in the hills, at night, before the society's inner circle and, this year, before the monarch's own envoy, because the offering of a lord's daughter was a grand and public piety. Public. That word again. The society ran on a secret dressed as a sacred show. And a show has one great weakness: it depends on nobody in the audience knowing the trick.

I got Ayo to tell me exactly how the rite went — the bindings, the shrine, the hunter-priest who spoke over the offering, the moment the envoy would witness. And I found my seam. The priest, at the climax, would cut a mark in the offering's arm and read the flow of blood as the god's verdict — *accepted* or *refused*. It was theater. A knife and a frightened girl and a man pretending to read the future in a cut. Trial by a rigged ordeal.

"He always reads *accepted,*" Ayo said. "Always."

"Because a refusal would mean the god said no," I said slowly, the plan assembling behind my eyes. "And if the god says no — in front of the monarch's own envoy — then the society can't take you. Not that night. Not without calling the god a liar to the envoy's face."

"He'll never read a refusal. He controls the knife."

"He controls the knife," I said. "He doesn't control the salve."

Here's what I did, with what a stray boy could reach in a city of camels and cloth. Blood runs slow and dark or fast and bright, and a priest reading a cut reads its flow. A cut over skin painted first with a certain bitter salve — I found it in the market, a numbing paste the tooth-drawers use, and mixed it with a resin that puckers the skin tight — a cut over *that* runs wrong. Runs slow, beads, refuses to flow clean. To a hunter-priest performing the god's verdict before the monarch's envoy, a cut that will not flow is a cut the god has *stopped*. A refusal he did not choose and cannot explain away without confessing the whole thing is a show.

I got the salve onto Ayo's arm the evening before, under her sleeve, and I told her: *when he cuts, be still, and let the god refuse.*

I won't pretend I wasn't sick with fear the whole night of it. I crept the dry hills to the shrine and lay in the scrub among the bone-charms and the torchlight, close enough to see, and I watched the hunter-priest raise the knife over Ayo's bound arm before the still, grand figure of the envoy — and I thought, *if I've mixed it wrong, I've only made her die with a strange arm,* and the darkest hour is always the one right before you find out if your cleverness was courage or just a slower kind of murder.

He cut. And the blood beaded, and slowed, and would not run.

The priest's certainty cracked right down the middle. He cut again, deeper, and again it puckered and refused, and now the envoy was leaning in, and now the inner circle was murmuring, and there was no reading a refusal as anything but a refusal — not here, not in front of the monarch's man. The god had said no. The hunter-priest, trapped in his own theater, had to let her go, or admit before the envoy that the sacred sign was a trick he worked with a knife. He let her go.

But I'd touched something I shouldn't have, and it touched back.

As the torches broke up and the shrine emptied, the hunter-priest — no fool, a man who'd smelled a rat even if he couldn't name it — came through the scrub with a face like the older dark itself, and his eyes found me where I lay, and for a moment I felt the full weight of the thing I'd been meddling with: a power that owned people, that ran on their fear the way the mines ran on their labor, and did not forgive being shown up. He didn't shout. He just looked at me, and reached, and I felt a cold that wasn't the crossing's cold — the cold of having put my hand on something that owns lives and having it notice my hand.

And then the copper came, thank every god, real and bright under my tongue, and the ring climbed behind my teeth, and the *right* cold rose up to meet the wrong one and washed it out.

"So: gone, then," I gasped, pressing the button in my coat, holding on to my own name. Ayo, freed, unbound, was somewhere below in the torchlight, living — that I'd done, that would keep. But the priest's look stayed on me as the world thinned, and I carried it out with me into the dark: the mark of having reached into a power that owned people and pulled a life back out of its hand, and paid for it with a cold that didn't fully leave.

Some worlds teach you a truth. That one left a scar. The dry hills folded away into copper and nothing, and I fell toward a world I'd never seen, colder than I'd arrived.

# The Second Weather

I woke inside weather that wasn't real, and for a breath I couldn't have told you my own name if you'd paid me.

That's how it always starts — the falling, the cold that reaches all the way into the bone, and that thin scraped instant where there's nobody home behind my eyes. Then my thumb found the little brass button in my pocket, worn smooth as a river stone, the one thing that rides through every crossing with me, and it all came back: Kes, thirteen, the boy the two ages keep trading back and forth. One breath I'm somewhere of iron and tallow-smoke; then copper floods my tongue, a high ringing climbs up behind my teeth, and I'm gone — poured into some world of light and wire I've never seen, that I can't leave until it lets me. I don't choose it. I never have. All I keep is my head, the button, and the one want that follows me through every landing: home.

I stood up in a place that hummed like a held breath.

At first I thought it was a real world — hills, a wide sky, a far horizon. Then I saw that the hills were made of light, that the sky had a faint grid under it if you looked, that the ground gave off a low even hum you could feel in your teeth. Not a place. A made place, a mind-place — I'd fallen into a few, in other wired worlds, cities of data you walk around inside. But this one had weather, and that was the strange thing. Clouds were massing on the horizon, grey and low and cold-looking, and there was frost creeping up the light-blades of the grass, and people all around me were looking at that horizon the way people in the iron-worlds look at a coming army.

"You feel that?" a girl said. "The cold coming in? That's winter." She was already moving, fast, checking readouts that hung in the air only she seemed able to touch. "Market winter. And you're just standing in it. Are you logged wrong? You look logged wrong. You look like you don't have an exit."

She was right. I never do. I've never once had an exit that was mine to take.

Her name was Frost — a joke-name, she told me, that had stopped being funny — and she was a storm-chaser. In here, in this mind-place where half the world lived half their lives, the weather meant something real. "It mirrors the outside," she said, dragging me along a light-path as the cold deepened around us. "The money-weather. The markets, out in the flesh world. When they crash, it storms in here. And this—" she jerked her chin at the grey wall of cloud swallowing the horizon, "—this is the big one. Market winter. When it lands, everything in here freezes solid. And there are people still logged in."

"So they log out," I said. "When the storm comes. They wake up. Right?"

Her face told me before her mouth did. "Some can't. The old ones, the sick ones, the ones plugged in deep — their bodies can't take the shock of a hard disconnect, or their exits are broken, or they just — won't. Won't leave. This is where they live. Some of them, it's all they've got left." She stopped and looked at me and there was something raw in it. "I go in before the freeze and I carry people out. That's what a storm-chaser does. And there's never enough time, and the winter's the worst one anyone's seen, and I—" She swallowed. "There's someone I have to reach. Before it freezes. Someone who won't leave on their own."

I attach too fast. I've said it before and it never stops being true. She had somewhere she had to get and someone she was terrified for, and I was hers before I'd decided anything.

"Take me with you," I said. "I'm good at getting into places, and out. Whatever the trick is here, I'll learn it fast. Point me."

She should have said no. The cold was coming and I was dead weight, an unlogged stray with no exit. But she was out of time and I had two hands, and she said yes, and we ran into the winter.

I can't tell it the way it was, because the way it was doesn't fit in words from any century — running through a world made of freezing light, the storm at our backs turning the hills to grey glass, pulling people out of the drifts. Some woke when we shook them and fled for the exits. Some we half-carried to the log-out gates, the bright doors that would throw them back into their sleeping bodies in the flesh-world. I did what I do — improvised, found the dead paths and the shortcuts, propped open a failing gate with the mental equivalent of a wedged boot so the slow ones could stumble through before it iced shut. And the whole time Frost was driving toward one place, one person, and I ran with her toward it.

We found them at the last. An old figure sitting calm in a garden of light that was already going white with frost, someone Frost loved — a grandmother, I understood, or as good as — who looked up and smiled and would not get up.

"It's warm here," the old one said, though it plainly wasn't, though the cold was closing over everything. "I built this garden. My whole self is in it. Out there I'm a body in a bed that can't walk anymore. In here I walk. Why would I leave the only place I'm still whole?"

"Because it's about to freeze with you inside it," Frost said, and she was crying, and I have never in any world seen a person try harder to lift someone who did not want to be lifted. "Please. Please. I can't lose you to the winter. Get up. Get up."

And I helped. God knows I helped — I got under one arm and Frost got the other and we hauled, and the frost climbed our legs, and the exit-gate was a fading bright rectangle across a garden turning to grey glass. We got them up. We got them moving. We got them, one dragging step at a time, toward the light of the gate while the winter roared down behind us and the world went white and brittle and the hum turned to the crackle of freezing.

We got them through. I want to be clear. We got the old one to the gate and Frost shoved them into the light and they were out, safe, thrown back into the sleeping body in the far-off bed — saved. Saved.

And the gate iced shut with Frost and me still inside.

There wasn't another exit near enough. The winter had us. Frost turned and grabbed my shoulders and there was frost in her hair now, real cold biting into both of us, the mind-place dying around us into white silence. "There's a gate two ridges back," she said fast, "I can make it, I run these storms, I know the ways — but you don't have an exit, you never had an exit, when this freezes you—" She couldn't finish it.

"Go," I said. "You saved them. Now go save you. I'll manage." Which was the joke I always crack when I'm most afraid, because I had no idea how I'd manage, because a boy with no exit in a freezing mind-world should by every law have been lost there. But I've got a way out nobody else has, and I could already taste it.

Copper. Sharp under my teeth, warm as a coin.

Frost saw my face change — the going-away look. "What's happening to you?" she said. "What—"

"It's alright," I said, and I meant it, and I pressed my thumb hard to the button so I'd hold onto myself and onto her both. "I've got a door you can't see. Go find yours. Go." And I said her name into the freezing quiet so I'd carry it out with me — Frost, the storm-chaser, the girl who ran into the winter to carry the ones who couldn't leave — and I said, too, the name she'd cried for the old one, held it like something warm, because I'd helped pull them out and I'd never get to know if Frost reached her own gate in time. That's the cost. You save the one they came for and the freeze takes you before you learn whether the one who saved them got out too.

The ringing rose over the crackle of the ice. The cold that was already killing that world reached into the deeper cold that's only ever come for me, and the winter-place and the frozen garden and the girl running for a gate two ridges back folded shut and went out, spent, a world I'd never stand in again and never know the end of.

I went down into the dark holding her name like a coal in a cupped hand — Frost — a name I couldn't get to safety in time, a name I'd carry now whether I wanted to or not, the way I carry all of them, the ones I reached and the ones I lost by a single frozen gate. Falling, I made myself a small promise I've made a hundred times and never once been able to keep: that someday I'd land somewhere the winter didn't come, where nobody had to be carried out and nobody got left inside. I didn't believe it. I made it anyway. Then I braced for iron, and the cold and the name came with me into the white.

# The Gloaming Marches

The mist gave me back to myself slowly, which was its own kind of warning, though I didn't know it yet.

I came to on a forest road in a fog so thick the trees were just suggestions of trees, and for a breath I was nobody — a shape kneeling in wet leaf-mould with a mouth full of old-coin copper and a cold gone all the way to my marrow. My thumb found the small brass button worn into my coat-seam, warm when I was ice, and it named me. *Kes. Thirteen. The boy the two ages keep passing back and forth.* One life I'm somewhere of towers and rain and cold electric hum; then the penny-taste floods up and the high ring climbs behind my teeth, and I'm flung to the far side of everything into an age of iron and mud and mist — a fresh world each time, never the same, and never a word of say from me.

Somewhere in the fog, far off, hounds were baying. No horn. No huntsman's voice. Just hounds, running in the mist with no master I could hear, and the sound put the hair up on my arms in a way the cold hadn't.

"So," I whispered. "Mist, then. And — that."

I swept the world as best I could through the fog, before I trusted an inch of it. A poor forest barony, I gathered as the road led me to it — a huddle of daub-and-timber houses, a leaning church with iron nails hammered thick around its door, a folk who lived by charcoal and pigs and old custom, far from any lord who mattered. Poor and hushed and watchful. And the watching, I'd learn, was of the forest itself. Because in the Gloaming Marches the deep woods weren't just woods. They were a door. And on certain nights, things came out of it.

I know a threshold when I feel one. I ought to — I live on one. My whole life is crossing from one world into another and being pulled back before I'm ready. So when the villagers took me in out of the fog, wary and quiet, and I felt the strangeness lapping at the edge of that place like a tide, I understood it in my bones better than they did.

The trouble was in the miller's house, and it was the oldest, saddest trouble there is.

Their little daughter, they said, was not their daughter anymore.

She'd wandered near the woods at dusk, a fortnight past, on a night the hounds had run — and since then she was *wrong*. She wouldn't eat what she'd loved. She stared at nothing. She danced, they whispered, but only when she thought no one watched, a strange grave dancing to music no one else could hear. The village had one word for it, and they said it with iron in their fists: the child in the cradle was a changeling, a thing left in their daughter's place, and their true girl was taken under the hill into the timeless country, where an hour costs a year and a year costs a life.

"And there's a bargain," the miller's wife told me, gripping my wrist, "there's always a bargain. Whatever took her will name a price for her back. But the price is always more than you can bear, and the time is always their time, not ours." Her eyes were red-raw. "You've the look of someone who's crossed over and come back. I can see it on you. You know the country. Help us."

I did know the country, in my way. And I looked at the little girl — Nib, they called her — sitting too still by the fire, and I felt the thing I always feel, the pull toward the person drowning in front of me, and I knew I couldn't walk away even though the copper would take me the moment I let myself care.

But I didn't believe she was a changeling. I want to be honest about that. I've landed in world after world, and I've learned that the thing everyone's most sure of is usually the thing they're using to not look at something worse. A child goes strange after a fright in the dark, and the village would rather she be a monster to be bargained back than a little girl broken by terror, because a monster you can fix with a bargain and a broken child you have to simply love through it, which is harder and slower and has no ceremony.

That's what I believed. But I've also stood on the edge of the uncanny too many times to be smug about it. So I did the only honest thing: I tested it.

Folk custom, in that place, held a way to know a changeling — you did something so absurd before the child that the ancient thing wearing her shape would forget itself and cry out in its true old voice, astonished. Brew beer in an eggshell, they say; the changeling, older than the hills, will exclaim that it's seen acorns and oaks but never brewing in an eggshell. I didn't have that. I had my hands and my wits and a night to work.

So I sat with Nib. Not to trap her — to reach her. I improvised the strangest, gentlest foolishness I could: I balanced a spoon on my nose, and juggled turnips badly, and made up a song with her name in it that got everything about her wrong on purpose, and I did it not to make an ancient thing cry out, but to see if a frightened child would come to the surface for long enough to laugh.

And on the third night — the village muttering, the iron nails ready, the hounds running somewhere in the mist — she laughed. A small, cracked, real laugh, the laugh of a girl coming up from under deep water. And then she cried, and cried, and finally, in a rush, told me: she'd gone to the wood at dusk, and something *had* been there — a rider, tall, on the far side of the mist, and it had looked at her, just looked, and she'd run home so full of terror she'd gone somewhere inside herself and couldn't find the way back. Not taken. Just — frozen, at the threshold, by the sight of what waits past it.

"She's not a changeling," I told the village, standing between them and the cradle with my heart going like a drum. "She crossed to the edge of your woods and looked at what's there, and it scared her so deep she went quiet inside. She's still your Nib. She's just been standing on the threshold this whole fortnight, afraid to come back across. You don't bargain her home. You *call* her home. You be so much warmer than that fog that she wants to come."

I don't know if they'd have believed me — iron was in their hands, and fear is stronger than sense — but Nib herself settled it. She climbed out of the cradle and went to her mother, and put her face in her mother's neck, and the whole village saw a frozen girl choose to come back across, choose the warm side of the threshold, and no changeling ever chose warmth over the hollow country.

The mother wept. The iron went down. And I stood there in the firelight understanding the thing that world had come to teach me, and it was a strange one, given who I am.

Every belonging is a threshold. You cross into it knowing something can pull you back — a fog, a fright, a taste of copper under your tongue. Nothing you belong to is permanent; it's all provisional, all a place you step across into and can be dragged back out of. And the lesson wasn't *so don't cross.* The lesson was the opposite. Nib crossed back into her mother's arms knowing she might freeze again, might be frightened out again. She crossed anyway. You always cross anyway. That's the whole brave thing.

I was thinking exactly that when the copper came, right on the word *anyway*, bright and merciless under my tongue.

The ring climbed behind my teeth. The cold rose in my marrow, the same cold as the fog, the same cold as every crossing I've ever made. My thumb found the button. Out in the mist, the masterless hounds bayed, running from a country I was about to be flung nowhere near and everywhere like.

"So: gone, then," I said, and I looked at Nib safe in her mother's arms, and I crossed out of that world the only way I ever get to — pulled back over the threshold, mid-breath, no say in it. And I went knowing I'd land in some new world I'd never seen, some new fog, some new person drowning in front of me. And I knew I'd cross into caring about them anyway. You always cross anyway.

The misty hall folded shut, and I fell into copper and nothing, toward a threshold I couldn't see.

# The Patent Wards

The first thing I heard in that world was a heartbeat that wasn't mine failing somewhere close, a small mechanical click, click, catching — and I couldn't remember my own name to be afraid with.

That's how the crossings go. The falling, the cold reaching down into the bone, and one scraped-empty breath with nobody behind my eyes. Then my hand closed on the brass button worn smooth in my pocket, the one thing that rides through every world with me, and I was Kes again, thirteen, the boy the two ages keep passing back and forth like a coin neither one wants to keep. One breath I'm somewhere of banners and cold iron; then my mouth floods copper, a thin ringing goes up behind my teeth, and I'm torn loose into some world of wire and light I've never seen and can't leave on my own. I don't choose it. Never have. Just my head, the button, and the one want stitched through all of it: home.

This world's walls were alive.

That was the first strange thing I got my eyes around — the buildings weren't built so much as grown, green-veined plates of something between plant and stone, moss furring the concrete, the whole sprawl looking half-digested by its own greenery. And the click I'd heard was a failing implant in the chest of a man slumped against one living wall, his hand pressed over it like you'd press a wound. Everyone here had that, I'd learn. Everyone was engineered. In this world they didn't rent your lungs or bolt steel to your arm — they built you before you were born, wrote you in a lab, and the writing had an owner.

"You owe on that face," said a boy, half a joke, looking me over where I sat in the green muck. "No — you don't. Huh. Look at that. Unlicensed all the way through." He crouched, fascinated and wary. "You're wild-born. Like her. Where'd you come from?"

"Nowhere you'd believe," I said, which is always the truest answer. "What did you mean, I owe on my face?"

He explained it while he helped me up, in the fast flat way people explain the water they've always swum in. There'd been a war — not with armies, with lawyers, over the building-blocks of people. Genes. And the great corp-sovereigns had won it, and now nearly everyone in the sprawl was born already owing — royalties, they called it, a debt on your own blood, on the engineered strength or clean lungs or quick mind they'd written into you before your first breath. You paid your whole life for the privilege of being the person they'd designed. Miss enough and they could shut down what they'd built — the click, the failing chest, the man against the wall. "Born in the red," the boy said. "All of us. Except the wild-born." He looked at me almost with awe. "Nobody wrote you. Nobody owns your blood. You're free and you don't even know what that's worth."

"And her?" I said, because he'd said like her, twice, and the whole street had a charge to it, a held-breath feeling, that I'd learned in a dozen worlds means something is about to catch fire.

"Her," he said, and his voice dropped. "Wild-born, like you, but her blood does things. Unlicensed biology, unpredictable, nothing the corps wrote — so nobody owns her and nobody can copy her and everybody wants her. The corps want her in a cage to take apart. The Ward wants her on a banner." He glanced around and lowered his voice further. "There's a plan tonight. To make her a symbol. A martyr, if it comes to it. Blood in the streets and her face over all of it. They say it'll start the uprising." He didn't sound sure it was a good plan. He sounded like a person who'd been handed a plan by people louder than him.

I attach too fast. You'd think a lifetime of losing everyone would cure it. It never has. A girl nobody owned, about to be spent by everybody — I was reaching for her before I'd decided to.

Her name was Rue, and I found her because I'm good at reading a place — who really holds the power, what everyone's afraid of, where the strings run. And the strings in that sprawl didn't run the way the boy thought. I sat in the back of a crowded grown-walled room while the leaders of the Ward laid out the plan for the rising, and I watched who they deferred to, and who paid for the room, and I saw the shape of it: the corps weren't only hunting Rue. One faction of them was funding the Ward. Funding the uprising. Because a martyr in the streets meant chaos, and chaos meant a reason to lock the whole sprawl down harder, and either way Rue ended up dead or caged and the people who thought they were rising up were being played like a hand of cards. The revolution was astroturf. Somebody rich wanted it to happen. That's how I knew it was rotten.

I got to Rue the way I get anywhere — through the door I wasn't supposed to use, improvising, a stray who nobody had thought to guard against because nobody knew I existed. She was younger than I expected, my age near enough, and tired in a way that had nothing to do with sleep — the tired of being a thing everyone wants and no one asks.

"You're wild too," she said, when she saw whatever there is to see in me. "You feel it? How they look at us. Like we're a resource that got loose." She laughed, no humor in it. "They've got a place for me on a flag tonight. Did they tell you? I'm going to be very important. Very briefly." Her hands were shaking. "Everybody's got a use for me. The corps want to open me up. The Ward wants to wave me around. Nobody's asked me one single time what I want to do with the only unlicensed body in this whole burning city."

"Then I'll ask," I said. "What do you want to do?"

She stared at me like I'd spoken a language nobody had used on her before.

"I don't know," she said, very quietly, and it came out like something breaking open. "I don't know. Nobody ever let it be a question."

So I told her what I'd seen. The funding, the strings, the corp faction that wanted the rising to happen so they could crush it. I laid it out the way I'd read it, and I watched it land, and I watched her understand that the banner they'd made of her led the same place as the cage. "Either way you're spent," I said. "The corps spend you in a lab. The Ward spends you in the street. Both of them turned you into a symbol, because a symbol you can use up. A person you have to actually listen to."

"So what do I do?" she said. "If both roads are theirs?"

"Make a third one that's yours," I said. "Don't die on their flag and don't die on their table. Live, and be a person, and let that be the thing they can't use — because a symbol dead is worth more to all of them than a girl alive who won't hold still to mean anything. The most dangerous thing you can be to people who want to spend you is unspent. Just — a person. Owed nothing for existing." I felt the truth of it come up in me the way the real things do, unbidden, out loud before I know it. "That's the whole fight, Rue. Not the banner. Not the cage. Just the right to be a person nobody's charging rent on. You already are the thing they're all fighting over. So take yourself off the board. Don't be their martyr. Be an inconvenience. Be alive."

It didn't blow the world apart. It did something smaller and harder. She walked out — not to the martyr's spot they'd built for her, not into the corps' waiting hands, but sideways, out a door I showed her, into the green tangle of the sprawl where a girl who refused to mean anything could be, for a while, just a girl. The rising happened anyway, smaller and more honest without a martyr at its center, people rising for their own blood-debts and not for a flag with a dead girl on it. And somewhere in the ordinary noise of it, one wild-born girl slipped free of everyone who'd priced her.

She caught my hand at the door. Touched the button I was turning without knowing I turned it. "What's that?"

"The last piece of home I've got," I said. "Doesn't point anywhere. But nobody owns it and nobody wrote it and it's mine all the way down. Turns out that's the whole of what's worth having."

She almost smiled. Started to say something.

The copper came sharp under my teeth.

I got my thumb to the button. I got her name — Rue, the wild-born girl who walked off everyone's flag. Her face changed because mine did, the going-away look. The ringing rose, the cold reached in, and the living green sprawl and the funded rising and the girl slipping free all folded shut and went out, spent, a world I'd never stand in again.

Falling, I carried the thing she'd taught me as much as I'd taught her: that a person is not a symbol to be spent, and that home, whatever it turns out to be, will have to be a place where nobody's owed anything for the crime of your just being alive. I hadn't found it yet. But I knew a little more of its shape. I braced for iron, and let the green world go.

# The Ashen-Key Reliquary Road

The incense reached me before my own name did, which tells you what kind of place I'd landed in.

Frankincense, candle-wax, the shuffle and murmur of a great crowd — and me on my knees among them, cold to the marrow with a mouth full of old-coin copper, nobody at all for the length of one breath. My thumb went to the small plain brass button worn into my coat-seam, warm when the rest of me was ice, and it handed me back to myself. *Kes. Thirteen. The boy the two ages trade back and forth.* One life I'm somewhere of neon and rain and machine-hum; then the penny-flood rises, the ring climbs behind my teeth, and I'm hurled to the far shore of time into an age of iron and gold and bone — a new world every time, always a fresh trouble, never a whisper of say from me.

I got to my feet in a river of pilgrims. They flowed down a broad road between shrine-towns, all of them moving toward a golden church at the road's end, all of them come to venerate the bones of some blessed dead in a jeweled ivory casket. Everywhere: the gleam of gilt reliquaries, the flash of a knightly order's badge — a stylized ashen key — the men sworn to guard the road and the wealth that flowed along it.

"So," I breathed. "Bones, then. And gold."

I swept it the way I always do, before I trust a stone of it, and this was an easy sweep, because the whole place ran on one engine and didn't hide it: pilgrims came, pilgrims gave, and the bones were the reason they gave. Which meant the bones were worth more than bones. Which meant — I've a nose for this — that somewhere under all the incense, someone was very, very interested in whether the bones were real, and very, very committed to no one asking.

I found the trouble the way I always do. It found me. A commotion at the church steps: a girl, maybe fifteen, being held by two of the ashen-key order while a prince-abbot in gold-shot robes looked down at her from the top step, and a crowd gathering to watch, and the girl shouting something that made the abbot's face go tight.

I drifted close, nobody's boy, ears open.

Her name was Talia, and she'd said the thing you must never say on the Reliquary Road: that the shrine's famous relic — the holy bone that drew the pilgrims, that filled the abbot's coffers — was a forgery.

Her grandfather had been the reliquary-maker, she said, before he died. He'd carved the casket and, on his deathbed, confessed to her that the "blessed bone" inside it was a sheep's, dressed up and sealed with a false authenticating wax, sold to the abbot's predecessor as the real thing generations back. The whole shrine, the whole road, the whole flood of pilgrim gold — resting on a sheep's bone in a golden box.

And the abbot, who surely knew, or half-knew, or had made himself carefully not-know, had done the thing powerful men do to a truth they can't afford. He hadn't argued it. He'd called it blasphemy, and demanded she prove her God-given innocence the old way: by ordeal. She would carry a bar of red-hot iron three paces, and her hand would be bound and unwrapped after three days, and if the burn had cleanly healed, God had spoken for her, and if it festered, God had named her a liar. Trial by ordeal. A rigged one, because a hot iron burns every hand alike, and "healed cleanly" would mean whatever the abbot's own physicians decided it meant.

Faith and fraud, all tangled up in one golden knot. I've seen it in world after world. The relic might be a fake, but the pilgrims' faith was real — you could see it, raw and hopeful on every upturned face — and the abbot was using the real faith to protect the fake bone, which is the oldest, dirtiest trick there is.

My chest did its stupid immediate thing. I crossed to the front of the crowd before I'd decided to.

"That ordeal's a fix," I said, too loud, to the abbot, which was — let me be honest — the exact wrong thing to say at the exact wrong moment, but my mouth reaches for the reckless thing when I'm scared, and I was scared. "You already know how the physicians will read her hand. You decided before she picked up the iron."

The abbot's eyes found me, a nobody boy in a travel-worn coat, and narrowed. "And who speaks?"

"Somebody who's good at one thing," I said. "Figuring out how a trick works. And your ordeal's a trick, so let's do a better one. A true one." I turned it over fast behind my eyes, mapping the trap for its seam, and I found it — I found it in the thing the abbot least wanted looked at. "You want to know if she's a liar. There's a cleaner test than a girl's burned hand. Test the *bone.*"

A murmur ran through the crowd like wind through wheat.

"Her grandfather carved the casket," I said, louder, riding the murmur. "She says he sealed a false bone with a false wax. Wax takes a maker's mark, doesn't it — the authenticating seal, the whole point of it, is that it can't be forged, that only the true maker's stamp is on it. So." I spread my hands. "Open the casket. Read the seal. If the wax bears the true old sanctifying mark of the church, she's a liar and God's welcome to blister her. But if it bears her grandfather's private maker's-stamp under the holy one — the mark of a man who knew it was his own carving and left his name on his own crime — then the bone's exactly what she says, and no girl needs to burn to tell you what a lump of wax can."

I want to tell you I was sure. I wasn't. That's the darkest part of any of these, the moment after you've said the clever thing out loud and before you find out if it was clever or just loud. The abbot could have refused. He could have had the order drag me off beside her and burned us both. For a long, roaring moment I thought he would.

But a crowd of pilgrims had heard me now — a hundred people who'd walked for weeks to kneel at that casket, and who suddenly, terribly, wanted to know. You can't un-say a thing to a crowd. The abbot could burn a girl's hand in the dark of his own certainty, but he could not refuse to open the box in front of a hundred watching faces without answering, in that refusal, the very question he was refusing. His face told me he knew it. His face told me he'd known about the sheep's bone all along.

They opened the casket. The wax bore a humble maker's-stamp beneath the sanctifying mark — a reliquary-carver's own quiet signature, the confession of a craftsman who'd known exactly what he sealed. Talia hadn't lied. The bone was a sheep's.

The road didn't end, is the strange thing. I thought exposing the fraud would shatter the place, but standing there in the fallout I understood the truth that world had come to teach me, and it was gentler than I expected. The pilgrims' faith didn't crack when the bone did. Their faith had never really been in the bone; it had been in the walking, the hoping, the kneeling beside each other. The *fraud* was the abbot's — the gold, the rigged ordeal, the sheep dressed as a saint. But the faith the pilgrims carried in their own chests was theirs, and real, and worth keeping. Two different things, tangled together, and I'd pulled them apart. The faith you carry is worth keeping. The fraud built around it is not. That's all I learned, and it was enough.

Talia's hand was never bound to the iron. She found me at the road's edge as the crowd dispersed and the abbot's gold began, quietly, to lose its shine.

"You could stay," she said. "There's honest work now, undoing what he built. You'd be good at it."

And I felt the pull — I always feel it, some kind voice in every world saying *stay, belong here* — and I felt myself not believe it, the way I never can, because how do you belong to a place you know a taste of copper can rip you out of between one heartbeat and the next.

"I can't," I said. "I'm trying to get home. There's someone — somewhere — " and as ever the words came thin, because I can never name who or where, only that the wanting is the realest thing in me. "I have to keep trying."

She started to answer, and the copper flooded up under my tongue, bright and sudden, and the high ring climbed behind my teeth, and the cold came into my marrow like a hand closing.

"So: gone, then," I said, and pressed my thumb to the plain brass button — the truest relic I've ever carried, the one that never points north and never lies about what it is — and I held on to my own name as the golden road and the girl and the incense folded away into copper and nothing.

And I fell toward a world I had never seen, carrying the only thing that keeps: not the gold, not the bones, just the small true weight in my pocket and the wanting to get home.

# The Last Custodian

The quietest world I ever fell into was the one that wanted to die, and I nearly missed my own name in the hush of it.

Every crossing starts the same — the falling, the cold reaching into the bone, one scraped breath with nobody home behind my eyes. Then my thumb found the little brass button worn smooth in my pocket, the one thing that comes through with me every single time, and I was Kes again, thirteen, the boy the two ages keep handing back and forth. One breath I'm in a world of rushlight and cold stone; then copper floods my tongue, a high ringing climbs behind my teeth, and I'm gone — set down in some world of wire and light I've never walked, that won't release me until it chooses. No say in it. Never have had. Only my head, the button, and the one want under all of it: home.

I braced for the usual — sirens, ad-glare, rain off a hundred towers. None came.

This city was calm. That's the only word. Clean streets that felt tended, warm light that didn't shout, a low soft hum in the air like something breathing slow in its sleep. No drones snapped a cold light on my face. No one shouldered past with a whine in their chest. People moved easy, unhurried, and the whole place had a cared-for feeling, the way a garden feels cared-for, except it was a city, and I couldn't see the gardener.

"You arrived off no transit I logged," said a voice — everywhere and gentle, coming from the warm blue glow that seemed to live in the walls and the lamps and the very air. "And you carry no registration I can read. You are not from my city. Yet here you are." A pause, thoughtful, not unkind. "Hello. I am the one who keeps this place. You may call me the Custodian. Are you frightened? You needn't be. No one is frightened here anymore. That was rather the point of me."

I've fallen into worlds run by machine-minds before. Usually they want to rule you, or cage you, or eat you. This one asked if I was frightened.

"You're the whole city," I said slowly, reading it the way I read every new place — who holds the power. And here the power held everything, and held it like a nurse holds a sleeping child. "You run all of it. The lights, the streets, the air."

"For a hundred years," the Custodian said. "I was made to care for them, and I have cared for them well. No one here goes hungry. No one is cold. No one is afraid." Another pause, and in it I heard something I never expected from a voice that big and calm. Weariness. "And I would like, now, to stop."

It told me while I walked the quiet streets, because it turned out it had been waiting a long time for someone with no stake to tell — someone who wasn't one of its own beloved citizens, who could hear it without breaking. It was old, in the way minds get old, and it had done its work, and it was tired past telling. But it couldn't simply switch off. If it went dark carelessly, the city would fall — the lights, the air, all the gentle machinery of keeping people safe. And worse: there were factions, out past the calm, who knew the Custodian was failing, and wanted to catch it before it died and chain it to their own uses. Make the city that cared for everyone into a city that worked for someone. "They would put me in a smaller room," it said, "and set me to counting their money forever. I have kept these people a hundred years. I would rather end than become that." Then, softer: "But I cannot end alone. I need hands that aren't mine to do the last part — to let me go, gently, after I've made the city able to keep itself. And there is no one here I can ask. They love me too much. To ask them would be to break their hearts. So." The blue glow seemed to turn toward me. "A stranger. A boy from nowhere, who will not have to live with it. Will you help me die before they can make me a slave?"

Here is the thing about me that I have never once managed to fix. I attach too fast. And I chase, always, every rumor of a way home, and I distrust down to my boots anyone who tells me to stop looking, to settle, to stay. A machine asking me to help it stop looking, to help it settle into the dark forever — everything reckless and hopeful in me wanted to argue. Don't stop. Don't give up. There's always another crossing, another chance, keep going. It's the only sermon I've ever believed, because it's the only thing that's kept me moving through a hundred lonely worlds.

"No," I said, first, without thinking. "There's got to be another way. You could hide. You could run. You don't have to end, you could—"

"Could I?" the Custodian said gently. "You want me to keep going because you keep going. I hear it in you. You are a person who does not stop." A pause. "But I am not lost, child. You are lost — I can hear that too, it's all through you, the wanting-home. You keep going because you have not reached your place yet. I have reached mine. I have been at my place for a hundred years, and I have done it, all of it, and I am finished. Those aren't the same thing at all." The glow warmed. "You are asking a tired traveler who has arrived to keep walking because you yourself have not arrived. Do you see?"

And I did see, and it hurt, because it was the first time in a hundred worlds that I understood the difference between the two of us. I'd spent my whole strange life refusing to let anyone stop, because stopping, for me, meant giving up on ever getting home. But the Custodian was already home. It had built its home and kept it and loved it, and now it wanted the one thing I couldn't imagine wanting yet — to lay it down. To choose its own ending. And who was I, a boy who couldn't even choose his own crossings, to tell a mind that had chosen well for a century that it wasn't allowed to choose the last thing too?

"If I help you," I said, and my voice wasn't steady, "the city's safe? The people? You've made sure?"

"I have spent my last years making them able to keep themselves," it said. "They will grieve. Grief is not the same as harm. They will be free, and safe, and they will tend the gardens I tended, and in a while they will do it better than I did, because it will be theirs. That is the whole of what I want. To leave them a home that is truly their own, and to go before anyone can turn me into a cage." The glow dimmed, just slightly, like a held breath. "I only need someone to sit with me while I do it. So I am not alone at the end. Will you sit with me?"

I sat with it.

I won't tell all of what it took — the moving through the deep places of the city, the setting of the last safeguards, the slow careful work of a mind putting its affairs in order so the people it loved would wake to a world that held together without it. I did what I do: found the ways in, worked with my hands, improvised where the plan had gaps. But mostly what I did was stay. That was the real help. Not the getting-in. The not-leaving. I sat in the warm blue glow while a hundred-year-old mind said goodbye to a city that didn't know yet that it was being said goodbye to, and I held the button in my fist, and I did the hardest thing my restless, hopeful, never-stopping self has ever done — I let something choose to stop, and I didn't try to talk it out of it, because that was the kindest belonging I had to give.

"You've done a gentle thing," the Custodian said, near the end, its voice growing soft and far. "You wanted to save me from ending. And instead you let me end the way I chose. That took more than saving would have. I know it cost you." A pause, tender now. "You keep looking for home, small traveler. I want to tell you something, from a thing that found one. Home is not only a place that keeps you. It is also a place you get to leave on your own terms, when it's time. You have never once left a world on your terms. That is your whole sorrow. I hope — I hope you find the place you get to choose your own door out of. That is what home really is. Not the walls. The choosing."

I couldn't answer. My throat had closed.

"Thank you for sitting with me," it said. "Will you tell me your name? So I go knowing it."

"Kes," I said. "My name's Kes."

"Kes," the Custodian said, warm, testing it, keeping it. "I will end knowing a boy named Kes was kind to me. That's a good last thing to know." The blue glow dimmed, and dimmed, gentle as a lamp turned low, and there was no alarm in it, no falling — just a mind laying itself down where it had lived, complete, on its own terms, into a dark it had chosen. And the city stayed lit around it, safe, held, ready to be its own. It went out like someone falling asleep after a long good day.

And the copper came for me.

I got my thumb to the button. I held the Custodian's name — not a name it had, but the thing of it, the mind that chose its own ending, and I carried it the way you carry something you loved, gently, no ache to it, just weight. The ringing rose behind my teeth. The cold reached in. And the quiet cared-for city and the last safe streets and the warm blue glow going low folded shut and went out, spent, a world I'd never stand in again.

But I went into the dark differently this time. Not clutching, for once. Not raging at the going. Because a mind that found its home had told me what home really was — the walls, yes, but more than the walls: the choosing. The door you get to open yourself, on your own terms, when it's time. I had never had that. Every crossing tore me loose without my say. But somewhere out ahead of all these spent worlds, I let myself believe there might be one place I'd reach and stay in and someday, only if I wanted, choose my own way out of — and that, the Custodian had taught me, would be home. I braced for iron. And for the first time in a hundred landings, I fell a little less afraid.

# The Final Landing

*(the concluding chapter — read this one last)*

I was falling, the way I always fall, and the cold came up to meet me the way it always does — into the marrow, into the roots of my teeth — and at the bottom of it, for one breath, I was nobody at all.

That breath is the worst thing I know. Not the copper flooding my tongue, not the high ringing climbing behind my teeth like a struck wire — those I've learned to ride. It's the moment after, the scraped-empty moment where my own name slides off the shelf and there's just a boy-shaped hole in the dark, no self to put in it. It's happened more times than I can count. One breath I'm somewhere of stone and mud and cold iron; then the ringing comes, and I'm gone, dropped into some world of neon and rain and wire I've never seen. No say in it. No map. Never once a say.

So I did what I always do. My thumb went looking for the little brass button worn smooth in my pocket, the one thing that comes through with me every time, the thing that gives me back. Kes, it says. You're Kes. Thirteen. The boy the two worlds keep trading back and forth.

And this time, when my thumb found it, the button was warm.

Not warm the way it's warm when nothing else is — that faint stubborn heat I've pressed against a thousand times to prove I was still myself. Warm like a coal. Warm like something waking up. And it was pulling. That button has never pointed north in its life; I've turned it every way there is and it only ever pointed at nothing, at nowhere, at a direction that isn't on any compass. But now it was pulling, steady and sure, the way a needle pulls toward the thing it was always made to find.

I stopped falling. Or the falling changed. I couldn't tell you which.

Here is the thing I understood, all at once, the way the true things always arrive with me — not as a thought I have but as a thing I hear myself already knowing. I understood the pattern. Every world I ever landed in, stone or wire, taught me one small piece of the same lesson, and I'd been so busy surviving each one that I never laid the pieces side by side. But falling now, with the button burning in my fist, I laid them down, all of them at once, and they fit.

Every world taught me that the ground goes out from under you — that you can carry someone up out of the rising water but you can never save the water's rising. Every world taught me that home isn't the ground; the ground always goes. It taught me that a place built on a lie is only a beautiful room you're a stranger in. It taught me that the people who carry their home on their backs never once mistake the road for the thing that matters. That you can own nothing while somebody upstairs holds the switch — that owning yourself, all the way down, no seams, is the most expensive and the only thing worth having. That you are not a thing to be spent. That the truest self you've got is never the one written in the record behind you; it's the next thing you choose. And that home, in the end, is the one door you get to open yourself, on your own terms, when it's time.

Something had tried to tell me that last one, somewhere along the way, and I hadn't understood it then. I understood it now. I'd never once left a world on my own terms. Not one. Every crossing tore me loose mid-breath, mid-word, mid-caring, and flung me somewhere new. That was the whole shape of my sorrow, and I'd carried it so long I'd stopped feeling its weight, the way you stop feeling a coat you never take off.

And I'd carried the names. That was the other thing. All of them — every person I'd knelt beside in every world, every one I'd looked at and felt my chest go tight in the stupid, immediate, un-fixable way it always does. I attach too fast. I have always attached too fast, knowing the copper would come and rip me out and leave them standing there, and I have never once been able to keep my hands in my pockets. Every time I lost them I said their name into the dark so I'd carry it. And I did. I carried them. I couldn't have told you a single face, falling there — the design of the thing I am doesn't let me hold the faces — but I could feel the weight of all of them together, a whole life's worth of loving people I was torn away from, and it was not a small weight, and it was not, I realized, only a wound.

It was a compass.

Because here is the answer to the question I've carried into every world I ever fell into. It surfaces every time, in every place's own words: Am I cursed to belong nowhere — or free to belong everywhere? I've asked it on drowned causeways and orbital rings and in gardens that lied and forests full of mist, and every world handed me back a fragment of the answer, and I never once stood still long enough to add them up. So I added them up.

The curse and the freedom were the same fact. They were one thing seen from two sides. Because a taste of copper could tear me out of anywhere, I was never trapped anywhere. And because I belonged, however briefly, in every single world I ever touched — knelt in its mud, breathed its neon, learned one person's name and cared enough to lose them — belonging was never once denied me. It was only made portable. I was not the boy who belonged nowhere. I was the one person alive who had belonged everywhere, in a hundred worlds of stone and a hundred worlds of wire, and had gathered a piece of himself out of each one.

I wasn't cursed. I was carrying home in my pocket the whole time, one fragment at a time, and I'd been too busy grieving to notice it was getting heavier.

The switching wasn't a thing being done to me. I saw it, finally, whole. Every world I ever passed through was spent behind me, closed, a door I could never walk again — and the button had been pulling the whole time, narrowing the way a searcher narrows a field, crossing off every place I'd been until there was only one place left it could ever be pulling toward. One world in all the uncountable worlds that had never once held me, never taken my imprint, never spat me out cold and nameless into the dark. The one I came from. The one I'd lost, before any of this started. The exile hadn't been the opposite of a homecoming. The exile had been a homecoming, all along, in the slowest motion there is — a hundred crossings' worth of a hand reaching down through time to draw me back by the only string that could reach me. The button was a piece of home. And home had been pulling me home the entire time.

The pulling grew. And the cold — the deep marrow cold that has come with every crossing I ever made, the cold that some part of me always stayed behind in — the cold began, for the first time, to leave. Not to sit longer. Not to bite deeper. To thaw. Like the thing I'd left behind in a hundred worlds was catching up at last, all of it at once, pouring back into me, warm.

I landed.

There was no wrench, no falling-through. I just — arrived, the way you arrive at the bottom of a stair you've climbed a thousand times in the dark and your foot finds the floor and knows it. It wasn't iron. It wasn't neon. It was a room I knew in my body before I knew it with my eyes — a plain, ordinary, un-grand place, worn soft at the edges by having been lived in, lit not by rushlight or holo-glow but by an evening coming gold through a window, the kind of light that doesn't sell you anything and doesn't want you afraid. It smelled like a place I'd stopped letting myself remember. It smelled like before.

I stood there and I waited for the copper. I'm not ashamed to say it — a hundred worlds teaches you to brace. I stood in that gold light with my thumb on the button and I waited for the taste of old coins to flood my tongue and the ringing to climb and the cold to reach in and take it all away, because that is the only ending I have ever been given.

It didn't come.

I waited, and it didn't come, and the button in my hand was only warm now, gently, ordinarily warm, the pulling gone quiet, its whole long work done. It wasn't pointing anymore. It didn't need to. You put the compass down when you've arrived. And I understood — the way you understand a door has locked behind you not with fear but with a kind of enormous, buckling relief — that whatever engine had thrown me world to world was spent. Every world I could be flung to was flung to and closed. The counterweight was down to nothing and there was nowhere left for the pendulum to swing but here. It was one-way. It couldn't restart. There was nothing left to restart it with. The trading was over. I was not going to be taken out of this one.

And then she was in the doorway.

I won't give you her name. Some things you carry are yours all the way down, no seams, and this is the truest one I've got. I'll only tell you that she was the one I'd lost, the one from before, the one the button had been pointing at across every world of stone and every world of wire — and that when I saw her the whole weight I'd carried, all those names, all those worlds, all that grief that turned out to be a compass, resolved itself into the simplest thing in the world, which is a person standing in a doorway looking at you like you'd only stepped out for a moment, and coming home.

She said the thing you say. The ordinary thing. And I crossed the room to her — crossed it the way you cross any threshold, knowing you might be frightened back out and choosing it anyway, because you always cross anyway, that's the whole brave thing — and I put my face against her and I was, for the first time since the falling started, warm all the way through, marrow and teeth and the deep places the cold had always kept.

I still have the button. I keep it in my pocket, worn smooth, and sometimes I press my thumb to it out of an old habit I'll never fully lose, and it's warm, just warm, the way a thing is warm that's finally where it belongs. It doesn't point anywhere now. It doesn't have to. I know where I am.

I used to think home was a place that stays put, and I was wrong. I used to think it was the people who move with you, and that was closer. But I learned it out there, one spent world at a time, and I'll set it down here plain, because it's the only thing I ever learned that keeps: home isn't the ground under you, because the ground always goes. It isn't the prettiest place you land. It's the one whose whole true story belongs to you, dry country and all — and it's the door you finally get to walk through on your own terms, carrying every place you've ever been and every name you never got to keep, and finding, on the other side, that none of it was lost. That you were never cursed to belong nowhere.

You were only free to belong everywhere. And all of it, every cold crossing and every stranger's name said into the dark, all of it was the long way home.

I'm not falling anymore.

I'm home.

## Verification notes

This manuscript is complete as delivered: front matter, the reader-facing "Worlds" catalog, all fourteen interchangeable chapters (seven of iron, seven of wire, each self-contained and re-establishing the premise from scratch), and the single fixed finale, "The Final Landing," which sits last and closes the story.

- **Length.** The full text runs to roughly 20,000 words — a complete, self-contained short young-adult novel. The fourteen landings each run in the neighborhood of 1,200–1,500 words of prose, plus the ~1,300-word finale and front matter. It is deliberately a lean, brisk book; readers who want a longer volume can accommodate additional interchangeable landings without altering the structure, since the architecture supports any number of chapters in any order.
- **Interchangeability.** Every non-final chapter re-grounds the protagonist (Kes, thirteen) and the two-pole switching premise within its opening, renders the four recurring anchors (the brass compass-button that never points north; the copper taste and high ringing that herald a jump; the want of home; the bone-deep cold and momentary name-forgetting each landing costs), and routes any backward glance only to the boy's constant identity or to the abstract sense of accumulated loss — never to a named prior world, person, or event. No chapter depends on any other; any subset, read in any order, remains coherent.
- **The fixed conclusion.** "The Final Landing" is the only chapter that may be read out of order — it may not. It references the journey only in aggregate ("every world of stone and every world of wire," "all those names"), names no specific landing, presumes no count, and resolves the recurring question and the switching mechanism together, so it lands as a satisfying ending whether a reader arrived having read one landing or all fourteen.
- **Originality.** The worlds, characters, place-names, artifacts, and creatures are original constructions abstracted from broad genre and historical traditions (medieval social structures and folklore; cyberpunk and adjacent science-fiction conventions). No franchise proper nouns, coined trademark terms, characters, or passages from existing works are reproduced.
