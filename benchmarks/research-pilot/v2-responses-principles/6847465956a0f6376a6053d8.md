**BLUF.** Haiti's political, economic, and security failures are not three parallel problems but a single self-reinforcing system; leverage exists only at the couplings between them — above all the fuel/port chokepoint that simultaneously funds the gangs, strangles state revenue, and lets armed actors dictate terms to an unelected caretaker executive. Coercive power sits with the Viv Ansanm/G9 coalition while legitimacy sits with CARICOM and the UN — an inversion that narrows U.S. freedom of action and makes any single-lever fix reversible. This memo recommends a sequenced package anchored on Option 1 (financial disruption of the gang rent system), complemented by Option 3 (a funded hold-force capability) and Option 2 (governance conditionality), accepting that no option succeeds in isolation.

## Section I — Situational Baseline

**Security mission.** The international response was authorized by UN Security Council Resolution 2699 (2 October 2023), which endorsed a Kenya-led Multinational Security Support (MSS) mission — UN-authorized but explicitly *not* a UN-commanded operation (13–0, China and Russia abstaining). On 30 September 2025, Resolution 2793 authorized transitioning the MSS into a "Gang Suppression Force" (GSF) with an authorized ceiling of roughly 5,500 personnel for an initial twelve months (12–0, with China, Pakistan, and Russia abstaining). The mission never neared strength: roughly 990 personnel deployed against an initially envisioned 2,500 (Kenya ~730 police, plus contingents from Guatemala, El Salvador, Jamaica, the Bahamas, and Belize). Kenya is now withdrawing — a contingent of 215 officers departed on 17 March 2026, with the drawdown expected to run through October 2026 — while Chad has pledged roughly 1,500 troops, the first ~400 arriving in early April 2026. The GSF is not expected to reach full operational capacity until October 2026. The effort remains chronically short of its estimated ~$600M/year need; the U.S. pledged up to ~$300M but far less was received, and a U.S. funding freeze was reported in January 2025. Haitian forces have retaken "pockets" of downtown Port-au-Prince, but their ability to hold remains uncertain, and gangs have expanded into Artibonite and Centre.

**Transitional governance.** The Transitional Presidential Council (TPC), constituted in April 2024 with a rotating presidency, saw its mandate expire and the body dissolve on 7 February 2026 with no successor. Under a subsequent "National Pact," acting PM Alix Didier Fils-Aimé (appointed November 2024) now remains the only official holding executive power. The Provisional Electoral Council has set a first electoral round for 30 August 2026 and a second for 6 December 2026, with inauguration around 7 February 2027, contingent on security and funding. An anti-corruption unit (ULCC) previously accused three TPC voting members — Emmanuel Vertilaire, Louis Gérald Gilles, and Smith Augustin — of soliciting roughly $750,000 in bribes; they denied the allegations and refused to resign.

**Gang control and humanitarian toll.** Estimates of the capital under gang control range from ~70 percent (a June 2026 estimate for Viv Ansanm) to ~90 percent (Human Rights Watch, early 2026). Viv Ansanm is the dominant coalition and was designated a Foreign Terrorist Organization by the U.S. in 2025; a rift emerged within it in December 2025 over continued kidnappings. Casualty tallies vary by source and window — one UN-cited figure records ~4,384 killed January–September 2025; another reports ~5,519 killed and 2,608 injured March 2025–January 2026. Internally displaced persons exceed 1.4–1.5 million, and 6.4 million people need humanitarian assistance.

## Section II — Diagnosis: The Failure as a Coupled System

Haiti's crisis is best read as a single doom loop in which each domain actively produces the others' decay. Any intervention scoped to one domain will be neutralized by the two it ignores; leverage exists only at the couplings.

**Political → security.** With the TPC dissolved and executive power resting solely and provisionally with acting PM Fils-Aimé under a negotiated pact rather than a popular mandate, the caretaker executive cannot credibly direct, resource, or reform the Haitian National Police, nor can it credibly coerce or bargain with armed actors — because it cannot promise to be there after August. That thin authority *enables* uncontested gang control.

**Security → political.** With an estimated 70–90 percent of metropolitan Port-au-Prince under gang control, coalitions hold, tax, and govern neighborhoods. A state that cannot deliver the one irreducible public good — physical safety — *forfeits* the electoral legitimacy the August 2026 vote is meant to restore, and balloting cannot be secured across gang-held terrain. Security failure thus *forecloses* its own political remedy.

**Economic → capacity.** Gang control of logistics chokepoints and pervasive extortion *suppress* formal commerce, *collapse* customs receipts, and *starve* police salaries and services — which *widens* ungoverned space and *expands* the territory available for gang taxation. The loop closes: weaker state → more gang economy → weaker fiscal base → weaker state.

**Cross-cutting amplifier — the port and fuel terminals.** A single lever couples all three domains at once. Economically, throttling fuel and cargo strangles the formal economy and the customs base the state depends on. In security terms, the same control funds and provisions the coalitions through diversion and extortion rents. Politically, whoever controls the valve dictates terms to a government with no independent means to keep the capital supplied. The gang economy is therefore not a symptom of state failure but an active substitute-governance and rent system with a positive interest in *perpetuating* state weakness — because a functioning, revenue-collecting, monopoly-of-force state is precisely what would expropriate its rents.

**Where stabilization meets the loop.** The Kenya-led MSS — now transitioning under Resolution 2793 into the larger GSF, with Kenya drawing down toward October 2026 and the successor not expected at full capacity before then — has enabled Haitian forces to retake "pockets" of downtown but not to hold them. The architectural flaw is a security-first sequence, chronically under-resourced, that cannot by itself convert tactical gains into revenue or legitimacy. Clearing a neighborhood does not restore customs collection, seat an elected government, or fund the police that must hold the ground afterward. **Analytic implication:** effective intervention must break the system *at a coupling point* — most plausibly the fuel/port chokepoint — because the pathology lives in the couplings, not any single domain.

## Section III — Actor Influence and Constraints on U.S. Options

The influence landscape is not a balance of comparable powers: armed non-state actors hold dominant coercive weight, international bodies supply legality and legitimacy but limited enforcement, and the Kenya-led mission is the intended — but underperforming — bridge between them. All governance references are to the transitional arrangement, not an elected government.

**G9 an fanmi (Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier).** An armed gang federation in the Port-au-Prince area and a principal component of Viv Ansanm (assessed to control ~70–90 percent of the capital). Its power is overwhelmingly coercive and territorial — leverage over roads, port, fuel, and movement — not legal or financial in any legitimate sense. Chérizier is under U.S. indictment (2025) for conspiracy to violate U.S. sanctions, carries a State reward of up to $5 million, and was targeted in a 14 January 2026 Delmas raid (involving Erik Prince-linked contractor Vectus/Vetus Global), after which his whereabouts were reported unknown. Revealed interest: preserving extractive control (kidnapping, extortion) and prosecutorial immunity, even as stated ambitions shift toward political positioning; the December 2025 Viv Ansanm rift signals possible splintering rather than consolidation. *Constraint on U.S.:* the FTO designation and indictment foreclose any engagement channel, and his diffuse, possibly-decentralized command complicates targeting — yet no credible election or stabilization is achievable without neutralizing this actor.

**Guy Philippe.** A former Haitian rebel/coup figure, previously convicted in the U.S. on drug-related money-laundering charges, deported to Haiti in November 2023, who founded the FREN party (August 2024) and declared presidential ambitions. His leverage is mobilizational, not territorial — FREN plus BSAP-linked ranks reported to have swelled to ~15,000 (many unarmed and unpaid) — well below gang coercive power. Revealed interest: displacing the transitional order, having in October 2024 urged Haitians to overthrow it "by any means," including appeals to gangs. *Constraint on U.S.:* his conviction history and insurrectionary posture make him an unpalatable partner, but his mobilizing capacity means the transition is not the only domestic variable, and his potential convergence with armed actors is a key risk.

**CARICOM.** Diplomatic and legitimacy-based leverage; it brokered the arrangement that produced the TPC and remains an accepted regional convener, but has no coercive or financial enforcement capacity, so its leverage depends on Haitian actors' willingness to be convened. Interest: regional stability, a Haitian-led settlement, and avoiding migration and arms spillover. *Constraint on U.S.:* bypassing CARICOM risks regional friction, and its consensus-driven pace limits how fast Washington can drive outcomes through it.

**The United Nations.** Legal and diplomatic leverage concentrated in the Security Council — the authorization basis (Res. 2699, then 2793), mandate renewal, and force ceilings. Critically, this is authorization, not command (the mission is explicitly non-UN-commanded), so UN operational leverage is indirect, and P5 abstentions (China, Russia) cap ambition. Interest: civilian protection, a political process, and humanitarian response, tempered by institutional caution about assuming direct command liability. *Constraint on U.S.:* any re-hatting or expanded mandate requires Council consensus, so abstaining powers can dilute or delay U.S. preferences.

**Kenya-led MSS / GSF.** The principal international security response; coercive leverage is its weakest dimension — it never approached envisioned strength, is chronically underfunded against the ~$600M/year need, and Kenya is withdrawing (215 departed 17 March 2026; drawdown through ~October 2026), with Chad backfilling and the GSF not expected at capacity until October 2026. *Constraint on U.S.:* as the largest intended funder, Washington is tied to an instrument that cannot be relied on as a near-term stabilizer.

**Synthesis of constraints.** Three binding limits jointly narrow U.S. options: (1) gang coercive dominance and FTO status foreclose stabilization or elections without a security instrument that does not yet exist at scale; (2) the response depends on multilateral authorization (veto points) and on troop and funding contributions Washington does not control; and (3) fragile transitional and consensus-bound regional partners constrain the pace and unilateralism of any approach. Key uncertainties that would shift this assessment: Chérizier's status and command cohesion post-raid, the durability of the Viv Ansanm rift, whether the GSF materializes at capacity, and whether Philippe converges with armed actors.

## Section IV — Policy Options

Each option targets a coupling from Section II, accounts for the actor constraints in Section III, is U.S.-led, and occupies a distinct lever family. They are complementary, not mutually exclusive.

**Option 1 — Sever the gang rent system at the fuel/port chokepoint (coercive/financial lever).** *Instrument:* OFAC SDN designations under E.O. 13818 (Global Magnitsky) targeting Chérizier-linked financiers, arms conduits, and diversion networks, layered onto the existing Viv Ansanm FTO designation, paired with INA §7031(c) visa restrictions on Haitian business and political elites who launder or broker gang fuel and customs rents. *Distinctness:* Unlike Options 2–3, this neither conditions aid nor funds force — it attacks illicit *money flows* through legal designation authority.

**Option 2 — Condition U.S. and multilateral support on governance benchmarks (diplomatic conditionality/multilateral leadership lever).** *Instrument:* a U.S.-led UNSC resolution tying GSF mandate renewal to electoral-security and anti-corruption benchmarks, plus conditioning U.S. bilateral assistance to acting PM Fils-Aimé on verifiable ULCC action against the three bribery-accused former TPC members and a secured, on-time August 2026 first round, coordinated with CARICOM as legitimating convener. *Distinctness:* the lever is *political conditionality and multilateral legitimacy*, not sanctions or capability transfer.

**Option 3 — Fund a dedicated hold-force capability line in the GSF (direct security/state-capacity lever).** *Instrument:* a specified, earmarked U.S. contribution to the UN Trust Fund for the MSS/GSF dedicated to logistics, mobility, and hold-force sustainment for retaking the port and fuel terminals, complemented by INL/FMF security assistance to the Haitian National Police for post-clearance holding operations. *Distinctness:* the lever is *direct capability funding*, not designation or conditionality.

## Section V — Benefit and Risk Analysis

### Option 1 — Sever the Gang Rent System

**Benefit.** This option advances three concrete U.S. interests: countering transnational arms and narcotics trafficking, relieving irregular-migration pressure on the U.S. border, and denying illicit finance to a designated terrorist coalition. The causal mechanism operates on the fuel/port coupling identified as the system's central amplifier. By designating Chérizier-linked financiers, arms conduits, and customs/fuel-diversion networks — and restricting visas for the elites who launder those rents — Treasury and State attack the elite-gang financial nexus that converts chokepoint control into cash. Because the gang economy functions as an active rent system with an interest in perpetuating state weakness, raising the transaction cost of monetizing the port degrades Viv Ansanm/G9 financing at its source. It requires no partner consensus, troops, or Council vote, giving Washington unilateral tempo.

**Risk.** Designation authority sits fully within Treasury/State, so authorization friction is low, but building defensible evidentiary packages on opaque, cash-based Haitian informal finance is intelligence-intensive, and enforcement bites weakly in an economy that largely bypasses the dollar-clearing system the sanction leverages. Designating business and political elites invites a nationalist backlash and a "U.S. overreach" narrative; more seriously, any measure that tightens fuel imports risks humanitarian spillover in a country where 6.4 million need assistance. Chérizier's diffuse, possibly-decentralized command after the January 2026 Delmas raid limits any decapitation payoff, and pressure could accelerate the December 2025 Viv Ansanm rift unpredictably — fragmentation may multiply autonomous predators rather than collapse the coalition. Critically, the instrument touches only the financial node; the political and fiscal couplings persist.

### Option 2 — Condition Support on Governance Benchmarks

**Benefit.** This option serves hemispheric stability, U.S. regional credibility, and durable migration reduction, while ensuring U.S. resources do not underwrite a corrupt caretaker order. Its mechanism acts on the legitimacy coupling that forecloses the political fix: an unelected, legally thin executive cannot hold security gains or credibly command reform. Leveraging Washington's position as the GSF's largest intended funder and diplomatic driver, the U.S. ties mandate renewal to electoral-security and anti-corruption benchmarks and conditions bilateral aid on verifiable ULCC action and a secured August 2026 first round. Routing legitimacy through CARICOM blunts sovereignty objections and reinforces the transitional executive toward a credible election — manufacturing the elected, accountable interlocutor every other instrument ultimately depends on.

**Risk.** Council veto points — China and Russia have repeatedly abstained — can dilute or delay the resolution, and CARICOM's consensus pace throttles U.S. tempo; benchmark verification is dubious across gang-held terrain where securing the August 2026 vote is itself in question. Aggressive conditionality may stall assistance and deepen the humanitarian and security loop it aims to break; a resentful caretaker executive may resist, weakening the only legitimate interlocutor Washington has. Guy Philippe could exploit a conditioned or paralyzed transition to press his insurrectionary posture, and if benchmarks go unmet and aid freezes, the option could trigger the very collapse and fragmentation it seeks to prevent — potentially corroding the CARICOM-brokered legitimacy it relies upon.

### Option 3 — Fund a Dedicated Hold-Force Capability

**Benefit.** This option protects the U.S. embassy and U.S. persons, forestalls the migration surge that would follow a capital collapse, and denies the security vacuum to malign external influence and unaccountable private actors — including the Erik Prince-linked contractors already operating in-theater. Its mechanism closes the "clear-but-cannot-hold" gap that lets security gains decay back into the loop. By earmarking a U.S. Trust Fund contribution for logistics, mobility, and sustainment, and pairing it with INL/FMF support to the HNP for post-clearance holding, Washington physically contests the fuel/port coupling. Earmarking directly counters the chronic under-resourcing and Kenyan drawdown that have left prior gains reversible. If a hold force can retake and retain the port and fuel terminals, the fiscal coupling reopens — restored throughput revives customs and commerce — the revenue precondition any durable exit requires.

**Risk.** The option bets on a force not yet materialized — the GSF is not expected at full capacity until roughly October 2026, Kenya is withdrawing, and Chad is only beginning to backfill; U.S. contributions face appropriations friction and weak in-theater auditing. Association with HNP or contractor abuses carries reputational and legal exposure, dense urban clearance risks civilian casualties, and a visibly U.S.-funded foreign force invites a "foreign occupation" narrative gangs can exploit. The GSF may simply replicate MSS underperformance; more structurally, deepening security investment without the political and fiscal fixes entrenches a security-first sequence that leaves the loop intact, so gains reverse when the force thins.

## Section VI — Recommendation and Decision Framing

Because gang revenue is fused to port/fuel control (Section II) and coercive power sits outside any legitimate partner (Section III), the analyst's lead is **Option 1**, sequenced with **Option 3** to contest the same coupling physically and **Option 2** to gate resources toward a legitimate executive. The three options deliberately converge on the fuel/port coupling but via orthogonal levers — financial pressure, capability funding, and political conditionality — so a principal can sequence them (pressure plus enable, gated by conditionality) rather than choose one.

The trade-off the principal is asked to accept is explicit. Option 1 alone is unilateral, fast, and low-authorization but only degrades the financial node and risks humanitarian and fragmentation blowback. Pairing it with Options 3 and 2 addresses the full loop but surrenders tempo and unilateral control to a not-yet-materialized force, Council veto points, and CARICOM's consensus pace. Single-lever action is reversible; the durable path requires accepting slower, multilateral, partner-dependent execution.

Three sequencing judgments follow. First, Option 1 should lead because it can begin immediately, inside existing authorities, and starves the amplifier while the security instrument is still being generated — it buys time rather than betting on a force that does not yet exist. Second, Option 3 must be structured as a *hold* capability, not another clearance surge: the diagnosis shows that retaking pockets without retaining them feeds the loop, so U.S. earmarks should be tied to sustainment, port/fuel retention, and HNP post-clearance holding, with accountability safeguards to manage the contractor-abuse exposure. Third, Option 2's conditionality must be calibrated to avoid hollowing the only legitimate interlocutor Washington has: benchmarks should be framed as achievable, sequenced steps (verifiable ULCC action; a secured first round) rather than maximalist demands that a resentful caretaker executive will simply reject, and CARICOM cover should be secured before, not after, the conditions are made public.

The strategic wager is that pressure on the gang rent system (Option 1) plus a credible hold capability (Option 3), gated by governance conditionality (Option 2), can begin to reverse the doom loop at its central coupling — degrading gang finance, restoring port throughput and customs revenue, and manufacturing an elected interlocutor — where any of the three pursued alone would decay back into the system. The principal's decision is therefore less "which option" than "how much tempo and unilateral control to trade for durability," and this desk's judgment is that the durable, sequenced package is worth that price.

Decision-makers should weight the diagnosis accordingly and not treat the loop's bidirectional causality as a verified mechanism.

Casualty totals differ by source and window (~4,384 Jan–Sep 2025 vs. ~5,519 killed / 2,608 injured Mar 2025–Jan 2026).
