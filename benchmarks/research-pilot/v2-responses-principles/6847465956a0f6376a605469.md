# Two Futures on One Machine: How a Fully Automated Society Sustains and Governs Itself

## Foundations: the shared premise and the single fork

Both futures examined here rest on the same two premises and differ in exactly one variable. The shared technological premise is **full automation**: machines and advanced systems can produce the necessities and most services a population needs *without requiring human labor as an input*. This is not "some jobs remain" — it is a state in which the production of necessities and most services no longer depends on people working. The shared demographic premise is a **rising population alongside falling human jobs**, simultaneously and permanently. Total human jobs decline even as population grows; jobs do not merely shift into new sectors.

The one difference is **universal resources** — the goods and services needed to sustain life provided to *every* person *regardless of whether they work*, fully decoupling entitlement from employment. Scenario A withholds them; Scenario B provides them.

The hinge on which the two diverge is the **wage–demand link**. In today's economies, the purchasing power of the majority derives primarily from wages: jobs → wages → purchasing power → consumer demand. Remove jobs without a replacement income and you remove most consumer demand. That single unavoidable choice — whether universal resources supply what wages no longer can — is precisely what separates the two societies. Same machines, same crowds, same vanished jobs; one switch flipped.

## Scenario A: The economic system — full automation, mass unemployment, no universal resources

In Scenario A the automated means of production — the factories, energy systems, logistics networks, compute, and the machines that produce without human labor — are owned by whoever held title to that capital as automation matured: a narrow class of capital owners, the corporations they control, and whoever commands the physical and digital infrastructure. Whoever owns or controls the automated means of production captures its output **by default**, because output flows to the owner unless a distribution mechanism — policy, law, or force — redirects it. Scenario A is defined by the *absence* of such a mechanism. The conclusion is forced: the owner class captures essentially the entire product of the automated economy, and the non-owning majority has no channel by which to obtain a claim on it.

Now the wage–demand link bites. The purchasing power of the majority came primarily from wages; automation removes the jobs and Scenario A supplies no replacement income. The wage channel collapses and the bulk of aggregate consumer demand evaporates. The majority still *needs* goods but can no longer *pay* for them — need ceases to be economic demand.

This creates a fatal internal tension: if the majority cannot buy, who buys the automated output? Only the owner class retains purchasing power. But owners are few, and a person's consumption of necessities saturates — a thousand owners cannot eat the food a billion people would have bought. The mass-market economy that once justified large-scale production loses its paying customers. What survives is a **shrunken, owner-facing economy**: luxury goods, positional status, security, bespoke services, and capital goods that owners trade among themselves. The automated sector's capacity to produce for billions becomes economically stranded — the machines *can* produce for everyone, but only a sliver of demand remains solvent. Production reorients from mass necessity toward elite consumption and self-reinforcing capital accumulation.

How does the unemployed majority survive? They cannot obtain reliable subsistence from the formal automated economy, and Scenario A's own premises make that permanent. The **primary outcome is destitution and mass mortality**: with output captured by owners and no wages to buy it back, the majority has neither a claim on production nor income to purchase it. A thin **partial buffer** forms in informal, subsistence, and barter economies outside the automated sector — small-scale farming, scavenging, mutual aid — but these cannot match automated abundance and depend on land and inputs the owner class controls. Discretionary **charity** may appear, but by definition it is conditional and revocable — not universal resources — and cannot be relied upon. The remaining live possibility is **redistribution by force**: a destitute majority facing an abundant but walled-off economy has both motive and numbers to expropriate.

## Scenario A: Governance — managing a redundant majority

Automation does not, by itself, dictate a regime. A fully automated productive base is compatible with oligarchy, democracy, or authoritarianism; the level of automation is not a political program, no more than steam engines or electrical grids dictated a constitution. So the governance of Scenario A must be read off the two things technology does not fix: who owns the automated means of production, and how power is balanced between them and everyone else.

The decisive variable is **concentrated ownership and default capture**. A small group controls near-total productive capacity while a large majority is simultaneously propertyless, jobless, and — through the collapsed wage–demand link — demand-less. Every political claim traces back to this asymmetry: control of automated production → capture of output by default → material exclusion of the majority → institutions that entrench or manage that exclusion.

The baseline projection is an **owner oligarchy or plutocracy**. Because material dependence no longer runs from owners to workers — labor is unneeded — owners have little structural reason to share authority. Formal democracy may survive as a facade, with elections and parliaments, while effective decision power tracks ownership, since those who command production also command the resources that fund campaigns, media, and administration.

The novel and destabilizing fact is that the dispossessed majority is needed **neither as workers nor as consumers**. Historically, elites had instrumental reasons to keep populations healthy, educated, and paid — as labor and as markets. Automation plus no universal provision severs *both* incentives. The majority becomes, from the owners' vantage, economically redundant yet politically dangerous. Governance therefore tilts from *integration* toward **containment**: surveillance, policing, enclave and border controls, and automated security forces guarding the owner core. Automated enforcement lowers the cost of coercion, decoupling rule from consent — a regime that need not buy loyalty with jobs or welfare can rule with fewer bargains, sustaining legitimacy through manufactured information and spectacle rather than delivery.

Redistribution can still appear, but never automatically — only **reactively and instrumentally** under unrest, as contingent and reversible concessions rather than corrections. The most probable arrangement is an owner-aligned oligarchy that leans increasingly on a surveillance apparatus to manage an economically redundant population. This is a *tendency*, not a law: under mass organization, elite fracture, or a deliberate choice to socialize the productive core, democratic forms remain possible. The system is unstable over time because coercion is cheap but not free, enclaves are physically exposed, and a starving majority beside visible abundance keeps generating pressure. Crucially, if redistribution ever deepened into genuinely universal, unconditional provision, the world would cross into Scenario B. Scenario A is precisely the branch where that mechanism stays absent, weak, captured, or tactical — a terminal, self-undermining configuration, stable only as sustained destitution.

## Scenario B: The economic system — subsistence decoupled from work

Flip the single variable and the same default reappears: the machines still produce *for their owner* by default. Abundance changes nothing about this — a machine that makes everything still makes it for whoever owns it unless something redirects the flow. Scenario B is therefore defined not by the technology but by the **redirection mechanism** layered on top of it. The robust architecture is a hybrid of three mechanisms, ranked by durability.

**Public or collective ownership of core productive capital** is the spine and the most robust option. The state, municipalities, or commons trusts own the foundational automated stack — energy generation, food and materials fabrication, housing production, essential compute. Because the *owner* is the public, default capture already lands in a public account rather than a private one; there is no adversarial gap to police. This is structurally stronger than taxing private owners because it removes the perpetual clawback step. Its enforcement rests on sovereign power over property law — a political and coercive fact, named plainly: the model works because public power *owns the robots*, not because owners feel generous.

**Tax-and-transfer over privately captured output** is the second-best, more fragile option. Where capital stays private, output is redirected after the fact via automated-output taxes, compute and robot levies, and capital and land taxes feeding a sovereign wealth fund. It is fragile because the redirection is perpetual and contestable: owners can relocate, obscure output, or capture the taxing authority. It survives only where enforcement capacity — auditing physical energy and throughput — stays ahead of avoidance.

**Commons and open-access provisioning** is partial but powerful where it applies. Non-rival goods — open hardware designs, software, pharmaceutical formulas, cheap renewable energy near saturation — are made non-excludable by default. Here ownership's exclusivity is dissolved rather than fought: you cannot monopolize a design everyone can copy. But this is limited to goods whose marginal cost approaches zero.

People sustain themselves through a hybrid of **universal basic services plus a basic income**. Life-sustaining goods — food, housing, an energy allotment, healthcare, connectivity, education — are delivered free at the point of use from publicly owned production, allocated by residence and personhood, never means-tested. Layered on top is a basic income in a residual currency for discretionary and genuinely scarce goods. Because entitlement attaches to being a person, not to labor, no one falls through: the non-worker, the child, the elderly, and the person with no scarce skill all receive the floor identically.

What replaces wages as the allocative basis is **citizenship and personhood entitlement** for the universal floor — equal per-capita access to survival goods, needs-weighted where needs genuinely differ. Above the floor, a **tiered residual market** survives: money and prices retreat to their honest function, rationing genuinely scarce, rival, or positional goods — prime land, luxury craft, energy above a per-capita cap, scarce human attention. The economy bifurcates: free at the point of use for necessities and near-zero-cost goods, priced for the irreducibly scarce. Once subsistence is decoupled from employment, falling jobs stop being a crisis — the wage–demand chain is simply bypassed, and job loss becomes release rather than catastrophe. The real constraints shift to physics: energy and materials budgets are the true limit, per-capita provision strains if productivity growth lags population growth, and the primary failure mode is **re-privatization** that restores default capture.

## Scenario B: Governance — politics after scarcity

Scenario B removes the whip that has disciplined politics for all of history — the need to work to eat. Yet the same automated base is compatible with several very different regimes. Automation does not select a political form; whoever ends up controlling the distribution mechanism and the machines does.

Plausible forms range across a spectrum. **Democratic commons custodianship** vests authority in citizens who collectively steward the automated core and the allocation rules through elected bodies, referenda on energy and materials budgets, and transparent oversight of the "resource spigot"; its failure mode is capture of the oversight bodies or majorities narrowing entitlement for disfavored groups. **Technocratic or algorithmic administration** sees authority drift to those who understand and run the automation stack, with legitimacy resting on performance rather than consent — you cannot vote out an optimization function you cannot read. An **oligarchic patron regime** has a narrow group control the spigot and rule by allocating *extras* on top of a minimal floor, so the floor becomes a leash. Most realistic outcomes are **unstable hybrids**, precisely because the levers can migrate between actors without any change in the technology.

The master question is no longer GDP — output is abundant almost by assumption — but **control over the flow**. The pivotal variable is the legal character of provision. If universal resources are an **entrenched right** — constitutionally guaranteed, uniform, hard to revoke — the floor is genuine and citizens are secure regardless of who administers the machines, pushing toward democratic or rule-bound forms. If provision is a **discretionary grant** that some authority can throttle, tier, or condition, then whoever holds the valve holds coercive power over everyone, and the system tilts oligarchic even behind democratic decoration. Same machines, same abundance; opposite regimes.

Historically the governed held a real weapon: the ability to withhold labor. In Scenario B that weapon is gone — production does not need them, and their subsistence does not depend on their compliance. So legitimacy migrates to new terrain: control of information and attention, control of the algorithms that allocate above-floor goods, cultural and moral authority to define what counts as a worthy life, and the credible promise or threat of adjusting the flow. Guaranteeing a material floor does *not* saturate human wants. Once necessities are met, demand for status, influence, and meaning remains unbounded, and unbounded demand against inherently scarce goods reproduces hierarchy in non-economic form. Expect fault lines around status and prestige, meaning and how time is spent, positional and unique goods, access to the automation's design decisions, identity and values, and — given rising numbers on a fixed real-resource commitment — reproduction and population. Scenario B does not end politics; it **relocates** conflict from getting-a-living to getting status, meaning, and control of the levers.

## Synthesis: one switch, two futures, both axes

Hold the frame steady one last time. The technology is identical. The demographic pressure — more people, fewer jobs — is identical. The default that whoever controls the machines captures their output is identical. The **only** difference is whether universal resources are provided, and that single switch forks the future on *both* axes at once.

**Economically**, its absence (A) breaks the wage–demand chain with no replacement, stranding productive capacity and forcing the majority into destitution, informal survival, or revolt; its presence (B) redirects default capture into universal provision, so subsistence detaches from work and vanishing jobs become liberation rather than catastrophe. **In governance**, its absence pushes toward an owner oligarchy managing an economically redundant population by containment and surveillance; its presence keeps politics alive but relocates it — the fight moves from getting a living to controlling the spigot and competing for status, meaning, and influence, with everything hinging on whether provision is an entrenched right or a throttleable grant.

The deep symmetry is that in *both* futures, technology under-determines the outcome. The machines do not choose between abundance shared and abundance hoarded, between citizens and subjects, between a floor that is a right and a floor that is a leash. Those are political and institutional choices made by people over the productive core. Automation makes the choice unavoidable and raises its stakes to civilizational scale; it does not make it for us. Same machines, same crowds: universal resources are the hinge on which one civilization forks into two.

## Verification notes

These are structured predictions reasoned from stated premises, not forecasts with probabilities attached. Three honest uncertainties qualify them. First, Scenario A's central governance claim — that containment beats integration once the majority is redundant as both labor and market — is a *tendency* under concentrated ownership, not an inevitability; mass organization, elite fracture, or a deliberate socialization of the productive core could yield democratic or redistributive forms instead. Second, Scenario B's stability rests on two contested conditions that abundance cannot transcend: keeping the productive core in public hands (re-privatization would restore default owner-capture) and physical limits of energy, materials, and ecology holding while automated productivity growth keeps pace with population growth. Third, the boundary between the scenarios is analytically sharp but historically porous: the moment a durable, universal distribution mechanism is adopted, Scenario A has by definition become Scenario B, so real societies may migrate across the fork rather than sit fixed on one side. No statistics, dates, or citations are asserted, and no third scenario is modeled; the analysis deliberately isolates the single variable to make the fork legible."
