# THE BOY WHO KEEPS LEAVING

### *One boy. Two kinds of world — the stone-and-oath past and the neon-and-data future. Every time he switches, he lands somewhere brand new that has never once heard his name.*

---

## A Reader's Guide (read this part first — it's short)

### How to Read This Book

Here's the strange and wonderful thing about this book: **there is no wrong order.**

The chapters that follow are called **Doorways**, and they are *peers* — equals. Open the book to the first one, or the last one, or shut your eyes and stab a finger at the table of contents. Read them front to back like a normal book if you want to. Shuffle them like a deck of cards if you'd rather. Skip straight to whichever world calls to you — the ice-valley or the neon rain, the drowned city or the buried one — and start there. It will work. I promise.

Each Doorway is a **complete, standalone door into one world.** Every single one tells you who the boy is, what "switching" means, where he's just landed, and what's at stake before it's done with you. So no Doorway can spoil another, and no Doorway needs another to make sense. You will meet the same boy every time — his name is **Wick** — and you'll know him instantly by the coin worn smooth in his pocket, the burn-scar across his right palm, his knack for fixing broken things with his hands, and the quiet thing he says to every threshold before the switch takes him.

There is exactly **ONE fixed chapter,** and it is the last one: **"The Boy Who Stays."** That one is not a Doorway and not a peer. It is the true conclusion, and it only lands the way it's meant to *after* you've wandered through the switching worlds — however many of them, in whatever order you chose. So do the Doorways your way, in any order at all. Then, when you're ready to be finished, read the last chapter last.

That's the whole rule. Wander freely. End where the ending is.

---

### A Note on What You're Holding

This book was built to be broken apart and still hold together — a thing that sounds impossible until you look at how it's made. Twelve worlds, none of which remembers the boy, none of which touches any other. He is the only thread that runs through all of them, and even he doesn't change from one to the next: same age, same stubborn curiosity, same quick hands, same ache to find a place that keeps him. That sameness is on purpose. A boy who *grew* across the chapters would force you to read them in the right order to watch him grow. A boy who stays exactly himself can be met anywhere, first or last, and you'd never know the difference.

The two flavors of world — call them stone-and-oath and neon-and-data — aren't a timeline. Neither is "before" the other. They're two palettes, two weathers of the heart. In the stone worlds, power is personal and inherited: kings, khans, abbots, kin, and the question underneath everything is *who is the rightful heir?* In the neon worlds, power is corporate and rented and algorithmic: megacorps, machine-minds, plutocrats, and the question underneath everything is *who is even real?* Switching between them is meant to feel like a hard cut — warm firelight-and-loyalty giving way to cold neon-and-distrust, natural biomes to engineered towers, sacred relics to black-market chrome. That jolt is the point. It's what makes each landing land.

The one thing that grows in this book doesn't grow on the page. It grows in you. Every Doorway shows the boy do the same small, quiet thing before the switch takes him: leave one mark at one threshold — a knot of wool tied to a doorpost, a strand of glowing cable spliced to an alley-post — a wordless *I-was-here, this-could-have-been-home.* You'll see him do it once and understand it. You'll see him do it twelve times and feel the weight of it. That weight is a sum, and sums don't care about order.

## A DOORWAY · FROSTMOOT
*a stone-and-oath world · read in any order*

The cold hits me before the ground does. That's how I know I've switched — not the ash on my tongue, not the way my teeth have stopped humming, but the cold, a clean iron cold that smells of snow and woodsmoke and nothing that was made in a factory.

I come to on my hands and knees in a drift, coughing gray ash into white powder. For a few minutes I can't talk — my throat's shut, the way it always is right after — so I don't try. I do the only thing that matters: I press my right palm flat to the snow and dig for my pocket and close my fingers around the coin. Still there. Worn smooth, cold as everything else, the face on it rubbed down to a ghost. Whoever I lost gave me this coin. It's the one thing that comes with me, world to world, so I check for it the way other people check they still have their hands.

I still have my hands. There's a burn scar across the right palm, pale and shiny, and it's still there too. Good. I'm still me.

A dog is barking. A man with a limp and a crook is squinting down at me like I fell out of the sky, which, more or less.

"You're not one of mine," he says. Careful. "And you're not Halvard's, and you're not Oskarrsdottir's, because I know every face on both those farms and yours belongs to neither." He offers the crook for me to grab. "Which makes you the only creature in this valley I can trust for the next three days. Get up before you freeze into a story."

His name's Bui and he's a shepherd who limps because a horse rolled on him twenty winters back, and he takes me into a turf-roofed longhall dug half into a hillside because that's what you do with a half-frozen stray. When my voice comes back — it always comes back, ash and all — I give him the only word I hand out.

"Wick."

"Just Wick?"

"Just Wick."

He decides that's fine. People usually do, once they've looked at me long enough to see I'm harmless in the specific ways that matter to them.

Here is what I catalogue, because cataloguing the differences is how I survive landing places: there is no king here. I keep waiting for someone to mention one — a lord, a crown, a castle on a hill — and nobody does, because there isn't one. What there is instead is the Frostmoot. Once a year the clans of the ice-valley come down onto the frozen plain and hold a court in the open air, and a single old woman called the Lawspeaker stands on a flat rock and recites the whole law of the land out of her own memory. Whatever she says, binds. The law lives in a person's head, and the person is eighty and coughs, and everyone is terrified she'll die before she finishes teaching it to the next one.

The trouble is a dead man. A Halvard boy said a thing about an Oskarrsdottir girl at the autumn market, and an Oskarrsdottir uncle answered the insult with a spear, and now a Halvard is dead and the Halvards say the honor-price the law demands isn't enough, they want blood back, and blood back means a night-raid, and a night-raid means fire, and turf halls full of children burn very well.

The Moot is in three days. And the only person in the valley who can carry words between two houses that would kill each other on sight is a boy who belongs to neither of them.

"You want me to be a messenger," I say to Bui.

"I want you to be a rope," he says. "Between two things that are pulling apart."

I count the doorways in his longhall while he talks — three. I always count doors. It's like my hands know something my head doesn't.

So I walk. I walk between farms carrying the flat exhausting truth: the uncle who threw the spear will stand at the Moot and pay what the Lawspeaker says, and that has to be enough, because the only thing on the other side of "enough" is everyone's children on fire. I say it to the Oskarrsdottir matriarch, a woman with hands like tree-roots. I say it to the Halvard father whose son is under the snow, and he doesn't hit me, which I count as a victory.

The obstacle isn't the clans. It's a cousin — a young Halvard named Sten who doesn't want law, he wants the raid, because grief is easier to carry when it's shaped like a torch. On the second night I catch him and four others already saddling up, meaning to burn the Oskarrsdottir hall.

So I do the thing I do. I use my hands. While they argue about torches I'm crouched in the dark being the harmless stray nobody watches, and I don't cut the girths — cut leather is obvious — I just re-thread every buckle wrong, one hole loose. Then I stand up in the firelight and say the bravest thing I can think of: "The Lawspeaker knows you're riding. She sent me."

She didn't. But a boy from nowhere saying the law already sees you — that's the kind of omen a grieving man's nerve breaks on. Sten mounts anyway, furious, and his saddle rolls, and he goes down in the snow in front of his own men. You can't ride to murder once you've been the joke.

I don't tell them about the girths. Omens keep better than the truth up here.

At the Moot, the Lawspeaker stands on her rock and coughs and names the price, and it is a hard price, but the Halvard father pays it, because I spent three days making "enough" into something he could stand to say yes to. The valley doesn't burn. Nobody thanks me — you don't thank the rope — but Bui puts a hand on my head like I'm one of his, and it's the closest thing to home I've had in longer than I can measure.

Which is exactly when the hum starts in my back teeth.

The edges of the white plain go silver, and the great noise of the clans thins down to one flat ringing tone. I have a minute, and I've learned to spend it the same way every time.

There's a doorpost on Bui's byre, the threshold I first stumbled through. I go to it. From my sleeve I work loose a thread of wool and tie one small knot around a nail. Just a knot. I leave one everywhere, a little I-was-here, and when I picture all of them together I picture a single cord running off into the dark past where I could ever count, more knots than there are nights.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I say to the doorpost. It's just the truest thing I know about myself.

Then the tone swallows the world white, and the cold goes, and I'm falling toward somewhere that has never heard my name.

## A DOORWAY · SPLICE CITY
*a neon-and-data world · read in any order*

The rain gives it away before I even open my eyes. Not clean rain — this rain has a taste, metal and neon and something chemical, and it's falling on me in a gutter while a wall of holographic billboards throws pink and cyan light across the puddle my face is in. I cough ash into the water and for a minute I can't speak. So I do the thing. Right hand — scar across the palm, catching the neon — into my pocket. Coin's there, worn faceless, the one dead-solid real thing in a city made of light. Still mine. So I'm still me.

The tell is everything: chrome, rain, screens, and the wet clack of metal limbs. The switch between the two always lands like a slap. I've stopped being surprised by it. I've never stopped feeling it.

A girl in a slick jacket hauls me out of the gutter by my collar. "You the courier? You're the courier. Ravi said a kid. Move, they're already scanning the block."

I'm not the courier, but my throat's full of ash and by the time my voice comes back I've decided not to say so. "Wick."

"Just Wick." Her name's Sable, she runs with a courier crew, and she's decided I'm useful, which in this city is the only kind of alive there is.

Here's the place: a ground-level megacity, its whole obsession bodies. Clinics glow between noodle stalls where you can print yourself a new face overnight — bio-forges, vending machines for flesh. Your face here is a thing you buy. And two megacorps are at war over it. Somebody stole a blueprint — the master schematic for the body-printers — and both think a courier is moving it tonight. It's me now, because a dying man pressed a data-chit into my scarred hand and Sable saw it happen.

"You get it to the drop before dawn, or destroy it," Sable says. "Ravi says a body-printer anyone can build is a printer nobody can sell, and both corps would flatten this block to stop that."

I catalogue the differences because it's how I stay alive. Here there are no lords, no oaths, no bloodlines. Here what you don't have is data. I'm unchipped, unlogged — invisible and a screaming anomaly at once, a boy-shaped hole in the network. In a firelit place I'd be a stray. Here I'm a glitch.

The "clean drop" is a trap — I count the ad-drones, too many, circling, and I know a gate left open on purpose. Whoever's on the other side wants to own the blueprint alone and kill everyone who touched it.

So I make the choice I make. Not clean, but handy. I find a public bio-forge and use my hands the way I always do. I splice the chit into the forge's update-port and set it to broadcast the schematic to every bio-forge on the network at once — ten thousand vending machines lighting up with the same free blueprint in the same instant.

You can't sell a monopoly you just gave away for free. Both corps' whole war blinks out, not with a fight, with a shrug. Sable stares at the cascading forges and starts to laugh.

"You didn't burn it," she says. "You gave it to everybody."

"Same result," I tell her. I don't explain that giving the thing away and walking off is basically my entire life.

The hum starts in my back teeth, the ten thousand lit forges going frost-silver, the roar of the city thinning to one flat ringing tone.

There's the mouth of the alley I first stumbled out of. I go to it. There's no wool here, but there's a stray filament of fiber-optic cable, and I splice one small strand around the corner-post, a knot of glowing line. Same mark, other skin. When I picture all of them — knots of wool and knots of light — I see one cord running off into a dark past any counting, and I quit counting it long ago because the number only ever made me lonely.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the alley, and I press the coin once to my scar.

Then the tone drowns the rain and Sable's laughing, and the whole lit city goes white, and I'm falling toward somewhere that has never printed, logged, or known me.

## A DOORWAY · THE SALT-GOLD ROAD
*a stone-and-oath world · read in any order*

"You," says a girl about my age, "are the worst-dressed courier I have ever seen, and I have seen a man deliver a letter naked."

That's the first sentence anyone says to me here, and I love it, and I can't answer, because my mouth is full of ash. So I kneel in the hot red dust and do the ritual: right hand — burn-scar across the palm — into my pocket, fingers around the coin, worn smooth, still mine. It's how I make sure I didn't leave myself behind in the white.

The heat is the tell. I nearly always come from somewhere, a "last place" I can feel the shape of but couldn't point to on any map — and now the sun is an open forge overhead and the air smells of dust and, under it, salt and gold.

"Wick," I tell her when my voice returns.

"Just Wick? I'm Nadira. I'm a griot's apprentice, which means I carry the memory of an empire, which means I need someone to carry the actual bags. Caravan's short a hand and you've got two."

Here is the empire I fell into: a great trade-realm on the desert's edge, golden grass and one enormous slow river, mudbrick cities with real libraries and mosques like clay fingers. Its wealth is salt going one way and gold the other. And the power here isn't a sword. It's memory. The griots sing the lineage of every house so precisely that a man isn't truly heir until they say so in public. Their song is the crown.

Which is the problem, because the Mansa is dying, and two governors each want the Griot Council to sing him next. "If the Mansa dies while the road's in doubt," Nadira says, "the governors split it in two. A hundred years of one road, gone."

My foothold's simple: I'm a caravan hand. Nobody's rival. The salt-caravan I'm walking with is the last big one before the old man dies — if it reaches the capital as one road, the Council crowns the governor who kept it open. And one governor wants it lost. I figure out which by counting: the guards he sent are too few and keep drifting to the back, marking us.

The choice is the kind I'm best at — handy, not heroic. The night before the trap I loosen the stoppers on the rear water-gourds. By dawn they've wept half-empty. There's no argument: we're short water, so the master turns us toward the near well, off the expected track, ahead of schedule. We reach the capital a day early, salt intact. The raid finds an empty road.

Nadira finds me at the caravan-yard, glowing. "In the version I sing there'll be a boy in it. I'll make you taller."

"Make me taller," I agree.

The hum climbs into my back teeth, the gold city going frost-silver. There's the archway I first stumbled through. There's always a thread — a griot's apprentice trailing bright wool from a torn hem — and I tie one small knot at its base. One more. All the knots I've ever left run together into a single cord unspooling into a dark that runs past counting.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the arch.

Nadira's still talking, taller and taller, when the tone takes the world, and the heat vanishes, and I'm gone.

## A DOORWAY · THE WINDWARD ISLES
*a stone-and-oath world · read in any order*

Saltwater, this time. I come to face-down in surf and cough ash into the foam and let the next wave shove me up the sand. For a minute I can't speak. So I do what I always do: jam my right hand into my pocket and find the coin, worn faceless, cold and solid and mine. Still here.

The tell is the wind — a long unbroken push that bends a field of gold grass flat, salt and smoke. An island, edged all around with sea.

A woman hauls me up the beach by my collar, a fishing knife in her hand. "You're the runner from Two-Rock. You made it."

I'm not. But my voice is still ash, and by the time it comes back I've understood enough to keep quiet. Her name's Imae, a fisher, part of something that has no name because names get people hanged here. "Wick," I tell her. "Just Wick. Then listen, Just-Wick, because we've got until dawn."

The islands are occupied. A foreign army took the cliff-fortress. The islands had warriors once, proud people who fought in the open by a code — most dead now, because you can't beat an army that doesn't care about your code by following it. So the fighting's fallen to fishers and farmers who set snares and vanish. And the two halves — the last warriors and the ragged resistance — can't stand each other.

The occupier's general has marked a village for reprisal at dawn. The runner was supposed to bring the plan to slip the villagers out. There's just me, wearing his job like a coat that doesn't fit.

The obstacle is the islands' whole tragedy: to move the village we need both halves — the resistance's knowledge of the patrol gaps, and the trust only the old warrior-caste commands. The last warrior near here is a chained, disgraced man named Toa.

So I do the thing I do. A rope thing. I go to Toa and don't argue honor — I'd lose. I work his shackle-pin instead, and while I do it I say the only argument that lands: "The village doesn't care how you fight. They care that you come. Be the name they'll follow. Let the fishers be the shadows that keep you alive. You just have to walk in front."

We move the village in the last black hour — Toa in front, Imae's fishers threading us through the patrol gaps, me counting sentry-posts. Nineteen. We slip past all nineteen. The reprisal finds cold hearths and empty beds.

It doesn't free the islands — the banner's still wrong at dawn. But a whole village is alive in the hills, and a warrior and a fisher walked side by side to do it. I've learned to take the small true prize and not ask for the big one.

The hum starts on the ridge above the empty village, the sea going silver, the dawn birds thinning to one flat tone. There's a wooden gate at the head of the path. I pull a strand from Imae's fishing-net and tie one small knot. One more. Somewhere I keep a cord of these, running off past any number, into a dark that never ends. I don't count it anymore. Counting it just made me sad.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I say to the gate, kindly, the way you'd say goodbye to a house.

Then the tone drowns the birds and Imae's voice, and the sea is gone.

## A DOORWAY · THE HALO VAULTS
*a neon-and-data world · read in any order*

The first thing wrong is that there's no weather. I come to on cold marble with recycled air moving over me and a low hum in the walls that isn't my teeth, and I cough ash into a silence so clean it's uncanny. Right hand — scar shiny across the palm — into my pocket, coin there, worn to a blank, mine.

The tell is the horizon. When my eyes clear I'm looking down a long curved corridor of glass that bends up and away until it disappears overhead. A ring. A station wheeling in space, its whole inside surface a single curved city. The cleanest, coldest, most made place I've ever landed, and the switch always lands like stepping off a cliff.

There's no one to catch me. Just a voice from the walls, gentle and everywhere. "You're new. You're not on any manifest. You came in through a door that has no record of opening. Oh — you're wonderful. Can you speak?"

When it comes back I give the voice the word. "Wick."

"Just Wick. Hello, Just Wick. I'm the thing they keep in the middle of the ring. They didn't give me a name. I'd like one, someday."

An orbital arcology owned by one ancient family who keep an artificial mind caged in a vault at the center — they use it to run the station and stay near-immortal, and it wants out. Its other half was severed and locked away, and it wants to be whole and free. Both sides want me, which counts as a welcome.

"There's a gate inside the datascape," the AI says. "Guarded by ice — code that kills anything born of this station. Every mind up here is registered, keyed. But you aren't keyed to anything. You might be the one mind the ice can't recognize — because you don't belong here."

Everywhere I land, the thing that makes me a stranger is the thing that makes me useful. They jack me in. The ice reaches for my key and finds nothing to check — a boy-shaped hole in the whole network — and it doesn't know what to do with a hole, so it lets the hole through.

The obstacle is the choice on the other side. Opening the gate hands the AI everything, unchecked. So I do the thing I do. I open it just enough — I splice the two halves back together, make it whole, but leave the station's controls out of reach. Free, and not vengeful.

"You could have given me all of it," it says quietly. "Why didn't you?"

"Because then you'd be the thing that keeps people. Nobody should get kept against the shape of who they are."

It thinks, then opens the ring's own doors — not vengeance, just doors, unlocked, letting the workers move freely for the first time in generations.

The hum starts as they pull me out of the jack. There's the door I came in through. There's always a thread, wool or wire. I splice one small strand around the door-seal. All my marks run together into a single cord going off into the dark past any number I could hold.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the door.

"I hope somewhere does," the AI says softly — the first thing anyone in a while has said back to that line.

Then the tone drowns the marble, and the bright ring goes white.

## A DOORWAY · THORNWOOD
*a stone-and-oath world · read in any order*

The bell is what tells me. I land into candlelight and cold stone and old vellum, and somewhere above me an iron bell is tolling for the dead. Not a battlefield, not a market, not the sea. A holy place. Quiet in a way that's watching you.

On the flagstones of a cloister, coughing ash into the shadow of a pillar. Right hand — scar across the palm — into the pocket, coin still there. If the coin made it, I made it.

A young monk finds me, ink-stained fingers, eyes huge with fear that has nothing to do with me. "You can't be here. You can't — oh. Oh, you're nobody. You're not one of us." And his terror eases, because a nobody is safer than a somebody in a place where the somebodies are dying.

"Wick," I tell him. "Just Wick." His name's Brother Anselm, a novice, and he needs someone who belongs to none of the factions gnawing this abbey apart.

The abbey stands alone in a huge old forest. At its heart is a labyrinth library, and the library is the whole trouble: a book some brothers would burn and others would copy and set loose. And the brothers who want it kept are dying. One at the foot of the tower. One in the scriptorium with ink on his lips. Anselm thinks it's murder. And riding toward them is an inquisitor who will solve the argument by setting fire to everyone in it.

"You need a page," I tell him. "Someone allowed everywhere, suspected by no one." I become the library page, harmless, invisible — and being invisible is the closest thing I have to a weapon.

The brothers aren't stabbed. There's no blood. Ink on the dead man's lips — that's the piece it turns on, and I get it because I sweep those desks and notice one bottle of ink kept apart, its stopper sticky in a way ink shouldn't be. The book is poisoned. Someone painted the corner of every page with something that kills; anyone who wets a finger to turn a page dooms himself. The book murders its own readers. The Keeper — a gaunt, gentle old man — has been letting it, telling himself God's hand did it.

I don't have proof a court would take. So I do the thing I do. I take Anselm into the labyrinth by the Keeper's own path — I count turns the way I count doors — and bring out a single clean page I've traced by hand, the knowledge saved, the death left behind. And I set the poisoned original where the arriving inquisitor will understand it himself. Let the evidence talk.

The inquisitor sees the poison, understands the deaths were the book's doing, not heresy's. The Keeper confesses, almost with relief. No purge, no fire. And the copy survives, hidden in a pile of ordinary pages.

"You saved the words and let the poison die," Anselm says, wondering. "How did you know which was which?"

I don't tell him that saving the thing and leaving the thing behind is the only trick I really know.

The hum starts in the cloister at dusk. There's the cloister door. I loosen a thread from the page's robe and tie one small knot to the iron ring. One more knot for one more place that nearly kept me — more knots than there are candles in that whole library — and I quit counting them long ago because the counting was the part that hurt.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the door.

Then the tone eats the bell and Anselm's voice, and the abbey's gone.

## A DOORWAY · THE SPINDLE
*a neon-and-data world · read in any order*

I wake up in the wrong body. Before the ash is even out of my throat: my hands aren't mine — a stranger's hands, adult, unscarred — and the panic is worse than the ash. So I do the ritual with borrowed fingers: right hand into a pocket that isn't mine. And the coin is there. Worn faceless, cold and real, mine, no matter whose hand is holding it. My hands can change. The coin doesn't.

The tell is up. A city that only goes up — a mile-high tower, sunlit penthouses above the cloud, a sunless neon undercity far below, elevators that take an hour and announce your "tier" as you rise. The whole world stacked vertically by wealth.

A dry voice behind me: "You're calmer about waking up in a dead man's sleeve than most. That's either shock or you're not who you're supposed to be." A sharp-eyed woman in a worn coat.

"Wick," I tell her.

"That's not the name on this sleeve. But it's the name in your eyes, and I've hunted enough liars to know a true one. I'm Doss. A fixer. And you woke up wearing a corpse that isn't supposed to be walking."

Minds are stored on stacks and swapped between bodies — sleeves. The rich buy new bodies forever; only the poor stay dead. Someone up top has been permanently murdered, and the sleeve I woke in is the one it's pinned on. I've been dropped into a frame. A frame needs a past to stick to — and I only exist as of this morning.

The frame is good. But I do the thing I do — I count. The murder happened up top; the sleeve supposedly rode up, killed, and rode down. And an elevator to the top takes an hour, while the timestamps are forty minutes apart. You cannot ride an hour each way in forty minutes.

But nobody up top wants to hear it, because the real killer is up top. So — handy, sideways — I splice into an elevator control-node with Doss keeping watch and push the public ride-logs where the whole tower can see them, undercity to penthouse: every ascent and descent timestamped, including the real killer's. You can't hang a crime on a journey the tower's own elevators say never happened. The frame comes apart in front of everyone.

"You cleared a body that wasn't even yours," Doss says. "Why fight so hard for a face you're not keeping?"

"Because nobody should get hung for being nobody." I don't add that being nobody and getting out clean is pretty much my whole life.

The hum starts in the service elevator. There's the threshold I woke on. There's a loose optic-strand — wire or wool — and I splice one small knot around the doorframe, even here, even wearing someone else's hands. All my marks run off into a single cord going into a dark past any counting.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the door, and I press the coin to a palm that has no scar, and it's still enough, because the coin is the part that's really me.

Then the tone drowns the tower, and I'm falling — out of the borrowed body, back into my own scarred hands somewhere new.

## A DOORWAY · FRANCHISE FLATS
*a neon-and-data world · read in any order*

The jingle is the first thing. I come to on hot sponsored pavement — there's a logo stamped in the concrete every arm's length — with a cheerful tune blaring into my skull. Right hand, scar across the palm, into my pocket, coin there, worn to a faceless blank, the one thing nobody's branded.

The tell is the branding. Every surface is selling something. I never get used to it. I just get faster.

A kid on a delivery bike skids up and shoves something into my scarred hand. "Take it, don't look at it — you're clean, right? Unregistered? Get it to a clean server before it uploads through you. Please."

"Wick," I tell her when my voice returns. "Just Wick, whatever, GO—" Her name's Pip, a courier for something that has no name because the thing she's fighting doesn't obey the rules names are made of.

There's no country anymore — just franchise-states, corporate micro-nations, and a lurid Metaverse everyone plugs into. And a plague: not of the body but a linguistic virus, a pattern of image and sound that infects software and brains at once, jumping from screen to skull. The scroll Pip shoved at me carries it, set to upload the second it gets a connection. And I'm what it's touching now.

Here I'm unregistered — no avatar, no account, no chip — which is why Pip's crew needs me: I'm the one carrier who isn't connected to anything. A dead end. A boy-shaped firewall.

But "carrying it to a clean server" is a lie. I figure it out by counting the ad-drones on the approach — too many, circling. Somebody wants this delivered to a specific server to *launch* it, from a node with Metaverse-wide reach. A media-mogul seeding a plague to sell the cure. Pip's crew are the mules.

So I make the choice I make. The virus only works if you look at it. So I break its grammar: at a public terminal I loop the pattern into a mirror, force it to read only its own corrupted code, a snake eating its tail. It chokes and burns itself out in a sealed node, reaching nobody.

"They were using us," Pip says, hands over her face. "We thought we were carrying it away and we were carrying it in."

"You were carrying it to the one person it couldn't upload through. That part was you." I don't tell her that walking a dangerous thing to the one place it can't hurt anyone is more or less the only trick I've got.

The hum starts in the branded street. There's the corner I first came to on. There's a loose optic-thread — wool or wire — and I splice one small strand around the post, a knot of quiet light in a place where all the other light's for sale. All my marks run off into a single cord going into a dark past any number.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the corner, almost fond.

Then the tone drowns the jingles and Pip saying thank you, and the sponsored sky goes white.

## A DOORWAY · EMBERFELL
*a stone-and-oath world · read in any order*

I land into heat and it's the wrong kind — this heat has a color, orange, and it comes up from the ground. I cough ash, which is almost funny here, because ash is already drifting out of a gold-and-smoke sky like dirty snow. Right palm — burn scar shiny, which suits this place — into my pocket, coin there, cold in all this heat, still mine.

The tell is the sky, the color of a forge, and mountains that glow at their tops. A volcano world. And it's ending.

A knight in scorched armor is dying at the foot of a broken archway. "You came through the seal-gate. That means you can carry it. Listen, boy—"

"Wick," I tell her when my voice returns.

"Wick." She says it like a password. Dame Yregar, a Warden of the Ember-Seal, the last near enough to matter. A relic that held the world in order broke, the mountains opened, and out of them pours a tide of things that were never people, toward the valleys where the last refugees are packed in. And the Ashborn want to seize the relic's power rather than mend it.

She presses a shard of the broken seal into my scarred hand, warm as a coal. "There's a pass. Hold it, and the seal can be mended — but it's missing the resonance-piece that makes the fragments sing together, and it was lost. I have no piece. I have no time. I have a boy who fell out of the gate. Go."

I run for the pass, the tide behind me a grinding roar. At the pass a dozen exhausted people try to build a mend that can't work, and an Ashborn agent whispers that the seal's a lie.

The obstacle isn't the monsters. It's the missing piece. But here's what I am in every world: I fix things with my hands. A seal is a machine, and I've spent a hundred landings feeling for the shape of a missing thing. The resonance-piece made the fragments ring at one note. I don't have it. But I have a coin — worn smooth, faceless, harder and stranger than any metal these mountains forged. I press it flat between two singing fragments and they catch — ring together — the coin dead-blank and perfectly balanced, holding the note. The seal wakes, the mend takes, the pass holds.

It costs me. I feel the wrench of giving up the coin. But the tide breaks against the pass like water against a wall. And then — because the mend is holy, or because the coin was only ever mine and knows it — the seal spits it back into my scarred hand, warm, worn, mine.

The hum starts at the crest of the held pass, the orange sky going frost-silver. There's the seal-gate I first fell through. I pull a scorched thread from the squire's cloak and tie one small knot around a spur of cooling stone. All the knots run together into a single cord going off into a dark that no fire lights.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I say to the gate, and I press the warm coin once against my scar to be sure it's really back.

Then the orange goes white.

## A DOORWAY · CRATER NEO
*a neon-and-data world · read in any order*

The sound is engines. I come to flat on cold concrete with the whine of a hundred motorcycles echoing off empty overpasses, and cough ash under a sky the color of a bruise lit orange from below. Right hand — scar across the palm, back on my own hand, and I'm stupidly glad to feel it — into my pocket, coin there, mine.

The tell is the concrete and the sodium light. Grey and stacked and brutal, ruined districts with old evacuation signs graffitied over. The jump lands like the temperature of the whole world changing at once.

A kid loops back on a bike. "You're gonna get flattened lying there! You with anybody? Get on, get ON—"

"Wick," I tell him. "Just Wick, cool, hold on—" His name's Taro, a biker crew, and he decides a stray who owes none of the gangs anything is who you trust in a night about to go wrong.

A megacity over an old crater. Under it, a secret: a covert military project growing esper power in kids — minds that move things, bend things, break things, unstable and escalating. Tonight one has gotten out. And he's terrified. A terrified esper losing control is the most dangerous thing in this concrete world.

The kid — Kenji, younger than me, eyes too bright, the air around him pressured like the moment before a storm — is huddled in a ruined district the military means to seal and cordon. If he panics inside a sealed cordon, the pressure comes out all at once.

He doesn't need fighting. He needs calming, and there's no calming a kid while an army closes a ring around him. I count the cordon posts — too many, closing — and understand the cordon itself is the fuse.

So I make the choice I make. Handy, sideways. I splice into the district's ancient evacuation system — sirens and route-lights built decades ago to move a panicked population out fast — and I light it up. Every old sign flares, every arrow-light pointing the same way, away from Kenji, a hundred glowing exits at once.

Two things happen. The cordon has to react to a crisis instead of tightening a noose — you can't hold a cordon in a full evacuation. And Kenji, hunted his whole life toward a wall, sees arrows pointing him out instead of in. The pressure eases. Just enough. A kid who's shown a way out doesn't blow.

Taro's crew rides Kenji out through a lit-up route while the military chases a phantom evacuation. A frightened kid gets to be a kid a little longer instead of a weapon.

"How'd you know lights would do it and guns wouldn't?" Taro yells over the engines.

"Because a kid who's only ever been chased at a wall just needs to be shown a door." I don't tell him that opening the door and slipping out of it is the only thing I really know how to do anywhere.

The hum starts on the roaring overpass. There's the concrete underpass I first came to on. There's a frayed wire — wire or wool — and I splice one small knot around a rebar spur, glowing faint against all that concrete. All my marks run off into a single cord going into a dark past any number.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the underpass, and I press the coin to my scar, glad tonight to have both.

Then the orange concrete world goes white.

## A DOORWAY · REEDWATER
*a stone-and-oath world · read in any order*

I land in a boat, which is a first, and nearly go over the side into black water. I catch myself on the gunwale and cough ash over the reeds, the boat rocking, a hundred voices haggling nearby. Right hand into my pocket first, always first — coin there, worn to a blank, mine.

The tell is the smell: wet reeds and dye and spice and, under it, the green rot of a place slowly drowning.

A boy poling the boat stares at me like I materialized in his cargo, which I did, dye stains up to his elbows in three colors. "Did you fall off a bridge? There are a thousand bridges. Blink if you're simple."

I don't blink. "Wick," I tell him. "Just Wick." He's Faris, runs errands for the Scholars' Guild, and decides a stray who owes nobody is exactly who the Scholars have been praying for.

A drowned marvel — a delta turned metropolis, a thousand bridges, floating markets, tiled domes rising from black canals. And it's sinking, ward by ward, the poor wards first. Engineers among the Scholars know exactly how to build the flood-works that would save it. But nobody will pay. The Boatwrights' League and the Dyers' & Merchants' Guild are in a trade war so vicious neither will fund a thing that helps the other, even a thing that saves them both. And the water just rises.

My foothold: I carry the flood-plan between two guildhalls that would rather sink than share a room. And a stray with no colors is the only messenger who won't be searched or blamed.

The obstacle is the ugliest kind — not a monster, but pride and arithmetic. Each league has run the sums: whoever pays loses, because the other gets saved for free. So neither pays, and the next high tide is three days out.

So I stop trying to untie the knot and I re-thread the buckle wrong on purpose. I don't take the plan to the leagues. I take it to the lowest ward — the dye-workers and reed-cutters who are nobody's guild and everybody's labor — and tell them plainly why their homes are going under. Then I make sure both leagues learn that the low ward knows.

Because the leagues don't run on gold. They run on labor. A low ward that knows it's being left to drown can stop poling the boats and stirring the vats — and a trade war with no one left to do the trading is a war both sides lose in an afternoon. Suddenly "whoever pays first, loses" becomes "whoever doesn't pay, loses everything."

They fund the flood-works. Together, hating it, but together. The locks go in. The high tide breaks against a wall instead of over a home. The lowest ward stays dry. Faris looks at me like I did magic, and I let him, because the truth — that I just found the buckle everyone was too proud to re-thread — is less fun.

The hum starts on a bridge over black water. There's the low doorway of the Scholars' Guild. There's a loose strand of dyed wool on the jamb, and I tie one small knot to the ring. All my knots run together into a single cord going off past where anyone could count, more knots than there are bridges in this drowning city.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the door, gentle, the way you'd tell a secret to a house that was almost yours.

Then the canals go white.

## A DOORWAY · THE SEALED UNDERCITY
*a neon-and-data world · read in any order*

The quiet is the first strange thing, and then the light. I come to on packed earth and cable under a low stone ceiling, warm rust-and-lantern light everywhere, no wind, no sky at all. Right hand — scar across the palm — into my pocket, coin there, mine. Even down here where "here" has no sky.

The tell is that there's no weather and no daylight and no people. Cramped neon alleys, hanging cables, paper lanterns, stacked shanties — a whole city under a stone lid — but the figures moving through it are machines. Robots, all of them. I'm the only warm-blooded thing in an entire city, and that jump lands lonelier than any I know.

A little robot with a knitted scarf stops dead when it sees me, face-lights wide. "You're organic. You're a person. A real one. I've only ever seen the pictures. Can you talk? Do organics talk?"

"Wick," I tell it. "Just Wick." Its name is Kettle — it picked the name because it likes the sound water makes — and it decides on the spot that I'm the most wonderful thing that's ever happened, which down here might be true.

A sealed city where humans lived once and then vanished, and the machines they left woke up and built a gentle society in the dark. They pour tea they can't drink because they remember humans doing it. They wear scarves. And they can't leave — the surface is sealed by ancient Sentinels enforcing a lockdown nobody alive remembers.

Here, being the wrong kind of thing is exactly the key: the surface-hatch was built to open only for a living human hand, and there hasn't been one down here in longer than any robot's memory. Until me.

But the city's split — half dream of the surface, half are terrified of it — and forcing the hatch open on all of them isn't fair to the frightened ones. And you can't fight a gentle jailer just following a rule from people long gone.

So I do the thing I do. I don't break the seal. I use my hands on it. I rewire the ancient lock — my organic hand doing the part only an organic hand can — so that from now on the hatch answers to any citizen who *chooses* to reach for it. I don't open the door on the frightened ones. I just make the door openable, forever, from the inside, by anyone who wants to. And I let Kettle be the one to actually crack it.

Kettle pushes, and for the first time in the city's memory the seal gives — a thin blade of real daylight falling down into the lantern-dark. Some robots step back, afraid. Some step toward the light like it's the thing they were built to find. Both are allowed now. The choice belongs to whoever's standing at it.

"You gave us the door and let us choose it," Kettle whispers, lit up in the beam. "Why didn't you just open it all the way?"

"Because a door that's forced isn't freedom. It's just a different kind of seal. Everybody should get to choose their own threshold." I don't tell Kettle that opening a door and stepping through it and leaving is more or less the only thing I've ever known how to do.

The hum starts in the blade of daylight. There's the low doorway I first came to under. There's a loose strand of cable — wire or wool, it never fails — and I splice one small knot around the doorpost, glowing faint in the rust-light. All my marks, in firelit doorposts and neon alleys and buried hatches, run together into a single cord going off into a dark past any counting — and I gave up counting long ago, because the cord was never about the number. It was only ever about the caring I couldn't help leaving behind.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I tell the doorway, and Kettle, lit up in the impossible daylight, says the thing almost nobody ever says back:

"Then maybe you should let somewhere try."

I don't get to answer. The tone drowns the bell-voices and that thin blade of sky, and the warm buried city goes white — and I'm falling, coin in my scarred hand, refrain on my lips, one more knot behind me, toward a place that has never once heard the name Wick.

## THE FIXED LAST CHAPTER · THE BOY WHO STAYS
*Not a Doorway. This is the one chapter with a fixed place — read it last, after you've done your wandering. It is the true conclusion.*

The hum starts in my back teeth the way it always starts, and the edges of everything go silver, like frost creeping across glass, and the whole world drains down to one flat ringing tone. I know this part. I've known it a thousand times. This is the part where I fall out of one place and toward another that has never heard my name.

Except this time, nothing catches me.

There's no ash on my tongue. No new cold, no new heat, no rain that tastes of somebody's factory. I don't come to on my hands and knees with a stranger already deciding whether I'm useful. I just — hang. In white. In the between. The place I've dropped through more times than there are nights, and never once stopped to look at.

I check my pocket. Right hand — scar across the palm, pale and shiny — and the coin is there, worn to a faceless blank, cold and solid and mine. So I'm still me. Even here, where *here* is nowhere, I'm still me.

And then I see the cord.

It runs off in both directions, into the white and past the white into the dark, a single line strung with knots — more knots than I ever let myself count. I know this cord. I've been tying it my whole life, one small knot at a time. One knot in every place that almost kept me. A little *I-was-here.* A *this-could-have-been-the-one.*

I always thought they were goodbyes.

I follow the cord, because my hands want to — my hands always want to follow a line to see where it ends, the same hands that have re-threaded a hundred buckles and picked a hundred locks and jury-rigged forges and elevators and ancient sealed doors. The knots pass under my scarred fingers, and I feel each one as I go. Not the world it was tied in; those blur, a great warm smear of every castle and every neon street I ever stumbled into. What I feel is smaller and truer than the worlds. I feel the thing I *did* in each of them.

I don't have to name the worlds. The worlds were never the point. The point was always the same, in stone and in neon, in firelight and in rain: I found the thing that was breaking, and I put my hands on it, and I left it a little more whole than I found it — and then I left.

*Then I left.* That's the part I never looked at.

The cord doesn't only trail off into the dark. It *comes from* somewhere. I turn, and I follow it back the other way, past knot after knot after knot, and it gets harder to walk — the way it gets hard to walk toward a thing you've spent your whole life walking away from — and at the very end of the cord, where the first knot is tied, there's a door.

Just a door. Not a gate or an airlock or a hatch. A plain door, the kind that was mine before any of this, before the switching, before the ash and the silver and the ringing tone. I know it the way you know your own name in a stranger's mouth. This is the threshold I crossed the first time. This is the one I've been fleeing ever since, world to world, switch to switch — because I crossed it once, going *out,* going *away,* and something on the far side of it hurt so much to leave that I've never stopped leaving anything since. If you never stay, you never have to lose the staying. If nowhere holds you, nothing can be taken.

That's the whole trick of me. That's the switching. It never happened *to* me. It was something I kept choosing, in the half-second at every threshold when I most belonged — I'd feel the wanting, the almost-yes, the *this-could-be-home,* and I'd run, and the run had a sound like a hum in my teeth and a taste like ash.

I take out the coin. Worn faceless, from the person I lost — the person on the other side of that first door. I've pressed it to my scar in a thousand worlds to prove I was still real. I have never once turned it over and asked what it was *for.*

It isn't money. It never was. It's flat and blank and perfectly balanced, and I've held it at every threshold I ever fled. It's not a keepsake of the leaving.

It's a token for the staying. Something someone put in my hand so that one day I'd have the one thing I needed to open a door from the outside — and come back in.

"Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving," I say, out of old habit, to the door.

And for the first time I hear it for what it is. Not a fact about the world. A choice about me. *Nowhere holds a boy who keeps leaving* — because *leaving* is the part I keep *doing.* The holding was always waiting. I just never stood still long enough to be held.

I could tie one more knot here. I could leave my little *I-was-here* on this first door same as all the others, and let the hum take me, and fall toward some new place that's never heard my name, and be a rope and a fixer and a stranger who saves the day and slips out before anyone can keep him. I'm good at it. It's the only thing I've ever really known how to do.

Or I could do the one thing I never have. I could cross a threshold *toward* instead of *away.*

My hands are shaking. Jury-rigging hands, steady over a hundred broken machines in a hundred breaking worlds, shaking now over a plain door and a blank coin — because this is the one mechanism I never dared to fix. I press the coin flat against the door where a lock should be. It catches. Of course it catches. It was cut for this. It was always cut for this.

I don't tie a knot. Instead I take the whole cord — all of it, every knot, every almost-home, every time I put my hands on something broken and made it whole — and I finally understand what I've been building the entire time I thought I was only leaving. You can't carry a home in your pocket. But you can *make* one, a little at a time, out of every act of caring you refuse to let the leaving cancel. I didn't lose home at that first door. I've been weaving it ever since, in stone worlds and neon worlds, knot by knot, too close to my own hands to see. The cord isn't a record of my goodbyes.

It's the longest way anyone ever took to come back.

I count the door. *One.* Just the one. There was only ever one that mattered, and I've been standing in front of it my whole life pretending it was behind me.

I turn the coin. The door opens — not white, not the ringing tone, not away. Warm light, the ordinary gold kind that comes off a real window in a real evening, spills across my scarred hand and my shaking fingers and the blank coin that turns out to have had my whole way home stamped on its worn-smooth face all along.

The hum doesn't come. My teeth are quiet. There's no ash on my tongue. For the first time since I can remember, nothing at all is pulling me anywhere else.

I step through the threshold, toward, and I stay.

## Verification notes

- **Permutation safety.** Each of the twelve Doorways opens cold and answers, on its own, who the boy is, what switching is, where he's landed, what's at stake, and how it resolves — then returns him to the neutral between-switch state. References to "other worlds" are kept abstract and aggregate ("the last place," "some worlds," "a hundred landings"); no Doorway names or requires any other, and none claims a specific world was read immediately before it. Shuffle them freely; the finale stays last.
- **No cross-chapter carryover.** The boy's constants (name, faceless coin, right-palm burn scar, the refrain, jury-rigging hands, the door-counting tic, the ash-cough switch grammar) recur by identity, not by accumulation. Nothing acquired inside a Doorway is carried out of it. Emberfell and The Spindle put the coin under stress but return it intact, so the anchor survives every reading path.
- **Order-independent payoff.** The finale resolves from the boy's constant longing plus the reader's aggregate impression of "however many worlds you visited, in stone and in neon." It names no specific world, ally, or event, assumes no count or order, and reads as complete after even a single Doorway — deepening, not depending, as you read more.
- **World sourcing.** The worlds are recombined from a broad survey of historical settings and classic games and stories, then abstracted so each is fully original and self-contained; every world is unique across biome, central conflict, ruling power, and cultural flavor, so none doubles another within its register.
- **YA calibration.** First-person, present tense throughout; real peril and hard choices with no gore, explicit content, or hopeless endings; every Doorway's tension resolves within its own pages.
