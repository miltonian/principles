# Why Palantir Became a Market Darling: The Story Behind Its 2024–2025 Breakout

In 2024, one of the best-performing stocks in the entire S&P 500 — the benchmark index of large U.S. companies — was not a chip designer or a social-media giant. It was a secretive, two-decade-old software company that had spent most of its life losing money and courting controversy. Palantir Technologies (New York Stock Exchange: PLTR) ended the year as the index's single best performer, with a total return of roughly 340% for calendar-year 2024 — outrunning even Nvidia. For a firm long dismissed as an unprofitable defense contractor with a Tolkien-inspired name, the turnaround was, by any measure, dramatic.

This article answers two questions. First, *who is Palantir*, and how did an idea born after 9/11 grow into a data-and-AI company worth watching? Second, *why did the breakout happen now* — what actually drove the financial and market results of 2024 and 2025? The two stories are distinct, and understanding the first is what makes the second make sense.

## Part 1 — Background: From a Security Problem to a Data Company

### A company born from a post-9/11 idea

Palantir was founded in 2003, in the anxious years after the September 11 attacks, around a deceptively simple question: how do you find the meaningful signal hidden inside oceans of messy, disconnected data? The name — borrowed from the "seeing stones" in J.R.R. Tolkien's *The Lord of the Rings* — captures the pitch: software that lets human analysts see connections across information that would otherwise stay scattered and invisible.

The founders are unusually well known for an enterprise software company. Peter Thiel, the investor and PayPal co-founder, provided the early money and the vision, drawing on fraud-detection methods PayPal had used to catch criminals moving money online. Alex Karp — a philosophy PhD, not a traditional Silicon Valley technologist — became chief executive and remains the company's combative public face. Co-founders Joe Lonsdale, Stephen Cohen, and Nathan Gettings rounded out the early team. Early backing is widely reported to have included In-Q-Tel, the venture-capital arm of the CIA, which both funded Palantir and became an early testing ground for its software.

That origin explains two things at once: why Palantir spent its first decade deeply embedded in government and defense work, and why it developed an unusual tolerance for the hardest, most sensitive data problems.

### Two engines: Gotham and Foundry

At its core, Palantir sells platforms — foundational systems that other organizations run their operations on top of — rather than a single app. Its identity rests on a small set of flagship products: **Gotham**, **Foundry**, and, more recently, **AIP**.

**Gotham** is the original product and the government backbone. Think of it as an intelligence-and-operations platform: it pulls together fragmented data — surveillance reports, sensor feeds, financial records, logistics information — and lets analysts and decision-makers see the whole picture in one place. Its reputation was forged in national-security and law-enforcement settings, where the cost of a missed connection can be measured in lives.

**Foundry** is the commercial version of the same idea. Instead of tracking threats, it helps large companies run their businesses, building what Palantir calls a "digital twin" of an organization — a single, constantly updated model of its data — so a manufacturer, hospital, bank, or airline can ask hard operational questions and act on the answers. The connective tissue beneath both products is what Palantir calls its *ontology*: a living map that links a company's raw data to the real-world people, objects, and decisions it represents, so information isn't just stored but can be put to work. Palantir and many analysts describe that map as the company's most durable competitive advantage — its "moat."

### The business model — and the controversies

Palantir grew up with an unusual way of selling and delivering software: it embeds its own engineers directly with the customer to wire the platform into messy, real-world workflows. Combined with a culture of deliberate secrecy, that approach produced sticky, deeply integrated installations — and a reputation for opacity.

That reputation has a sharper edge. Because Palantir's software is built for exactly the most sensitive data, its government work has repeatedly drawn scrutiny — over contracts with U.S. Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE), its reported role handling health data for the U.K.'s National Health Service, and its use in predictive policing. Supporters argue the company simply builds tools for institutions that already exist; critics see a firm too comfortable at the intersection of surveillance and state power. Either way, this backdrop is part of why Palantir was long viewed as a company apart — admired by some, distrusted by others, and hard for ordinary investors to categorize.

### The long road to profit

For much of its first decade, Palantir was seen as a government-and-defense company: prestigious and durable, but reliant on lumpy, slow-closing, politically sensitive contracts. Foundry marked a deliberate turn toward the commercial market, as Palantir began signing corporate customers across energy, manufacturing, healthcare, financial services, and aerospace.

The company went public in September 2020 through a direct listing on the New York Stock Exchange — selling existing shares straight onto the market rather than raising new money in a traditional offering. But going public did not settle the central debate. For years afterward, Palantir kept posting net losses under standard U.S. accounting rules (known as GAAP, or Generally Accepted Accounting Principles). Skeptics pointed to the large amount of stock the company handed employees as pay — a real cost that lowers reported profits — as evidence, in their view, that the underlying economics were unproven. Its first-ever profitable quarter under those standard rules did not arrive until the fourth quarter of 2022 (announced in February 2023), with its first full profitable year in 2023. Until then, the standoff between bulls and bears came down to a single tension both sides recognized: *impressive technology, unproven economics.* Resolving that tension is where the second half of the story begins.

## Part 2 — Why 2024–2025 Happened: The Causes of the Breakout

Having spent two decades building data systems for governments and then corporations, Palantir entered 2024 with technology widely discussed but economics still unproven. What changed over the following eighteen months — measured in stock appreciation, revenue growth, profitability, and customer expansion — was a commercial and market breakout, not a change of mission. One plausible read of the causes is a single chain: surging demand for its Artificial Intelligence Platform (AIP) during the generative-AI boom coincided with accelerating revenue and customer growth, alongside sustained profitability, addition to major stock indexes, and, ultimately, top-of-index stock returns.

### The demand catalyst: AIP meets the generative-AI boom

The proximate cause, in management's telling, was demand. Palantir launched AIP — its Artificial Intelligence Platform — in April 2023, just as the generative-AI wave was cresting across corporate technology budgets. AIP layers large language models — the technology behind tools like ChatGPT — on top of the trusted, governed data inside Gotham and Foundry, and connects that AI to specific actions a business is allowed to take.

Rather than sell the platform through conventional trials, Palantir leaned on a distinctive approach it calls "boot camps" — short, hands-on sessions that put a prospective customer to work on its own data. The company credits these sessions for turning many prospects into paying customers during 2024 and into 2025, most visibly in its U.S. commercial business.

The scale of that conversion shows up in the sales pipeline. The value of signed-but-not-yet-delivered U.S. commercial contracts — what Palantir calls "remaining deal value" — reached about $1.79 billion in the fourth quarter of 2024, up roughly 99% from a year earlier, and about $2.32 billion in the first quarter of 2025, up roughly 127% year over year. The total value of U.S. commercial contracts signed in the first quarter of 2025 was reported at about $810 million, up roughly 183% year over year. Management repeatedly attributed this acceleration to demand for AIP. That attribution is worth labeling carefully: Palantir does not break out a separate "AIP-only" revenue line, so the AIP-as-catalyst story rests on the timing — AIP's 2023 rollout followed by the commercial surge — together with management's own account. It suggests, rather than independently proves, that AIP demand was the substantial driver.

### Accelerating revenue, led by U.S. commercial

The clearest marker of the breakout is that reported revenue growth did not merely continue — it accelerated. In 2023, Palantir reported revenue of about $2.23 billion, up roughly 17% from the year before. Full-year 2024 revenue came in at about $2.87 billion, up roughly 29%. The quarterly figures show the same pattern in sharper relief: fourth-quarter 2024 revenue was about $828 million, up roughly 36% year over year, and first-quarter 2025 revenue was about $884 million, up roughly 39%. A company that had been growing in the high teens had, within roughly two years, pushed its reported growth rate toward 40% — the reverse of the slowdown usually seen as a business gets bigger.

Commentators and management alike point to U.S. commercial revenue — the part of the business most exposed to AIP demand — as the engine behind that acceleration. U.S. commercial revenue grew about 64% year over year in the fourth quarter of 2024 and about 71% in the first quarter of 2025. Because this segment was expanding faster than the company as a whole, it lifted the overall growth rate — the mechanism by which broad generative-AI demand became a company-wide result rather than an isolated bright spot.

Customer numbers moved in step. Total customers reached about 711 at the end of 2024, up roughly 43% year over year. Within that, the U.S. commercial customer base grew about 73%. Deal activity moved the same way: Palantir reported closing about 129 deals worth at least $1 million in the fourth quarter of 2024, of which about 58 were worth $5 million or more and 32 were worth $10 million or more, with a comparable set in the first quarter of 2025. Together, more customers, larger deals, and a faster-growing commercial segment show how the demand catalyst turned into faster revenue.

### Crossing into sustained profitability

Rapid growth alone would not have set Palantir apart; plenty of software firms grow while losing money. What separated 2024–2025 is that the growth was profitable — and had lasted long enough to count as sustained. Palantir posted its first profitable quarter under standard accounting rules in the fourth quarter of 2022 and its first full profitable year in 2023. The streak extended to roughly nine straight profitable quarters through the end of 2024, and about ten through the first quarter of 2025. For full-year 2024, Palantir reported net income of about $462 million, including roughly $79 million in the fourth quarter alone. The key word is *sustained* — profit across many quarters and full years, not one lucky period.

The mix of growth and profit shows up in a figure Palantir reports called its "Rule of 40" score, which it put at about 81% for the fourth quarter of 2024. The Rule of 40 is a rough industry yardstick that adds a software company's revenue-growth rate to its profit margin; anything above 40 is considered healthy. Palantir's figure is calculated on an adjusted basis (leaving out certain costs), but even so the company presents it as a sign of strong combined growth and profitability. Analysts have read the shift into profit as settling — at least for now — the "unproven economics" half of the old bull-and-bear tension: the demand surge was not being paid for with mounting losses.

### The market's verdict: index inclusion and best-in-class returns

Turning profitable also cleared a gate. Consistent profitability under standard accounting rules is generally treated as a practical requirement for joining the S&P 500, and Palantir was added to that index effective September 23, 2024 (announced by S&P Dow Jones Indices on September 6, 2024), replacing American Airlines Group. It was later added to the Nasdaq-100 effective December 23, 2024. Index inclusion is best read as confirmation of the operating results already described rather than a cause of them — though it mattered in practice, because it forced funds that automatically track those indexes to buy the stock.

By the market's own measure, the response was striking. Palantir was the best-performing stock in the S&P 500 for calendar-year 2024, with a gain of roughly 340% over the year. The momentum carried into 2025 alongside the accelerating revenue. In short, index inclusion and the record share price ratified the operating results already described — not the other way around.

---

## Closing: Two Stories, One Setup

Palantir's breakout is best understood as two stories braided together. The first is a two-decade infrastructure bet: a post-9/11 idea, the Gotham and Foundry data platforms, the ontology approach, a hard-won pivot into commercial markets, and years of losses that left its economics in doubt. The second is a catalyst: AIP arriving just as the generative-AI boom made trustworthy, usable enterprise AI what every executive wanted — a shift that, on management's telling, appears to have converted years of accumulated capability into faster revenue and, at last, sustained profitability.

The forward risk sits inside that very success. With a reported Rule of 40 around 81% for the fourth quarter of 2024 and a stock that returned roughly 340% in a single year, expectations are now set for rapid growth to continue. The economics are no longer obviously unproven — but the share price now appears to assume the exceptional becomes routine. Whether it does is the open question the next chapters of Palantir's story will answer.
