# When Labour Is No Longer Needed: Two Futures for a Fully Automated Society

Begin from a stipulation rather than a prediction. Suppose that advanced automation has matured to the point where human labour is, for most purposes, simply no longer required — machines and systems produce, distribute, diagnose, decide, and maintain at a level that makes the vast majority of paid human work economically redundant. This essay does not litigate whether that condition is coming, how soon, or by what path. It treats near-total automation as a given — a thought experiment's fixed starting condition — and asks the more interesting question: once labour is no longer the thing that connects most people to the means of life, what fills the gap, and how is the resulting society held together and governed?

Everything turns on a single concept, so it is worth defining with care. By "universal resources" I mean a non-wage channel to subsistence: some standing arrangement — whether delivered as goods, as services, or as income — through which every person can access the means of survival and participation without holding a job. The crucial word is non-wage. Universal resources are not the same thing as "everyone having work" or a return to full employment; they are precisely what a society reaches for when work is no longer the mechanism of provision. They concern distribution and access, not productive capacity. Automation determines how much can be made; the universal-resources channel determines whether, and how, what is made reaches people who earn no wage.

That distinction lets us build a deliberately clean comparison. The two scenarios examined below hold two things constant: automation is maxed out in both, and mass job-displacement is a settled fact in both. They differ on exactly one variable — whether the non-wage subsistence channel exists. In Scenario A the channel is absent: automation has erased most jobs and nothing systematic replaces the wage as the route to survival. In Scenario B the channel is present and generous: automation has erased most jobs, but universal provision is so readily available that no one needs employment at all. Same technology, same collapse of the labour market; one axis of difference. Isolating that axis is what makes the exercise legible.

This is foresight, not prophecy. The point is not to name the one true future but to map a space of plausible ones — to run two internally consistent narratives and study the dynamics each configuration sets loose. Any real trajectory may blend the two, oscillate between them, or veer off entirely; the value lies in understanding the forces, not in pretending to a forecast.

A warning is in order about how these scenarios feel before they are examined. The mind reaches instinctively for a tidy moral: no universal resources means a bleak dystopia of abandonment, while universal resources mean an abundant utopia of freedom. That intuition is natural, and worth naming so it can be set aside — because this essay declines to pre-assign those verdicts. Each configuration has live, ambiguous readings. A population whose every material need is met may still find itself a comfortable but disempowered class — secure yet stripped of leverage, purpose, and any structural reason for the powerful to heed it, its very ease becoming a kind of gilded irrelevance. And a society that provides nothing to the displaced need not simply implode; it may stabilise for a long time through patronage, placation, spectacle, or the managed dependence of the many on the few. Promise and peril run through both. Which is preferable is exactly the question left open; the even-handedness is the method, not an evasion of it.

One further force presses on both worlds equally: a rising population. More people is a compounding stressor whichever scenario obtains — more mouths to feed without wages where nothing is provided, and more claims on the universal channel, straining its political and material sustainability, where everything is. Population magnifies whatever dynamic each configuration already contains. It is a shared amplifier, not the thing that tells the two futures apart; the dividing line remains, throughout, the presence or absence of the non-wage channel. Before splitting the two societies apart, though, we must lay down two shared axes on which both will stand — because the governance answers for A and B only make sense on common ground.

## The Common Ground: Two Cross-Cutting Axes

The first axis concerns the locus and scale of governance — what, exactly, "the system" is. The word smuggles in an unstated assumption, because a system implies a governing unit, and in a fully automated economy at least three candidates compete for that role, none obviously the winner. The first is the nation-state, the default twentieth-century container in which economy and welfare were bundled together: a bounded citizenry, a tax base, a treasury, a monopoly on the rules of provision inside its borders. Almost every intuition about "providing universal resources" quietly assumes this container. The second is a supranational or global order — treaties, trading blocs, international bodies — which earns its place precisely because automated production, mobile capital, and human migration do not respect lines on a map; if value is generated by compute and machines that can be sited anywhere and sold everywhere, the state may be too small a unit to govern the thing that actually matters. The third candidate is the most easily dismissed and should not be: in an economy where the productive base is fully automated, the owners of that base — the platform, cloud, and compute proprietors, "cloud-capital" — set the terms on which anyone accesses goods, services, and information. They price access, extract rent, and can grant or withhold participation at a scale that rivals or outright displaces the formal state. Whoever controls the automated means of production is, functionally, a government, whether or not it holds elections. And these three are not mutually exclusive: the most realistic locus may be a hybrid — a nation-state captured by or fused with cloud-capital owners, formal sovereignty running on infrastructure it does not itself control.

Fixing the unit matters because it exposes a hidden premise in the phrase "universal resources easily available for everyone." Universal is never planet-wide by default. Someone has to pay for, produce, and distribute those resources, which means there is always a bounded provider — an entity with a defined membership. "Universal" is really universal-within-a-boundary: the boundary of a state, a bloc, or the customer base of an owner. That sets a condition both scenarios inherit, and it takes the shape of a contrast. Picture one automated, wealthy polity providing for its own members, set against a rising global population that includes large, high-population regions which are not automated, plus the migration pressure that wealth gradients always generate. The boundary question — who is inside the provision, who is outside it, and what the outside does about being outside — conditions everything downstream. In the abundance case it decides who "everyone" actually means; in the mass-unemployment case it decides where the displaced go and which polity, if any, is obliged to absorb them. Either way, "the system" cannot be analysed until we have named its edges.

The second axis lies beneath the first and is where the two futures converge before they split. Historically, ordinary people held economic leverage because production needed them. The ability to withhold labour — to strike, to be scarce in a skill, to cast a vote backed by economic indispensability — gave workers a bargaining chip against owners and states alike. Provision could be demanded because the machine did not run without hands. Full automation removes that chip: when the productive base no longer requires human labour to function, the threat of withdrawal loses its bite, because there is nothing left to withhold. This holds in both scenarios, whether or not resources are redistributed. Redistribution can restore income; it does not, by itself, restore leverage.

The load-bearing conclusion follows. Because labour's bargaining power evaporates either way, the concentration of power over the automated productive base is the problem the two futures share. They do not differ in whether power concentrates — it concentrates in both — but in how that concentrated power is exercised, and above all whether it is checked: by redistribution treated as a binding obligation, by political constitution, by mere patronage, or not at all. This is exactly why governance, not feasibility, is the hard question. The optimistic literature on post-scarcity abundance — Fully Automated Luxury Communism and its kin — is weakest precisely where it is quietest: it describes the plenty but rarely theorises who controls the plenty, or how control is transferred and constrained. That missing theory of power is the gap this axis names. So refuse the reflexive mapping in advance: "resources provided" does not automatically read as freedom, and "no resources" does not automatically read as tyranny. A fully provided-for population can be comfortable yet politically disempowered — the "useless class" of Harari's warning, materially secure but governed by a data- and capital-owning elite it can no longer bargain with. And a no-redistribution order need not collapse: it may stabilise through patronage or managed dependency, the rent-extracting arrangement that "techno-feudalism" describes. In each case the governing variable is the distribution of power over the automated base, not the presence of income alone.

With that shared ground laid — who governs and within what boundary, and how concentrated power is checked once labour can no longer check it — the scenarios can diverge.

## Scenario A: No Universal Resources

### The Economic System

In a wage-based economy, the overwhelming share of consumer demand is funded by labour income: people buy because they are paid to work, and their spending funds the next round of production. Strip out most wages and provide no non-wage channel to replace them, and the question becomes brutally simple: where does purchasing power now sit? It concentrates. It pools among the owners of the automated productive assets — compute, platforms, data, robotics, intellectual property — plus a thin residue of still-remunerated specialists whom the machines cannot yet cheaply replace. The majority hold little to nothing, because the mechanism that historically routed value to them — selling their labour — no longer clears.

This produces a genuine internal tension. Automated production can generate goods in near-limitless volume at collapsing marginal cost, yet an income-less majority cannot buy any of it. A market economy runs on paying customers, so as purchasing power thins, mass-market demand hollows out even as capacity soars. One might expect a self-correction — the old Fordist logic that owners must pay workers enough to buy the cars they build — but automation severs precisely that loop: because owners no longer need the many as workers, they no longer need them as consumers either. The rational response is not to reflate mass demand but to retreat into a luxury economy in which the owning class produces for, trades with, and services itself, while the broader market contracts. The system can therefore stall or bifurcate rather than rebalance — a real instability, not a paradox that quietly resolves itself.

Absent redistribution, value increasingly accrues not as wages diffused across a workforce but as rent extracted by whoever owns the automated base — tolls on access to compute, platforms, data, and the machines themselves. This configuration has a name: "techno-feudalism," the contested claim that we are drifting from a capitalism of profit-through-markets toward an order of rent-extracting cloud-capital lords who own the digital fiefs everyone else must pay to use. It is a debated lens rather than settled fact, but it captures the mechanism precisely, and it clarifies what the central problem actually is: not merely that people lack income, but that power over the productive base is concentrated in very few hands. Income could in principle be handed down; leverage cannot be so easily restored.

Rising population does not change this variable; it amplifies it. More jobless, income-less people deepen the demand collapse and enlarge the "surplus population" problem — a growing mass economically superfluous to a system that neither needs its labour nor can sell to it. Here the boundedness axis becomes decisive. If provision, such as it is, stays inside one automated, wealthy polity, that polity can in principle wall itself off, serving only its own owners and clients and treating the outside as externality. But the surplus does not stay put: non-automated, high-population regions and the migration gradients wealth always generates press on the automated core from without, while its own displaced press from within. Whether the pressure is contained by exclusion or spills across borders is itself a governance choice, not an economic given.

None of this is guaranteed to convert into shared abundance, and history warns against assuming it will. In 1930 Keynes forecast that rising productivity would deliver a roughly fifteen-hour work week by around 2030. Productivity and wealth rose enormously — and the leisure never came, because ever-escalating consumption norms and relative-status competition absorbed the gains. The lesson carries directly: higher productive capacity does not automatically become shared plenty. Without a deliberate non-wage channel, the surplus pools at the top rather than spreading outward. Abundance in capacity is not abundance in access.

### How Such a Society Is Governed

The governance question here is not how the jobless get income — that is the economics — but who exercises the concentrated power over the automated base, and what, if anything, checks it. It becomes the defining political question because of the loss of the labour lever: the strike, the scarce skill, and the vote courted for its economic weight all rested on a credible threat to withhold work, and full automation dissolves that threat. Power over the productive base concentrates with little to counterweight it, and the political order reorganises around that concentration rather than around the old contest between labour and capital. Two contested theorisations name the resulting stratification: techno-feudalism, associated with writers such as Yanis Varoufakis, casts the cloud-capital owners as a quasi-aristocracy living off rent; Harari's "useless class" describes a population rendered economically and militarily unnecessary beneath a data-owning elite. Both are debated lenses, not settled fact, but together they sketch a resource- and data-owning minority set against a jobless, propertyless majority.

Which unit actually governs is genuinely open. On one reading the nation-state endures, keeping its monopoly on force, its courts, and its borders, and merely regulating and taxing the owners, who remain subjects of a sovereign that could in principle break them up. On the other, whoever controls the infrastructure, compute, and means of production is already the effective government — a company town at civilisational scale, the state a shell captured by or fused with cloud-capital. The likeliest outcome sits between: formal sovereignty hollowed by dependence on private infrastructure the state cannot itself run. And because provision presupposes a bounded provider, this is conditioned from outside — a rich automated polity's internal order sits inside a world of high-population, non-automated regions and migration pressure, which pushes any elite toward hardened borders.

Crucially, this order need not simply collapse into grim abandonment; how it is held stable has no single answer, and several mechanisms are live, alone or in combination. It might rest on surveillance and dependency: information asymmetry, algorithmic monitoring, and the majority's structural reliance on elite-owned infrastructure make control cheap and largely invisible. It might rest on patronage and placation: the surplus population pacified with discretionary charity, cheap automated goods, and engineered distraction — crucially a revocable favour contingent on elite goodwill, not the universal, rights-based entitlement that would make this Scenario B. Or it might rest on disenfranchisement and managed exclusion: the surplus walled off, denied political voice, segregated legally or geographically, and simply governed out. None is inevitable, and each carries different costs. What sharpens all of them is that the surplus population is growing: a larger excluded majority makes surveillance more expensive, patronage more fiscally demanding, and exclusion more coercive and fragile — so the stakes of whichever mode obtains rise over time, which is exactly why the exercise-and-checking of power is the defining problem.

That points to the checking side. The conventional checks are weakened at the root: elections matter less to elites who no longer need the masses as workers or consumers; labour movements have no work to withdraw; the market for labour, once a diffuse constraint on owners, has vanished. Yet the checks are not simply zero. Elite factional competition can pit owners against one another; any regime still craves legitimacy and fears unrest, giving a restive majority a residual if blunt lever; the masses may retain some value as consumers or as harvestable attention; and external pressure — rival states, supranational bodies, reputational cost — can constrain the worst conduct. Whether these harden into durable constraints or remain thin is the open question on which Scenario A's political character ultimately turns.

## Scenario B: Universal Resources

### The Economic System

Scenario B's defining feature is a non-wage channel to subsistence — goods, services, or income reaching people independently of any job — and its first hard question is fiscal. For a century, states funded collective provision chiefly out of wages, through income and payroll taxes. Automation erodes exactly that base at the moment the need for provision peaks: the wage bill thins as the claimant pool grows, and a rising population only tightens the vise. Every serious proposal therefore tries to attach a fiscal or ownership handle to wealth produced by capital and code rather than by people, and three genuinely distinct families are on record. Bill Gates's 2017 suggestion of an automation or "robot" tax would follow the function of a displaced worker, taxing the machine roughly as the lost wage was taxed, and routing the proceeds into transition support and services. Andrew Yang's "Freedom Dividend" instead shifts the base itself away from payroll toward capital and consumption — a value-added tax on what the automated economy produces, redistributed as a flat, unconditional dividend to every citizen. The OpenAI 2026 blueprint's version changes ownership rather than taxation, capitalising a share of the compute-and-capital base into a commonly held fund whose yield flows to citizens as part-owners. Tax the machine, shift the base, or own the asset: three different mechanisms, not three names for one.

That such a channel is administratively governable is not speculative. The Alaska Permanent Fund has for decades capitalised a portion of its oil and resource royalties and paid every resident an annual dividend regardless of work — routine to administer, durable across political cycles, treated by residents as a shared entitlement that has survived changes of administration and economic cycles. But its honest limit must travel with it: the dividend is a modest annual supplement, not a subsistence-replacing income. It proves the mechanism, not the magnitude; nothing about a functioning small dividend guarantees the architecture scales to fund full subsistence for an entire population. And against all three proposals stands a credible objection, held by serious economists and investors and not to be waved away: taxing automation and capital may suppress the very innovation that generates the surplus — raising the cost of the automation society is banking on, deterring investment, chasing mobile capital to lighter-touch jurisdictions, or inviting definitional arbitrage over what counts as a "robot." Financing Scenario B is thus a live, contested dispute, neither fantasy nor solved.

If subsistence goods become near-costless to produce, money does not disappear, but its centre of gravity moves. Reproducible goods — anything a machine can copy or grow at scale — trend toward cheapness. What stays scarce is everything not reproducible: land and prime location, authentic originals, human attention, personal care, credentials of rank, proximity to power. Abundance does not abolish scarcity; it relocates it onto these positional goods, which become the real arena of price, competition, and inequality. A society can meet everyone's material needs and still stratify sharply over who lives where, whose time is sought, and whose status is recognised. Material floor and positional ceiling can rise together — a comfortable base beneath a steep, still-contested summit.

This relocation of scarcity answers what still motivates production once wage compulsion is gone — for the automated base must still be maintained, improved, and defended. The plausible replacements for the paycheck are the motives positional scarcity keeps alive: status and reputation, the intrinsic pull of mastery and curiosity, relational and care bonds, and the desire to acquire the scarce goods abundance cannot hand out equally. The engineers, auditors, and stewards who keep the systems running would do so not because they would otherwise starve but because the work confers standing, meaning, and access to what remains scarce. Whether that supply of stewardship is reliable — whether prestige and curiosity durably staff the critical infrastructure — is a genuine open question, not a guaranteed yes.

The tempting picture is a population dissolving into leisure, and here the same Keynesian caution that shadowed Scenario A applies with equal force. Keynes's fifteen-hour week never arrived even as wealth rose, because consumption expanded and relative-status preferences absorbed the gains; people did not stop wanting more than their neighbours simply because they had enough. Scenario B inherits this logic. Freed from wage compulsion, effort is far likelier to redirect than to vanish — toward status competition, creative and entrepreneurial production, and relational or care pursuits — precisely because positional goods remain scarce and worth striving for. And this is where the scenario's valence stays honestly open. The redirection could be genuine flourishing: work chosen, not coerced. Or it could harden into a hierarchical status economy layered on top of a passive, provided-for base — the comfortable but disempowered "useless class" the framing flagged — in which the dividend secures comfort while agency migrates to those who own the base and win the positional contests.

Two pressures finally test whether "easily available for everyone" survives contact with arithmetic. The first is per-capita math: a dividend indexed to head-count is a fixed or automation-bounded pool divided by a rising denominator, so as population grows the per-person share falls unless automated output grows at least as fast; indexing instead to output stabilises the tie between abundance and productivity, but then provision tracks the machines, not human need, and can fall if productivity stalls. The second is the boundary. "Universal" always presupposes a bounded provider. A closed, automated, wealthy polity provisioning lavishly for its own citizens is a fundamentally different economic object from provision for a rising global population that includes vast non-automated, high-fertility regions and generates persistent migration pressure at the border of membership. Abundance inside the fence and abundance for all of humanity are not the same promise, and demographic growth forces a choice between them — a choice this economics cannot itself settle, because it depends on who controls the channel, sets its index, and draws the boundary of "everyone."

### Governing the Commons: Who Rules When No One Needs a Job

Provision solves income without solving power, so the decisive question is who runs the machine and on whose terms. Ownership of the machines and control of the provisioning channel are separable, and the second is where sovereignty actually lives: you can own compute without setting the dividend, and set the dividend without owning a single server. Four controllers are plausible — a democratic state that holds the base in a public fund and sets the formula by statute; a public or commons trust whose trustees steward assets on behalf of members; a handful of cloud-capital firms that provision as a private product, the techno-feudal case; and an algorithmic administration in which allocation runs as code, so whoever writes and tunes the model effectively decides. "Control," concretely, is the power to do three things: turn provision off, change the allocation formula, and set the terms of membership and access. Whoever holds those three switches is the de facto sovereign, elections or none — and the central problem of Scenario B is that these switches concentrate by default in the hands that already own the base.

Provision is not self-executing; someone fixes the level, the mix, who counts as a member, and how residual positional goods are shared. Legitimate procedures exist, each legitimate in proportion to how much of a real switch it moves. Constitutional entrenchment of provision as an inalienable right removes the off-switch from ordinary politics, converting a gift into a floor — but fails if the right is paper without a funding guarantee. Referenda on the dividend supply direct consent, but are vulnerable to captured information and to majorities stripping non-members. Citizen assemblies chosen by sortition resist capture and actually represent the provided-for, but fail where they are merely advisory or where experts and the algorithm pre-frame every option. Transparency and audit of the allocating algorithm is an information right — you cannot consent to what you cannot see — but fails when audits are performative, the model proprietary, or the complexity simply defeats scrutiny.

The crux is how power is checked when no one depends on employment. The labour lever is gone, so what new levers remain? The vote — but only if the state, not private firms, holds the switches; voting for a body that lacks the off-switch is theatre. Governance rights over the machines — board seats, golden shares, commons-trust membership — the strongest substitute, because it attacks control directly. Exit and mobility to another provider — weak when providers are few and borders walled. The threat of ungovernability — but a provided-for, entertained, surveilled population has little credible disruption, and automation has already reduced the elite's need for its cooperation. Information rights — necessary but not sufficient; knowledge without a switch is only witnessing. Assessed honestly, these are weaker than the strike, because nearly every one depends on the owners' consent or the state's willingness to coerce them, whereas the strike was self-enforcing: production stopped without you. Only one lever genuinely rivals the old one — direct ownership and governance of the base, which makes the check structural again rather than granted.

That raises the transition problem, and it needs a mechanism, not a wish. Plausible paths include taxation and socialisation of automated output feeding a sovereign or commons fund that progressively buys equity in the base; public compute utilities that treat foundational models and compute as regulated infrastructure, like the grid or water; and staged mutualisation via public stakes and golden shares as a condition of operating. Each is blocked by the same forces: incumbent owners' structural power, capital and compute flight to friendlier jurisdictions, and regulatory capture in which owners write the rules of their own socialisation. The deepest blocker is circular — the transition is hardest precisely because the class that would force it has already lost the labour leverage that historically won such concessions. Realistic triggers are therefore crisis, elite defection, or provision extracted as the price of legitimacy while owners still need mass consent.

This is where the standing charge against Fully Automated Luxury Communism — that it has no theory of power, transition, or currency — must be met rather than waved away. On power: name who holds the three switches, and demand the check be structural — ownership and governance of the base, not mere distribution downstream of it. On transition: the mutualisation and public-utility pathways above, with the blockers named. On currency and allocation: money persists for positional and scarce goods, and those prices and residual allocations are governed by assembly, referendum, and audited algorithm — abundance relocates scarcity, it does not abolish the need to decide.

And so the abundant-ideal reading must itself be refused. A population that is provided-for but economically irrelevant is, in Harari's sense, a potential useless class — fed, entertained, pacified, and leverageless. Material security is not freedom. The failure mode is precise: formal democratic stewardship exists on paper — constitutions, assemblies, a dividend voted on — while real control stays with whoever runs the machines and tunes the allocating model. The assembly deliberates; the code decides; the owners set the code. That is techno-feudalism with a ballot box. Do the arrangements above prevent that gilded cage or merely soften it? Honestly, on their own, most only soften it: dividends, referenda, transparency, and rights-on-paper raise comfort and voice without moving the switches. The cage genuinely opens only where the check becomes structural — where ownership and operational control of the base and its allocating algorithm actually pass into democratic or commons hands, so that shutting provision off or rigging the formula lies within no private party's power. It stays gilded-but-locked wherever provision remains a gift from owners who keep the off-switch, however generous the gift. The distinguishing condition is not the size of the dividend but the location of the switches.

## What Actually Decides Which Future Obtains

Set the two societies side by side and the deepest lesson is one neither scenario states alone: the presence of the technology decides almost nothing. Full automation is held constant across both futures, and even the single distinguishing variable — whether a non-wage channel exists — turns out to be less determinative than it first appears. Scenario A can harden into repressive enclosure or settle into a durable neo-feudal calm bought with revocable patronage; Scenario B can flower into chosen work and self-government or ossify into a comfortable, powerless dependency administered by whoever owns the code. The same machines underwrite both the promise and the peril in each. What separates the good version of either future from its bad twin is not the level of abundance but the disposition of power — and two levers, running through both scenarios, govern that disposition.

The first is who controls the automated productive base. In every branch we traced, the load-bearing question reduced to the location of the switches: who can turn provision off, rewrite the allocation formula, and set the terms of access. Where those switches sit with a narrow set of owners — cloud-capital lords in A, or a firm-and-algorithm elite behind a paper democracy in B — the outcome tends toward rent-extraction and a leverageless majority, whether that majority is abandoned or pampered. Where control over the base is genuinely distributed, so that no private party alone holds the off-switch, the historic check that labour once supplied is reconstituted in a new form. Redistribution moves income downstream; only the distribution of ownership and operational control moves power. That is why the abundance scenario's optimism is hollow without a theory of who rules the commons, and why the scarcity scenario's fate hangs on whether any counterweight to the owners survives.

The second lever is the scale at which governance is constituted. "Universal" is always universal-within-a-boundary, and the boundary is not a detail but a determinant. A bounded, wealthy, automated polity can provision its own members lavishly while the switches stay in private hands and the excluded press at the border; a genuinely global order changes both who counts as "everyone" and whom the displaced can appeal to. Whether the effective governing unit turns out to be the nation-state, a supranational order, or the owners of the base themselves — or some hybrid of the three — shapes every downstream answer: where the surplus population goes in A, who is inside the provision in B, and against whom, if anyone, concentrated power can be checked. The rising global population presses on this axis from both sides at once, deepening the surplus problem where nothing is shared and testing per-capita sustainability where everything is.

None of this yields a prophecy, and it should not. These are two internally consistent maps of a plural possibility space, not a forecast of the one future that will arrive; a real trajectory may braid them together or diverge from both. But the maps agree on where to look. If we want to know which future we are drifting toward, we should watch not how advanced the machines become — that is stipulated in every branch — but who comes to own and operate the automated base, and at what scale the authority over it is constituted and checked. Technology sets the stage; those two levers write the play.
