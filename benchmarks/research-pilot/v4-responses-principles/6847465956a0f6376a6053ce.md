# The Health Benefits of Outdoor Exposure and Vitamin D
### A public-health research report for the general public

---

# Executive Summary

Sunlight and time outdoors shape our health in two very different ways — and telling them apart is the heart of this report. Some benefits come from the Vitamin D your skin makes from sunlight. Others come from simply being outside, and happen even when your Vitamin D level never changes. A pill can replace the first kind. It cannot replace the second. This summary gives you the whole picture: what the science shows, and what we advise. For your own situation, talk with a clinician.

## Key Findings (what the research shows)

- **Vitamin D's strongest, best-proven job is protecting your bones.** It lets your gut absorb calcium and helps that calcium harden into your skeleton. Severe shortage causes rickets (soft, bowed bones in children) and osteomalacia (soft, aching bones in adults), and it adds to the risk of fractures from thinned bone in later life.
- **Being outdoors helps you in ways that do not depend on Vitamin D at all.** Sunlight (UVA) prompts your skin to release nitric oxide, which relaxes blood vessels and lowers blood pressure — with no change in your blood Vitamin D level. Time in nature lowers stress hormones and slows the heart within minutes, far too fast to be Vitamin D. And children who get at least about 2 hours of daylight a day are less likely to become nearsighted, through a light signal in the eye, not Vitamin D.
- **Vitamin D pills are not proven to prevent cancer or heart disease.** The largest trial to date, VITAL (about 25,871 people), found no drop in invasive cancer or major heart events. Do not take supplements expecting to ward off these diseases.
- **Needs and risks differ by group.** Breastfed infants, adults over 65, pregnant and lactating women, people with darker skin, people with obesity, and older men each face a distinct, measured benefit or risk — detailed later, with numbers.

## Key Recommendations (what to do)

- **Aim for the recommended daily amount.** Health authorities advise about 600 IU (International Units) per day for ages 1–70, and 800 IU per day after 70. Roughly 4,000 IU per day is a safety ceiling, not a target — do not mega-dose.
- **If you buy a supplement, Vitamin D3 is generally the more reliable choice** than D2 for raising and keeping up your blood level. Both work.
- **Do not rely on the sun as your planned Vitamin D source.** The same sunlight (UVB) that makes Vitamin D also causes skin cancer, and dermatologists do not recommend the sun or tanning beds for it. How much sun makes Vitamin D depends on skin tone, latitude, season, time of day, how much skin is bare, and age — and above about 35° latitude, winter sun is too weak to help. Never burn.
- **Food is a real alternative or complement.** Fatty fish, egg yolks, and fortified foods (in the U.S., especially Vitamin-D-fortified milk) all supply it; about 400 IU a day is enough to prevent rickets and osteomalacia.
- **Most healthy adults do not need a Vitamin D blood test.** Routine screening is not advised; testing is reasonable for certain medical conditions or symptoms — discuss it with a clinician.

## Risks at Both Extremes, and the Uncertainty

Too little Vitamin D harms bones; too much sun raises skin-cancer risk, and too many pills can cause toxicity (dangerously high blood calcium). The safe path is the middle: meet your needs through food and sensible supplements, protect your skin outdoors, and avoid mega-doses. Finally, this science is still evolving. Experts genuinely disagree on the right intake, on where "deficiency" begins (above 30 versus about 20 ng/mL on the blood test), and on benefits beyond bone. The bone science is solid; these edges are not — and this guidance may change as we learn more.

# How to Read This Report

## What we're sure of — and what we're not

Some things about Vitamin D are settled. Your body needs it to build strong bones. A serious lack of it can genuinely harm your health. This report treats that core as solid ground.

But the science is still moving. Experts do not yet agree on everything. Three things are honestly unsettled:

- **How much you need.** The daily amount, measured in units called IU, is still debated.
- **What counts as "too low."** There is a blood test that measures your Vitamin D level. Expert groups draw the line between "deficient" and "enough" at different points.
- **Benefits beyond bones.** Claims about immunity, mood, the heart, and cancer are weaker and more mixed than the bone-health evidence.

We do not pick a winner in these debates. We flag them, so you can see where the ground is firm and where it is soft. None of this means "do nothing." Useful, practical guidance still holds. We simply mark what is well-proven and what may change as we learn more.

## How to tell "what science shows" from "what to do"

This report keeps two things apart, in every section:

- **What the research shows** — the facts. These are what studies actually observed. They rest on peer-reviewed evidence.
- **What to do** — the advice. This is guidance built on those facts. It also involves judgment and expert opinion, and it could reasonably change.

Think of it as two layers. The advice sits on top of the facts. It is one step further from certainty. As new studies arrive, the advice is more likely to shift than the facts beneath it. You will see these two headings, word for word, throughout the report. Once you know them, you can always tell a fact from a piece of advice.

# Sunlight and Vitamin D: How They're Connected

**What the research shows**

Your skin is a small factory. When one type of sunlight (UVB) lands on it, your skin turns a starting material — a cholesterol-like molecule called 7-dehydrocholesterol — into Vitamin D3. This is why sunlight and Vitamin D are always discussed together. For most of human history, the sun was the main source of the vitamin.

But this factory has limits, and those limits are the reason the rest of this report exists. The same UVB that makes Vitamin D also damages skin and causes skin cancer. The factory slows and stops on its own: Vitamin D synthesis is most efficient at only about **one-third of one MED** (the "minimal erythemal dose," the amount of sun that just barely reddens your skin), and it **halts at about one full MED** [Refs 12, 11]. In plain terms — your skin makes the most Vitamin D from short, sub-sunburn amounts of sun, and burning yourself does not add more.

The factory also shuts down for months at a time in winter at higher latitudes, and it works far more slowly in some people than others. Because of all this, the sun is a real but unreliable and risky source. The rest of this report walks through the numbers — how much sun, how much supplement, and the two forms of the pill — and then the risks at both ends.

*A note on the blood test: the "Vitamin D level in your blood" is measured as 25(OH)D. Experts disagree on where "too low" begins — see "What Counts as Deficient?" below.*

*First, the part of the science we are most sure about: your bones.*

# What Vitamin D Reliably Does for Your Bones

**What the research shows**

Vitamin D's strongest, best-proven job is building and protecting your bones. Decades of research have settled this. Other possible effects get less certain attention elsewhere in this report. But the bone story is the one scientists are most sure about.

It starts with calcium. Calcium is the mineral that makes bones hard and strong. Your body cannot pull much calcium out of the food you eat unless you have enough Vitamin D. Think of Vitamin D as a key. Without that key, the door in your gut stays shut, and most of the calcium in your meal passes straight through you. With the key, your intestine can absorb the calcium and send it into your blood.

Vitamin D does a second job, too. It helps your body lay that calcium down into bone. This is called mineralization — the process of hardening bone with minerals like calcium. So Vitamin D works at both ends: it lets calcium in, and it helps calcium settle into your skeleton.

Bone is not dead scaffolding. It is living tissue. Your body breaks down and rebuilds it all the time. That constant upkeep needs a steady supply of calcium, and that supply needs Vitamin D.

When Vitamin D runs very low for a long time, clear diseases appear.

In children, severe deficiency causes **rickets**. This is the classic disease that first linked Vitamin D to bone health. A child's bones are still growing. Without enough Vitamin D, those growing bones turn soft. They can bend and bow under the child's weight, which leads to bowed legs and other lasting deformities in the skeleton.

In adults, the same shortage causes **osteomalacia** — the adult version, in which fully grown bone loses mineral and turns soft. Grown bones stop lengthening, so they do not bow like a child's. Instead they ache, weaken, and break more easily.

Vitamin D deficiency also plays a part in **osteoporotic fractures** — broken bones caused by weak, thinned bone, most often in older people. Here the wording matters. Low Vitamin D contributes to these fractures. It is one cause among several, alongside aging and naturally low bone density. It is not the only cause, and Vitamin D on its own does not prevent or cure this bone thinning. It is one important piece of a larger picture.

So the pattern is steady across a lifetime: children face rickets, adults face osteomalacia, and older adults face a higher risk of fractures. Strong bones depend on Vitamin D.

*Bones are the Vitamin D story. But sunlight and the outdoors do more for you than make Vitamin D — and these next benefits happen even when your Vitamin D level never moves.*

# What Being Outdoors Does for You — Beyond Vitamin D

Sunlight and time outside help your body in ways that have nothing to do with Vitamin D. The Vitamin D level in your blood can stay flat, and you still gain. Being outdoors is good for you through channels that have nothing to do with Vitamin D. Here are three clear examples.

**What the research shows**

**1. Sunlight can lower your blood pressure — and not through Vitamin D.**
Your skin stores a molecule called nitric oxide. It tells blood vessels to relax. One kind of sunlight, called UVA (a type of sunlight), releases that stored nitric oxide into your blood. Your vessels widen, and your blood pressure drops a little. Controlled studies in people show this effect. Here is the key point. It happens without raising the Vitamin D level in your blood (the marker doctors measure, called 25(OH)D). The marker does not move, but the blood pressure does. So this is a sunlight effect on your skin and blood vessels. It is not a Vitamin D effect.

**2. Time in nature calms your body within minutes — far too fast to be Vitamin D.**
Spending time in green space, like a park or woods, lowers your stress. Studies show a drop in cortisol (your main stress hormone) and a slower heart rate. The change starts within minutes. Vitamin D takes hours or days to form and act in the body. So this quick calming cannot be Vitamin D. The speed is the proof. Something else is at work — the sights, sounds, and calm of nature acting on your nervous system.

**3. Daily daylight protects children's eyesight — through the eye, not Vitamin D.**
Children who spend at least 2 hours a day in outdoor daylight are less likely to become nearsighted. Nearsighted means far-away things look blurry, and doctors call it myopia. Bright outdoor light makes the back of the eye (the retina) release a signal called dopamine. This retinal dopamine helps the eyeball grow to the right shape, which keeps sight sharp. Studies that follow schoolchildren over time support this. It is a light-in-the-eye effect. Vitamin D plays no role here.

**The thread that ties these together:** each benefit runs on its own track. Blood pressure falls even though the blood Vitamin D marker stays flat. Stress drops far too fast for Vitamin D to explain. Children's eyes respond to bright light, not to Vitamin D. Being outdoors helps you even when your Vitamin D level does not change.

*For simple steps that follow from these findings — including getting daily time outside, and about 2 hours of daylight for children — see "How Much Sun?" and "The Two Extremes to Avoid." You can gain these benefits in shade and with sun protection; they do not require unprotected UVB dosing.*

*So sunlight gives you two kinds of payoff at once. Here is how to tell them apart at a glance — and why it matters for what you do next.*

# Same Sunshine, Two Different Pathways

**What the research shows**

It is easy to assume that everything good about sunshine comes from Vitamin D. It does not. Sunlight is a single upstream cause, but it branches into two very different downstream pathways. One pathway runs *through your skin making Vitamin D* — a slow, chemical process that changes your blood chemistry over hours and days. The other pathways act *directly*, the moment light hits your skin and eyes — relaxing blood vessels, calming your nervous system, and shaping how a child's eyes grow. These second pathways do not involve Vitamin D at all. Same sunlight, two separate payoffs.

Here is the split, side by side:

| Benefits from Vitamin D itself | Benefits of being outdoors that do NOT depend on Vitamin D |
|---|---|
| **Absorbing calcium.** Vitamin D lets your gut take in calcium from food — without it, most calcium passes straight through. | **Lower blood pressure.** A type of sunlight called UVA prompts your skin to release stored nitric oxide, a molecule that relaxes blood vessels. Your blood pressure drops — *even though the Vitamin D level in your blood does not change.* |
| **Building bone.** Vitamin D helps that calcium harden into your skeleton (mineralization). | **Fast stress relief.** Time in nature lowers cortisol (your main stress hormone) and slows your heart rate within *minutes* — far too fast for Vitamin D. |
| **Preventing bone-softening disease.** Enough Vitamin D prevents rickets and osteomalacia, and helps guard against fractures from thinned bone in later life. | **Protecting children's eyesight.** At least about 2 hours of daylight a day protects children from becoming nearsighted (myopia), through dopamine released in the retina (the back of the eye) — not Vitamin D. |

The left column holds Vitamin D's strongest, best-proven work: bones and calcium. This is the tier of the science we are most confident about. (Other possible Vitamin D effects — on mood or immunity, for example — are far less certain and are treated cautiously elsewhere in this report.) The right column holds benefits that happen *regardless* of what your Vitamin D level does. Each one carries its own proof of independence: the blood marker stays flat, the effect is too fast, or the action is in the eye — not the skeleton.

**What this means for you**

The practical upshot is simple, and it changes how you should think about a Vitamin D pill. A supplement can restore the *left column* — it can top up the Vitamin D your bones need. But a pill cannot lower your blood pressure through your skin, cannot calm your nervous system, and cannot protect a child's developing eyes. Those benefits come from *being outside*, not from a capsule.

That is why "just take a supplement" is an incomplete public-health message. The very same act — getting outdoor light on your skin — drives the Vitamin D pathway *and* the independent pathways at the same time. A supplement covers only one branch of the fork. (How much sun, how much supplement, and how to do it safely — including sun protection — are covered in their own sections.)

*The science above is clear about what Vitamin D does. It is just as clear about something it does not do.*

# What Vitamin D Does NOT Do: Cancer and Heart Disease

**What the research shows**

Many people take Vitamin D pills hoping to fend off cancer or heart disease. The best evidence today does not back this up. Taking Vitamin D supplements has not been shown to prevent cancer or heart and blood-vessel (cardiovascular) disease. The strongest test so far is a large randomized controlled trial (a study that sorts people into groups by chance) called VITAL. It followed 25,871 people. Those who took Vitamin D had no fewer invasive cancers, and no fewer major heart events such as heart attacks and strokes, than those who took a dummy pill instead. So do not count on high-dose supplements to guard against these diseases. That benefit simply has not been proven. Vitamin D still matters for other reasons, such as keeping bones strong.

One important limit on this finding: VITAL tested *prevention in generally healthy people who mostly already had enough Vitamin D*. It does not speak to *treating* someone with a diagnosed deficiency — that is a separate question, and correcting a real shortage still matters. Do not read the null result as "Vitamin D never matters."

---

*If supplements won't prevent those diseases, a fair next question is: what even counts as "low" Vitamin D? The answer is less settled than you might expect.*

# What Counts as "Deficient"? The Moving Cutoff

**What the research shows**

There is no single agreed number for vitamin D "deficiency" (not having enough). Doctors check your level with a blood test called 25(OH)D (the vitamin D level measured in your blood). The result comes in ng/mL (nanograms per milliliter, the unit on the lab report). But experts draw the line in different places. The Endocrine Society says "sufficiency" (having enough) means a level above 30 ng/mL. The Institute of Medicine sets its bar lower, at about 20 ng/mL. So the same result can look fine under one standard and too low under another. Someone near the line might be called deficient by one guideline but not the other. The line you pick changes who counts as deficient. A higher bar (above 30 ng/mL) labels more people deficient. A lower bar (about 20 ng/mL) labels fewer. This gap is one reason later advice can differ.

Worth knowing: the 2024 Endocrine Society guideline itself declined to set a target 25(OH)D number for the general public, and does not urge routine testing (next section). So do not treat either cutoff as *the* official line to chase.

*Given that experts disagree on the cutoff, should you get tested at all?*

# Should You Get a Vitamin D Blood Test?

For most healthy adults, the short answer is no. Routine testing is not recommended.

**What the research shows**

The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline recommends against routinely testing the blood marker doctors use to gauge Vitamin D status (25-hydroxyvitamin D, or 25(OH)D) in healthy people. Its exact words: it "suggest[s] against routine testing for 25-hydroxyvitamin D levels in any of the populations studied, since outcome-specific benefits based on these levels have not been identified." When the expert panel reviewed the evidence, it could not find a clear health payoff from screening the general population. So it advises against it.

Be clear about what kind of statement this is. This is a recommendation, not a settled biological fact. It is expert judgment drawn from the evidence, and it could shift as we learn more. The panel was honest that a lot here remains uncertain — and it graded this advice "suggest" (conditional/weak), not "recommend" (strong).

A big reason for the advice is that experts do not agree on where "too low" begins (see "What Counts as Deficient?" above). The Endocrine Society treats a blood level above 30 ng/mL as having enough. The Institute of Medicine uses about 20 ng/mL. So a single result that lands in the gap can be called "deficient" under one standard and "fine" under the other. The same number, two verdicts. Testing healthy people invites that confusion without a clear benefit.

The guideline looked at generally healthy adults. It did not hand out a checklist of who should be tested. People with medical conditions that change how the body handles Vitamin D sit outside that "healthy adult" picture — for example, poor gut absorption, kidney disease, or bone disease such as osteoporosis; certain medicines; or older or housebound people who get very little sun. For those situations, a test may be worthwhile. But that is a judgment call, not a blanket rule.

One more point that prevents a common mix-up. The guideline suggests some groups **supplement** without ever **testing** — for example, children and teens (1–18), adults 75 and older, pregnant people, and adults with high-risk prediabetes. So supplementing does not require a blood test first.

**What to do**

If you are a healthy adult, you probably do not need a Vitamin D blood test. If you have a health condition, take medicines that affect Vitamin D, or have symptoms that worry you, talk it over with a clinician. They can decide, with you, whether a test makes sense for your situation.

# How Much Sun? (And What Changes It)

This is the question most people ask: how long should I be in the sun? There is no single answer. The time changes with your skin tone, where you live, the season, the time of day, how much skin is bare, and your age. Below we first show what the research measured. Then we tell you what to do with it.

**Read this first — the sun trade-off.**
UVB is a type of sunlight (the part that reaches your skin's surface). It does two opposite things at once. It lets your skin make Vitamin D. It also causes skin cancer. These are the same rays. Because of this, experts say there is no proven "safe" sun dose that gives you the most Vitamin D without raising skin-cancer risk (American Academy of Dermatology). So the American Academy of Dermatology does not recommend getting Vitamin D from the sun or from tanning beds. We never recommend tanning beds. And you must never burn to make Vitamin D — avoiding sunburn is not optional. The minutes below describe what sun studies measured. They are not permission to sunbathe.

One more note. Our main guideline — the 2024 Endocrine Society guideline — gives no sun-exposure minutes at all. It points people to diet and supplements instead. The minutes below come from separate sun-and-skin studies, which we name as we go. And one caution: sun "minutes" and supplement "IU" are not a simple swap. The sun studies below aim at different targets (some at 400 IU, some at 1,000 IU, some at "maintenance," some at a fraction of a sunburn dose), so you cannot cleanly trade a number of minutes for a pill dose.

**What the research shows**

**Skin tone (Fitzpatrick skin type).** "Fitzpatrick skin type" is a 1-to-6 scale of how skin reacts to sun. Type I is very fair skin that burns fast. Type VI is deep brown or black skin. Melanin (the pigment that darkens skin) blocks some UVB. So darker skin needs more time for the same amount of Vitamin D. In one 2024 model of the Mediterranean basin, using the 10:00–16:00 window and a 1,000 IU/day target, fair skin (types I–IV) needed about **5.05 minutes** as an annual average and the darkest skin (type VI) about **25.25 minutes** (Scientific Reports 2024) [Ref 15]. Another 2024 global model found the darkest skin needs roughly **4 times** the time of fair skin at the same place and season (Nutrients 2024) [Ref 10]. Reviews put the contrast even more sharply in some settings: light skin (types I–II) can reach a blood level above 50 nmol/L from about **30 minutes a day** of summer sun at a Boston-type latitude, while dark skin (types V–VI) may need **up to about 2 hours** for the same result; type II skin can synthesize roughly **30 times faster** than type V under matched conditions [Ref 12]. A separate equivalency model for type III skin (25.5% of the body bare, targeting 400 IU) estimated about **3–8 minutes** at solar noon in Boston (April–October) and about **3–6 minutes** in Miami [Ref 22 — see Verification notes].

**Latitude and season, together.** Latitude is how far you live from the equator. The farther from the equator you go, and the deeper into winter, the lower the sun sits in the sky — and the weaker the UVB. Here is one clear-sky, midday model (Nutrients 2024) [Ref 10]. Read it as "about":

| Place and season | Fair skin (types I–IV) | Darker skin (type V) |
|---|---|---|
| 40°N, June (summer) | about 4 min | about 11 min |
| 40°N, March (spring) | about 7 min | about 18 min |
| 50°N, June (summer) | about 5–6 min | about 12–13 min |
| 50°N, March (spring) | about 12 min | about 31 min |

Winter is the bigger problem. Above about 35° latitude, winter sun is too weak to make much Vitamin D at all. Scientists call this a "Vitamin D winter." In Boston (42.2°N), the skin makes almost none from about November to February. Farther north, in Edmonton (52°N), the gap runs from about October to March (Webb and Holick 1988) [Ref 13]. Across Europe (from about 35°N to 69°N), this dead season lasts from about **2 to 8 months**, roughly from 37°N up to 69°N, depending on how far north you live [Refs 23]. In global clear-sky modeling, beyond about ±40° latitude the midday UV index can drop below 2 — a "Vitamin D winter" for every skin type — in some months [Ref 10].

A Canadian study looked at real summer sun at 13 sites (CMAJ Open 2015) [Ref 14]. In summer, type II skin with about a quarter of the body bare could reach a full 1,000 IU daily dose at all 13 test sites. But in fall and winter, and at the northern sites, the sun was too weak for any skin tone. As a real-world footnote, even sunscreen (SPF-15) users on high-UV summer vacations still raised their blood Vitamin D by about **13.0 to 19.0 nmol/L** [Ref 12] — some sun gets through everyday protection.

**Time of day.** The sun works best when it is high in the sky, near midday. High sun means the rays pass through less air (a smaller "solar zenith angle"), so more UVB reaches you; efficient synthesis happens when the sun sits fairly high, at an elevation of roughly 45° or more. Studies point to a rough 10 a.m. to 4 p.m. window (JBMR Plus 2021; Scientific Reports 2024) [Refs 16, 15]. *Information gap:* we did not find a solid peer-reviewed figure, in minutes, for something like 9 a.m. versus noon. Studies describe this by the sun's angle, not by an exact minute count.

**How much skin is bare (body-surface-area, or BSA).** "Body-surface-area" simply means the share of your skin that is uncovered. More bare skin makes more Vitamin D in the same time. As a rough rule, doubling the bare skin about halves the time you need. Bare face and hands (about **5%** of your skin) can be enough in strong sun; about **15%** bare skin can reach a high-normal blood level (review) [Ref 12]. The exact BSA fraction differs by study, which is one reason the minute-figures above are not directly interchangeable: the type-III model used **25.5%** BSA [Ref 22], the Canadian model compared about **¼ versus ⅛** of the body [Ref 14], and the controlled-dosing systematic review used arms of **24%** and **35%** BSA [Ref 11]. That same review boiled the dose–response into an equation — Δ25(OH)D (nmol/L) = 3.6 × ln(dose) + 2.5, explaining about two-thirds of the variation (r² = 0.66) [Ref 11]. *Information gap:* there is no single agreed closed-form formula for BSA in the literature. Studies report this as a rough "inverse" pattern, not one precise equation.

**Age.** Older skin makes less Vitamin D from the same sun. This is because aging skin holds less of the starting material (called 7-dehydrocholesterol) that sunlight turns into Vitamin D. But the size of the drop is genuinely contested. A classic 1985 study found skin 7-dehydrocholesterol falls by roughly **50% from age 20 to age 80**, with a **more than 4-fold** smaller blood response to standard UVB in people aged 62–80 versus 20–30 (MacLaughlin and Holick, Journal of Clinical Investigation 1985) [Ref 17]. A secondary estimate puts the skin's making-capacity at age 65 at only about **25%** of a 20-to-30-year-old's for the same radiation [Ref 19]. A 2020 single-bout sun study found about a **13% drop per decade** — age explained about **20%** of the differences between people, and production by about age 70 was roughly **half** that of a 20-year-old (older adults peaked at 7.7 ± 5.7 versus younger adults' 11.2 ± 8.8 nmol/L; ages 20–37 vs 51–69) (Nutrients 2020) [Ref 4]. But a 2024 Manchester study found **little or no difference** — similar baseline skin 7-dehydrocholesterol and a similar response to UV — directly challenging the 50% figure (Nutrients 2024) [Ref 18]. So the direction is likely downward with age, but the exact amount is unsettled.

**The pattern.** Every one of these pushes the needed time UP: darker skin, higher latitude, winter, early or late hours, less bare skin, and older age. Stack several together and the sun may not be a workable source at all.

**What to do**

- **Do not rely on the sun as your planned Vitamin D source.** The dermatology view is clear: get Vitamin D from food and supplements, not from sun or tanning beds. See "How Much to Supplement?" for the actual numbers.
- **Never use tanning beds for Vitamin D.** Their bulbs give off mostly UVA, which barely makes Vitamin D but still raises cancer risk.
- **Never burn.** If your skin starts to turn pink, you have already had too much. Sunburn brings risk, not extra benefit — remember, synthesis halts at about one full sunburn dose.
- **Expect winter to give you little or none.** At mid-latitudes above about 35°, plan on food and supplements from roughly late fall through early spring.
- **Know your own factors.** If you have darker skin, are older, or keep most skin covered, the sun is an even weaker source for you. Lean on supplements.
- **Protect your skin for any longer time outside** — shade, clothing, and sunscreen. You can still enjoy being outdoors (see "Beyond Vitamin D") while protecting your skin. Sun protection does not cancel the other gains of being outside.
- **For your own situation, ask a clinician.** This is general guidance, not a personal prescription.

*Because the sun is an unreliable and risky source, supplements are the steadier route. Here is how much.*

# How Much to Supplement? (Daily IU)

How much vitamin D you should take each day is not a settled question. Expert groups have studied the same evidence and still landed in different places. That is exactly why there is no single, agreed "consensus number." Everything below is a **recommendation** — a body's best advice from evolving evidence. It is not a fixed fact of nature, and it could change as new studies arrive.

**What the research shows**

- The IOM (Institute of Medicine, part of the National Academies) set official intake targets. Its Recommended Dietary Allowance — the RDA, meaning the daily amount that meets most people's needs — is **600 IU** (International Units) per day for ages 1 to 70, and **800 IU** per day for adults over 70. *(NIH Office of Dietary Supplements, citing the IOM Dietary Reference Intakes.)* [Ref 2]
- The same IOM set a Tolerable Upper Intake Level — the UL, meaning a safety ceiling — of about **4,000 IU** per day for adults. This is a limit, not a goal. *(NIH Office of Dietary Supplements, citing the IOM Dietary Reference Intakes.)* [Ref 2]
- In 2024 the Endocrine Society changed its earlier position. It used to point toward higher doses. Now it "suggest[s] against vitamin D supplements at doses beyond the reference dietary intakes" for healthy adults under 75 — in other words, most healthy adults should simply follow the RDA above. *(Demay MB and colleagues, Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, 2024.)* [Ref 1]
- The guideline names exceptions for whom it suggests supplementing above the RDA: children and adolescents (1–18, to prevent nutritional rickets), adults **75 and older** (to lower mortality risk), pregnant people, and adults with high-risk prediabetes. The exact daily IU for these groups is not firmly pinned down — the trials behind the advice used a range of doses.
- So two respected bodies once differed, and now sit closer together. But this is a recent reversal, not a locked-in agreement — which is why the "right" daily number stays contested.

**What to do**

- If you are a generally healthy adult, aim for the RDA: about **600 IU** per day up to age 70, and about **800 IU** per day after 70. Both bodies now point this way.
- Do not treat **4,000 IU** per day as a target. It is the upper limit — the most you should take without a clinician's advice. This section is not telling you to start high doses.
- You can reach these numbers with either form of the vitamin: Vitamin D2 or Vitamin D3. For the plain difference between the two forms, see the next section.
- If you fall into an exception group (a young child, an adult 75+, pregnant, or with high-risk prediabetes), a clinician can help you choose a dose — and remember, supplementing does not require a blood test first.

*If you do take a supplement, you'll face one choice on the label: D2 or D3.*

# Vitamin D2 vs. Vitamin D3 — The Plain Difference

Vitamin D supplements come in two forms: D2 and D3. Both work. But they are not the same, and the difference matters when you pick a bottle off the shelf.

D2 is called ergocalciferol (the plant- and fungus-based form). It comes from sources like yeast and mushrooms exposed to UV light.

D3 is called cholecalciferol (the animal-based form). It comes from oily fish, and your own skin makes it from sunlight. If you avoid animal products, you can still get D3. A vegan version is made from lichen, a plant-like organism. So D3 is not always off-limits for vegans.

**What the research shows**

The main way to judge these forms is how well they raise your serum 25(OH)D (the blood test that shows your Vitamin D level). Here is how they compare, point by point:

| | Vitamin D2 (ergocalciferol) | Vitamin D3 (cholecalciferol) |
|---|---|---|
| Where it comes from | Plants and fungi (yeast, UV-treated mushrooms) | Animals (oily fish; skin-made from sun); vegan D3 from lichen |
| Raising your blood Vitamin D level | Raises it, but less effectively | Raises it more effectively |
| With a large, occasional dose (bolus dosing — one big dose now and then, not daily) | Weaker effect | Clearly stronger effect |
| How long the effect lasts | Shorter | Lasts longer |
| Shelf life in the bottle | Stays potent for less time | Stays stable and potent longer |

Randomized trials and reviews of many studies have found that D3 raises the blood Vitamin D level more than D2. In one well-known 2011 trial, D3 raised serum 25(OH)D about **56% to 87% more** than an equal dose of D2, and was more than **3 times** as effective at building up the body's fat-stored Vitamin D (Heaney, Armas and colleagues, Journal of Clinical Endocrinology & Metabolism, 2011) [Ref 20]. The gap is widest with large, occasional (bolus) doses. Other trials and reviews find the two forms much closer to equal at everyday maintenance doses (Tripkovic and colleagues) [Ref 21] — so this is a directional advantage, not a fixed multiplier.

To be clear, D2 is not weak or unsafe. It still works, and it is a useful option. D3 is simply the more powerful and more stable of the two.

**What to do**

If you buy a supplement, D3 is generally the more reliable choice for raising and keeping up your blood Vitamin D level. (How much to take is covered in "How Much to Supplement?" above.)

---

*Beyond pills, everyday food can carry you a long way too.*

# Getting Vitamin D from Food and Fortified Products

Sunlight is not the only way to get vitamin D. Food works too. For many people, food and fortified products are a real, reliable way to meet basic vitamin D needs — either instead of sun, or alongside it.

**What the research shows**

- Some foods are naturally rich in vitamin D. Fatty fish carry the most.
- A few everyday foods give smaller, useful amounts.
- Many common products are "fortified," which means vitamin D is added during processing. In the United States, vitamin-D-fortified milk is the classic example.
- About **400 IU** of vitamin D a day is enough to prevent the main deficiency diseases. Those are rickets (soft, weak bones in children) and osteomalacia (soft, weakened bones in adults). Think of 400 IU as the floor that keeps bones from going soft — not a peak or ideal target.

Good food sources include:

- **Fatty fish** — such as salmon, mackerel, and sardines (the richest sources).
- **Egg yolk** — a smaller, everyday source.
- **Fortified foods** — especially U.S. vitamin-D-fortified milk, plus many fortified cereals and other products.

**What to do**

- Build a few vitamin D foods into your week. Try to include fatty fish, eggs, and fortified milk or similar products.
- If you get little sun, lean more on food and fortified products to help fill the gap.

*So far we've spoken to the average adult. But needs change with age, sex, skin tone, and body size.*

# Different People, Different Needs (by Age and Sex)

Vitamin D and sunlight do not treat everyone the same. Your age, your sex, your skin tone, and your body size all change how much Vitamin D you make and how much you need. Below are six groups that the research singles out. Each one carries its own risk. This section reports **what the research shows**. It does not hand out doses — those appear in "How Much Sun?" and "How Much to Supplement?"

## Breastfed infants

Breast milk is one of the best foods for a baby. But it carries very little Vitamin D. Human milk supplies only about **25 to 78 IU per liter** (below 0.6–2.0 micrograms per liter) — far below what a baby needs (NIH Office of Dietary Supplements, 2024) [Ref 2].

Because of this gap, a fully or partly breastfed baby can run low on Vitamin D. Over time, that raises the risk of rickets — soft, weak growing bones. To close the gap, the American Academy of Pediatrics advises **400 IU** of Vitamin D a day for these infants, starting soon after birth (Wagner & Greer, American Academy of Pediatrics, 2008, Pediatrics) [Ref 3].

**Watch this:** the low Vitamin D in breast milk is a property of the milk itself. It is not a sign that anything is wrong with the mother or the baby.

## Adults over about 65

As skin ages, it makes less Vitamin D from sunlight. Experts call this cutaneous synthesis — your skin making Vitamin D from sunlight. Older skin holds less of the raw material the process needs.

One controlled sun-exposure study measured the drop. It found a **13% fall in Vitamin D3 production for each decade of life**. Age alone explained about **20%** of the differences between people (Chalcraft et al., 2020, Nutrients) [Ref 4]. So the same time in the sun gives an older adult less Vitamin D than it gives a younger one. (As the "How Much Sun?" section notes, the exact size of this age-related decline is contested — a 2024 study found little difference — so treat the 13%-per-decade figure as one estimate, not a settled law.)

**Watch this:** this decline is about aging skin. It is separate from the male-specific finding for older men further down.

## Pregnant and lactating women

A mother's Vitamin D level matters for her newborn. When a mother is low, her baby is far more likely to be born with hypocalcemia — low blood calcium, which can trigger problems such as seizures.

A 2023 review pooled 11 studies (452 newborns with hypocalcemia versus 2,599 without). It found that mothers who were deficient in Vitamin D had newborns with much higher odds of hypocalcemia — an odds ratio of **7.83 (95% CI 3.62–16.92)** (Li et al., 2023, Journal of Pediatric Endocrinology and Metabolism) [Ref 5]. Newborns who were themselves deficient also had higher odds, at **2.87 (95% CI 1.17–7.04)**.

Some research also links low Vitamin D in pregnancy to a higher chance of preeclampsia — a dangerous rise in blood pressure during pregnancy (about OR **1.58, 95% CI 1.39–1.79**; BMC Pregnancy and Childbirth, 2024) [Ref 6]. This link is an association, not proof of cause.

**Watch this:** the neonatal low-calcium finding is the strongest, best-checked point here. The preeclampsia link is weaker, supporting evidence.

## People with darker skin

Skin pigment, called melanin, acts like a natural sunscreen. It blocks some of the UVB — a type of sunlight (UVB) — that the skin uses to make Vitamin D. So darker skin needs more sun time to make the same amount.

The measured gap is large. In a national U.S. survey (NHANES 2001–2010) of people not taking supplements, Vitamin D deficiency (below 20 ng/mL) was found in **75% of non-Hispanic Black adults, 44% of Hispanic adults, and 20% of non-Hispanic White adults** (Ames, Grant & Willett, 2021, Nutrients) [Ref 7]. Severe deficiency (below 10 ng/mL) was even more lopsided: **17% among African Americans versus 1% among European Americans** — a **15- to 20-fold** difference.

**Watch this:** a lower blood level in darker-skinned people is real. But experts caution that its full health meaning is still not clear (NIH Office of Dietary Supplements, 2024) [Ref 2].

## People with obesity

People with obesity often show lower Vitamin D in their blood. The main reason looks simple: the same Vitamin D is spread thin across a larger body. Researchers call this volumetric dilution.

A study of **686 adults** not taking supplements tested this idea. A model based on body weight "completely removed" the obesity-related part of the differences in blood Vitamin D. The authors concluded that spreading the vitamin across body size — not fat "trapping" it — best explains the low levels, and that intake "needs to be adjusted for body size" (Drincic et al., 2012, Obesity) [Ref 8].

**Watch this:** because the vitamin is spread across a bigger body, a person with obesity may need a larger intake to reach the same blood level (see "How Much to Supplement?").

## Elderly men

Older men show a sex-specific link worth noting: Vitamin D status tracks with testosterone, the main male sex hormone.

In a study of **1,406 older men** (mean age about 65, and 87.4% of them low in Vitamin D), a 10 ng/mL drop in blood Vitamin D went with a **0.95 nmol/L lower testosterone (95% CI −1.46 to −0.45; p<0.001)** and a **26% higher risk of low testosterone** (total testosterone below 12 nmol/L) (Miao et al., 2025, Frontiers in Endocrinology) [Ref 9]. Important caveat: this link faded once the researchers accounted for age and body weight. So it is an association, and body fat may be the real driver.

**Watch this:** this is a link, not proof that Vitamin D raises testosterone in older men.

*With those groups in mind, here is the big-picture safety message that ties the whole report together: avoid both extremes.*

# The Two Extremes to Avoid: Too Little and Too Much

Vitamin D works best in the right amount, from the right source. Too little causes real harm. So does too much. This section covers both, then gives you simple ways to stay in the safe middle.

## Risks of too little

**What the research shows**

The clearest, best-proven harms of too little Vitamin D affect your bones. Your body needs Vitamin D to absorb calcium from food and build strong bones. Without enough, bones suffer first.

- **Rickets** in children — soft, weak bones that can bend or bow as a child grows. It is the classic sign of Vitamin D deficiency in the young.
- **Osteomalacia** in adults — soft bones too, which can cause bone pain and muscle weakness.
- **Weaker bones and fractures** as we age. Low Vitamin D adds to bone thinning (called osteoporosis), which makes breaks more likely, often at the hip or wrist.

You may see hopeful claims that more Vitamin D prevents cancer or heart disease. As noted earlier (see "What Vitamin D Does NOT Do"), the largest trial to test this — VITAL, about 25,871 people — found no such benefit. Treat these wider benefits as unproven. The solid case for Vitamin D is about bones.

## Risks of too much

**What the research shows**

Too much comes in two very different forms. Keep them separate.

1. **Too much sun.** Sunlight (UVB) triggers your skin to make Vitamin D. But the same UVB also damages skin. It causes sunburn now and, over time, skin cancer. UVB is a known cause of cancer. For this reason, the American Academy of Dermatology does **not** recommend getting Vitamin D from the sun or from tanning beds. It advises getting Vitamin D from food and supplements instead.

2. **Too many supplements.** Taking very high doses of Vitamin D pills over time can cause poisoning. This raises calcium in your blood too high (a state called hypercalcemia). It can cause nausea, weakness, and kidney problems. Important: this comes from mega-dosing pills, **not** from sunlight. Your skin controls itself and will not overmake Vitamin D from the sun — recall that skin synthesis halts at about one sunburn dose. Staying at or below the roughly 4,000 IU/day upper limit keeps you well clear of toxicity.

## How to avoid both

**What to do**

- **Get Vitamin D from food and supplements, not from deliberate sun-seeking.** This meets your needs without raising skin-cancer risk. (See "How Much to Supplement?" for amounts.)
- **Protect your skin when you are outdoors.** Use sunscreen, seek shade, wear clothing, and avoid the strongest midday sun. You can still enjoy the outdoors and get its other benefits.
- **Stay within recommended supplement doses.** More is not better. Do not mega-dose, and treat about 4,000 IU/day as a ceiling, not a goal. (See "How Much to Supplement?" for the numbers.)
- **Ask a clinician if you are unsure.** They can advise on the right amount for you and, if needed, order a blood test.

The goal is simple: enough Vitamin D for strong bones, from safe sources, without tipping into too much.

---

## Bottom Line

Vitamin D matters most for one proven thing: strong bones. Being outdoors gives you extra benefits a pill cannot — lower blood pressure, less stress, and healthier eyesight in children. Aim for the recommended daily amount (about 600 IU up to age 70, 800 IU after 70) through food and sensible supplements, choose D3 if you buy a pill, protect your skin outdoors, and skip the mega-doses. Most healthy adults don't need a blood test. And where the science is still unsettled — the exact intake, the deficiency cutoff, and benefits beyond bone — we've said so plainly. Talk with a clinician about your own situation.

## Verification notes

The following points about evidence quality and source-tracing should be resolved before any formal publication. They do not change the practical guidance above.

- **Age-related decline in skin synthesis is genuinely contested.** MacLaughlin & Holick 1985 [17] report a ~50% fall in skin 7-dehydrocholesterol from age 20→80 (and a >4-fold smaller blood response); Chalcraft 2020 [4] reports ~13% per decade (~half by 70); the 2024 Manchester study [18] found little or no difference. A secondary source [19] estimates ~25% of youthful capacity by age 65. Do not present a single number as settled.
- **D2 vs. D3 potency is directional, not fixed.** Heaney/Armas 2011 [20] found D3 raised 25(OH)D about 56–87% more (and >3× the fat stores), with the gap largest at big bolus doses; several trials and meta-analyses [21] find the forms close to equivalent at daily maintenance doses.
- **Sun "minutes" and supplement "IU" are not interchangeable.** The exposure figures target different endpoints (400 IU, 1,000 IU, "maintenance," or a fraction of a sunburn dose) and different bare-skin fractions (5%, 15%, 24%, 25.5%, 35%, ¼ vs ⅛ BSA), so they cannot be cleanly traded for a pill dose.
- **Two figures need direct source verification.** The pregnancy **preeclampsia OR 1.58** [6] was drawn from a search-level snippet, not a confirmed fetch; treat it as supporting, not anchor evidence. The type-III **Boston/Miami sun-minute figures (3–8 / 3–6 min, 25.5% BSA)** reached us via a secondary lay site — the primary is Terushkin et al., *J Am Acad Dermatol* 2010 [22]; verify directly.
- **No morning-vs-noon minute figure exists.** The time-of-day dependence is published only via solar-angle / UV-index models, not a discrete "9 a.m. vs noon" minute count; and there is no single closed-form BSA equation.
- **The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline gives no sun-exposure minutes and sets no target 25(OH)D number.** All duration figures come from separate photobiology studies; the >30 vs ~20 ng/mL cutoffs are the Endocrine Society's and the IOM's standing definitions, not a screening instruction.
- **The D3>D2 effect is reported as an attributed range** (56–87% more, >3× fat stores) rather than a single invented multiplier; one originally-illustrative comparative figure was dropped rather than fabricated.
