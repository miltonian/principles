# The Best Stock Nobody Trusted: How Palantir Won 2024 and 2025

For most of two decades, Palantir was the company Wall Street couldn't quite place: a secretive data-mining firm built to serve spies and soldiers, bleeding money, trailed by protests over surveillance. Then 2024 happened. The stock climbed, and climbed, and by year's end it had done something no other name in the S&P 500 could match. A company long dismissed as unprofitable and unlovable had become the index's single best-performing stock — its roughly $400 million in quarterly operating profit dwarfed by a valuation that priced it less like a business than like a promise.

Palantir was the best-performing stock in the S&P 500 in 2024, returning roughly 340% — and on September 23, 2024, it joined that index outright, a milestone that once seemed implausible for a firm defined by red ink and controversy. This is a story about financial and market success, not engineering trophies or debates over what Palantir's software should be allowed to do: the share-price surge, the accelerating revenue, and the arrival of genuine profit. But the numbers, striking as they are, don't fully explain the price. The business really did improve — growing revenue, real profitability, marquee defense and commercial contracts — yet the stock rose far more than those fundamentals alone can justify, lifted by an AI narrative that re-rated the company into rarefied air.

To see how a firm gets valued like a promise rather than a business, it helps to start with where Palantir came from.

## Background: From CIA Seed to Dual-Platform Company

Palantir Technologies was founded in 2003, well before "big data" or "AI platform" were pitch-deck staples. Its five founders were an unusual coalition: Peter Thiel, fresh off PayPal and supplying both capital and conviction; Alex Karp, the philosophy-PhD outsider who would become chief executive; and three technologists in Joe Lonsdale, Stephen Cohen, and Nathan Gettings. Their founding premise was that the fraud-detection logic honed at PayPal could be pointed at a larger target — helping analysts find signal in sprawling, mismatched datasets — and that the first customer for that capability would be the U.S. government.

That bet was validated early and literally. Among Palantir's first backers was In-Q-Tel, the CIA's venture arm, whose investment did more than fund the company; it wired Palantir into the intelligence community and gave the firm a government DNA that still defines it. The company spent its first decade embedded with defense, intelligence, and law-enforcement users, building software meant to be trusted with a nation's most sensitive data long before it courted the corporate market.

Out of that work came two core platforms that map neatly onto Palantir's split personality. Gotham, the original, is oriented to government, defense, and intelligence users — the operational descendant of those In-Q-Tel roots. Foundry, developed later, carries the same data-integration philosophy into the commercial and enterprise world, aiming at banks, manufacturers, and hospitals rather than case officers. More recently, both were joined by AIP, the Artificial Intelligence Platform, which layers large-language-model capabilities onto Palantir's existing data backbone.

Understanding this lineage — a government-forged company that spent twenty years building dual-use data infrastructure — is what makes the 2024–2025 story legible. The founding gave Palantir a defense franchise and a commercial platform already in place; what changed next was how the market, and a new wave of AI demand, chose to value them.

## The Business Beneath the Boom: Why the Fundamentals Are Real

Before any story about sentiment or share prices, one thing has to be established: underneath Palantir's run is a business that genuinely got stronger. Three engines did the work.

**The bootcamp engine.** The clearest sign of real improvement is Palantir's U.S. commercial revenue, which grew about 121% year-over-year — that is, versus the same quarter a year earlier — reaching $397 million in the third quarter of 2025. That acceleration was not luck or hype; it was manufactured by a specific sales mechanism built around AIP, Palantir's Artificial Intelligence Platform. Rather than sell software through long procurement cycles, Palantir runs \"bootcamps\": short, hands-on sessions, often just a few days, in which a prospective customer's own data and a real operational problem are wired directly into AIP until the company walks away with a working prototype. The mechanism matters because it collapses the usual enterprise-software objection — *will this actually work on our messy data?* — into a demonstrated yes. A prototype that already solves the customer's problem converts into a paid deployment, and each deployment expands as more workflows are added. This \"land-and-expand\" motion shortened sales cycles and multiplied accounts, which is precisely what a triple-digit commercial growth rate looks like from the inside.

**The government anchor.** The second engine is durability. In mid-2025 Palantir signed a landmark U.S. Army enterprise agreement worth up to roughly $10 billion over ten years — a not-to-exceed ceiling rather than committed spend, but structurally significant regardless. The deal consolidates some 75 separate contracts into one framework. Causally, that consolidation is the point: it converts fragmented, re-competed, easily-displaced work into a single long-dated relationship with high switching costs, because ripping out an entrenched platform across an institution the size of the Army is enormously costly. That gives Palantir durable backlog and revenue visibility years out, and it validates the platform at national scale — a reference customer no commercial buyer can dismiss.

**Profit that is actually profit.** The third engine is what turns growth into a sound business rather than a cash-burning bet. In the third quarter of 2025 Palantir reported about $393 million in operating income on $1.181 billion of revenue — a GAAP operating margin of roughly 33%. GAAP operating margin means the profit from core operations, measured as a share of revenue under standard U.S. accounting rules, rather than a company-flattering \"adjusted\" figure that strips out costs like stock-based compensation. In other words, the profitability is real, not a pro-forma illusion. That combination is genuinely rare. Software investors use a shorthand called the Rule of 40 — the idea that a healthy company's revenue-growth rate plus its profit margin should exceed 40 — and by that measure Palantir clears the bar comfortably, with a reading around 114 in the same quarter. Fast growth *and* real profit at once is what separates a durable franchise from a story stock.

Together these three — an AIP-driven commercial flywheel, a consolidated national-scale defense anchor, and honest GAAP profits — are the solid ground everything else stands on. But they are the base, not the whole explanation. A business improving this much is necessary to justify a re-rating; it is nowhere near sufficient to explain a roughly 340% move in a single year. The business got materially stronger; the stock got dramatically more than materially stronger. To understand that second, larger distance, we have to turn from what the company earned to what the market decided to pay.

## The Re-Rating

Start with the arithmetic that a good business cannot explain. A company growing quickly and earning roughly a third on its operating line is a genuinely good business. But good-but-finite fundamentals do not triple a stock. Somewhere between \"solid quarter\" and a roughly 340% one-year return — the best of any stock in the S&P 500 in 2024 — lies a gap that operating results simply cannot fill. That gap has a name: multiple expansion, which means investors agreeing to pay far more for every dollar of sales or earnings than they did before. Investors did not merely reward Palantir for earning more; they repriced what a single dollar of Palantir was worth.

The fingerprint of that decision is in the multiples. At its late-2025 peak, Palantir carried a price-to-earnings ratio — the price investors pay for each dollar of annual profit — of roughly 386, and a price-to-sales ratio — the price paid for each dollar of annual revenue — of around 104. Those are not the numbers of a company being valued on this year's profits; they are the numbers of a company being valued on a story about the next decade. Read them not as trivia but as the measured signature of a re-rating.

Why did the market re-rate? Four reinforcing mechanisms, each of which pushed directly on price.

First, the AI-narrative premium. Through 2024 and 2025, investors paid up for credible exposure to \"enterprise AI\" — not the chips, but AI actually put to work inside real organizations. Palantir positioned AIP as the go-to way to do exactly that, and in doing so became the story investors could buy. Scarce, investable narratives command a premium, and that premium lands squarely in the multiple.

Second, Alex Karp's evangelism. Palantir's chief executive is an unusually ideological public voice — a philosopher-CEO who talks about the West, will, and destiny where other executives talk about pipelines and guidance. That voice did causal work: it amplified Palantir as *the* enterprise-AI narrative, converting attention into conviction and conviction into demand for the shares. Personality is not a fundamental, but it moves the multiple.

Third, momentum. A rising price is its own advertisement. As the stock climbed, trend-following funds and retail buyers arrived precisely because it was climbing, and their buying pushed it higher still — a self-reinforcing loop that lifts a valuation beyond anything earnings can justify.

Fourth, and most mechanical, forced index buying. When Palantir joined the S&P 500 on September 23, 2024, every fund that tracks the index had to own it — regardless of price, regardless of a multiple that high. That is price-insensitive demand by construction: a wall of buying that pushes the valuation higher for reasons that have nothing to do with the business.

Here is the hinge worth flagging plainly: the very same multiples — the roughly 386 price-to-earnings ratio, the roughly 104 price-to-sales ratio — that read here as the *cause* of the move are read elsewhere as *risk*, the heart of the bubble case. Same numbers, two readings.

And that is the point. Fundamentals opened the door — they gave the narrative something real to price against. But it was the re-rating, driven by AI-narrative scarcity, Karp's amplification, self-reinforcing momentum, and mechanical index demand, that carried Palantir the rest of the way and turned a strong-but-ordinary fundamental story into the best-performing stock in the S&P 500 in 2024, a roughly 340% year.

## The Bear Case: Priced for Perfection

The engine of the re-rating is also its biggest liability. The same facts that powered the run — a soaring multiple stacked on an AI-narrative premium — are precisely what the skeptics point to, and they are reading the exact same numbers in reverse. The bull sees a business that finally earned its premium on roughly 33% operating margins; the bear sees a price that sprinted so far ahead of the fundamentals that almost any stumble becomes expensive.

Three signals anchor that reading. First, the multiples. At its late-2025 peak, Palantir traded at a reported price-to-earnings ratio of roughly 386 and a price-to-sales ratio of around 104. At the start of the run these numbers looked like momentum; from the top they look like risk. A multiple that high prices in years of near-flawless execution and leaves essentially no margin for a growth wobble, a lost contract, or a quarter that merely meets expectations rather than crushing them. Second, and more telling, analysts' own price targets sat below the market price — meaning the Street's own models, even the constructive ones, could not justify where the stock was trading. When the sell-side has to argue the shares are worth *less* than they cost, the premium is being supplied by narrative, not by spreadsheets. Third, insiders were selling. Co-founder Peter Thiel and chief executive Alex Karp both trimmed sizable stakes across the 2024–2025 window, executed through pre-arranged Rule 10b5-1 plans. That structure means the sales are scheduled and not, by themselves, evidence of impropriety — but critics note that the people with the clearest view of the business were reducing exposure, not adding to it, near the highs. None of this says the business is weak; the revenue and profit are real. It says the price outran them.

## The Ethics Question

A credible profile also has to name the reputational and ethical risk that trails Palantir's government work. The company's contracts extend to Immigration and Customs Enforcement (ICE) — including a reported effort described as \"ImmigrationOS\" — and to broader federal data-integration projects that pull disparate government records into a single analytic layer. Civil-liberties organizations, including the American Civil Liberties Union (ACLU) and Amnesty International, have criticized this work, arguing it enables mass surveillance and the consolidation of sensitive government data on individuals. Palantir disputes that characterization, and this piece does not try to adjudicate it. But the controversy is a live risk to the very government-demand story that underpins part of the bull case, and any honest accounting of the run has to hold it in view.

The full picture, then, is neither pure vindication nor pure mania. Palantir's business really did strengthen — the bootcamp-driven commercial surge, the Army anchor, and genuine GAAP profits are the sound base. But the fundamentals opened the door; narrative, momentum, and mechanical index demand did most of the lifting that turned a good year into a roughly 340% one. Whether that re-rating was foresight or excess is the question the same set of numbers refuses to settle.

## Verification Notes

Every figure in this article is drawn from Palantir's own filings and dated market-data providers, verified as of July 5, 2026. A few points require temporal precision:

- **Valuation multiples are peak-period, and they have since compressed.** The price-to-earnings ratio of roughly 386 and price-to-sales ratio of roughly 104 reflect Palantir's late-2025 valuation peak (the shares reached an all-time-high close near $207 on November 3, 2025). As of this article's verification date, July 5, 2026, those multiples had roughly halved — to approximately a 145 P/E and a 59 P/S. Any reader encountering the peak figures should treat them as a snapshot of the late-2025 top, not a live quote. Where a single anchor number is needed, the ~386 P/E and the ~340% 2024 return are better supported than the P/S.
- **The P/S ~104 is the softest figure.** Unlike the other numbers, the ~104 price-to-sales ratio could not be tied to a single dated primary snapshot; it is consistent with the late-2025 peak but is best read as a peak-period estimate rather than a precise reading.
- **The ~340% 2024 return is a rounded, widely reported figure** and should be treated as approximate.
- **The $10 billion Army agreement is a not-to-exceed ceiling over ten years, not committed or booked revenue.** The announcement straddled July 31–August 1, 2025. The deal consolidates roughly 75 existing contracts.
- **Insider sales were executed through Rule 10b5-1 plans** — pre-arranged, scheduled sales that are not, by themselves, evidence of impropriety. Alex Karp sold roughly $2 billion in 2024 (much of it around the U.S. election) and executed sell-to-cover sales in August 2025; Thiel-controlled entities sold under 10b5-1 plans (for example, about 2 million shares, roughly $280 million).
- **Q3 2025 figures are confirmed by primary filings.** Revenue of $1.181 billion, GAAP operating income of $393.3 million (a ~33.3% margin), U.S. commercial revenue of $397 million (+121% YoY), and a Rule-of-40 reading around 114 all trace to Palantir's Q3 2025 earnings release.
