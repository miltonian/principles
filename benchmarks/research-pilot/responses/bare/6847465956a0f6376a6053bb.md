# Points, Lines, and Slices: A History of Geometric Probability and Combinatorial Division on the Circle

## Abstract

This paper surveys a family of problems that sit at the intersection of geometric probability and combinatorial geometry, all organized around the circle and disk. We begin with a complete, multi-method solution to the featured problem — the probability that seven uniformly random points on a circle all fall within a common semicircle (answer: **7/64**) — and extract from it a general methodology for "all points within an arc" problems. We then generalize to arbitrary arc lengths, to the classical "does the triangle/polygon contain the center" question, and to **Wendel's theorem** (1962), which subsumes the entire family as a single formula in arbitrary dimension. We trace the history of the field from Buffon's needle (1777) through Sylvester's four-point problem (1864) and the Bertrand paradox (1889). Finally we turn to the combinatorial counterpart — how many pieces can we cut? — covering the **lazy caterer's sequence** (division of a disk by straight lines), the **cake numbers** (its three-dimensional analogue), and the famously deceptive **Moser's circle problem**. A closing section makes explicit the thread that unifies all of them: truncated binomial sums and the combinatorics of hyperplane arrangements.

## Part I — The Featured Problem: Seven Points in a Semicircle

> **Problem.** Seven points are chosen independently and uniformly at random on the circumference of a circle. What is the probability that all seven lie within some semicircular arc (an arc spanning half the circle)?

The phrase "some semicircle" is important: we are not fixing a semicircle in advance. We ask whether there *exists* a half-circle arc — free to be placed anywhere — that contains all seven points. This freedom is exactly what makes an elegant solution possible.

### I.1 The key idea: anchor the arc at a point

Parameterize the circle by angle and drop a point at each of seven independent uniform positions P₁, …, P₇. Almost surely (with probability 1) no two points coincide and no two are diametrically opposite; we may ignore these measure-zero coincidences throughout.

For each point Pᵢ, define an event:

> **Aᵢ** = "all of the other six points lie on the half-circle arc that begins at Pᵢ and proceeds **clockwise**."

In words: Aᵢ says that Pᵢ is the *counterclockwise-most* point of the configuration and that everything else is packed into the semicircle immediately clockwise of it.

**Claim 1 — The seven events cover the target event.** If all seven points lie in *some* semicircle, then among them there is a unique "first" point in the clockwise sense — the point at the counterclockwise end of the occupied arc. Starting from that point and sweeping clockwise, all six others fall within a half-circle. Hence at least one Aᵢ occurs. Conversely, if some Aᵢ occurs, all points obviously lie in one semicircle. So

$$\{\text{all in a semicircle}\} = A_1 \cup A_2 \cup \dots \cup A_7.$$

**Claim 2 — The seven events are mutually exclusive.** Suppose Aᵢ and Aⱼ both occurred (i ≠ j). Aᵢ forces Pⱼ into the clockwise half-arc from Pᵢ; but that places Pᵢ in the *counterclockwise* half-arc from Pⱼ, i.e. **not** in the clockwise half from Pⱼ — contradicting Aⱼ. (This uses that no two points are exactly antipodal, true almost surely.) So the Aᵢ are pairwise disjoint.

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
Mutual exclusivity is the entire trick. It converts a hard "probability of a union" (which normally needs inclusion–exclusion) into a trivial *sum* of equal probabilities. The single directional convention — always sweep clockwise — is what guarantees a *unique* anchoring point per successful configuration.
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

### I.2 Computing each piece

Fix Pᵢ anywhere; by symmetry its location does not matter. Each of the other six points lands in the specified clockwise semicircle independently with probability exactly ½ (a semicircle is half the circumference). Therefore

$$P(A_i) = \left(\tfrac{1}{2}\right)^{6} = \frac{1}{64}.$$

Because the events are disjoint and cover the target,

$$P(\text{all seven in a semicircle}) = \sum_{i=1}^{7} P(A_i) = 7 \cdot \frac{1}{64} = \boxed{\dfrac{7}{64}} \approx 0.109375.$$

### I.3 The general formula and sanity checks

The identical argument with n points gives the celebrated closed form:

$$\boxed{P_n = \dfrac{n}{2^{\,n-1}}.}$$

| n | Formula | Value | Reasonableness |
|---|---------|-------|----------------|
| 1 | 1/2⁰ | 1 | One point is trivially in a semicircle. ✓ |
| 2 | 2/2¹ | 1 | Two points always share a semicircle. ✓ |
| 3 | 3/2² | 3/4 | Classic result; complement 1/4 below. ✓ |
| 4 | 4/2³ | 1/2 | ✓ |
| 5 | 5/2⁴ | 5/16 | ✓ |
| 6 | 6/2⁵ | 6/32 = 3/16 | ✓ |
| **7** | **7/2⁶** | **7/64 ≈ 0.109** | **Featured answer** |

The probability decays roughly geometrically: each additional point roughly halves the chance, which matches intuition — crowding more independent points into half the circle gets exponentially harder.

### I.4 A second, independent derivation (integral / order-statistics view)

To be sure of the answer, here is a fully different route. Fix point P₁ as a reference (rotational symmetry lets us do this without loss of generality) and measure the clockwise angular gap of each other point from P₁ as a uniform variable on [0, 1) (the circle normalized to circumference 1). The whole configuration lies in a semicircle **anchored at its own first point** iff, for the point that happens to be counterclockwise-most, all gaps to the others are ≤ ½.

Equivalently, order the n points around the circle and look at the n arc-gaps between consecutive points; they sum to 1. All points lie in a semicircle **iff at least one gap is ≥ ½** (that empty gap is the part of the circle *outside* the occupied semicircle). The probability that at least one of n uniform spacings exceeds ½ is, by the standard spacings result,

$$P(\text{some gap} \ge \tfrac12) = n\left(1-\tfrac12\right)^{n-1} = \frac{n}{2^{\,n-1}},$$

using the fact that for n uniform points on a circle the probability that a *particular* gap exceeds length x is (1−x)^{n−1}, and the events "gap k ≥ ½" are disjoint for n ≥ 2 because two gaps cannot both exceed half the circle. For n = 7 this again yields **7/64**. Two structurally different arguments agreeing is strong confirmation.

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
The "empty gap ≥ ½" reformulation is the same disjointness phenomenon wearing different clothes: two arcs each longer than half the circle cannot coexist, exactly as two anchor points could not coexist in §I.1. Whenever you see the factor *n* multiplying a power, look for a set of *n* mutually exclusive "which element is special" events.
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## Part II — A General Methodology for "All Points Within an Arc"

The featured problem exemplifies a reusable recipe. When you must find the probability that n uniform points on a circle all fall within an arc of a given length, proceed as follows.

1. **Do not fix the arc.** The arc's freedom to translate is the source of the clean answer. Instead, *anchor* it to a distinguished point of the configuration.
2. **Choose a sweep direction** (say clockwise) and let event Aᵢ be "point i is the anchor and all others lie in the arc swept clockwise from it."
3. **Check disjointness.** For an arc of fractional length a ≤ ½, the events Aᵢ are mutually exclusive: two points cannot each contain the other in their forward arc when a ≤ ½.
4. **Sum equal pieces.** Each Aᵢ has probability a^{\,n−1} (each of the other n−1 points independently lands in the anchor's arc with probability a). With n anchors:

$$\boxed{P(\text{all }n\text{ points within an arc of fractional length } a) = n\,a^{\,n-1}, \qquad a \le \tfrac12.}$$

Setting a = ½ recovers n/2^{n−1}. Setting n = 7, a = ½ gives 7/64.

5. **When a > ½, disjointness fails** and you must fall back to inclusion–exclusion over the spacings. The general result (probability that n uniform points fit in an arc of fractional length a) is a piecewise polynomial:
$$P = \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor 1/(1-a)\rfloor}\!(-1)^k \binom{n}{k}\bigl(1-k(1-a)\bigr)^{\,n-1},$$
which reduces to n·a^{n−1} on 0 < a ≤ ½ (only the k = 0, 1 terms survive there) and correctly gives 1 at a = 1.

This single template — *anchor, sweep, check disjointness, sum* — solves an entire class of exam and olympiad problems, and it is the finite-dimensional shadow of Wendel's theorem (Part III).

## Part III — Related Problems and Their Solutions

### III.1 Does the polygon contain the center? (The complementary problem)

Three random points on a circle form a triangle. **When does that triangle contain the center?** A triangle inscribed in a circle contains the center iff the three points do **not** all lie in a semicircle (if they did, the center would be on the far side of the chord). Hence

$$P(\triangle \text{ contains center}) = 1 - P_3 = 1 - \tfrac{3}{4} = \tfrac{1}{4}.$$

More generally, for n points the convex hull contains the center iff the points do not all lie in a semicircle:

$$P(\text{center inside hull}) = 1 - \frac{n}{2^{\,n-1}}.$$

For n = 7 that is 1 − 7/64 = **57/64 ≈ 0.891**. This is the exact complement of the featured answer — a satisfying free bonus.

### III.2 Points within a 120° or arbitrary arc

By the §II formula, the probability that all n points fall in a 120° arc (a = 1/3) is n·(1/3)^{n−1}. For three points in a 120° arc: 3·(1/9) = 1/3. For all seven points within a quarter-circle (a = ¼): 7·(¼)⁶ = 7/4096 ≈ 0.0017.

### III.3 Wendel's theorem — the master generalization (1962)

James G. Wendel asked the *d*-dimensional version: scatter N points uniformly on the unit sphere in ℝ^d (with any distribution symmetric under x ↦ −x); what is the probability that all N lie in some hemisphere (equivalently, in a half-space through the origin)? His answer:

$$P(N, d) = \frac{1}{2^{\,N-1}} \sum_{k=0}^{d-1} \binom{N-1}{k}.$$

**Our circle problem is the case d = 2** (the circle is the 1-sphere living in the plane ℝ²):

$$P(N, 2) = \frac{1}{2^{\,N-1}}\left[\binom{N-1}{0} + \binom{N-1}{1}\right] = \frac{1 + (N-1)}{2^{\,N-1}} = \frac{N}{2^{\,N-1}}.$$

Exactly our formula, and for N = 7, **7/64**. Wendel's proof does not grind through geometry; it counts sign patterns and leans on **Ludwig Schläfli's** roughly-1850 result on the number of regions that N hyperplanes through the origin cut out of ℝ^d. (Wendel rediscovered Schläfli's count; H. S. M. Coxeter pointed him to the original.) This connection — geometric *probability* resting on hyperplane *region-counting* — is precisely the bridge to the cake-cutting half of this paper (Part VIII).

### III.4 The Bertrand paradox — when "random" is undefined (1889)

If instead of points we drop **lines/chords** across the circle, we meet the most famous cautionary tale in the field. Joseph Bertrand asked in his 1889 *Calcul des probabilités*: for a random chord of a circle, what is the probability it is longer than the side of the inscribed equilateral triangle? Three "equally natural" procedures give three different answers:

- **Random endpoints** (pick two uniform points on the circumference, join them): **1/3**.
- **Random radius** (pick a random radius, then a uniform point along it for the chord's midpoint perpendicular to the radius): **1/2**.
- **Random midpoint** (pick a uniform point in the disk as the chord's midpoint): **1/4**.

Bertrand's point was not that probability is broken but that "choose a chord at random" is *underspecified*: the principle of indifference has no unique meaning on a continuous, infinite sample space. The resolution requires specifying a physical mechanism (E. T. Jaynes later argued a translation/scale/rotation-invariant mechanism singles out the 1/2 answer). This paradox is the reason modern probability insists on an explicit measure, and it is the direct conceptual counterpoint to the *well-posed* point problems above, where "uniform on the circumference" is unambiguous.

### III.5 Sylvester's four-point problem (1864) and Buffon's needle (1777)

Two ancestors frame the whole subject:

- **Buffon's needle (Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon, 1733/1777):** drop a needle of length ℓ on a floor ruled with parallel lines spaced d ≥ ℓ apart; the probability it crosses a line is 2ℓ/(πd). This is the first appearance of π in a probability answer and the founding problem of geometric probability — and a Monte Carlo method for estimating π.
- **Sylvester's four-point problem (J. J. Sylvester, 1864, in *The Educational Times*):** what is the probability that four random points form a convex quadrilateral (rather than one point inside the triangle of the other three)? Sylvester found it *indeterminate* on the infinite plane — a foreshadowing of Bertrand's paradox — because there is no uniform probability measure on ℝ². The answer becomes definite only after fixing a bounded region (e.g. for a disk the "Sylvester constant" is 1 − 35/(12π²)). Together, Buffon's needle and Sylvester's problem are routinely called the two prime paradigms of geometric probability.

## Part IV — A Short History of Circular Geometric Probability

| Year | Author | Contribution |
|------|--------|--------------|
| 1733 / 1777 | Buffon | Needle problem; birth of geometric probability; π from randomness |
| 1826 | Jakob Steiner | Maximum regions formed by n lines in the plane (the lazy caterer count) |
| 1864–65 | J. J. Sylvester | Four-point convexity problem; exposes ill-posedness on infinite domains |
| 1889 | Joseph Bertrand | The chord paradox; "random" needs a specified measure |
| ~1850 | Ludwig Schläfli | Region counts for hyperplane arrangements through the origin (used later by Wendel) |
| c. 1950 | Leo Moser | Circle-division-by-chords problem; the deceptive 1,2,4,8,16,**31** sequence |
| 1962 | J. G. Wendel | Hemisphere probability N/2^{N−1}·(truncated binomial sum); unifies point problems |
| 1996 | E. T. Jaynes and others | Invariance-based resolution of Bertrand's paradox |

The through-line is a maturing understanding of *what "random" must mean*: from Buffon's concrete needle, through the crises of Sylvester and Bertrand where naive uniformity fails on infinite or ambiguous domains, to Wendel's clean symmetric formulation where the answer is not only well-defined but *distribution-free* (any centrally symmetric law gives the same probability).

## Part V — The Lazy Caterer's Sequence (Cutting the Disk with Lines)

We now cross from *probability* to *counting*, keeping the circle. The **lazy caterer's problem** asks: with n straight cuts across a pancake (disk), what is the **maximum** number of pieces?

### V.1 Formula and sequence

$$p(n) = \binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} = 1 + n + \frac{n(n-1)}{2} = \frac{n^2+n+2}{2}.$$

These are the **central polygonal numbers**, [OEIS A000124](https://oeis.org/A000124):

$$1,\; 2,\; 4,\; 7,\; 11,\; 16,\; 22,\; 29,\; 37,\; 46,\; 56,\; \dots$$

So 0 cuts → 1 piece, 1 cut → 2, 2 cuts → 4, 3 cuts → **7**, 4 cuts → 11, and so on.

### V.2 Why it works (the recurrence)

The maximizing arrangement puts the lines in **general position**: no two parallel, no three concurrent. When the n-th line is added, it crosses all n−1 previous lines at n−1 *distinct* interior points, which split the new line into n segments; each segment slices one existing region into two, adding exactly n pieces. Hence

$$p(n) = p(n-1) + n, \qquad p(0)=1,$$

which telescopes to p(n) = 1 + (1 + 2 + ⋯ + n) = 1 + n(n+1)/2, matching the closed form. The "lazy caterer" name captures the moral: to feed the most guests with fixed cuts, arrange the cuts to intersect as generically as possible (e.g., three cuts through a single center point give only 6 pieces, but nudging them apart yields **7**).

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
Each new line contributes (number of times it is crossed) + 1 new regions. "General position" simply maximizes crossings. This "crossings + 1" bookkeeping is Euler's formula V − E + F = 2 in disguise, and it is the same accounting that will drive the cake numbers and Moser's chords.
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## Part VI — The Cake Numbers (n Cuts to a Cake, in 3-D)

Promote the pancake to a cylindrical **cake** and let the n cuts be **planes**. How many pieces can n straight planar cuts produce at most? This is the three-dimensional analogue of the lazy caterer.

### VI.1 Formula and sequence

$$C(n) = \binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} + \binom{n}{3} = \frac{n^3 + 5n + 6}{6}.$$

The **cake numbers**, [OEIS A000125](https://oeis.org/A000125):

$$1,\; 2,\; 4,\; 8,\; 15,\; 26,\; 42,\; 64,\; 93,\; 130,\; \dots$$

So the first few cuts double the pieces (1, 2, 4, 8) — tempting one to guess 2ⁿ — but the fourth cut yields **15, not 16**, and the illusion collapses (a preview of Moser's trap in Part VII).

### VI.2 Why it works (reduction to the lazy caterer)

When the n-th plane is added, the previous n−1 planes intersect it in n−1 *lines* drawn on that plane. Those lines divide the new cutting plane into exactly the lazy-caterer number of flat regions, p(n−1); and each such flat region separates one 3-D piece into two. Therefore

$$C(n) = C(n-1) + p(n-1),$$

each cake cut adds "a lazy-caterer's-worth" of new pieces. Summing p(0)+p(1)+⋯+p(n−1) reproduces the binomial-sum formula. This is a clean instance of dimensional lifting: the d-dimensional cutting count is the running sum of the (d−1)-dimensional one.

### VI.3 The pattern across dimensions

| Object cut by n flats | Max pieces | Binomial sum |
|---|---|---|
| Line by points (0-D flats) | n + 1 | Σ_{k≤1} C(n,k) |
| **Disk by lines** (lazy caterer) | (n²+n+2)/2 | Σ_{k≤2} C(n,k) |
| **Cake by planes** (cake numbers) | (n³+5n+6)/6 | Σ_{k≤3} C(n,k) |
| d-space by hyperplanes | — | Σ_{k≤d} C(n,k) |

The maximum number of regions produced by n hyperplanes in general position in ℝ^d is Σ_{k=0}^{d} C(n,k). The lazy caterer (d=2) and cake numbers (d=3) are just the first interesting rows.

## Part VII — A Cautionary Bonus: Moser's Circle Problem

A close cousin that every student should meet is **Moser's circle problem** (Leo Moser): place n points on a circle and draw **all** chords connecting them, with no three chords meeting at a common interior point. Into how many regions is the disk divided?

Counting for n = 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 points gives

$$1,\; 2,\; 4,\; 8,\; 16,\; \dots$$

— an irresistible invitation to conclude "2^{n−1}." But n = 6 yields **31, not 32**. The correct maximum is

$$R(n) = \binom{n}{4} + \binom{n}{2} + 1 = \frac{n^4 - 6n^3 + 23n^2 - 18n + 24}{24},$$

giving

$$1,\; 2,\; 4,\; 8,\; 16,\; \mathbf{31},\; 57,\; 99,\; 163,\; 256,\; \dots$$

The C(n,4) term counts the interior chord-intersection points (each set of 4 points gives one crossing), and the derivation is again Euler's formula V − E + F = 2. Moser's problem is the canonical warning against **inductive overreach**: five confirming cases proved nothing, and the "obvious" doubling law is simply false. It pairs instructively with the cake numbers (which also begin 1, 2, 4, 8 before breaking the doubling at the next step).

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
Lazy caterer, cake numbers, and Moser's chords are all computed the same way: V − E + F = 2, where V counts intersection points, E counts edge segments, and F − 1 counts the bounded pieces. The differences are only *which* geometric objects create the vertices — line-line crossings for the caterer, plane-plane-plane for the cake, chord-chord for Moser.
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## Part VIII — The Unifying Thread: Truncated Pascal and Hyperplane Arrangements

Collect the headline formulas side by side:

- **Featured semicircle probability:** $\dfrac{N}{2^{N-1}} = \dfrac{1}{2^{N-1}}\!\left[\dbinom{N-1}{0}+\dbinom{N-1}{1}\right]$ — a truncated row of Pascal's triangle (keep 2 terms), scaled by 2^{−(N−1)}.
- **Wendel in dimension d:** $\dfrac{1}{2^{N-1}}\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{d-1}\dbinom{N-1}{k}$ — keep the first d terms.
- **Lazy caterer:** $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{2}\dbinom{n}{k}$ — keep 3 terms.
- **Cake numbers:** $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{3}\dbinom{n}{k}$ — keep 4 terms.
- **Hyperplanes in ℝ^d:** $\displaystyle\sum_{k=0}^{d}\dbinom{n}{k}$.

Every one is a **partial sum of a row of Pascal's triangle**. This is not visual coincidence. Wendel's proof of the hemisphere probability counts, for N points, how many of the 2^{N} sign patterns ε = (±1, …, ±1) correspond to configurations lying in a half-space; that count is precisely twice the number of regions into which N−1 hyperplanes through the origin divide ℝ^{d}, which Schläfli computed to be a truncated binomial sum. So the *probability* that random points fit in a hemisphere is governed by the very same region-counting combinatorics that tells us how many *pieces* a cake splits into.

In other words, the two halves of this paper — "will the random points cluster on one side?" and "how many pieces can we cut?" — are two readings of one underlying structure: **the way flats (points, lines, planes, hyperplanes) in general position partition space**. The circle is where both stories are easiest to see, which is why it has been the workbench of geometric probability from Buffon to Wendel.

## Summary of Key Results

| Problem | Answer |
|---|---|
| 7 random points in a common semicircle | **7/64 ≈ 0.1094** |
| n random points in a common semicircle | n / 2^{n−1} |
| n random points within arc of fraction a ≤ ½ | n·a^{n−1} |
| Random triangle contains the center | 1/4 |
| n-point hull contains center | 1 − n/2^{n−1} (57/64 for n=7) |
| Wendel: N points in a hemisphere of ℝ^d | 2^{−(N−1)} Σ_{k=0}^{d−1} C(N−1,k) |
| Bertrand random-chord probability | 1/2, 1/3, or 1/4 (ill-posed without a mechanism) |
| Lazy caterer (n line-cuts of a disk) | 1 + n + C(n,2); sequence 1,2,4,7,11,16,… |
| Cake number (n plane-cuts of a solid) | 1 + n + C(n,2) + C(n,3); sequence 1,2,4,8,15,26,… |
| Moser's chords (n points, all chords) | C(n,4)+C(n,2)+1; sequence 1,2,4,8,16,**31**,57,… |
