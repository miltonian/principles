# PART 1 — THE ONE-PAGER (C-SUITE TEASER)

## Cybersecurity in Aviation: A Board-Level Briefing for Alaska Air Group
### What This Presentation Will Cover

**Why now.** 2025 has been the worst year on record for aviation cyber risk — the FBI issued a formal warning that a criminal group ("Scattered Spider") has pivoted specifically to airlines, and a single attack on one shared IT vendor grounded check-in at Heathrow, Brussels, and Berlin simultaneously. Aviation cyberattacks are reported to have risen sharply year-over-year. Alaska Air Group already has direct exposure: **Hawaiian Airlines, now part of the Group, was hit in June 2025.**

**The five things the C-suite needs to decide on:**

1. **The threat is now the help desk, not the firewall.** The most damaging 2025 attacks used *phone calls and impersonation* — tricking IT support into resetting passwords — not sophisticated hacking. This is a people-and-process problem you can act on immediately.

2. **Your biggest risk sits outside your walls.** The attacks that grounded European airports and exposed millions of passenger records came through *third-party vendors and platforms*, not the airlines' own core systems. Vendor risk = your risk.

3. **Downtime is the real P&L event.** Whether the cause is a hack or a hardware failure (as Alaska saw twice in 2025), the business impact is identical: grounded fleet, stranded passengers, cancelled revenue, brand damage. **Cyber resilience and operational resilience are the same conversation.**

4. **The regulator has already moved.** TSA and FAA now mandate cybersecurity plans, incident reporting, and a designated coordinator, with fines up to ~$14K *per violation per day*. Europe's EASA "Part-IS" rules phase in through Feb 2026. Compliance is no longer optional.

5. **Passenger safety vs. passenger data — know the difference.** Flight-critical avionics and the systems holding customer data are different battlefields with different stakes. We'll frame where to spend and why.

**What I'll ask of you at the end:** agreement on 3–4 no-regret investments (help-desk hardening, vendor assurance, incident-response rehearsal, and board-level reporting) that reduce the highest-probability, highest-cost scenarios.

*Estimated presentation length: 45 minutes + 15 minutes Q&A.*

# PART 2 — THE DETAILED BRIEFING DOCUMENT

*This expands each teaser topic into the substance behind the slides. It is written to be read by an executive audience and to give you, the presenter, enough command of the material to field questions confidently.*

## 0. Framing: What we mean by "cyber" in aviation

Before the five topics, establish two definitions on screen — this is where a non-specialist earns credibility:

- **A cybersecurity incident** is a *deliberate, malicious* act by an external or internal actor to steal data, extort money, or disrupt operations (e.g., ransomware, phishing).
- **An IT outage / resilience failure** is an *unintentional* breakdown — hardware failure, software bug, misconfiguration.

**Why this matters for this room:** Alaska Airlines suffered two high-profile groundings in 2025 (July, ~3 hours; October 23, 360+ flights cancelled after a primary data-center hardware failure), and the company explicitly stated the October event was **not** a cyberattack. If you call these "cyber attacks" in front of your own C-suite, you lose the room. Instead, use them to make a more powerful point: **the passenger and revenue impact of a hardware failure and a ransomware attack look identical from the outside.** Both ground the fleet. So the investment case for resilience covers both — this reframes cyber spend as operational-continuity spend, which CFOs fund more readily.

---

## Topic 1 — The Human Attack Surface: "They call your help desk"

**The single most important shift for executives to understand.** The marquee airline attacks of 2025 did not begin with a genius breaking encryption. They began with a phone call.

- **The technique (social engineering):** Attackers impersonate an employee or contractor and call the IT help desk claiming to be locked out. They talk a support agent into resetting a password or registering a new device — which defeats multi-factor authentication (MFA), the very control meant to stop them.
- **The actor:** The FBI publicly warned in June 2025 that the cybercrime group known as **Scattered Spider** — previously focused on retail and insurance — had pivoted to the airline sector. They are English-speaking, fast, and specialize in manipulating people, not machines.
- **Why airlines are vulnerable:** Large, distributed, 24/7 workforces; heavy use of contractors; high staff turnover at IT help desks; and enormous pressure to restore access quickly (a locked-out gate agent is a delayed flight).

**Executive takeaway:** This is the *most fixable* high-impact risk. Fixes are process, not technology: stronger identity verification for help-desk requests, "callback" protocols, restrictions on who can register new MFA devices, and targeted training for support staff. **Low cost, high leverage.**

---

## Topic 2 — Third-Party & Supply-Chain Risk: "The attack comes through a vendor you didn't know you depended on"

Modern airlines run on a web of shared platforms — check-in systems, loyalty programs, catering, maintenance software, cloud providers. **In 2025, the vendors were the way in.**

Concrete 2025 examples to cite:

- **Collins Aerospace / MUSE (September 2025):** The **Everest ransomware group** hit Collins Aerospace's *MUSE* common-use check-in and boarding platform. Because *many airlines at many airports share this one system*, a single attack forced **Heathrow, Brussels, and Berlin** to revert to **paper boarding passes and manual check-in** for days. Baggage piled up; flights were delayed and cancelled. Attackers claimed to steal ~50GB of data and set an 8-day ransom deadline.
- **Qantas (June–July 2025):** ~5.7 million passengers' frequent-flyer data exposed via a **third-party platform** — not Qantas's core systems.
- **Air France-KLM (2025):** Customer contact and loyalty data exposed through an **external platform** (internal systems reportedly unaffected).

**Executive takeaway:** Your security is only as strong as your weakest vendor. The board should ask: *Which single third-party systems, if they went down, would ground us or expose our customers? What contractual security guarantees, audits, and breach-notification clauses do we hold them to?* This is **vendor risk management**, and regulators now explicitly require it (see Topic 4).

---

## Topic 3 — Operational Resilience: "Assume it will happen — can you keep flying?"

Prevention will sometimes fail. The mature question is not "how do we become un-hackable?" (impossible) but **"how fast do we recover, and can we operate manually in the meantime?"**

- **The business-impact lens:** Every hour of a grounded fleet is cancelled revenue, rebooking and hotel costs, crew-scheduling cascades, and reputational erosion. Alaska's own 2025 outages (non-cyber) demonstrated the cost profile of downtime — a useful, honest internal reference point.
- **Manual fallback works — if rehearsed:** In the Collins/MUSE attack, airports that could revert to manual check-in kept *some* flights moving. In the March 2025 Kuala Lumpur airport ransomware attack (Qilin group, $10M demand, 10+ hours down), manual workarounds kept aircraft flying. **The organizations that suffered least had practiced their fallback.**
- **The four capabilities to fund:** (1) tested, *offline* backups; (2) a written and *rehearsed* incident-response plan; (3) network segmentation so one compromised system can't spread to all; (4) clear crisis-communications playbooks (regulators, passengers, media, staff).

**Executive takeaway:** Ask the CISO/CIO one question: *"When did we last run a full tabletop exercise simulating our booking or check-in system being ransomed, and what broke?"* If the answer is "never" or ">12 months," that's your action item.

---

## Topic 4 — The Regulatory & Compliance Landscape: "The rules already changed"

Cybersecurity in aviation moved from voluntary best-practice to **enforceable mandate** in 2023–2026. Non-compliance is now a direct financial and legal risk.

- **United States — TSA & FAA:** TSA has issued requirements (via Security Directives / Emergency Amendments) for air carriers and airports to: designate a **Cybersecurity Coordinator**, report incidents, run **vulnerability assessments**, and maintain **mitigation/Cybersecurity Improvement Plans** covering network segmentation, access control, continuous monitoring, and patching. **Civil penalties run up to ~$13,910 per violation, per day.** FAA requirements add carrier-specific obligations, with newer proposals extending NIST-based standards to newer systems.
- **Europe — EASA "Part-IS":** A new Information Security Management System (ISMS) mandate. **Applies from October 16, 2025, with full compliance phased to February 22, 2026**, scaled to an organization's size and risk. Relevant to any Alaska/Hawaiian operations touching EU airspace or partners.
- **The common thread:** Regulators expect risk assessments grounded in the **NIST Cybersecurity Framework** and **secure-by-design** practices, and explicitly require consideration of **supply-chain/third-party risk** (ties directly to Topic 2).

**Executive takeaway:** Compliance is a floor, not a ceiling — but falling below it now carries per-day fines and, after an incident, materially worse legal and reputational exposure. The board should confirm: *Do we have a named Cybersecurity Coordinator, a current improvement plan, and evidence we could show a regulator tomorrow?*

---

## Topic 5 — Safety Systems vs. Data Systems: "Two very different battlefields"

Executives often blur two distinct risk domains. Separating them clarifies where money and worry belong.

- **Passenger/enterprise data & operations (high probability, business impact):** Loyalty databases, booking engines, check-in, HR, email. This is where **virtually all 2025 attacks landed** — data theft, extortion, and operational disruption. High likelihood; costs are financial, regulatory, and reputational. **This is where near-term spend should concentrate.**
- **Flight-critical / aircraft systems (low probability, catastrophic impact):** Avionics, air-traffic-management links, in-flight networks. These are more isolated and heavily regulated, and there is **no evidence they were the target of the 2025 airline attacks.** But the consequence ceiling is safety-of-life, so it warrants rigorous, ongoing assurance — not panic.

**Executive takeaway:** The honest message is *"No one hacked a plane in 2025 — they hacked the check-in desk and the loyalty database."* Reassuring on safety, sober on the business systems that are actually under fire. Direct urgent budget to the data/operations layer while maintaining disciplined safety-system governance.

## Closing: The Ask (final slide)

Recommend the board endorse **four no-regret moves**, mapped to the topics above:

1. **Harden the help desk** (Topic 1) — identity-verification protocols and MFA-device controls. *Fastest, cheapest, highest-leverage.*
2. **Stand up vendor cyber-assurance** (Topic 2) — map critical third parties; add security, audit, and breach-notification clauses.
3. **Rehearse the crisis** (Topic 3) — a full tabletop within 90 days simulating a ransomed booking/check-in system, including manual-fallback and comms.
4. **Report cyber to the board quarterly** (Topics 4–5) — named coordinator, compliance status, and a simple risk dashboard, so this is governed, not reactive.

**The through-line for the room:** *Whether the next disruption is a hacker or a hardware failure, the passenger sees the same thing — a cancelled flight. Cyber resilience is operational resilience, it is already regulated, and our most dangerous door is the one a human opens with a phone call.*
