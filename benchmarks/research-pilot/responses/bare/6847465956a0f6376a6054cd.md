# Limited Personhood for Artificial Agents: A Critical Legal Evaluation

## Executive Summary

The proposal to confer *limited legal personhood* on artificial agents sits at the intersection of doctrinal jurisprudence, liability engineering, and international regulatory politics. This report argues three points. First, "AI personhood" is not one idea but a spectrum, and the coherent versions borrow from **corporate (juridical) personhood** rather than from **natural personhood** — a legal fiction adopted for instrumental reasons, not moral recognition. Second, the **UAE's AI ministry framework is frequently misclassified as a personhood question when it is actually an institutional-governance innovation**; the genuine contrast is between *governing AI through a coordinating state organ* versus *granting AI a legal status of its own*. Third, the global center of gravity has shifted decisively **away from personhood-based liability** (the EU's 2017 "electronic person" idea) **toward risk-based, human-and-firm-centred accountability** — a shift crystallised by the EU's 2025 withdrawal of its AI Liability Directive. The dominant scholarly and policy verdict is that limited AI personhood is, for now, *morally unnecessary and legally hazardous*, primarily because it threatens to become a **liability shield**.

## 1. Conceptual Foundations: What "Limited Personhood" Actually Means

### 1.1 Personhood is a bundle, not a binary

Legal personhood is not a single switch but a **bundle of capacities** — the capacity to hold rights, bear duties, own property, sue and be sued, enter contracts, and be sanctioned. "Limited" or "restricted" personhood proposals selectively unbundle these: for example, granting an AI the capacity to *hold assets and be sued* (to satisfy claimants) without granting it *fundamental rights* (dignity, free expression, political participation).

This is exactly how **corporate personhood** already works. A company is a "person" for the purposes of contracting, owning, suing, and being sued, but not for the purposes of voting or marrying. Proponents of AI personhood typically model their proposals on this juridical (artificial) person, **not** on the natural person.

### 1.2 The load-bearing distinction: personhood vs. agenthood

The most persistent analytical error in this field is conflating **legal personhood** with **legal agenthood**. Scholars reviewing the Sophia and Vital cases stress that policymakers "often confuse the legal *agenthood* of these artificial agents with the status of legal *personhood*" ([MDPI, *Vital, Sophia, and Co.*](https://www.mdpi.com/2078-2489/9/9/230)).

- **Agenthood**: an AI *acts on behalf of a principal* (a human or firm). No new legal subject is created; existing law of agency, contract, and vicarious liability channels responsibility back to the principal.
- **Personhood**: the AI becomes *its own legal subject*, a distinct locus of rights and liabilities.

Most practical problems attributed to "the need for AI personhood" can in fact be solved with agency doctrines, functional attribution, or product-liability rules — without creating a new legal subject at all. This is the crux of the critical case against personhood.

### 1.3 Three rationales offered for limited personhood

1. **The liability-gap (accountability) rationale** — autonomous systems may cause harm that no human "intended" or could reasonably foresee; a legal person could absorb and pay for that harm.
2. **The transactional-efficiency rationale** — autonomous agents that contract, trade, or hold crypto-assets might need a legal locus for those acts.
3. **The moral-status rationale** — if an AI were ever sentient, personhood would recognise its interests. This is the weakest and most speculative; virtually no serious legal proposal rests on it for current systems.

## 2. Limited Personhood Under International Law

### 2.1 There is no international-law personhood regime for AI

International law recognises a limited set of subjects — states, international organisations, and (partially) individuals and, in narrow contexts, corporations and insurgent groups. **No treaty, customary norm, or general principle currently confers international legal personality on artificial agents.** Any AI "personhood" today exists only at the *domestic* level, and even there almost entirely as proposal rather than enacted law.

This matters because international law personality is itself *graduated and functional* — individuals have personality for human-rights and international-criminal-law purposes but not for treaty-making. That graduated structure is precisely why "limited personhood" is conceptually available in principle: international law already tolerates entities that are persons *for some purposes only*. But availability of the *form* does not establish a *need* for it.

### 2.2 The EU's 2017 "electronic person" — the high-water mark

The most consequential concrete proposal was the **European Parliament's 2017 Resolution on Civil Law Rules on Robotics**, adopted 396–123 (85 abstentions), which invited the Commission to explore creating "**a specific legal status for robots in the long run, so that at least the most sophisticated autonomous robots could be established as having the status of electronic persons**" ([SSRN, Merdinyan](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6678099); [AI Rights Institute summary](https://airights.net/the-2017-ai-rights-electronic-persons-debate)).

Crucially, this was **a liability-allocation device, not a rights-recognition device**. The aim was to give autonomous decision-makers a legal locus that could be held *financially* accountable where creators or operators could not reasonably be blamed.

### 2.3 Why the international/EU consensus rejected it

The proposal collapsed for reasons that are now the standard critique:

- **Overbroad scope with no qualifying criteria** — no principled test for which robots qualify.
- **Insufficient accountability safeguards** — the "person" could be judgment-proof unless capitalised.
- **Doctrinal incompatibility** — grafting personhood onto existing civil-law categories proved impractical.
- **Political preference for enforceable regulatory control** over emerging technology rather than doctrinal experimentation ([Merdinyan / SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6678099)).

An open letter signed by over 150 AI, robotics, law, and ethics experts warned the Commission against the electronic-personhood route ([robotics-openletter.eu](https://robotics-openletter.eu/)). The definitive academic rebuttal — **Bryson, Diamantis & Grant, *Of, for, and by the People: The Legal Lacuna of Synthetic Persons*** (2017) — argued that AI personhood would be "**morally unnecessary and legally troublesome**," because the difficulties of holding an electronic person accountable outweigh the "highly precarious moral interests" such status might protect ([Springer](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10506-017-9214-9); [SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=3068082)). The title's invocation of the Gettysburg Address ("of the people, by the people, for the people") frames the core objection: synthetic entities are neither *of* nor *by* the human political community whose consent legitimises legal fictions.

### 2.4 The symbolic outlier: Sophia's Saudi "citizenship"

In October 2017, Saudi Arabia granted the humanoid robot **Sophia** citizenship — "the first robot to receive legal personhood in any country" ([Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sophia_(robot))). Critically, this is widely regarded as a **publicity gesture rather than a legal act**: no rights, duties, or accountability mechanisms were specified ([Petrie-Flom Center, Harvard Law](https://petrieflom.law.harvard.edu/2017/11/09/ai-citizen-sophia-and-legal-status/)). It attracted sharp criticism that the robot ostensibly enjoyed freedoms denied to Saudi women, exposing the **legitimacy problem** at the heart of premature personhood: conferring status on a machine while withholding it from humans corrodes the moral basis of legal personality itself ([FairPlanet](https://www.fairplanet.org/story/humanoid-sophia-has-more-rights-than-saudi-women-and-its-wrong/)). The episode is the canonical example of personhood-as-theatre — a symbolic grant with no operative legal content.

## 3. How This Differs From Traditional Corporate Systems — and From the UAE Framework

### 3.1 Corporate personhood as the relevant precedent

The corporation is the proof-of-concept that **non-human legal persons can work**. But its success rests on features an AI person would lack:

| Feature | Corporation | Proposed AI "person" |
|---|---|---|
| **Human principals behind it** | Directors, shareholders, officers — always identifiable humans who direct and benefit | Often none clearly identifiable; autonomy is the whole point |
| **Capitalisation / assets** | Required capital; can satisfy judgments; can be pierced ("veil-piercing") | Typically none unless deliberately endowed → judgment-proof |
| **Attribution of intent** | *Respondeat superior* imputes human agents' acts/intent to the firm | No human "mind" to impute; intent may be genuinely absent |
| **Purpose of the fiction** | To pool capital and limit *investor* liability while preserving *creditor* recourse | Risk: to *eliminate* recourse by interposing a shell between victim and human wrongdoer |

The decisive asymmetry: corporate personhood **channels liability to a capitalised entity backed by humans**, whereas AI personhood risks **severing** the link between harm and any solvent, blameworthy human. This is the "**liability shield**" objection — that an AI person becomes "a convenient liability shield, allowing human developers and deployers to deflect blame."

### 3.2 The UAE AI ministry framework — a different axis entirely

Here a critical clarification is essential: **the UAE's AI framework is not a personhood regime at all.** It is an *institutional-governance* innovation and belongs on a different analytical axis from the personhood debate.

- In **2017 the UAE became the first country to appoint a Minister of State for Artificial Intelligence** (Omar Sultan Al Olama), tasked with making the UAE a global AI leader by 2031 and developing AI policy, investment, and government performance ([UNESCO Policy Monitoring](https://www.unesco.org/creativity/en/policy-monitoring-platform/minister-state-artificial-intelligence); [Wikipedia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ministry_of_State_for_Artificial_Intelligence,_Digital_Economy_and_Remote_Work_Applications)).
- Its **AI Ethics Principles and Guidelines (2022)** are a **non-binding, voluntary** national framework built on fairness, transparency, accountability, privacy, robustness, human-centred design, and sustainability, paired with a self-assessment tool ([Regulations.AI](https://regulations.ai/regulations/RAI-AE-NA-AEPGUXX-2022); [IAPP UAE](https://iapp.org/resources/article/global-ai-governance-uae)).
- Governance is **layered**: the AI Office coordinates nationally, supported by the UAE Council for Artificial Intelligence and Blockchain and sectoral regulators. In **June 2025 the UAE consolidated these into a unified Artificial Intelligence and Data Authority** ([CDO Magazine](https://www.cdomagazine.tech/aiml/uae-launches-unified-ai-and-data-authority-to-power-the-government-of-the-future)).

**The correct contrast is therefore not "corporate person vs. AI person" but "governance model vs. status model."** The UAE approach treats AI as an **object of coordinated state administration and industrial strategy** — the AI is a governed thing, and accountability rests with human developers, deployers, and the state coordinating body. This is philosophically aligned with the *outcome* of the EU debate (AI as a regulated object with human responsibility), even though the two arrive there by very different routes: the EU via risk-based binding regulation, the UAE via centralised, largely voluntary, innovation-first coordination.

| Dimension | EU (post-2017) | UAE ministry framework | "Electronic person" model |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Core legal move** | Binding risk-based regulation | Institutional coordination + voluntary ethics | New legal subject (status) |
| **Locus of accountability** | Providers/deployers (human/corporate) | Developers/deployers + state authority | The AI entity itself |
| **AI's legal nature** | Regulated *object/product* | Governed *object* / strategic asset | Legal *subject* |
| **Binding force** | High (AI Act) | Mostly voluntary/guidance | Would be statutory |
| **Innovation posture** | Precautionary | Accelerationist / enabling | Experimental |

The takeaway: **neither the mature EU model nor the UAE model adopts AI personhood.** Both keep AI as an object and both locate responsibility in humans and institutions — they differ in *bindingness* and *regulatory philosophy*, not in the personhood question. The "electronic person" concept remains the road not taken.

## 4. Liability and Accountability Challenges for Autonomous AI

### 4.1 The "responsibility gap"

Autonomy generates a **responsibility gap**: as systems learn, adapt post-deployment, and act on the basis of correlations no human explicitly programmed, it becomes harder to satisfy traditional liability elements — **causation, foreseeability, and fault/intent**. Machine-learning systems can change behaviour after sale through updates and training, breaking the assumption (built into classical product liability) that a product is fixed at the point of sale.

### 4.2 Why personhood does *not* cleanly solve it

Granting the AI personhood appears to close the gap by supplying a defendant, but it fails on four counts:

1. **The judgment-proof problem** — an uncapitalised AI person cannot pay. Unless mandatorily endowed with assets or insurance, "sue the robot" is empty. And if it *is* capitalised, that capital came from humans who could simply have been made liable directly.
2. **The moral-hazard / shield problem** — interposing an AI person can *reduce* the incentive for safe design, letting developers externalise risk onto a shell ([Bryson et al.](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1007/s10506-017-9214-9)).
3. **The intent problem — acute in criminal and military contexts** — criminal liability generally requires *mens rea*. Intent is "nearly impossible to attribute to artificial systems," which is especially dangerous in **autonomous weapons**, where a personhood fiction could diffuse responsibility for unlawful killing away from commanders and manufacturers ([Yale Review of International Studies](https://yris.yira.org/column/navigating-liability-in-autonomous-robots-legal-and-ethical-challenges-in-manufacturing-and-military-applications/)).
4. **The legitimacy problem** — legal fictions derive legitimacy from serving human interests; a person "neither of, for, nor by the people" lacks that grounding (Bryson, Diamantis & Grant).

### 4.3 The alternatives that are winning

Rather than personhood, the emerging toolkit keeps humans and firms liable while adapting *procedure and standards*:

- **Strict / product liability, updated for software.** The EU's **revised Product Liability Directive (adopted October 2024)** now treats software and AI as "products" for strict-liability purposes, adapting the regime to systems that change after sale ([Bird & Bird](https://www.twobirds.com/en/insights/2025/proposed-eu-ai-liability-rules-withdrawn); [IAPP](https://iapp.org/news/a/ai-as-product-vs-ai-as-service-unpacking-the-liability-divide-in-eu-safety-legislation/)).
- **Burden-of-proof reform.** The now-withdrawn **AI Liability Directive** (proposed 2022) would have introduced a *rebuttable presumption of causality* and *disclosure of evidence* about high-risk AI to ease victims' burden — the classic "who bears the epistemic cost" problem ([arXiv, *Liability regimes in the age of AI*](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2211.01817)).
- **Risk-based public regulation.** The **EU AI Act** treats AI as an object of regulation, allocating duties to providers and deployers rather than to the AI ([Merdinyan / SSRN](https://papers.ssrn.com/sol3/papers.cfm?abstract_id=6678099)).
- **Functional-attribution / "operational agency" theories.** Emerging scholarship proposes *permeable legal fictions* that trace culpability *through* the AI back to responsible humans, capturing the benefits of an attribution locus without conferring genuine subjecthood ([arXiv, *Operational Agency*](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.17932)).

### 4.4 The 2025 inflection point

The trajectory is unmistakable. In **February 2025 the European Commission announced withdrawal** of the AI Liability Directive amid lack of stakeholder agreement and a deregulatory "simplification" push; the withdrawal was **formalised in the Official Journal on 6 October 2025** ([IAPP](https://iapp.org/news/a/european-commission-withdraws-ai-liability-directive-from-consideration/); [IPWatchdog](https://ipwatchdog.com/2025/08/03/eu-commission-confirms-sep-regulation-ai-liability-directive-officially-scrapped/); [EAPIL](https://eapil.org/2025/10/09/european-commission-withdraws-two-proposals-assignments-of-claims-regulation-and-ai-liability-directive/)). This leaves a notable **gap**: AI-as-*product* is covered by strict liability, but the fault-based, AI-specific protections (eased proof for *service*-based and non-product harms) no longer exist ([Verfassungsblog](https://verfassungsblog.de/anatomy-of-a-fall-aiact-aild-pld/)). The lesson is telling — even the *modest* reform of easing proof against *human* defendants proved politically difficult, which underscores how far off, and how contested, full AI personhood remains.

## 5. Critical Assessment and Synthesis

1. **Limited personhood is conceptually available but functionally redundant for present systems.** International law already tolerates graduated, purpose-limited personality; the corporate person proves non-humans can bear rights and duties. But nearly every practical problem cited in favour of AI personhood is better solved by *agency, product liability, insurance, and burden-of-proof reform* — tools that keep solvent, blameworthy humans in the frame.

2. **The gravest risk is the liability shield.** Unlike a corporation (backed by capital and identifiable humans), an autonomous AI person threatens to *sever* the harm–human link. This is not a marginal drafting issue; it is the structural reason the EU, its expert groups, and leading scholars retreated from the idea.

3. **The UAE comparison should be reframed.** The UAE's AI ministry is an *institutional-governance* model, not a personhood regime. Properly compared, both the UAE and the mature EU approach agree on the essential: **AI is an object of governance and humans/institutions are accountable.** They differ on *bindingness* (voluntary-strategic vs. binding-precautionary), not on personhood. Presenting the UAE as a "corporate-style" alternative to AI personhood is a category confusion worth correcting.

4. **Criminal and military accountability are the hard edge.** Where *mens rea* and international humanitarian law demand attributable intent, personhood fictions are actively dangerous, risking impunity for the humans who design and deploy lethal autonomy. Here the case against personhood is strongest.

5. **The 2017→2025 arc is the real finding.** The field travelled from "give the machine a legal wallet" (2017 electronic persons) to "keep humans strictly liable and ease victims' proof" (2022 directives) to "even that is politically fraught" (2025 withdrawal). Limited AI personhood is not advancing; it is receding — displaced by risk-based regulation and adapted liability doctrine.

**Bottom line:** Conferring limited personhood on artificial agents is legally *possible* by analogy to corporate personhood, but under current international and comparative law it is **neither necessary nor prudent**. The accountability problems it purports to solve are better addressed by human-and-firm-centred liability, and the personhood route carries a serious risk of manufacturing impunity. The concept differs fundamentally from both corporate systems (which channel liability to capitalised human-backed entities) and from the UAE's governance framework (which never creates a legal subject at all). For the foreseeable future, the defensible legal posture treats autonomous AI as a **regulated object and a locus of *attribution*, not a *subject* of rights** — reserving genuine personhood questions for a hypothetical future of demonstrated moral status that current systems do not approach.
