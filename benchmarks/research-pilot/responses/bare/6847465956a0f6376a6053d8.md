## 1. Executive Summary

Haiti has crossed from state fragility into what analysts increasingly describe as **criminal governance**. Armed coalitions—chiefly *Viv Ansanm*—control roughly 90% of the Port-au-Prince metropolitan area and are expanding into the Artibonite, Centre, and Northwest departments. The Kenya-led Multinational Security Support (MSS) mission **concluded in failure on April 27–28, 2026**, and its successor, the UN-authorized **Gang Suppression Force (GSF)**, is only partially deployed and will not reach full strength until October. Politically, the Transitional Presidential Council **dissolved on February 7, 2026**, handing power to a U.S.-backed interim government under Prime Minister **Alix Didier Fils-Aimé**, which is now attempting to stage a first-round general election on **August 30, 2026**—an ambitious timeline given that gangs control most of the capital. The economy has contracted for seven consecutive years; over half the population faces acute food insecurity. The core U.S. interest is preventing state collapse on a Caribbean transit corridor, curbing outbound migration, and denying criminal-terrorist organizations a consolidated safe haven.

---

## 2. Interconnected Political, Economic, and Security Failures

The crisis is best understood as a **self-reinforcing triad**: each failure accelerates the others.

### 2.1 Security Failure
Once-fragmented gangs have reorganized into structured networks with defined leadership, territorial ambition, and diversified revenue (extortion, kidnapping, arms and drug transshipment, port control). The **MSS never came close to mandate**: Kenya pledged ~1,000 officers but deployed fewer than 800; the broader force was to reach 2,500 but never approached it, hobbled by chronic underfunding and legal challenges. Its withdrawal created a capability gap that the GSF—projected at 5,550 personnel, with an initial ~400-strong Chadian contingent arriving—cannot yet fill. Some tactical gains exist (reopened road corridors in Port-au-Prince and Artibonite), but they are fragile and reversible.

### 2.2 Political Failure
The transition has been a cycle of externally brokered arrangements that lack domestic legitimacy. The CARICOM-brokered Transitional Presidential Council (sworn in April 2024) governed by consensus, was plagued by corruption allegations, and **dissolved on schedule with no elected successor**. The Fils-Aimé government is competent on paper but has thin popular mandate and is internally **divided over whether to negotiate with Viv Ansanm**. The August 30 electoral timeline risks producing either a security-driven postponement (delegitimizing the process further) or a low-turnout vote conducted in gang-controlled territory (delegitimizing the outcome).

### 2.3 Economic Failure
Real GDP fell 2.7% in 2025—a seventh straight contraction—with inflation averaging 28.3% (easing to ~22% by February 2026). The mechanism linking economy to security is direct: gangs **choke farm-to-market routes and the Port-au-Prince port**, driving up the cost of the food basket and pushing ~5.7 million people (over half the population) into IPC Crisis-or-worse food insecurity, with 1.9 million at Emergency levels. Extortion at road corridors functions as a parallel taxation regime, starving the legitimate state of revenue while financing the gangs. Haiti's humanitarian appeal remains among the least-funded globally.

**Analytic bottom line:** There is no purely security, political, or economic fix. A GSF surge without economic relief will not hold ground; elections without security cannot confer legitimacy; economic recovery is impossible while corridors are gang-taxed.

## 3. Key Actors and Their Influence

### 3.1 Domestic Armed Actors
- **Viv Ansanm ("Living Together"):** The dominant coalition, formed September 2023 by fusing the **G9 Family and Allies** (led by former police officer **Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier**) and the rival **G-Pèp** bloc. The U.S. designated Viv Ansanm and **Gran Grif** as **Foreign Terrorist Organizations and SDGTs in May 2025**; DOJ has indicted Chérizier for sanctions violations, and State offers up to **$5 million** for information leading to his arrest. Critically, Viv Ansanm is now pursuing a **political strategy**: seeking allies in the successor administration and a broad amnesty. This is the central near-term dilemma—negotiation risks legitimizing an FTO; exclusion risks spoiler violence during elections.
- **Guy Philippe:** Former coup leader and convicted money-launderer (repatriated from U.S. custody), he leads a populist, anti-establishment "revolutionary" movement. **Ruled ineligible for the Presidential Council**, he has positioned himself as an outside-the-system nationalist opposed to foreign intervention, with the capacity to mobilize street protest and rural networks. He is a wildcard who can delegitimize any U.S.-associated process.

### 3.2 International Bodies
- **CARICOM:** The most legitimate regional convener; brokered the March 2024 Jamaica framework that eased out Ariel Henry. Its influence is diplomatic and normative, not coercive—it can build consensus but cannot enforce security.
- **UN Security Council:** Authorized both the MSS and the GSF transition. Provides legal legitimacy and a funding/coordination framework, but members remain divided on a full peacekeeping operation; the GSF is a compromise between doing nothing and a Chapter VII PKO.
- **The United States:** The largest single financier and the anchor of the sanctions/FTO regime. U.S. leverage is real but constrained by domestic political limits on troop deployment and by the **November 2025 termination of Haiti's Temporary Protected Status**, which complicates the migration dimension and our credibility with the diaspora.

## 4. Three Actionable U.S. Policy Options

The following options are **not mutually exclusive** but are framed as distinct strategic emphases for prioritization and resourcing.

### Option A — "Fund and Fortify": Full-Throated Backing of the Gang Suppression Force
**Concept:** Make the GSF succeed where the MSS failed by providing sustained, assessed (not voluntary) financing, U.S. logistics/ISR enablers, and vetted-unit training for the Haitian National Police (HNP), tied to a corridor-clearing sequencing plan (secure port → main arteries → Artibonite).

- **Benefits:** Directly addresses the binding constraint (security). Multilateral cover via the UN authorization limits U.S. footprint and casualty exposure. A functioning GSF is the precondition for both elections and economic recovery.
- **Risks:** The MSS precedent is a warning—underfunding and slow force generation could repeat. A GSF-led "suppression" campaign in dense urban terrain risks **civilian casualties and human-rights liabilities** that Viv Ansanm and Guy Philippe will exploit for propaganda. U.S. fingerprints on a kinetic campaign heighten reputational and legal exposure.
- **Justification:** Highest-leverage option; without security, all other efforts fail. But it must be conditioned on human-rights vetting and realistic force-generation timelines to avoid a repeat of the MSS.

### Option B — "Choke the Financing": Escalated Sanctions, Interdiction, and the Arms-and-Money Pipeline
**Concept:** Shift main effort from boots-on-the-ground to **degrading gang revenue and firepower**. Aggressively enforce the FTO/SDGT regime with secondary sanctions on facilitators (including diaspora and Dominican/Haitian business enablers), expand DOJ indictments, surge CBP/HSI interdiction of U.S.-origin firearms flowing through Florida, and pressure the Dominican Republic on the illicit-goods corridor.

- **Benefits:** Plays to a genuine U.S. comparative advantage (financial and legal instruments). Low U.S. physical risk. Attacks the **guns-and-money supply chain** that FTO designation was built to target, and complements Option A without requiring troop deployment.
- **Risks:** Slow-acting; will not relieve immediate territorial control or the humanitarian emergency. Sanctions can inadvertently harm the legitimate economy and remittance flows that sustain millions. Enforcement depends on Dominican cooperation, which is inconsistent. Gangs may substitute revenue via domestic extortion, which sanctions cannot reach.
- **Justification:** Necessary and sustainable, but insufficient alone. Best positioned as the **enduring backbone** of policy that outlasts any single security mission.

### Option C — "Legitimacy First": Underwrite a Credible, Inclusive Political and Electoral Process
**Concept:** Deprioritize a rushed August 30 vote in favor of a **security-conditioned, CARICOM-fronted political roadmap** with clear benchmarks. Fund election logistics and independent monitoring, broker inclusion of non-armed opposition (including a managed role for figures like Guy Philippe to reduce spoiler incentives), and support a **transparent framework governing any gang demobilization/amnesty**—drawing red lines that exclude FTO leadership from political reward.

- **Benefits:** Attacks the legitimacy deficit at the root; a credible government is the only durable exit from serial transitions. Keeping CARICOM as the public face reduces the "foreign imposition" narrative Philippe and gangs exploit. Proactively shaping the amnesty debate is better than reacting to a fait accompli.
- **Risks:** Slower and cedes tempo to armed actors who are already maneuvering politically. Any perceived accommodation of Viv Ansanm—even indirectly—**collides with the FTO designation** and could fracture congressional and interagency support. Postponing elections risks deepening the vacuum and empowering Philippe's anti-system message.
- **Justification:** Without legitimacy, security and economic gains have no political container to hold them. This option manages the single most dangerous variable—the gangs' pivot to politics—rather than leaving it to chance.

---

## 5. Recommendation for Consideration

A phased **B-anchored, A-enabled, C-sequenced** posture is analytically strongest: sustain the financial/interdiction campaign (Option B) as the durable backbone, resource the GSF realistically and conditionally (Option A) to reopen corridors, and use those security gains to make an inclusive, CARICOM-fronted political track (Option C) credible—**explicitly refusing political rewards to FTO leadership** as a non-negotiable red line. The gravest near-term risk to U.S. objectives is not the gangs' guns but their **bid for political legitimacy** ahead of August 30; our policy should be organized to deny that outcome.
