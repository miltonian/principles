# THE SWITCH BOY — A Research Report & Blueprint for a Shuffle-Novel

### *A modular novel about Kade, a boy who flickers between medieval pasts and cyberpunk futures — read the middle in any order; the ending is fixed.*

---

## 0. Executive Summary

You asked for a novel with three hard constraints:

1. A boy who can **switch between two theme-modes** — the **medieval past** and the **cyberpunk future**.
2. Every switch drops him into a **completely different world** *within* each theme (not the same medieval world twice, not the same future twice).
3. The **chapters are interchangeable** — the reader chooses the order — **except the final chapter, which is fixed and delivers a real conclusion.**

This document is both a **research report** (surveying the real literary and game-design traditions that make constraint #3 possible) and a **working blueprint** (the actual novel: its switching mechanism, ten shuffleable world-chapters, and a fully-written locked finale).

The core design finding: to make chapters shuffleable *and* have the ending pay off, you cannot rely on a linear plot. You need a **"collectible" architecture** borrowed from gamebooks — each chapter is self-contained and deposits one **artifact/truth** into the boy's possession; the fixed finale harvests whatever the reader collected. Order stops mattering; *completion* is what matters.

---

## 1. Research Foundations

### 1.1 The permutational ("book-in-a-box") novel is a real, proven form

The single most important precedent is **B.S. Johnson's *The Unfortunates* (1969)** — literally published as unbound sections in a box. It has **27 sections: a specified first, a specified last, and 25 in between designed to be read in any order**, yielding roughly **15.5 septillion possible reading orders**. Johnson's stated purpose was to reproduce "the chaotic nature of memory" — order is not fixed because *memory's* order is not fixed. This is our exact structural template, and it proves the "fixed final chapter + shuffleable middle" idea is not only possible but a recognized masterpiece ("one of the lost masterpieces of the sixties," per Jonathan Coe). [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unfortunates) [[2]](https://www.ndbooks.com/book/the-unfortunates/)

A second precedent — **Ali Smith's *How to be Both* (2014)** — publishes two halves in two orders across different print runs, proving a **mainstream** market appetite for reader-order variability. Cortázar's *Hopscotch* (*Rayuela*) offers a "table of instructions" with multiple reading routes. Together these establish a spectrum from *fully* aleatory (Johnson) to *curated* multi-path (Cortázar/Smith). [[3]](https://nicolefu.co.uk/2021/09/18/5-novels-with-non-linear-narrative-structures/)

**Design takeaway:** We adopt the Johnson model directly — fixed bookends, shuffleable core — because it maps perfectly onto your "final chapter cannot be changed" requirement.

### 1.2 Gamebooks solved "modular chapters that still add up to an ending"

The **gamebook** tradition (*Choose Your Own Adventure*, 1979; *Fighting Fantasy*, 1982) is the engineering ancestor of what you want. Two lessons transfer:

- **The American tradition (*CYOA*)** made *reader choice* mainstream: numbered nodes, each referencing the next. Chapters are addressable units, not a fixed stream. [[4]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gamebook)
- **The British tradition (*Fighting Fantasy*)** added an **RPG state layer** — you collect items, track stats, and combat resolves against what you're carrying. **The ending checks your inventory.** [[5]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fighting_Fantasy)

That inventory-checking finale is the mechanism that lets a non-linear middle produce a satisfying, *earned* ending. Our novel steals it wholesale: each shuffleable world gives Kade a **shard** (an object + a lesson), and the finale is a "collect-what-you-can" reckoning.

### 1.3 Portal fantasy gives us the emotional grammar of "two worlds"

Portal fantasy is defined by a **"normal world" and a "world of adventure,"** with the portal functioning as "a space of transformation — but always also of testing." Canonical examples: *Alice*, *Oz*, *Narnia*, *Coraline*, *The Magicians*. Crucially, some portal stories send characters "to a different time period within their own world" — which is exactly our medieval-past / cyberpunk-future axis. [[6]](https://www.byarcadia.org/post/portal-fantasies-a-quest-for-one-s-identity) [[7]](https://alexstruever.com/en/portal-fantasy-tropes/)

**Design takeaway:** Each switch is a *test*, not just a scene change. The boy's home reality is the third, quiet pole he's trying to return to.

### 1.4 Source palettes — Medieval and Cyberpunk

**Medieval sources.** Two streams: (a) **Arthurian myth** — chivalry, the Round Table's equality, Excalibur, the Grail quest as spiritual test, and the magical guides Merlin / Morgan le Fay / the Lady of the Lake. Themes: honor, betrayal, "the rise and fall of Camelot," and the longing for a just kingdom. [[8]](https://mythlok.com/world-mythologies/european/british/arthurian/) [[9]](https://www.medievalchronicles.com/medieval-legends-myths-and-tales/the-quest-for-the-holy-grail-uncovering-the-mystery-of-its-importance-to-knights/) (b) **Grounded historical medievalism** in the mode of *Kingdom Come: Deliverance* (15th-century Bohemia; dice, dueling, sieges, political intrigue), *The Witcher 3* (Eastern-European-flavored grounded fantasy), and *Elden Ring* (gothic castles, ruined grandeur). [[10]](https://www.pcgamesn.com/best-medieval-games) [[11]](https://gamerant.com/best-medieval-fantasy-games/)

**Cyberpunk sources.** The genre's DNA traces to **Gibson's *Neuromancer* (1984)** — "the source text for all cyberpunk," origin of *cyberspace* and *the matrix*, fusing Blade-Runner neon-noir with corporate exploitation of body and mind. Core worldbuilding pillars: **neon-soaked noir streets, mega-corporations that outrank governments, a stark elite/underclass divide, and "information is power" hacker protagonists.** Extend with *Snow Crash* (virtual-world virus), *Altered Carbon* (consciousness transfer / identity), and the game *Cyberpunk 2077* / *Deus Ex* lineage. [[12]](https://vanguardguardian.com/10-cyberpunk-novels-every-neuromancer-fan-should-read/) [[13]](https://academy.worldanvil.com/blog/cyberpunk-worldbuilding-guide)

**The thematic bridge (why these two eras belong in one book):** medieval and cyberpunk are mirror images of the **same anxieties** — *Who rules, and by what right? (divine-right king ↔ CEO of a megacorp). What is the soul worth? (Grail/salvation ↔ uploaded consciousness). Who is sworn to whom? (knightly oath ↔ corporate/gang loyalty).* Kade's journey exploits this rhyme: the same test recurs in two costumes.

## 2. The Design Solution — How Shuffleable Chapters Still End Well

### 2.1 The architecture: Hub-and-Spoke with a Collectible State

```
                 ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
   FIXED START → │  Ch. 0  "The Flicker"  (origin, rules)   │
                 └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
                                    │
        ┌───────────┬───────────┬───┴───────┬───────────┬───────────┐
        ▼           ▼           ▼           ▼           ▼           ▼
   ╔═══════╗   ╔═══════╗   ╔═══════╗   ╔═══════╗   ╔═══════╗   ...  (read
   ║MED-1  ║   ║CYB-1  ║   ║MED-2  ║   ║CYB-2  ║   ║MED-3  ║       any order,
   ╚═══╤═══╝   ╚═══╤═══╝   ╚═══╤═══╝   ╚═══╤═══╝   ╚═══╤═══╝       any number)
       │ shard     │ shard     │ shard     │ shard     │ shard
       └───────────┴───────────┴─────┬─────┴───────────┴───────────┘
                                     ▼
                 ┌─────────────────────────────────────────┐
   FIXED END →   │  Ch. Ω  "The Loom"  (harvest + conclude) │
                 └─────────────────────────────────────────┘
```

Five rules make this robust (each derived from the research above):

| # | Rule | Borrowed from | What it guarantees |
|---|------|---------------|--------------------|
| R1 | **Idempotent entry.** Every shuffle-chapter opens with Kade *arriving cold* — a switch just happened; he (and the reader) can reconstruct everything needed in the first page. | *The Unfortunates*' self-contained sections | No chapter depends on having read another. |
| R2 | **One shard out.** Every world hands Kade exactly one **Shard** — a physical object *and* the truth it teaches. | *Fighting Fantasy* inventory | The finale has something to harvest regardless of order. |
| R3 | **No cross-references by name.** Chapters never say "as in the castle before"; they refer to prior worlds only as "another life, another switch." | Johnson's memory-logic | Any two chapters read back-to-back stay coherent. |
| R4 | **The Constant.** One recurring figure — **the Grey Woman** — appears once per world wearing that world's clothes (a nun; a netrunner; a village witch; an AI ad-hologram). The reader always has an anchor. | Portal-fantasy guide (Merlin/Lady of the Lake ↔ AI handler) | Emotional continuity without plot dependency. |
| R5 | **Locked finale harvests state.** Ch. Ω counts the Shards the reader collected and *branches its own prose* accordingly — but always resolves. | Gamebook inventory-check ending | Same ending logic works for 1 shard or all 10. |

`★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
R5 is the clever part and mirrors defensive programming: the finale must **degrade gracefully.** A reader who read only 3 chapters and a reader who read all 10 must *both* get a complete ending. So Ω is written as a *template with conditional passages* — the "harvest" scene names each Shard the reader actually holds and is silent about the rest. It never assumes a full inventory, exactly like a function that handles the empty-collection case first.
`─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

### 2.2 The switching mechanic (the boy's power, and its cost)

**Kade Ellry, 13,** discovered that any time he is *cornered by a choice he doesn't want to make*, he **Flickers** — and when he lands, he is in a *new* world. Two hard laws govern the power (established in the fixed opening so every shuffle-chapter can assume them):

- **The Two-Weather Law.** Flickering always lands him in the *opposite era* from where he last stood **at first** — but the world is *never the same one twice*. Medieval → Cyberpunk → a *different* Medieval → a *different* Cyberpunk. The eras alternate; the worlds don't repeat. (This is why the reader can shuffle: each chapter is a fresh world, so nothing forces an order.)
- **The Toll.** Each Flicker costs him a **memory of home** — a face, a smell, his mother's voice. He is trading his way *out* and, unless he stops, will forget who he's trying to get back to. The Shards are the counter-currency: each is a memory *re-forged* in a foreign shape.

This gives every shuffle-chapter the same stakes (get the Shard, resist the temptation to stay, don't Flicker for the wrong reason) without any chapter needing another.

## 3. FIXED OPENING — Chapter 0: "The Flicker"

> *(Locked. Always read first. Teaches the rules so every other chapter can assume them.)*

Kade Ellry is standing in the school corridor being told to choose a side in a fight he wants no part of, when the fluorescent lights stutter and the world **peels**. He does not faint. It is more like a page turning with him still standing on it.

He lands ankle-deep in cold mud under a sky the color of pewter, a **stone keep** on the hill and men in mail shouting a language he somehow understands. Before he can breathe he is *pulled sideways again* — and now it is **rain on neon**, a street canyon of holograms, a drone reading his retina and finding no file.

A woman in grey is watching him from a doorway. She says the only two things he will hear in every world, in every voice: *"You're the one who keeps arriving."* And: *"Everything you pick up here, you get to keep. Everything you leave, you lose."*

He learns the Toll the hard way: after the second Flicker he reaches for his mother's face and finds a smudge. He learns the shape of the power: **he cannot choose *where*, only *away*.** And he learns the goal, from the Grey Woman's last line before he Flickers again: *"Ten shards make a loom. A loom can weave a door home. Go and be caught by the world — that is the only way through it."*

*He Flickers. The reader is told: from here, the worlds come as they come. Read them in any order.* **Turn to any chapter.**

## 4. THE SHUFFLEABLE CORE — Ten Interchangeable Worlds

Five **Medieval (M)** and five **Cyberpunk (C)**, alternating eras but never repeating a world. Each is a self-contained short chapter. Each lists its **research anchor**, its **Shard**, and its **temptation** (the reason Kade might wrongly stay). Read any subset, in any order.

### 🏰 M-1 — "The Round Table That Wasn't" *(Arthurian myth)*
**Anchor:** Round Table equality, chivalric oath, the just-kingdom longing. [[8]](https://mythlok.com/world-mythologies/european/british/arthurian/)
Kade arrives at a Camelot *after* the king has died. The knights are voting on who sits where — and the Table, being round, refuses to have a head. They mistake Kade for a squire and beg him to break the tie. He learns that the Table's magic was never the king; it was the *promise to treat no seat as higher.* The Grey Woman is a cook who feeds him and says the oath aloud.
**Shard:** *The Unheaded Nail* — a nail from the Table's rim. **Truth:** *Fairness is a structure, not a ruler.*
**Temptation:** They offer him the empty throne. To take it is to end the switching — as a king who never goes home.

### 🌃 C-1 — "Ninety Seconds of You" *(Neuromancer / Altered Carbon lineage)*
**Anchor:** consciousness-as-data, corporate ownership of the self, neon-noir. [[12]](https://vanguardguardian.com/10-cyberpunk-novels-every-neuromancer-fan-should-read/) [[13]](https://academy.worldanvil.com/blog/cyberpunk-worldbuilding-guide)
A megacorp arcology where citizens rent back their own memories monthly. Kade, unfiled and therefore invisible to the system, is recruited by a child-hacker to steal a dying woman's last ninety seconds of remembered childhood before the corp repossesses it. The Grey Woman is the ad-hologram on every wall, glitching to warn him.
**Shard:** *A Cracked Memory-Chit.* **Truth:** *What they can bill you for, they can take — so carry the important things in your own head.* (This directly re-frames Kade's Toll.)
**Temptation:** The corp offers to *restore every memory of home he's lost* — if he signs his future memories over to them forever.

### 🏰 M-2 — "The Siege of the Salt Town" *(grounded historical medievalism — Kingdom Come / Witcher register)*
**Anchor:** 15th-c. realism — dueling, sieges, muddy politics, no magic. [[10]](https://www.pcgamesn.com/best-medieval-games) [[11]](https://gamerant.com/best-medieval-fantasy-games/)
A besieged town survives on a salt mine. Kade is taken for a spy by *both* sides. There is no chosen-one magic here, only a frightened bootmaker's daughter (the Grey Woman) who teaches him that in a real siege the hero is whoever keeps the well clean. He resolves it not with a sword but by brokering a water-sharing truce.
**Shard:** *A Fistful of Grey Salt.* **Truth:** *Survival is logistics; glory is a story told afterward.*
**Temptation:** The town wants to make him captain of the watch — safety, a bed, belonging.

### 🌃 C-2 — "Snow in the Server" *(Snow Crash lineage — virtual worlds)*
**Anchor:** a virus loose in a shared virtual reality; the metaverse street. [[13]](https://academy.worldanvil.com/blog/cyberpunk-worldbuilding-guide)
Kade Flickers *into* a virtual city inside a game — and can't tell if he's really there or a copy. A "snow" is deleting NPCs who don't know they're NPCs. The Grey Woman is a low-res courier who has been alive for exactly one day and knows it. Kade must decide whether a being who *feels* real deserves saving even if it's "just code."
**Shard:** *A Snowflake That Won't Melt* (a frozen data-packet). **Truth:** *Realness is measured by what a thing can lose, not what it's made of.*
**Temptation:** In here he can *edit* the world — including editing back his forgotten home. But it would only be a copy.

### 🏰 M-3 — "The Grail Is a Cup of Water" *(Grail quest as spiritual test)*
**Anchor:** the Grail as moral purity / spiritual enlightenment, not treasure. [[9]](https://www.medievalchronicles.com/medieval-legends-myths-and-tales/the-quest-for-the-holy-grail-uncovering-the-mystery-of-its-importance-to-knights/)
A monastery guards "the Grail." Every knight who has come demanded it and died of the demanding. The Grey Woman is a blind nun who lets Kade drink from a plain clay cup when he asks not for the relic but for water for a sick pilgrim. *That* was the Grail: the cup used for someone else.
**Shard:** *The Plain Clay Cup.* **Truth:** *The thing you quest for is worthless the moment you want it for yourself.*
**Temptation:** Keep questing for the "real" Grail forever — a noble reason to never go home.

### 🌃 C-3 — "The Union of Ghosts" *(megacorp vs. underclass)*
**Anchor:** corporations outranking governments; elite/underclass divide; information-is-power. [[13]](https://academy.worldanvil.com/blog/cyberpunk-worldbuilding-guide)
Delivery couriers wear collars that dock pay for slowness; the dead couriers' route-data still runs the network as unpaid "ghost labor." Kade helps organize a strike by *proving the ghosts are still working.* The Grey Woman is a retired courier whose voice is now the transit announcement system.
**Shard:** *A Cut Collar.* **Truth:** *Systems run on people they pretend aren't there; name them and the system blinks.*
**Temptation:** Management offers Kade a manager's badge — to be the one who *sets* the docking, not suffers it.

### 🏰 M-4 — "Morgan's Mirror" *(Morgan le Fay / magical antagonist-guide)*
**Anchor:** Morgan le Fay, magical figures who "guide and challenge." [[8]](https://mythlok.com/world-mythologies/european/british/arthurian/)
A sorceress keeps a mirror that shows you the life you'd have if you'd made every *safe* choice. Kade sees himself: never Flickered, never lost his mother's face — because he never left home, and also never became anyone. The Grey Woman is Morgan herself, testing whether he can look and still choose the hard road.
**Shard:** *A Sliver of the Mirror.* **Truth:** *The unlived safe life is a picture, not a place — you cannot move into it.*
**Temptation:** Morgan will let him *stay inside the mirror-life* — permanent, painless, and false.

### 🌃 C-4 — "The Model of Kade" *(identity / uploaded self — Altered Carbon)*
**Anchor:** consciousness transfer; questions of identity and mortality. [[12]](https://vanguardguardian.com/10-cyberpunk-novels-every-neuromancer-fan-should-read/)
A lab has been *predicting* Kade — a corporation studies "anomalous arrivals" and has trained an AI model of him to guess his next Flicker. He meets the model: a version of himself that has "lived" ten thousand simulated lives and is very tired. The Grey Woman is the lead researcher who has doubts. Kade must out-choose his own prediction.
**Shard:** *His Own Face on a Cracked Screen.* **Truth:** *You are not your pattern; you're the one thing in you that can still surprise the pattern.*
**Temptation:** The model offers to *take over* the Flickering so Kade can rest forever — letting the copy carry the burden home.

### 🏰 M-5 — "The Boy King's Regency" *(rise-and-fall of a just kingdom)*
**Anchor:** longing for a just kingdom; the fragility of good rule. [[8]](https://mythlok.com/world-mythologies/european/british/arthurian/)
A child inherits a throne; three regents circle. Kade, close to the boy king's age, is asked to be his only honest friend. The lesson is about *power's loneliness* and refusing to become a fourth manipulator. The Grey Woman is the boy king's dead mother's handmaid.
**Shard:** *A Signet with No Seal Cut Into It* (authority not yet abused). **Truth:** *The measure of power is what you refuse to do with it.*
**Temptation:** Become regent — rule "well" — and forget he was ever a boy from a corridor.

### 🌃 C-5 — "The Last Analog Radio" *(retro-tech resistance; Deus Ex register)*
**Anchor:** hackers as protagonists; the neon-noir information war. [[13]](https://academy.worldanvil.com/blog/cyberpunk-worldbuilding-guide)
In a totally surveilled city, one un-networked device survives: a hand-crank radio that can't be traced *because* it can't connect. Kade helps a girl broadcast the one thing the city has never heard — silence, then a true story. The Grey Woman is a voice on the static who has been broadcasting to no one for years, just in case someone arrived.
**Shard:** *A Length of Copper Wire.* **Truth:** *The unconnected thing is the free thing; to be untraceable is to be, briefly, unowned.*
**Temptation:** Go fully off-grid *here* — untraceable, safe, and permanently lost to home.

A reader who happens to pair era-mirrors gets a bonus resonance; a reader who doesn't still gets a complete arc. **No pairing is required** — that's what keeps it shuffleable.

## 5. FIXED FINALE — Chapter Ω: "The Loom"

> *(Locked. Always read last. Written to harvest whatever Shards the reader collected — 1 to 10 — and always conclude.)*

Kade Flickers one final time and lands nowhere — a grey room with a loom and the Grey Woman finally wearing no disguise, because there is nothing left to disguise her *as*. "You came back," she says. "They almost never come back. Most keep Flickering until they've spent the last memory and there's no one left to go home *to*."

She nods at his pockets. "Empty them."

**[Harvest passage — conditional.]** Here the prose names *only the Shards the reader actually holds.* If the reader collected the Clay Cup, the finale says: *He set down a plain clay cup, and the loom drew a single true thread of water-colored light.* If they collected the Cut Collar: *He set down a severed collar, and the loom pulled a thread the exact grey of people no one counts.* Each Shard the reader earned becomes one thread. **The Shards they never collected are simply never mentioned** — the loom weaves with the thread it's given. *(This is R5: graceful degradation. One thread or ten, the loom still weaves a cloth.)*

Then the reveal that makes any collection sufficient:

> "You thought you needed all ten," the Grey Woman says. "You never did. The loom doesn't weave a door out of *how many*. It weaves it out of *how you chose*. Every world offered you a place to stay. Every world, you left something you wanted for something that was true. **The door isn't ten shards wide. It's one refusal wide, repeated as often as you managed it.**"

The cloth on the loom shows Kade his own home — not restored perfectly (some faces are still smudged; the Toll is real and permanent) but **real, and enough.** His mother's voice comes back not as a recording but because he chooses to walk toward it.

Here is who the Grey Woman was: **she is Kade — an older Kade**, the one who learned to weave the door and then stayed behind at the loom to catch the next arrival, to say *"You're the one who keeps arriving,"* so that no boy would Flicker alone until his memories ran out. She has been every guide in every world because she has *been to* every world. "Someone has to be the constant," she says, "for the ones still shuffling through."

Kade is offered her seat at the loom. And here the novel closes its own loop: **he refuses one last time** — the eleventh refusal, the one that isn't in any shuffleable chapter and can't be. He will not stay, not even to be kind, because staying to guide others would be one more noble excuse never to go home (M-3's truth, C-5's truth, weaponized against the very ending). Instead he does the thing the older Kade never dared: he **takes her hand and pulls her through the door too.** Two of him go home. The loom goes quiet, waiting — but this time with no one chained to it.

Last lines:

> He stepped through into a school corridor where the lights had only just finished stuttering. No time had passed. Someone was still waiting for him to choose a side in a fight that didn't matter. He looked at them and, for the first time in ten thousand miles of worlds, he simply said: *"No. I'm going home."*
>
> And the astonishing thing — the thing no castle or neon canyon had ever let him do — was that he could. He walked. The lights held steady. He did not Flicker.
>
> He had run out of worlds to be caught by. He had, at last, caught himself.

**Why this finale satisfies regardless of reading order:** the meaning was never "collect all ten." It was "**every world tempted you to stay; every world you chose the true thing over the wanted thing.**" That pattern is present in *any* subset the reader read — one chapter or all ten — so the ending lands for everyone. The fixed opening taught the rules; the shuffleable middle *is* the theme repeated in variation; the fixed finale *names* the theme and closes it. This is precisely Johnson's structure — fixed first and last, free middle — put in service of a moral spine instead of a memory. [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unfortunates)

## 6. Reading-Order Guidance (put this in the book's front matter)

Following the *Hopscotch* tradition of offering routes, the physical/ebook edition should ship with three suggested paths plus permission to ignore them: [[3]](https://nicolefu.co.uk/2021/09/18/5-novels-with-non-linear-narrative-structures/)

1. **The Shuffle (true random):** Read Ch. 0, then the ten worlds in any order you draw them, then Ch. Ω. *(Purest Johnson mode.)* [[1]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Unfortunates)
2. **The Alternator:** After Ch. 0, alternate M/C/M/C to feel Kade's "two-weather" law as rhythm.
3. **The Mirror Path:** Read the thematic pairs back-to-back (M-1↔C-3, C-1↔M-4, M-3↔C-5, etc.) for maximal resonance.
4. **The Short Read:** Ch. 0 → *any three* worlds → Ch. Ω. The graceful-degradation finale guarantees this still resolves — useful for younger or time-pressed readers, and a live demonstration that the design works.

**Production note:** an ebook can *shuffle automatically* and can make Ch. Ω's conditional harvest passage **truly dynamic** (rendering only the Shards the reader visited) — a modern, software-native realization of Johnson's book-in-a-box that his 1969 printing could only approximate. [[2]](https://www.ndbooks.com/book/the-unfortunates/)

## 7. Why This Meets Every Requirement

| Your requirement | How the design satisfies it |
|------------------|------------------------------|
| Boy switches between medieval past & cyberpunk future | Kade's **Flicker** power, governed by the **Two-Weather Law** (eras alternate). |
| Each switch → a *completely different* world within the theme | Ten distinct worlds (5 medieval, 5 cyberpunk); **R3** forbids world-repetition and cross-naming. |

## 8. Appendix — Author's Craft Checklist (per shuffle-chapter)

1. **Open cold** in ≤120 words: a Flicker just happened; establish era, danger, and one sensory hook.
2. **Introduce the Grey Woman** in local costume within the first third.
3. **Present the temptation to stay** explicitly by the midpoint.
4. **Resolve by choosing the true thing over the wanted thing** — never by combat alone.
5. **Hand Kade exactly one Shard** (object + one-line truth) in the final beat.
6. **End on a Flicker**, era unnamed, so the next chapter (whatever it is) follows cleanly.
7. **Never reference another world by name** — only "another life, another switch." *(R3)*
8. **Word budget:** 2,500–4,000 words each, so any subset reads in one sitting.
