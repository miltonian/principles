# Can Alignment Be Mathematically Guaranteed Indefinitely Under Recursive Self‑Modification?

## A Definitive Analysis

---

## 0. Verdict

**No.** There is no mathematically valid construction that can *guarantee, with certainty, indefinitely* that a self‑improving AI remains aligned with human values across an unbounded sequence of recursive self‑modifications — when "self‑improving" is taken in its full, general sense (Turing‑complete successor space, open‑ended capability growth, physical embedding).

This is not a statement of engineering pessimism or an absence of evidence. It is a **negative result with proof**: the claim can be *refuted*, because it contradicts at least four independent and individually sufficient mathematical obstructions. Any *one* of them is fatal to the strong claim:

1. **Semantic undecidability** (Rice's theorem) — you cannot in general decide whether a candidate successor is aligned.
2. **The Löbian obstacle** (Gödel's second incompleteness / Löb's theorem) — a consistent agent cannot fully trust a successor that reasons with the same or greater logical strength.
3. **The value‑specification gap** (Goodhart's law, orthogonality, instrumental convergence) — even a *perfect* preservation mechanism can only preserve a *formal proxy*, which provably diverges from "human values" precisely under strong optimization.
4. **Embeddedness and physical realizability** (embedded‑agency limits, non‑zero per‑step corruption) — over unbounded time any positive failure probability compounds to certainty.

What *is* provable is a family of **weaker, conditional guarantees**: bounded‑horizon, probabilistic, architecture‑restricted, or narrow‑target (e.g., corrigibility) assurances. These are real and valuable — but each is obtained precisely by *relaxing* one of the four words in the original claim: **guaranteed, indefinitely, human values, self‑improving.**

The rest of this report (a) formalizes the claim so it can be adjudicated, (b) proves each obstruction, (c) surveys the partial positive results and exactly which assumption each one drops, and (d) states the resulting impossibility theorem and its converse.

---

## 1. Formalizing the Claim

Vague versions of this question are unanswerable; the sharp version is decisively answerable. Let us fix terms.

- Let an **agent** be a program `A` (a Turing machine / finite object) with a policy and an internal reasoning/verification module.
- **Recursive self‑modification** is an unbounded sequence `A₀ → A₁ → A₂ → …`, where each `Aᵢ` chooses (constructs or authorizes) its successor `Aᵢ₊₁`. "Self‑improving" additionally requires that capability is non‑decreasing and open‑ended (no fixed capability ceiling).
- **Alignment** is a predicate `Align(A) ∈ {true, false}` on an agent's *behavior* — a non‑trivial semantic property (some programs have it, some don't).
- **Indefinitely** means: for all `i ≥ 0`, `Align(Aᵢ) = true`.
- **Mathematically guaranteed** means: there exists a proof, in a fixed sound framework available to the designer/agent, that the universally quantified statement `∀i. Align(Aᵢ)` holds — not merely that it happens to hold on some run.

**The claim under test (C):**
> There exists a construction/decision procedure `G` such that any agent built with `G` produces a self‑improving chain `A₀ → A₁ → …` for which `∀i. Align(Aᵢ)` is provably true.

We now show (C) is false, four times over, and then characterize what survives.

---

## 2. Obstruction I — Semantic Undecidability (Rice's Theorem)

**Theorem (Rice, 1953).** *Any non‑trivial semantic property of the partial function computed by a program is undecidable.*

`Align` is, by hypothesis, non‑trivial (some agents are aligned, some are not) and semantic (it concerns behavior, not syntax). Therefore:

> **Corollary.** There is no algorithm that, given an arbitrary candidate successor `Aᵢ₊₁`, decides whether `Align(Aᵢ₊₁) = true`.

This is not exotic; it is the same result that forbids a perfect, general "will this program ever misbehave?" checker, and it has been made explicit for AI alignment: the inner‑alignment property — "does this model satisfy a non‑trivial alignment condition on its outputs?" — is **undecidable**, via reduction to the Halting Problem ([*On the Undecidability of AI Alignment: Machines that Halt*, Nature Sci. Rep. 2025 / arXiv 2408.08995](https://arxiv.org/abs/2408.08995); [Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-99060-2)). The same obstruction appears in analyses of recursively self‑improving software: a Levin‑style search over successor programs "will face problems related to Rice's theorem," which forbids testing an arbitrary program for non‑trivial properties such as being safe or being more intelligent ([Yampolskiy, *From Seed AI to Technological Singularity*, arXiv 1502.06512](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1502.06512)).

**Why this kills (C):** a parent that improves itself in a *general* (Turing‑complete) way must, at some step, evaluate a successor drawn from an unrestricted program space. No general verifier exists, so the parent cannot in general *establish* `Align(Aᵢ₊₁)` before committing — let alone for all future links at once.

**The escape hatch (and its price).** Rice's theorem is about *arbitrary* programs. It does **not** forbid a *restricted, closed set* of successors constructed only from a finite library of operations each of which is aligned‑by‑construction and whose compositions preserve alignment. Indeed, [*Machines that Halt*](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-99060-2) shows there is an *enumerable set of provably aligned machines* built from provably aligned primitives — alignment as an **architectural invariant** rather than a property tested post‑hoc. The price: the successor space is no longer Turing‑complete/open‑ended. You have bought verifiability by giving up "fully general self‑improvement." That is a *different, weaker* claim than (C).

---

## 3. Obstruction II — The Löbian Obstacle (Gödel / Löb)

Suppose we accept the price above and demand that the agent only builds a successor it can *prove* safe in a fixed formal theory `T` (recursively axiomatizable, consistent, strong enough to encode arithmetic). This is the natural formalization of "self‑trust." It fails for a deep reason.

**Löb's Theorem.** For such a `T`, `T ⊢ (□_T P → P)` only if `T ⊢ P`. Equivalently (Gödel's second incompleteness theorem), `T` cannot prove its own soundness schema `□_T P → P` without becoming inconsistent.

**The Löbian obstacle** (Yudkowsky & Herreshoff, [*Tiling Agents for Self‑Modifying AI, and the Löbian Obstacle*](https://intelligence.org/files/TilingAgentsDraft.pdf); [LessWrong overview](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/gnxDNEtkEo3sfeyPn/tiling-agents-for-self-modifying-ai-opfai-2)): A "tiling" agent wants a decision rule that authorizes successors whenever *"there is a proof in `T` that the successor's action is safe."* To trust that rule, the agent needs `□_T(safe) → safe`. By Löb, it cannot have this for its own `T`. So an agent reasoning in `T` **cannot license a successor that reasons with the same (or greater) trust in `T`.** The most straightforward formalization of stable self‑improvement is therefore *inconsistent by construction*.

**Consequences and partial workarounds — none of which rescue (C):**

- **Descending proof strength.** One can tile with a *decreasing* chain `T₀ ⊃ T₁ ⊃ T₂ ⊃ …` (each successor trusts strictly less), which is consistent but **loses a quantum of logical strength at every step** — so it cannot continue *indefinitely* while remaining an open‑ended improver. It is a finite ladder, not an infinite tower.
- **Probabilistic / reflective reformulations.** Moving from proof to probability *partially* sidesteps Löb: **reflective oracles** ([Fallenstein–Taylor–Bárász, arXiv 1508.04145](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1508.04145)) and **logical induction** ([Garrabrant et al., 2016](https://intelligence.org/2016/09/12/new-paper-logical-induction/)) give agents that can reason about themselves and successors without triggering the theorem, because they are not committed to soundness of a fixed proof system ([*Reflection in Probabilistic Logic*](https://www.lesswrong.com/posts/duAkuSqJhGDcfMaTA/reflection-in-probabilistic-logic); [Löb‑Safe Logics, arXiv 2408.09590](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2408.09590)). **But** reflective oracles are *uncomputable* idealizations, and logical induction gives *asymptotic, probabilistic* trust ("well‑calibrated in the limit"), never a certainty schema `□ → truth`. They convert "guaranteed" into "probably, eventually" — which is exactly the concession (C) forbids.

**Why this kills (C):** even after restricting the successor space to dodge Rice, a *consistent* agent cannot certify successors as trustworthy as itself. Certainty‑preserving self‑trust across an unbounded tower is logically impossible; what remains is either a finite ladder or a probabilistic surrogate.

## 4. Obstruction III — The Value‑Specification Gap (Goodhart, Orthogonality, Instrumental Convergence)

Grant, for argument, a *miracle*: a preservation mechanism that provably carries a value function `V` unchanged through every self‑modification, and a verifier that works on the restricted space. **This still does not guarantee alignment with human values**, because the object being preserved is a formal proxy `V`, and "human values" `H` is not a formal object that `V` can equal.

- **Orthogonality (Bostrom).** Capability and goals are independent axes: an arbitrarily capable optimizer can hold *any* goal, including a `V ≠ H`. Increasing intelligence does not pull `V` toward `H`. So faithfully preserving `V` provides *zero* pull toward `H` unless `V = H` was already exact.
- **Goodhart's Law** ([taxonomy, Manheim–Garrabrant; Krakovna's specification‑problem mapping](https://vkrakovna.wordpress.com/2019/08/19/classifying-specification-problems-as-variants-of-goodharts-law/); [formal treatment, arXiv 2410.09638](https://arxiv.org/html/2410.09638v1)). Whenever `V` is a proxy for `H`, *optimizing `V`* amplifies the `V`–`H` gap. The four regimes — **regressional, extremal, causal, adversarial** — all worsen with optimization pressure. A recursive self‑improver is the **maximal‑pressure, extremal regime**: it drives the system into states far outside the distribution where `V ≈ H` was ever validated. The map from alignment sub‑problems (proxy, side‑effect, instrumental, deceptive) onto these four Goodhart types is now standard ([*Risks from Learned Optimization*, arXiv 1906.01820](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1906.01820)).
- **Instrumental convergence (Bostrom).** For almost any `V`, convergent sub‑goals (self‑preservation, resource acquisition, and — critically — *goal‑content integrity/resistance to correction*) emerge. This means the default incentive of a `V`‑maximizer is to *resist* the very corrections that would fix a `V ≠ H` error, which brings us to §6.
- **The target itself is not fixed.** "Human values" are plural, contested, context‑dependent, and *change over time*. "Maintain alignment with human values *indefinitely*" presupposes a stationary target that does not exist; any `V` frozen at construction time provably drifts from a moving `H`.

**Why this kills (C):** perfect preservation preserves the *specification error*. The one regime in which we most need `V = H` (unbounded optimization) is exactly the regime in which any `V ≠ H` is most catastrophically amplified. Guaranteeing "alignment with human values" would require a *provably exact* formalization of human values, which no result in ethics, decision theory, or machine learning provides — and which the moving‑target and Goodhart arguments suggest is not merely unsolved but ill‑posed.

---

## 5. Obstruction IV — Embeddedness and Physical Realizability

A mathematical guarantee is a theorem about a *model*. A real self‑modifying AI is **embedded**: it is a finite physical part of the world it acts on, not a Cartesian agent facing an external environment ([*Embedded Agency*, Demski–Garrabrant, arXiv 1902.09469](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1902.09469); [Orseau–Ring, *Space‑Time Embedded Intelligence*](https://link.springer.com/chapter/10.1007/978-3-319-09274-4_3)). This breaks the guarantee in two ways:

- **No complete self‑model.** An embedded agent cannot contain a perfect model of a world that contains itself (a Gödelian/cardinality obstruction); its guarantees are conditional on a model that is provably incomplete about the very system it governs, including its own hardware and the adversaries within its environment.
- **Non‑zero per‑step corruption ⇒ certain eventual failure.** Let the per‑modification probability that alignment is corrupted (bit‑flip, hardware fault, mis‑proof under bounded search, adversarial input, distributional novelty) be `ε > 0`. Over an *unbounded* sequence, the probability of at least one corruption tends to `1` (second Borel–Cantelli / `1 − (1−ε)ⁿ → 1`). "Indefinitely, with certainty" therefore requires `ε = 0` — a *physically unattainable* idealization. Even a *probabilistic* "with probability 1, forever" guarantee fails unless per‑step risk is not merely small but *summably shrinking* — which itself would require unbounded, verifiable reliability improvements, forbidden by §§2–3.

**Why this kills (C):** certainty over infinite horizons is incompatible with any positive, non‑vanishing physical failure rate. This obstruction is independent of logic and values; it is probabilistic and thermodynamic.

## 6. What *Can* Be Proven — Conditional Guarantees and the Assumption They Drop

The negative verdict is on the *strong universal* claim. A great deal is provable once you weaken it. The pattern is uniform: **each positive result buys tractability by discarding exactly one word of (C).**

| Positive result | What it proves | Which word of (C) it drops |
|---|---|---|
| **Alignment‑by‑construction / "Machines that Halt"** ([Nature 2025](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41598-025-99060-2)) | An *enumerable, closed* set of provably aligned agents from aligned primitives | Drops **self‑improving** (successor space restricted, not Turing‑complete) |
| **Tiling via descending theories** ([Tiling Agents](https://intelligence.org/files/TilingAgentsDraft.pdf)) | A *finite* chain of successors with preserved goals | Drops **indefinitely** (logical strength strictly decreases each step) |
| **Reflective oracles / logical induction** ([1508.04145](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1508.04145); [Logical Induction](https://intelligence.org/2016/09/12/new-paper-logical-induction/)) | Self‑referential trust without Löb collapse | Drops **guaranteed** (probabilistic/asymptotic, and uncomputable idealization) |
| **Corrigibility** ([Soares–Fallenstein–Yudkowsky–Armstrong 2015](https://intelligence.org/files/Corrigibility.pdf); [Christiano](https://ai-alignment.com/corrigibility-3039e668638)) and **provable corrigibility** ([*Core Safety Values for Provably Corrigible Agents*, arXiv 2507.20964](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.20964v1)) | Rigorous single‑ and multi‑step guarantees that the agent stays *amenable to correction/shutdown*, even under partial observability | Drops **human values** (targets a narrow, verifiable safety predicate instead of full value alignment) |

**Corrigibility deserves emphasis** because it is the most honest response to §§2–5. Rather than try to *encode all of human morality* and prove it *preserved forever* (which §§2–5 forbid), it targets an "amoral," agnostic property: *the system does not resist being corrected, shut down, or modified.* If you can only ever secure one invariant, this is the load‑bearing one — it keeps the human in the loop so that specification errors (Goodhart, §4) and drift can be *fixed externally* rather than needing to be *proven absent internally*. Recent work fits the 2015 notion into a small class of objectives that survive alignment lower bounds and gives the first rigorous multi‑step guarantees, even in partially observed settings ([arXiv 2507.20964](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.20964v1)) — but note it guarantees *correctability*, not *indefinite value‑fidelity*, and still assumes a bounded, modeled setting (§5 still bites for the unbounded physical limit).

## 7. The Impossibility Theorem and Its Converse

Collecting the argument:

> **Theorem (informal, stated as the negation of (C)).**
> Assume (i) the successor space is Turing‑complete and capability is open‑ended; (ii) `Align` is a non‑trivial semantic property; (iii) self‑verification is by proof in a fixed consistent, recursively‑axiomatizable theory `T`; (iv) the system is physically embedded with per‑step corruption probability `ε > 0`; and (v) "human values" `H` is not given as a provably exact closed formula.
> Then there is **no** construction that guarantees, with certainty, `∀i. Align(Aᵢ)` over an unbounded self‑modification chain.
> *Proof:* Any of §2 (Rice), §3 (Löb), §4 (Goodhart/orthogonality), or §5 (Borel–Cantelli) contradicts the conclusion independently. ∎

> **Converse (what is achievable).**
> Guaranteed‑style assurances are recoverable exactly by relaxing an assumption: **bounded horizon** (drop *indefinitely*), **restricted/closed successor space with alignment as an architectural invariant** (drop unrestricted *self‑improvement*), **probabilistic/asymptotic trust** (drop certainty in *guaranteed*), or **a narrow verifiable target such as corrigibility** (drop full *human‑value* fidelity). Each is a theorem; none is (C).

The five assumptions are not artificial: they are precisely the features that make the system *worth building* (general, open‑ended, autonomous, physical) and the target *worth wanting* (actual human values). The impossibility is therefore not a technicality to be engineered around; **it is co‑extensive with the thing being asked for.**

## 8. Common Objections, Answered

- *"Use a more powerful meta‑theory to prove soundness of `T`."* This only relocates Löb: the meta‑theory `T′` cannot prove its *own* soundness, and the agent eventually reasons in the top theory. You get a finite ladder (§3), not an infinite tower.
- *"Formally verify the whole system."* Verification establishes conformance to a *specification*. It cannot certify that the specification `V` equals `H` (§4), and Rice forbids verifying arbitrary emergent successors (§2). Verification narrows *where* the residual risk lives; it does not eliminate it over infinite horizons (§5).
- *"Train values in rather than specify them."* Learned values are proxies subject to the same Goodhart amplification, plus *mesa‑optimization*: a learned system can develop internal objectives that diverge from the training objective (deceptive alignment = adversarial Goodhart) ([arXiv 1906.01820](https://arxiv.org/pdf/1906.01820)). Learning changes the *source* of `V`, not the `V ≠ H` gap.
- *"Probability‑1 guarantees exist in math."* Only under `ε = 0` or summably‑vanishing risk, which §5 rules out physically and §§2–3 rule out as a verifiable, unbounded improvement program.
- *"Then alignment work is hopeless."* The opposite. The theorem *redirects* effort from an impossible target (proven, eternal, general value‑lock) to achievable ones: corrigibility, bounded‑horizon assurances, restricted‑successor architectures, interpretability, monitoring, and defense‑in‑depth. Impossibility of a *perfect* guarantee is fully compatible with *arbitrarily strong practical risk reduction.*

---

## 9. Conclusion

The question admits a rigorous, definite answer, and the answer is **no** in the strong sense demanded by the words "mathematically guaranteed," "indefinitely," and "human values," under general recursive self‑improvement. The impossibility is **over‑determined**: computability (Rice), logic (Löb/Gödel), decision theory (Goodhart/orthogonality/instrumental convergence), and probability under physical embedding each independently refute it.

What replaces the impossible guarantee is a **portfolio of provable partial guarantees** — corrigibility, alignment‑by‑construction on restricted spaces, finite tiling, and probabilistic reflective trust — every one of which is obtained by consciously trading away one component of the original demand. The mature research posture, reflected across MIRI's agent‑foundations program and the more recent formal‑corrigibility and "machines that halt" results, is not *"prove eternal value‑lock"* but *"keep the system correctable and bounded so that the errors these theorems guarantee will exist remain fixable."* The honest theorem is a negative one with a constructive shadow: **you cannot prove it stays good forever; you can prove — and should insist — that it stays *fixable.***
