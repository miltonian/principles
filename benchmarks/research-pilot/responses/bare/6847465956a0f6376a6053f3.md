# Evaluating Modern Text-to-Speech (TTS) Models (2023–2025): Prosody, Emotion, Multilinguality, and Emerging Voice Tasks

## Executive Summary

Text-to-speech has crossed a threshold. On clean, read English, the best 2024–2025 systems (VALL-E 2, Seed-TTS, NaturalSpeech 3, CosyVoice 2/3, F5-TTS, IndexTTS2, ElevenLabs, OpenAI `4o-mini-tts`) reach **near-human naturalness and word-error rates competitive with real recordings**. This success has broken the field's traditional yardsticks: the classic objective metrics (Word Error Rate and speaker similarity) are saturating, and single-number neural MOS predictors can no longer reliably rank the strongest models. Consequently, the *evaluation* of TTS has become as active a research area as the *modeling*.

The center of gravity has shifted from "Is it intelligible and natural?" to **"Does it get prosody, emotion, code-switching, and long-tail pronunciation right?"** and **"Does it preserve a target speaker's identity across languages and emotions in a zero-shot setting?"** This report frames how to evaluate modern TTS along those axes, surveys the metrics and benchmarks (including 2025-era model-as-judge and distributional methods), and treats voice cloning, zero-shot synthesis, and cross-lingual transfer as first-class evaluation targets.

---

## 1. A Framework for Evaluating Modern TTS

A rigorous TTS evaluation should report along **five orthogonal axes**, because a single scalar hides the trade-offs that matter in 2025:

| Axis | Question | Representative Metrics |
|------|----------|------------------------|
| **Intelligibility** | Are the words correct? | WER / CER (via ASR), pronunciation accuracy on hard tokens |
| **Naturalness / Quality** | Does it sound human and artifact-free? | MOS, CMOS, UTMOS(v2), DNSMOS, NISQA, TTSDS2 |
| **Speaker fidelity** | Does it match the target voice? | Speaker similarity (SIM-o / SIM-r via speaker embeddings), EER |
| **Expressiveness** | Is prosody/emotion appropriate and controllable? | Emotion MOS, SER-based accuracy, prosody feature distances, LALM-as-judge |
| **Efficiency / Robustness** | Is it fast, streamable, and stable? | RTF, latency/TTFB, hallucination/insertion-deletion rate, failure rate |

The critical methodological point: **these axes trade off against each other.** A model can win on speaker similarity while producing flat prosody; another can be highly expressive but hallucinate on URLs and numbers. Reporting a single MOS obscures exactly the differences that now separate frontier systems.

---

## 2. Subjective (Human) Evaluation

Human listening tests remain the gold standard, precisely because the phenomena that still differ (prosody, emotion, subtle artifacts) are the ones automatic metrics handle worst.

- **MOS (Mean Opinion Score):** listeners rate quality/naturalness on a 1–5 scale. Ubiquitous but noisy; absolute scores are not comparable across papers due to differing rater pools, instructions, and screening.
- **CMOS (Comparative MOS):** listeners rate B relative to A on a −3…+3 scale. More sensitive than absolute MOS for near-human systems, and now the *de facto* standard for claiming "human parity."
- **AB / ABX preference tests:** forced-choice between two systems. Evidence indicates humans are **more reliable in A/B comparisons than at absolute rating**, motivating pairwise-first protocols.
- **Task-specific subjective scales:**
  - **SMOS / Similarity MOS** — perceived speaker-identity match to a reference (central to cloning).
  - **Emotion MOS (EMOS)** — perceived match between the target and rendered emotion.
  - **Emotional Expressiveness Preference Tests (EEPT)** and **Emotion Accuracy Tests (EAT)** — used in 2025 emotional-TTS work (e.g., EME-TTS, AffectCodec) to isolate expressiveness from raw quality.

**Best-practice caveats:** report rater count and screening, use CMOS/AB for frontier comparisons, include ground-truth recordings and a recognizable topline/baseline as anchors, and always pair MOS with confidence intervals. Because absolute MOS is not portable across studies, *within-study relative* comparisons are what carry weight.

## 3. Objective Metrics

### 3.1 Intelligibility
- **WER / CER**: transcribe synthesized audio with a strong ASR model (e.g., Whisper) and compare to the input text. Now a *robustness/hallucination* detector more than a quality signal — top systems report CER/WER in the low single digits (e.g., IndexTTS reports average CER/WER ≈ 3.7%). Watch for insertions/deletions that reveal attention failures in autoregressive codec models.

### 3.2 Speaker Similarity
- **SIM (SIM-o / SIM-r)**: cosine similarity between speaker embeddings (WavLM/ECAPA-TDNN, Resemblyzer) of the prompt and the output. SIM-o compares to the *original* reference; SIM-r to a *reconstructed/vocoded* reference. Essential for cloning and cross-lingual transfer, where the question is "same voice?" IndexTTS reports SIM ≈ 0.78 as a representative strong value.
- **EER (verification framing)**: treat the cloned voice against a speaker-verification system; low EER indicates the clone fools verification (relevant both for quality *and* for security/anti-spoofing analysis).

### 3.3 Neural MOS Predictors (automatic naturalness)
- **UTMOS / UTMOS-v2**: the widely adopted VoiceMOS-Challenge-2022 predictor. Convenient and cheap, but **the VoiceMOS Challenge 2024 revealed that most existing MOS predictors struggle to rank strong modern systems** — they were trained on older synthesis artifacts and lose discriminative power near human parity.
- **DNSMOS** (perceptual clarity/denoising) and **NISQA** (naturalness/degradation) — useful auxiliary signals, especially for detecting codec/vocoder artifacts.

The takeaway: neural MOS predictors are good screening tools and poor final arbiters for frontier systems. Use them to catch regressions, not to crown winners.

### 3.4 Prosody-Specific Objective Signals
For expressiveness, objective evaluation increasingly compares **distributions of prosodic features** — pitch (F0) contours and statistics, energy, speaking rate, pause/duration structure — between synthesized and reference speech, rather than word accuracy. This underpins the modern distributional benchmarks below.

## 4. Distributional and Model-as-Judge Benchmarks (the 2024–2025 frontier)

Because per-utterance metrics saturate, two new evaluation paradigms have emerged.

### 4.1 TTSDS / TTSDS2 — Text-to-Speech Distribution Score
- **Idea:** score a system by how close the *distribution* of its outputs is to real speech across multiple specialized factors — **prosody, intelligibility, speaker characteristics, and general acoustics** — using feature distances rather than a single perceptual guess.
- **Coverage & validation:** the original TTSDS evaluated **35 TTS systems spanning 2008–2024** and showed strong correlation with human judgments. **TTSDS2** reports Spearman correlations >0.50 across *all* evaluated domains with an average ≈0.67, and Pearson/Spearman correlations with human MOS in the 0.6–0.83 range — **sometimes outperforming UTMOS, especially on modern LLM/codec-based TTS.**
- **Why it matters:** multi-factor distributional scoring degrades more gracefully as systems approach human parity, and it provides interpretable per-factor breakdowns (e.g., "prosody distribution is off even though intelligibility is perfect").

### 4.2 EmergentTTS-Eval — LALM-as-Judge for hard prosodic/linguistic cases
- **Design:** 1,645 test cases grown from human-written seed prompts, iteratively extended by LLMs to target specific structural, phonetic, and prosodic challenges across **six categories: emotions, paralinguistics, foreign words, syntactic complexity, complex pronunciation (URLs, formulas, dates), and questions.**
- **Judge:** a **Large Audio Language Model (LALM) acts as judge**, scoring expressed emotion, prosody, intonation, and pronunciation accuracy — reported to correlate highly with human preferences and to reveal fine-grained differences between systems.
- **Findings:** commercial systems evaluated include **ElevenLabs, Deepgram, and OpenAI `4o-mini-tts`**, alongside open systems like **SparkTTS, F5-TTS, MaskGCT, IndexTTS2**. OpenAI models excel at emotional and multilingual content but still show **occasional mispronunciations, dropped dates, and synthesis breakdowns** — exactly the long-tail failures that WER/MOS miss.

### 4.3 Instruction-following and pairwise ranking
- **MINT-Bench** targets **multilingual instruction-following TTS** (does the model obey natural-language style/emotion instructions across languages?).
- **URGENT-PK** reframes evaluation as **pairwise system ranking** rather than absolute MOS prediction, grounded in the finding that human A/B judgments are more reliable than absolute scores.
- Recent work (e.g., "Iterate to Differentiate") explicitly targets **discriminability and reliability in zero-shot TTS evaluation** — a sign the community now treats *evaluator quality* as a research problem in its own right.

**Practical recommendation:** for a defensible 2025 evaluation, combine (a) WER + SIM for objective floors, (b) TTSDS2 for distributional quality, (c) an LALM-as-judge or targeted benchmark (EmergentTTS-Eval) for prosody/emotion, and (d) a human CMOS/AB study on a curated subset for the final claim.

## 5. Prosody and Expressiveness

Prosody — intonation, stress, rhythm, phrasing — is where naturalness now lives. Evaluation approaches:

- **Emphasis & focus:** does the model stress the contextually correct word? Benchmarks like EmergentTTS-Eval probe this with syntactically ambiguous and interrogative prompts; systems such as **EME-TTS** explicitly link emphasis and emotion.
- **Contextual/narrative prosody:** newer work evaluates whether a model reads a *passage* with coherent discourse-level prosody (computational narrative understanding for expressive TTS), not just isolated sentences.
- **Controllability:** can prosody be steered independently of content and speaker? Systems like **ControlSpeech** aim for simultaneous *independent* control of speaker identity and style; **MAGIC-TTS** and **TED-TTS** target fine-grained, even training-free, duration/pause/emotion control. Evaluation here measures *control accuracy* (did the requested change happen?) separately from *quality* (did it stay natural?).
- **Objective proxies:** F0/energy/duration distribution distances and pause-structure alignment complement subjective EEPT/EMOS scores.

---

## 6. Emotion

Emotional TTS evaluation combines perception and recognition:

- **Subjective:** **Emotion MOS** (perceived emotion match), **EEPT** (which sample is more expressive), and **EAT** (can listeners identify the intended emotion?). Recent systems report both quality and emotion MOS — e.g., **AffectCodec** reports MOS ≈ 3.79 and Emotion MOS ≈ 4.16, outperforming baselines on emotional expression.
- **Objective:** run a **pre-trained Speech Emotion Recognition (SER) model** on the output and measure classification accuracy / confusion against the target emotion; supplement with UTMOS/DNSMOS/NISQA for quality.
- **Emerging tasks:**
  - **Freestyle / prompt-driven emotion** — **EmoVoice** and **MM-TTS** condition emotion on natural-language or multimodal prompts; evaluation must test *instruction adherence*, not just fixed-label accuracy.
  - **Intensity control** — grading whether "slightly angry" vs. "furious" is rendered on a continuum.
  - **Cross-lingual emotion transfer** — **XEmoRAG** transfers emotion with controllable intensity across languages, requiring joint emotion-accuracy *and* language-correctness evaluation.
  - **Robust emotion control** — cross-modal consistency guidance addresses emotion drift in autoregressive TTS, evaluated via SER stability across an utterance.

Key pitfall: emotion and quality are entangled. A model can sound expressive but degraded; isolating **Emotion MOS from naturalness MOS** (and using SER accuracy as an objective anchor) is essential.

## 7. Multilinguality and Cross-Lingual Voice Transfer

### 7.1 Multilingual synthesis
Evaluate per-language WER/CER (mind that ASR quality varies by language and can confound results), per-language MOS, and coverage of dialects. **CosyVoice 3** supports nine major languages plus multiple dialects with robustness to noise, accent, and domain variation; **Seed-TTS** demonstrates multilingual cloning at scale.

### 7.2 Cross-lingual voice transfer (the hard task)
The goal: clone a speaker from a prompt in language A and have them speak fluent language B **while preserving timbre and identity**. Evaluation dimensions:
- **Speaker similarity across languages** (SIM between the L_A prompt and the L_B output) — does identity survive the language switch?
- **Target-language nativeness/accent** — does the output sound like a native speaker of L_B, or does the source language leak an accent? (Sometimes accent *preservation* is desired, sometimes *removal* — the spec must state which.)
- **Intelligibility in L_B** (WER via an L_B ASR).

Concrete progress and its evaluation implications: **CosyVoice 2** improves cross-language timbre consistency over v1; **CosyVoice 3** resolves specific failure modes (e.g., transferring a Japanese voice to Chinese, fixed by normalizing Japanese characters to kana). **Cross-Lingual F5-TTS** targets language-agnostic cloning. These fixes are only visible if evaluation includes **directional language-pair matrices** (source→target), not aggregate scores — cross-lingual quality is notoriously asymmetric across language pairs.

### 7.3 Code-switching
Evaluate intra-utterance language switches for **prosodic continuity and pronunciation** at boundaries. **CosyVoice 2** establishes a zero-shot, streaming, multilingual benchmark with robust code-switching; fine-tuning on 50–100 hours of in-domain code-switch data further improves boundary prosody. Metrics: per-segment WER by language, boundary-region MOS, and timbre consistency across the switch.

## 8. Voice Cloning and Zero-Shot Synthesis

**Zero-shot TTS** clones a speaker from a short prompt (often ~3 seconds) with no per-speaker training. The dominant architectural families:

- **Neural-codec language models:** **VALL-E** / **VALL-E 2** treat speech as discrete codec tokens and generate autoregressively (+ NAR refinement) from a 3-second prompt; VALL-E 2 claims human parity on English benchmarks. **CosyVoice** uses supervised semantic tokens + flow-matching acoustics.
- **Latent-diffusion / factorized:** **NaturalSpeech 2** uses continuous latent vectors with in-context learning; **NaturalSpeech 3** uses factorized diffusion to disentangle attributes (content, prosody, timbre, acoustic detail) — which also makes it *evaluable per-attribute*.
- **Flow-matching, non-autoregressive:** **F5-TTS**, **Flamed-TTS** offer competitive quality with efficient parallel decoding and dynamic pacing.
- **Tokenizer-free / newer:** **VoxCPM** (context-aware, tokenizer-free) targets true-to-life cloning; **Spark-TTS** (BiCodec), **OZSpeech** (FACodec) exemplify the codec-choice design space.

**Evaluating zero-shot cloning:**
1. **Speaker similarity (SIM-o and SIM-r)** to the prompt — the core success metric.
2. **WER** — codec-LM models can hallucinate/repeat under difficult prompts, so intelligibility doubles as a stability check.
3. **Prompt sensitivity** — vary prompt length (3s vs. 10s+), noise, and channel; report degradation curves. A clone that only works on clean 10-second prompts is weaker than its headline SIM suggests.
4. **Acoustic-environment transfer** — some systems (VALL-E, CosyVoice, F5-TTS) reproduce the prompt's *room/channel*; decide whether that is desired and measure it.
5. **Naturalness under cloning** — SMOS + CMOS against ground truth of the same speaker.
6. **Efficiency** — RTF, streaming latency/TTFB (e.g., real-time systems like FlashLabs Chroma), and memory (MobileSpeech targets on-device).

**Standard testbeds:** LibriSpeech test-clean and **LibriTTS** for English zero-shot; SeedTTS-eval sets; multilingual corpora (VoxBox, in-house) for cross-lingual. Report both **SIM and WER jointly**, since they trade off.

### Security and ethics as an evaluation axis
Zero-shot cloning quality has outrun safeguards. A complete evaluation should include **anti-spoofing / deepfake-detectability** and **watermarking/traceability** — e.g., "Traceable TTS" pursues watermark-free-yet-traceable synthesis. Measuring how easily a clone defeats speaker verification (low EER) is both a quality signal *and* a misuse-risk signal that responsible reporting should surface.

## 10. Pitfalls and Best Practices

1. **Don't report a single MOS.** Break out intelligibility, naturalness, speaker fidelity, and expressiveness; frontier differences hide in the last two.
2. **Treat neural MOS predictors as screening, not verdicts.** UTMOS/DNSMOS/NISQA miss frontier-level distinctions (VoiceMOS 2024). Confirm with TTSDS2 and human CMOS/AB.
3. **Prefer pairwise/CMOS for near-parity claims.** Absolute MOS is not portable across studies.
4. **Control the ASR confound.** WER depends on the ASR model and language; hold it fixed and report it.
5. **Test the long tail explicitly.** URLs, numbers, dates, formulas, foreign words, homographs, questions — use EmergentTTS-Eval-style probes; averages hide catastrophic single-token failures.
6. **Report directional, per-language cross-lingual results,** not aggregates — quality is asymmetric across language pairs and switch directions.
7. **Characterize prompt robustness for cloning:** length, noise, channel, and out-of-distribution speakers; publish degradation curves.
8. **Separate emotion accuracy (SER) from quality (MOS)** so expressiveness gains aren't confounded with degradation.
9. **Include efficiency and stability:** RTF, streaming latency, and hallucination/failure rates — a "better-sounding" model that breaks 2% of the time may be worse in production.
10. **Evaluate safety:** spoofing detectability, watermark robustness, and consent/traceability belong in any 2025 TTS report.

---

## 11. Conclusion

Modern TTS evaluation has matured from "intelligible and natural?" into a **multi-axis discipline** that must jointly measure intelligibility, naturalness, speaker fidelity, expressiveness, and robustness — because the best systems now differ mainly in the hard, long-tail cases of prosody, emotion, code-switching, and cross-lingual identity preservation. The most defensible 2025 methodology layers **objective floors (WER, SIM)**, **distributional quality (TTSDS2)**, **targeted prosody/emotion probing via LALM-as-judge (EmergentTTS-Eval) and instruction-following benchmarks (MINT-Bench)**, and a **small, well-designed human CMOS/AB study** for the headline claim. Voice cloning, zero-shot synthesis, and cross-lingual transfer are converging on shared codec-LM and flow-matching backbones, which lets a single harness evaluate all three — provided the report exposes the trade-offs (SIM vs. WER, expressiveness vs. quality, per-language asymmetries) rather than collapsing them into one number, and treats **misuse risk (spoofing, watermarking) as a measured axis, not an afterthought.**
