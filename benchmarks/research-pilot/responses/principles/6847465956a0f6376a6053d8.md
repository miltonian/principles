**BLUF / EXECUTIVE SUMMARY**

Haiti is not experiencing three parallel emergencies — political, economic, and security — but a single self-reinforcing loop in which each failure manufactures the next and returns to deepen the first. Gang territorial control collapses state revenue; fiscal collapse hollows out the policing that might contest that control; and the resulting security vacuum blocks the credible elections and routine administration that would produce a mandate-holding government. As of July 2026 no nationally elected officials hold office; executive authority rests with the CARICOM-brokered Transitional Presidential Council (CPT), installed in 2024, and the under-resourced, Kenya-led Multinational Security Support (MSS) mission has not dislodged gangs from metropolitan Port-au-Prince. This memo lays out three distinct, actionable U.S. policy options — multilateral security scaling, political-transition brokering, and economic/humanitarian-plus-sanctions pressure. All three are bounded by one binding constraint: the leading armed actors are sanctioned or U.S.-convicted, so direct U.S. engagement with them is legally and politically foreclosed, and U.S. leverage must run through partners. No option is recommended here; the three are presented as trade-off profiles for decision-makers to weigh.

---

**SITUATION ASSESSMENT: A SINGLE SELF-REINFORCING LOOP**

The central analytic error that has defeated prior interventions is treating Haiti's failures as separable. They are coupled. The loop — not any one node — is the object requiring diagnosis, and isolated fixes fail precisely because a healthy node is captured or starved by the sick ones adjacent to it.

The loop's entry point is territorial. Armed gangs — most prominently the G9 an fanmi ak alye coalition, alongside other armed factions — control much of the Port-au-Prince metropolitan area and its approaches. Because Haiti's economic life runs through this corridor, that control is fiscal as well as physical. Gangs seize or blockade port facilities, choke the main road arteries linking the capital to the interior and the border, and throttle the customs receipts and internal commerce that constitute the state's principal revenue. Control of physical territory converts directly into control of the state's cash flow.

The second link follows mechanically. A state cut off from customs and commerce cannot fund itself, and a state that cannot fund itself cannot pay, equip, or field the police and enforcement institutions that would otherwise contest gang control. Fiscal collapse hollows out precisely the coercive capacity needed to reopen the ports and roads — so the territorial loss that caused the revenue loss is left uncontested, and often expands. The state's inability to reassert itself is downstream of its inability to pay for the attempt.

The third link closes the circuit. A persistent security vacuum makes credible elections and routine administration impossible: there is no secure environment for polling, civil administration is degraded, and large displaced populations cannot be reliably registered or reached. This is the direct cause of Haiti's defining current-state fact — no nationally elected officials in office, executive authority held by the CPT installed in 2024, and general elections still unheld. The CPT's structural weakness is a symptom of the loop, not an independent variable: its legitimacy deficit is downstream of the security vacuum that prevents the vote that would confer a mandate. An unelected transitional body lacks the authority to force the hard choices that reversing any prior link would require — and so the political failure feeds back to sustain the security and fiscal failures that produced it.

International security assistance has not broken the cycle. The MSS mission is UN-authorized but is not a UN peacekeeping operation; it is under-resourced and has not decisively dislodged gang control of the metropolitan area. Its limits are themselves downstream of two loop nodes — the security vacuum it was sent to fill and the fiscal collapse that leaves no capable domestic partner to hold cleared ground.

Humanitarian and migration conditions function as accelerants rather than a separate track. Gang control of the capital drives internal displacement and acute food insecurity, further shrinking the taxable economy and the reachable electorate — deepening both the fiscal and political nodes — while sustained outward migration pressure converts Haiti's internal loop into a direct and recurring U.S. interest that the options below must weigh.

The decision-relevant conclusion is straightforward: because the failures are coupled, the policy question is not "which node is worst" but "which node is severable" — where can U.S. leverage break the circuit rather than merely patch a single link.

**ACTOR AND STAKEHOLDER ANALYSIS**

*Domestic — and the engagement constraint.* The G9 an fanmi ak alye is a coalition of armed gangs — not a political party or a legitimate armed movement — led by Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier, a former police officer. Chérizier's law-enforcement background is analytically significant: it lends the coalition operational capabilities (policing tactics, weapons handling, command structure) and underpins rhetorical claims to a quasi-political or "revolutionary" legitimacy it does not in practice possess. The G9 exercises both territorial and economic control over large parts of Port-au-Prince — enforced through armed checkpoints and leverage over ports, fuel distribution, and commercial corridors, and monetized through extortion and de facto "taxation" of goods and movement. This combination gives it a demonstrated ability to paralyze the capital at will. Chérizier is a sanctioned/designated figure, subject to UN and U.S. sanctions.

Guy Philippe presents a distinct but similarly constraining profile: a former coup/rebel leader with a history at the center of armed challenges to Haitian governments, convicted in the United States on money-laundering charges and repatriated from U.S. prison in 2023. He re-entered Haitian politics as head of a mobilization/protest movement positioned explicitly against the transitional authorities. His standing is genuinely ambiguous — he retains grassroots mobilizing reach and can convene meaningful street-level support — but his U.S. criminal record taints any prospective U.S. association, and his oppositional posture places him at odds with the very transition Washington backs.

These two profiles jointly narrow the U.S. option space. As an analytic judgment rather than settled fact: the G9's sanctioned status and Philippe's U.S. conviction make direct U.S. engagement with either actor legally and politically fraught. Engaging the G9 would mean dealing with a sanctioned armed-gang coalition — legally barred or heavily restricted and reputationally toxic; engaging Philippe would legitimize a U.S.-convicted felon opposed to the transition. Permissible U.S. options therefore skew away from bilateral deals, recognition, funding, or partnership with either actor and toward indirect, multilateral, or transitional-authority-mediated approaches. This is a hard boundary condition — foreclosed absent a change in the actors' status — that the options below must respect.

*External stakeholders and the nature of U.S. leverage.* Roles among external actors are distinct and non-interchangeable. CARICOM — the 15-member Caribbean Community — is the primary regional diplomatic broker, working chiefly through its "Eminent Persons" group, which convened Haitian stakeholders and facilitated the arrangements that produced the CPT. Its leverage is convening legitimacy and a neutral table, not coercive capacity. The UN is the global body performing two separate functions: Security Council authorization supplies the legal mandate for both the MSS mission and the sanctions regime, while its specialized agencies furnish humanitarian coordination and human-rights monitoring. The UN does not broker the regional politics, and CARICOM does not confer Security Council authorization; the two must not be conflated.

The United States is the decisive external stakeholder through four concrete channels: largest bilateral donor, principal MSS funder, primary migration destination, and driver of Security Council action. The essential point concerns the *nature* of this leverage: it is real but exercised largely through partners rather than unilateral force. Washington has deliberately routed political legitimacy through CARICOM, legal authorization through the Security Council, and boots on the ground through third-country troop contributors — the Kenya-led MSS — rather than deploying its own forces. Two constraints explain the choice: domestic reluctance to commit U.S. forces, and the reputational legacy of prior U.S. interventions in Haiti, which makes an overt American troop presence both politically costly and counterproductive to the legitimacy of any Haitian transition. U.S. influence is therefore strongest where it can finance, authorize, and convene, and weakest where outcomes must be compelled on the ground against actors beyond the reach of financing and resolutions. That gap is the defining feature of the U.S. position.

**U.S. POLICY OPTIONS**

The three options below attack different nodes of the loop through different primary levers — multilateral security, political-transition brokering, and economic/humanitarian-plus-sanctions pressure. They are distinct trade-off profiles, not ranked alternatives, and are not mutually exclusive. None rests on unilateral U.S. force or on direct engagement with proscribed actors.

**Option A — Multilateral Security Scaling**
*Approach:* Concentrate U.S. weight on strengthening or reconfiguring the Kenya-led MSS mission, or a more robust UN-authorized successor — through funding, enabling capabilities (logistics, aviation, intelligence, medical), and additional troop contributors — always via partners, never through U.S. force on the ground.
- *Benefits:* Directly targets the loop's entry node, gang territorial control. Reopening ports and road arteries could restore customs revenue and commerce, easing the fiscal collapse that hollows out policing. Leverages the channel where U.S. influence is strongest while keeping the American footprint indirect, preserving credibility with CARICOM and troop contributors.
- *Risks:* Prior security surges have not decisively dislodged gangs; added resources may buy tactical gains without holding cleared ground absent a fundable Haitian partner. Escalation risks civilian harm and displacement, intensifying migration pressure. A heavier UN-authorized arrangement requires Security Council consensus that is not assured, and sustained funding exposes the U.S. to open-ended cost.
- *National-interest justification:* Restoring control of the capital's commercial corridors serves counter-narcotics and counter-trafficking interests, reduces the instability driving irregular migration toward the U.S., and routes enforcement through partners consistent with the constraint on U.S. force.

**Option B — Diplomatic / Political-Transition Brokering**
*Approach:* Place U.S. weight behind CARICOM's Eminent Persons process and the CPT to widen the political settlement, sequence credible elections, and shore up transitional legitimacy.
- *Benefits:* Addresses the loop's closing node — the legitimacy deficit that leaves no mandate-holding government able to force hard choices. A broader settlement could improve the political conditions on which security and economic measures depend, while keeping the U.S. in its strongest supporting posture and reinforcing partner credibility at comparatively low direct cost.
- *Risks:* Brokering cannot proceed where the security vacuum makes credible polling impossible; premature elections risk a contested, low-legitimacy outcome. Any widened settlement must reckon with spoilers and mobilization figures — including Guy Philippe — but the U.S. must not negotiate with, legitimize, or fund a U.S.-convicted actor or the sanctioned G9. Such figures can be managed only through Haitian-led or CARICOM-led processes; this legal red line both constrains the option and limits its reach against those holding territory.
- *National-interest justification:* A legitimate, mandate-holding government is the durable exit from the crisis and the precondition for sustainable migration and stability outcomes. Investing in the CARICOM-led track advances regional-partner credibility and a Haitian-owned solution while respecting the engagement constraint.

**Option C — Economic / Humanitarian-plus-Sanctions Pressure**
*Approach:* Pair targeted sanctions on gang leaders, financiers, and their enablers with scaled humanitarian relief and institutional support (police, ports, customs, anti-corruption) — starving armed groups of resources while sustaining the population.
- *Benefits:* Attacks the fiscal-and-enforcement node from two sides: sanctions degrade the financing and weapons pipelines sustaining gang control, while institutional support rebuilds the customs and policing capacity the state cannot currently fund. Humanitarian scaling mitigates the displacement and food insecurity that accelerate the loop — all through legally available multilateral and partner channels.
- *Risks:* Sanctions bite slowly and can be evaded through informal networks; against already-designated actors, marginal effect may be limited without robust enforcement by others. Institutional aid risks capture or diversion given weak oversight. Humanitarian access itself depends on the security conditions this option does not directly resolve, and expanded assistance carries recurring cost.
- *National-interest justification:* Disrupting gang finance and trafficking serves counter-narcotics and counter-trafficking priorities directly, while sustaining the population and rebuilding customs and police capacity addresses migration drivers and the revenue collapse at the loop's core — through instruments that avoid both unilateral force and any dealing with proscribed actors.

These options represent distinct trade-off profiles across the security, political, and economic levers for decision-makers to weigh. They are not mutually exclusive — a sequenced or combined package is available — and are presented without recommendation.
