SUBJECT: Haiti — The Compound Crisis and Three U.S. Policy Options (Decision Memo)
DATE:    July 5, 2026

BOTTOM LINE UP FRONT

Haiti is in state collapse, not merely political turmoil. As of today the country has no elected or mandated national authority: the Transitional Presidential Council (TPC) — the internationally brokered interim body — saw its mandate lapse on February 7, 2026 with elections unheld, and the council dissolved with no dedicated successor. Executive power transferred to a caretaker Cabinet of Ministers under Prime Minister Alix Didier Fils-Aimé, with Laurent Saint-Cyr as rotating TPC president overseeing the handover — an arrangement backed by a contested February 24, 2026 "National Pact for Stability and the Organization of Elections," but holding no electoral mandate (France Diplomatie, https://www.diplomatie.gouv.fr/en/presse-et-ressources/decouvrir-et-informer/actualites/haiti-fin-du-mandat-du-conseil-presidentiel-de-transition; GlobalSecurity mirror, https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/news/2026/02/mil-260209-france-meae01.htm; Haitian Times, https://haitiantimes.com/2026/02/24/haitian-political-actors-signed-a-pact/).

The armed coalition Viv Ansanm ("Living Together") controls roughly 90% of the capital per UN reporting (some analyses cite ~70%), over half the population faces acute hunger, and the state does not hold Port-au-Prince. These are not three separate emergencies. Political illegitimacy, economic and humanitarian collapse, and security failure are mutually causal — each manufactures the other two — so any single-domain fix will be unwound by the domains it ignores.

Measured against the only yardstick that matters for U.S. interests — durable Haitian state capacity tied to hemispheric stability and migration management, not short-term relief — I recommend Option 2: decisive U.S. backing of the UN-authorized Gang Suppression Force (GSF), on the strict condition that the security surge is subordinated to a Haitian-led political timeline and reinforced by the financier-sanctions campaign of Option 1. The central risk to watch is a half-funded force that clears ground it cannot hold, repeating past failure at lasting sovereignty cost. One cross-cutting fact should temper any clean "gang-suppression" framing from the outset: BINUH attributed the majority (>69%) of first-quarter-2026 killings and injuries to Haitian security-force operations against gangs — some using a private military company's armed drones — not to the gangs themselves.

I. THE INTERCONNECTED CRISIS

Haiti faces a single, self-reinforcing collapse in which each failure produces the conditions for the other two. Treating governance, economics, and security as parallel problems misreads the mechanism and guarantees that gains in one domain are reversed by the two left untreated.

The legitimacy vacuum feeds the security collapse. With the TPC mandate lapsed February 7, 2026 and no vote held, authority passed to the caretaker Cabinet under PM Fils-Aimé, recognized as a single-headed executive by the parties and civil-society groups that signed the February 24 National Pact — a political accord, not a constitutional mandate, and one whose legitimacy is contested (Haitian Times, https://haitiantimes.com/2026/02/24/haitian-political-actors-signed-a-pact/). No fully legitimate body now exists to direct, fund, or politically sustain the Haitian National Police (HNP) and the international security effort. That gap is concrete. The Provisional Electoral Council (CEP) published a final calendar in January 2026 setting first-round general elections for August 30, 2026 and a second round for December 6, 2026, with voter registration slated April 1–June 29, 2026 (Haitian Times, https://haitiantimes.com/2026/01/06/haiti-cep-maintains-elections-august-30-2026/; Caribbean National Weekly, https://www.caribbeannationalweekly.com/news/haiti-publishes-revised-electoral-calendar-sets-august-2026-election-dates/). That calendar has already slipped: mid-2026 the CEP postponed voter registration amid a budget dispute, with the Fils-Aimé government rejecting the council's requested budget and pausing the process. Its two stated prerequisites — nationwide security restoration and full financing — remain unmet, and elections are not held as of today (GlobalPost, https://globalpost.com/stories/haiti-postpones-voter-registration-for-long-awaited-elections/; Haitian Times, https://haitiantimes.com/2025/11/04/haiti-elections-impossible-for-2026-turnover/). Some ~300 political groups reportedly registered for the polls, which the UN characterized as "hope" — but registration is not voting, and no vote has been conducted (UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/03/1167143). Meanwhile the security intervention — mid-transition from the underfunded, Kenya-led Multinational Security Support (MSS) mission to the larger, roughly 5,550-authorized GSF under UN Security Council Resolution 2793 (September 2025) — has deployed only a fraction of its ceiling (UN Meetings Coverage, https://press.un.org/en/2025/sc16185.doc.htm; Security Council Report, https://www.securitycouncilreport.org/monthly-forecast/2026-04/haiti-34.php). This vacuum of legitimate direction has ceded ground to Viv Ansanm.

The security collapse, in turn, forecloses any restoration of legitimate authority. With Viv Ansanm controlling roughly 90% of Port-au-Prince (UN, June 2026) and pushing into the Artibonite and Centre departments, credible elections are physically impossible on terrain gangs govern — the same voter registration, campaigning, and polling that the calendar requires cannot occur where gangs hold the ground (UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167732). The killing sustains the vacuum. The UN reported more than 5,500 killed by gang violence in 2025, roughly on par with the ~5,600 of 2024, and BINUH recorded roughly 1,642 killed in Q1 2026 — statistically level with the 1,617 of Q1 2025 (UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/03/1167188; BINUH, https://binuh.unmissions.org/en/press-releases/haiti-more-than-1600-people-killed-in-the-first-quarter-of-2026-a-human). Cumulatively through roughly June 2026, the UN reported 2,300+ killed and ~1,100 injured, with a kidnapping figure of ~100 that appears partial and low-confidence (UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167732). Insecurity thus perpetuates the very vacuum that enabled it.

Economic and humanitarian collapse supplies the violence with fuel, and predation deepens the collapse. Six consecutive years of recession, inflation near 30%, and roughly 5.91 million people — about 53% of the population — in IPC Phase 3+ acute food insecurity (March–June 2026 projection, including ~1.9 million in Phase 4 Emergency and ~3.8–3.9 million in Phase 3 Crisis) create both a recruitment pool and a predation economy for armed groups (IPC, https://www.ipcinfo.org/ipc-country-analysis/details-map/en/c/1159760/; ReliefWeb/IPC snapshot, https://reliefweb.int/report/haiti/haiti-57-million-people-face-high-levels-acute-food-insecurity-gang-violence-tightens-its-grip-across-country-ipc-acute-food-insecurity-snapshot-september-2025-june-2026). Notably, no famine (IPC Phase 5) has been declared for the 2026 projection — humanitarian assistance is credited with averting the earlier-feared catastrophic scenario. Running the loop the other way, gang control of the capital, ports, fuel arteries, and arterial roads strangles commerce, agriculture, and aid, driving internal displacement to roughly 1.5 million by June 2026 (up from ~1.4 million recorded in the IOM DTM Round in September/December 2025) — record levels, with "more than one in ten" Haitians displaced — and renewing the hunger that renews the recruitment pool (IOM DTM Round 12, https://dtm.iom.int/reports/haiti-report-internal-displacement-situation-round-12-december-2025; UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167732; NPR, https://www.npr.org/2026/06/17/g-s1-128536/un-chief-visits-haiti).

The political and economic failures close the final loop. The legitimacy vacuum blocks coherent fiscal policy, donor confidence, and reconstruction — the stalled electoral budget and a persistent security-funding shortfall both trace to the absence of accountable, mandated authority — prolonging the recession. That devastation, in turn, hollows out the political base: mass hunger and the displacement of more than one in ten Haitians erode the social fabric on which any legitimate participation or governance would rest.

Strategic implication. Because these failures are interlocked, single-domain interventions will be undercut by the domains they ignore. A security surge that clears ground it cannot hand to legitimate, funded authority will not hold; an election pressed onto terrain the state does not control will not be credible; relief delivered into a predation economy will be taxed by the actors it is meant to relieve. U.S. policy must be sequenced against these loops, not against one symptom.

II. KEY DOMESTIC ACTORS

The opposition to the Haitian state is fragmented across rival, incompatible power centers — not a unified front — which is why any deal with one party fails to bind the others.

The G9 an fanmi / Viv Ansanm armed coalition. The dominant force in the capital is the G9 an fanmi / Viv Ansanm coalition. In 2024 the G9 Family federation merged with its former rival, the G-Pep bloc, to form Viv Ansanm under the command of Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier. The consolidation of two previously warring alliances under a single leadership is the decisive development — it converted fragmented, mutually hostile gangs into a coordinated bloc capable of city-wide offensives, which is why "G9" and "Viv Ansanm" now denote one entity rather than two (DNI/NCTC, https://www.dni.gov/nctc/terrorist_groups/viv_ansanm.html).

Gangs under the coalition control roughly 90% of Port-au-Prince, a figure UN reporting still affirms as of June 2026, though some analyses cite a lower ~70% (UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167732; DNI/NCTC, https://www.dni.gov/nctc/terrorist_groups/viv_ansanm.html). This control is governance in fact if not in name: checkpoints, extortion, and command of the ports, fuel arteries, and the roads in and out of the capital. The coalition has also pushed beyond the capital, with Viv Ansanm-aligned gangs seizing Bel Air in central Port-au-Prince on March 17, 2026 — at least eight dead including two police, more than 3,000 displaced — and expanding into the Artibonite and Centre departments; at least 26 gangs operate in and around the capital (Reuters via conflict.sbs, https://www.conflict.sbs/articles/haitian-crisis-weekly-2026-03-19; UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/03/1167188). The conclusion these figures compel is unavoidable: the Haitian state does not control its own capital.

Battlefield control converts directly into bargaining power. With the TPC mandate lapsed and a contested PM-led caretaker Cabinet governing without an electoral mandate, no settlement, transition timeline, or credible vote can be secured over territory Viv Ansanm holds — giving the coalition an effective veto and the capacity to spoil any arrangement from which it is excluded. Chérizier has voiced political ambitions, and PM Fils-Aimé has rejected negotiating with gangs, emphasizing a security-first approach ahead of the planned vote (Caribbean National Weekly, https://www.caribbeannationalweekly.com/news/haiti-pm-rejects-gang-negotiations-intensifies-security-ahead-of-2026-vote/). Two calibrations temper this assessment: the coalition's durability is untested — no major fracture is reported as of July 5, 2026, but cohesion under sustained GSF pressure is unproven (International Crisis Group, https://www.crisisgroup.org/rpt/latin-america-caribbean/haiti/110-undoing-haitis-deadly-gang-alliance) — and Chérizier's actual electoral viability, as distinct from his coercive weight, remains speculative.

Guy Philippe and the FREN platform — a rival, not an ally, of the gangs. Guy Philippe is a former police commissioner and rebel leader who helped force the 2004 ouster of President Aristide, was later elected senator (never seated), was extradited to the United States, convicted of money laundering, served a federal sentence, and was repatriated to Haiti in November 2023. That biography — armed insurgency against a sitting president, followed by imprisonment by the very foreign power now underwriting Haiti's security transition — gives him durable nationalist, anti-foreign-intervention credibility and a pre-existing network of ex-military and armed sympathizers to draw on.

Since repatriation, Philippe has built the FREN platform — relaunched with Nader Joisélus as national coordinator, on a platform of four pillars (patriotism, work, justice, good governance) — as his political vehicle, calling repeatedly for "revolution" and rupture with the transitional order and rejecting the CPT/TPC and foreign-led security missions (HaitiLibre, https://www.haitilibre.com/en/news-42940-haiti-politic-return-of-guy-philippe-at-the-head-of-a-new-political-organization.html; Le Midi, https://lemidiinfo.com/frenune-nouvelle-structure-politique-pour-soutenir-guy-philippe/). His coercive muscle is assessed to draw on ex-FAd'H (former army) elements and the BSAP (Brigade for the Security of Protected Areas / environmental-protection agents). Capacity should not be overstated: this base is assessed as limited, regionally concentrated, and reliant on protest mobilization and episodic armed pressure — not a national army capable of holding the capital.

Philippe positions FREN as a sovereignist, order-restoring alternative to Viv Ansanm, competing for the same anti-government, anti-Core-Group space. He is not part of the gang coalition: his claimed legitimacy rests on nationalist politics, ex-military discipline, and street mobilization; the gangs' rests on territorial extortion and kidnapping. No Philippe–gang accommodation and no Viv Ansanm fracture are documented in the reporting window, and the two remain analytically distinct challenges to the state. Reporting on FREN is genuinely thin: his posture toward the February 24 National Pact is unconfirmed, and no formal 2026 negotiating role is documented (Chatham House context, https://www.chathamhouse.org/2026/01/roadmap-security-and-governance-reform-haiti/summary).

The implication is that Haiti's opposition to the state is fragmented across at least three incompatible poles — the internationally-backed caretaker authorities, the gangs, and Philippe/FREN. A deal with any one pole does not bind the others; an excluded pole retains incentive and means to spoil it. Philippe holds spoiler capacity disproportionate to his troop strength, plus a potential — but risky — role as an armed nationalist interlocutor whose inclusion could legitimize extra-constitutional force. His nationalist framing makes isolation-via-foreign-force especially likely to strengthen him.

Non-armed domestic actors — the financier nexus and the fractured political class. The private-sector economic elite is the hinge where economic and security failure fuse into one. A handful of import-oligarchy families — concentrated in port and customs concessions, fuel distribution, food imports, and consumer-goods monopolies — have historically converted control of Haiti's few functioning chokepoints into both rents and coercive reach. The mechanism is direct: whoever controls a port terminal, a customs gate, or a fuel-supply line controls the revenue that could fund the state and the physical corridors gangs must cross. Financing, arming, or tolerating armed groups lets elites police competitors, discipline labor, capture contraband margins that evade customs, and extract protection value — while the state, starved of that same customs revenue, cannot pay the security forces that would end the arrangement. This is the economic-security fusion in one sentence: the rents that enrich the oligarchy are the same revenues whose diversion guarantees the security vacuum the gangs fill.

This is a swing actor, internally split, and the section's central discomfort. The elite's interest is now unstable: low-grade disorder long served them by suppressing competition and keeping the state too poor to tax or prosecute them, but the disorder has exceeded its sponsors' control — Viv Ansanm shows no reported fracture and has expanded beyond the capital, and kidnapping and extortion increasingly target the propertied class itself, driving capital flight and hollowing the very import trade the elite monopolizes. Their leverage remains substantial and largely intact: control of ports, fuel, and food gives them a chokehold on both the humanitarian supply chain and any future government's revenue base; liquidity and offshore assets give them financing and exit capacity. U.S. and international sanctions on individual business figures (not only gang leaders) have imposed real reputational and banking friction, but no dated Feb–Jul 2026 elite defection or realignment is reported, and the broader import-oligarchy's operating model appears undisturbed; sanctions have bitten individuals more than the system. The families indispensable to economic stabilization are the same ones implicated in the security collapse, and any that visibly aligns with Washington risks being branded a foreign instrument.

On the political side, the actors richest in legitimacy hold the least coercive capacity. Montana Accord-type civil-society coalitions offer moral authority and a demonstrated capacity to convene a broad, credibly domestic coalition — precisely the "Haitian-led" legitimacy foreign actors cannot manufacture — but little money, no votes yet, and no force. The churches (the Catholic Church's mediating authority, dense evangelical/Protestant networks, and Vodou associations' grassroots reach) offer unmatched mobilizing and convening power largely untainted by the foreign-instrument problem — among the most credibly Haitian-led legitimacy sources available. Traditional parties and leaders bring patronage networks, name recognition, and signatures on the National Pact, but chronic fragmentation and low legitimacy make them mostly obstacle/spoiler. Bar associations, human-rights groups, business chambers, and the diaspora (the financial lifeline via remittances) round out the field — builders where they underwrite accountability, but the chambers overlap with the compromised oligarchy, and diaspora influence, if externally channeled, carries the occupation-legacy cost. Any settlement must bridge that legitimacy-coercion gap without importing the fix.

III. INTERNATIONAL BODIES AND THEIR INFLUENCE

Collective external leverage is real but structurally mismatched to the crisis.

CARICOM. The Caribbean Community remains the principal regional convener and inter-Haitian broker. Through its Eminent Persons Group (EPG) it brokered the 2024 TPC and has continued "good offices" mediation since. Its leverage is legitimacy, not force: as a Caribbean body it can convene Haitian factions without the occupation baggage carried by Northern powers, but it commands no security instrument and depends entirely on Haitian actors honoring what it brokers. In January 2026 the EPG warned of the approaching February 7 mandate end and the "tardiness" of stakeholders; in May 2026 it publicly disclaimed involvement in the "Washington-Haiti-CARICOM Socio-Political Relief Solution" (organized by CADIH); and in June 2026 it postponed its in-person Haiti visit, reverting to virtual "good offices" engagement toward elections and constitutional order (CARICOM, https://caricom.org/statement-caricom-eminent-persons-group-postponed-visit-to-haiti/; CARICOM, https://caricom.org/statement-by-the-caricom-eminent-persons-group-epg/; Voice St. Lucia, https://thevoiceslu.com/2026/01/caricom-eminent-persons-group-on-the-approaching-end-of-the-transitional-presidential-council/). CARICOM can midwife agreements; it cannot enforce them or substitute for a functioning CEP.

The UN security track (central). Intervention is mid-transition from the Kenya-led MSS mission to the larger, ~5,550-authorized (5,500 uniformed + 50 civilian, 12-month mandate) GSF under Resolution 2793 (UN Meetings Coverage, https://press.un.org/en/2025/sc16185.doc.htm; Security Council Report, https://www.securitycouncilreport.org/monthly-forecast/2026-04/haiti-34.php). This is a transition in progress, not a completed deployment. The first GSF troops from Chad arrived April 1, 2026 — 400 of a planned two-battalion ~1,500 contingent — based at Camp Vertières; Kenyan MSS officers remain until replacements arrive, and full operational capacity is not expected until October 2026 (Wikipedia/Gang Suppression Force, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gang_Suppression_Force). The prior MSS baseline (June 2025) was ~991 personnel (Kenya 732, Guatemala 150, El Salvador 78, Jamaica 23, Bahamas 6, Belize 2), with Bangladesh, Benin, and Guinea pledged. Command is stood up: Force Commander Maj. Gen. Erdenebat Batsuuri (Mongolia, from April 2026), Deputy Col. Eldon Morgan (Jamaica), GSF Special Representative Jack Christofides (appointed December 2, 2025), and a UN Support Office in Haiti (UNSOH) under ASG Daniela Kroslak (appointed March 19, 2026). UNSG Guterres visited Port-au-Prince and Camp Vertières on June 16, 2026, saying the deployment "offers a real opportunity" but "only if the international community assumes its responsibilities" (NPR, https://www.npr.org/2026/06/17/g-s1-128536/un-chief-visits-haiti; UN News, https://news.un.org/en/story/2026/06/1167732).

Three binding limits apply. Capacity: troop generation and logistics lag badly; boots on the ground are a fraction of authorization. Funding: the GSF has not escaped the MSS's voluntary-trust-fund pathology — against a ~$600M annual need, roughly $85M had been collected; the U.S. pledged up to $300M ($200M mission + $100M humanitarian) but froze funding in January 2025, and Canada added ~$123M — the UN itself assesses this "won't be enough" (The New Humanitarian, https://www.thenewhumanitarian.org/analysis/2025/12/03/haiti-depth-new-gang-suppression-force-what-it-means-haiti; Wikipedia/Gang Suppression Force, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gang_Suppression_Force). Legitimacy: Haitian wariness of foreign forces is grounded in MINUSTAH's (2004–17) cholera introduction and sexual-abuse record — a consent-of-the-governed question a caretaker government of contested mandate cannot fully answer. Sharpening that risk, BINUH attributed more than 69% of Q1-2026 killings and injuries to security-force operations against gangs (some using a private military company's drones), with ~33 deaths from alleged police summary executions and documented civilian and child casualties (e.g., Martissant), and only ~4% attributed to self-defense groups (BINUH, https://binuh.unmissions.org/en/press-releases/haiti-more-than-1600-people-killed-in-the-first-quarter-of-2026-a-human). This is a first-order legitimacy and accountability variable for the GSF, not a footnote.

The Core Group. The Core Group — the informal club of foreign ambassadors and envoys (U.S., France, Canada, the EU, and the UN/OAS Special Representatives) — is widely credited with shaping Haiti's recent leadership selection. No distinct 2026 communiqué was found in the window, but France (a member) issued the operative February 9, 2026 statement on the mandate's end (France Diplomatie, https://www.diplomatie.gouv.fr/en/presse-et-ressources/decouvrir-et-informer/actualites/haiti-fin-du-mandat-du-conseil-presidentiel-de-transition; GlobalSecurity mirror, https://www.globalsecurity.org/military/library/news/2026/02/mil-260209-france-meae01.htm). Its influence runs through quiet coordination, and Haitians across the spectrum resent it as external tutelage. This is where the sovereignty tension bites hardest: the United States sits inside the Core Group and is therefore an implicated party, not a neutral broker. Against the memory of the 1915–1934 U.S. occupation, any U.S.-advanced initiative is read through an occupation-legacy lens — a structural constraint on every option below.

The OAS. The Organization of American States retains a diminished but real role in electoral observation and technical support, relevant given the stalled CEP calendar (first round nominally August 30, 2026). Its own contested Haiti record limits its authority; in practice it overlaps with CARICOM and the Core Group more than it complements them, and its distinct value is narrow electoral technical assistance.

IFIs and donors. The IMF, World Bank, and IDB hold conditionality, budget-support, and reconstruction leverage, but that leverage decays in a state with collapsed institutions and gang-controlled territory: disbursement presupposes a counterpart able to absorb funds and deliver reforms, which the caretaker Cabinet may not durably be.

Synthesis. CARICOM and the OAS supply legitimacy without force; the GSF supplies partial, underfunded force without settled legitimacy; the Core Group and IFIs supply money and coordination but at the cost of sovereignty resentment. No single body can enforce an outcome, and the U.S. is inside — not above — the most resented instrument.

IV. EXISTING U.S. LEVERS

Two active U.S. tools form the baseline the options build on; each carries a contested downside that pulls against stabilization.

The May 2025 FTO designation. In May 2025 the United States designated the Viv Ansanm coalition and the Gran Grif gang as Foreign Terrorist Organizations, unlocking asset freezes, prosecution exposure for facilitators, financial-system isolation, and a signaling effect that treats the coalition as terrorist rather than ordinary criminality. The contested cost is humanitarian. Because Viv Ansanm controls roughly 90% of Port-au-Prince, lawful delivery of food, water, medical care, and IDP support frequently requires transacting, negotiating passage, or paying incidental fees to designated actors. Under U.S. material-support law, such conduct can expose NGOs, UN implementing partners, faith-based groups, contractors, and diaspora remitters to criminal liability regardless of humanitarian intent — chilling the very aid reaching the ~5.9M food-insecure and ~1.5M displaced. The unresolved trade-off is enforcement reach versus humanitarian throughput: absent OFAC/Treasury humanitarian general licenses or tailored carve-outs, the designation criminalizes the aid pipeline; narrow licensing preserves access but can create leakage the gangs exploit. No OFAC general license, expanded designation, or enforcement action is reported in the Feb–Jul 2026 window, so the licensing question remains open. The BINUH finding that a majority of Q1-2026 casualties trace to security-force/PMC-drone operations, moreover, complicates the clean "terrorist vs. state" framing the designation assumes — and if the FTO is wielded to backstop GSF/HNP kinetic operations, that casualty attribution could create legal and reputational exposure for U.S. support.

Haitian Temporary Protected Status (~330,000). TPS covers approximately 330,000 Haitian nationals and is revocable; its load-bearing benefit is the remittance and diaspora stability that partly sustain Haiti's economy through six years of recession and ~30% inflation. Because it is revocable, its deportation pressure cuts against stabilization: termination or non-extension would convert protected residents into removable persons pushed into a state already in legitimacy vacuum, gang-saturated, and unable to absorb returnees, while severing the remittances the U.S. otherwise relies on — a self-defeating dynamic. No TPS extension, termination, or litigation development is reported in the window; the extension-versus-termination decision remains unsettled and should not be treated as closed.

V. POLICY OPTIONS

The three options are genuinely different strategic postures, presented for side-by-side comparison against the state-capacity/stability/migration yardstick. Because the United States is an implicated party inside the resented Core Group, each carries the occupation-legacy liability differently.

Option 1 — Light-Footprint Containment and Financier Sanctions (a standing pressure campaign). Actions. Minimize U.S. physical presence and apply pressure at the crisis's economic hinge. Escalate OFAC/Treasury designations against the import-oligarchy families and political patrons who fund, arm, and instrumentalize Viv Ansanm — targeting individuals, not only gang leaders — paired with the May 2025 FTO designation to isolate the coalition from the financial system. Sustain maritime interdiction and Dominican-border coordination to choke arms and contraband, and press for parallel Canadian and EU designations to close jurisdictional gaps. Critically, issue OFAC humanitarian general licenses concurrently, so material-support liability does not criminalize aid into the ~90% of Port-au-Prince the coalition controls.
Benefits. Strikes the economic-security fusion directly — cutting the rents that finance predation is the one move that can degrade gang capacity without inserting foreign troops, and it uses instruments the U.S. already holds. It carries the lightest sovereignty footprint and leaves the security lead to Haitian and multilateral hands.
Risks and who bears them. Sanctions bite individuals faster than the system; the oligarchy's offshore liquidity and port chokeholds can absorb designations without changing behavior, and the families indispensable to restarting the import economy are the same ones being sanctioned — a genuine bind. If humanitarian carve-outs lag, the FTO designation criminalizes the aid pipeline reaching the food-insecure and displaced, and Haitian civilians and NGO implementers bear that cost. Extraterritorial sanction reach is the sovereignty exposure: aggressive U.S. financial coercion over Haitian nationals can be cast as economic tutelage, feeding the occupation-legacy narrative Philippe/FREN is positioned to exploit.
Against the yardstick. Builds state capacity indirectly and slowly — by starving the predation economy that hollows out the fisc — but delivers little immediate relief and does nothing to reclaim territory. Its sovereignty cost is manageable in form because it keeps boots off Haitian soil, even as its extraterritorial reach strains that principle in substance.

Option 2 — Robust Backing of the UN Gang Suppression Force (a sequenced roadmap). Actions. Put decisive U.S. resources behind the security track. Phase 1: release the pledged funding (the U.S. commitment of up to $300M, frozen January 2025) and anchor the trust fund against its shortfall — roughly $85M collected against a ~$600M annual need — while providing airlift, logistics, engineering, and ISR to accelerate the MSS-to-GSF handover under Res. 2793 toward its ~5,550 ceiling. Phase 2: embed GSF operations in a vetted partnership with the HNP and hard-wire conduct safeguards, given BINUH's finding that security-force and drone operations drove a majority of Q1-2026 casualties. Phase 3: tie clearance of held terrain to a restored electoral process, so ground the GSF secures is handed to legitimate, funded Haitian authority rather than held indefinitely by foreign forces.
Benefits. The only option that can physically contest Viv Ansanm's control of the capital and thereby make credible elections — and any restoration of legitimate authority — geographically possible. It is multilateral by design, diluting the U.S. signature, and it directly rebuilds the coercive core of state capacity.
Risks and who bears them. Troop generation and sustainment, not pledges, are the binding constraints; an underfunded or half-deployed GSF that clears ground it cannot hold repeats the MSS's failure and burns U.S. credibility. Boots-on-the-ground optics are the acute sovereignty exposure: against MINUSTAH's cholera and abuse record and the 1915–34 occupation, a U.S.-financed foreign force can be branded an imposed fix, handing Philippe/FREN a nationalist rallying point. The security-first posture also assumes a single adversary — but FREN/BSAP is a separate anti-government pole a gang-focused campaign does not bind and could inflame. The Phase 2 conduct safeguards are not optional garnish: with a private military company's drones implicated in the majority of Q1-2026 casualties, U.S. backing risks endorsing an unaccountable use of force absent hard guardrails. Haitian civilians bear the cost if force outpaces accountability.
Against the yardstick. Of the three, this most directly rebuilds state capacity — but only if Phase 3 discipline holds. Security that is not handed to legitimate Haitian authority is short-term suppression, not a capacity gain; the option's whole value depends on subordinating the foreign surge to a Haitian-led political sequence.

Option 3 — Conditional Disengagement with Migration Management and TPS Calibration (a conditions-based posture). Actions. Step back from an active shaping role and condition U.S. support on Haitian-led benchmarks — a credible successor to the lapsed transitional authority, a financed CEP, demonstrable electoral preparation — rather than underwriting a contested caretaker cabinet. Manage consequences primarily through the migration lever: deepen Dominican-border and hemispheric spillover planning with regional partners, and deliberately calibrate TPS for the ~330,000 Haitians — extension or phased adjustment tied to conditions on the ground — rather than an abrupt revocation that would push removals into a collapsing state and sever load-bearing remittances.
Benefits. Most fully respects the Haitian-led principle by refusing to impose a foreign fix, husbands U.S. resources and credibility for a moment when a legitimate counterpart exists, and can create real incentives for the political class to consolidate.
Risks and who bears them. Disengagement's sovereignty exposure is abandonment: stepping back from a state where gangs hold the capital cedes the field to Viv Ansanm and Philippe/FREN, and a vacuum the U.S. declines to shape will be shaped by actors hostile to hemispheric stability. Conditionality assumes a Haitian partner capable of meeting benchmarks the security environment currently forecloses — they may simply never be met. Haitian civilians bear the humanitarian cost of reduced engagement, and the migration lever, if TPS is mishandled, converts into precisely the border and spillover crisis the option means to manage.
Against the yardstick. Protects long-term state capacity in one narrow sense — it refuses to substitute for Haitian ownership and avoids propping up an illegitimate arrangement — while risking that "capacity" never materializes because the U.S. withdrew the support that could have enabled it. It most explicitly trades away short-term relief; its defensible core is the migration-management and TPS-calibration discipline that prevents U.S. immigration decisions from actively deepening Haiti's collapse.

VI. RECOMMENDATION

I recommend Option 2 as the anchor of U.S. policy: back the GSF decisively enough to make territory recoverable, but subordinate the surge to a Haitian-led political timeline (Phase 3 discipline), and reinforce it with Option 1's sanctions campaign against the financier nexus that would otherwise regenerate the gangs. Option 3's TPS calibration and border planning should be retained as the migration-management floor beneath both — not adopted as a standalone posture, which risks abandonment. Measured against durable state capacity and hemispheric stability rather than short-term relief, this is the only course that can reclaim the capital while ensuring the ground is handed to legitimate Haitian authority. Security without financial pressure treats the symptom; sanctions without security cannot retake the capital; neither builds capacity unless handed to legitimate Haitian authority.

The principal decision is whether to release and anchor U.S. GSF funding now, conditioned on conduct safeguards and a binding political hand-off sequence. The key risk to watch is a half-deployed, underfunded force clearing terrain it cannot hold — the failure mode that would repeat MINUSTAH's legacy and deepen sovereignty resentment. Given BINUH's attribution of the majority of Q1-2026 casualties to security-force/PMC-drone operations, the conduct-safeguard clause is not optional: U.S. backing must not be read as endorsing an unaccountable use of force. The near-term action is to pair any funding decision with issuance of an OFAC humanitarian general license, so the FTO designation stops criminalizing the aid pipeline the strategy depends on.
