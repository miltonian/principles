# INTERNAL STRATEGIC REPORT — BUREAU OF WESTERN HEMISPHERE AFFAIRS
## The Haiti Crisis: Assessment and U.S. Policy Options
**Prepared for U.S. policymakers | For internal use — not for external distribution | As of 2026-07-04**

---

### 1. Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF)
Haiti is undergoing the simultaneous collapse of three core state functions — the monopoly on legitimate force, revenue collection and public-service delivery, and governing legitimacy — that reinforce one another in a self-deepening downward spiral, leaving armed gang federations in control of an estimated ~90% of Port-au-Prince (a stale estimate) and Haiti without an elected national government since 2021. For the United States, this directly implicates near-term interests: collapse-driven irregular migration to U.S. shores, regional stability on Hispaniola and across the Caribbean, two-directional narcotics-and-arms trafficking, and the credibility of a U.S.-co-sponsored multilateral security effort — even as the legitimacy vacuum widens the opening for strategic competitors. Three distinct policy levers are available and are presented as trade-offs, not a ranked recommendation: (1) enable the UN-authorized, non-UN-commanded Gang Suppression Force; (2) apply targeted legal-financial coercion against gang financiers, elite enablers, and the southbound U.S. arms pipeline; and (3) pursue humanitarian-economic stabilization paired with a regional migration compact. **A cross-cutting analytic judgment (surfaced by the drafting team and preserved here): because the three collapses are causally interlocked, the three options are complementary rather than substitutable — Option 3 in particular depends on Option 1 or 2 for durability, and any strategy that sequences the domains is structurally self-defeating.**

### 2. Failure Assessment: Three Interlocked Collapses
Haiti's crisis is not three parallel problems but three collapsed state functions locked in a mutually reinforcing loop; none can be repaired in isolation. These are collapses — functions that have ceased to operate and been assumed by non-state actors or vacated — not ordinary institutional weakness. Each collapse converts directly into U.S. exposure.

**Security (monopoly on force) — collapsed.** The state no longer contests armed control of its own capital: the Viv Ansanm coalition (the merged G9 an fanmi and G-Pèp under Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier) holds ~90% of metropolitan Port-au-Prince (stale estimate) and has pushed into Artibonite and Centre. This is coercive governance passing to armed federations, not a merely weak police force — and it is the vector for migration surges and unpoliced trafficking corridors that reach U.S. shores.

**Economic/fiscal (revenue and services) — collapsed.** Gang control of ports, fuel terminals, and highways captures the customs chokepoints that are the fiscal spine of a trade-dependent state; revenue is diverted to rival armed authorities rather than merely collected inefficiently. Service delivery has failed in step (≈40% of displacement sites without health services; food insecurity projected at 5.91M / 53% of the population in IPC Phase 3+, ~2M in Phase 4; no verified Phase 5 figure). The same captured ports are the trafficking and interdiction chokepoints the U.S. needs functioning.

**Political (governing legitimacy) — collapsed.** Haiti has had no elected president and no functioning elected legislature since the July 2021 assassination of President Jovenel Moïse. The 2024 CARICOM-brokered Transitional Presidential Council (TPC) dissolved on 7 February 2026 with no successor, vesting sole executive authority in the unelected, U.S.-backed PM Alix Didier Fils-Aimé; the August 2026 vote is widely expected to slip. No national popular mandate exists — the opening strategic competitors can exploit.

**Causal interlock (the throughline).** *Political → security:* the 2021 vacuum removed any nationally mandated executive able to fund or lawfully direct the police, which fragmented and enabled gangs to seize ports, customs, and fuel terminals. *Security → economic:* that seizure, plus highway blockades, strangled fuel, food, and medical logistics — producing the food-insecurity and service figures above. *Economic → political:* a government delivering neither services, security, nor elections forfeits performance legitimacy, the visible product being TPC infighting and its 2026 dissolution. *Reinforcing loop:* collapsed security prevents revenue collection and forecloses elections, deepening illegitimacy, which weakens the capacity to fund a credible security response — pushing reliance onto external forces (the UNSC-authorized, non-UN-commanded MSS-to-GSF transition, still early-stage), after which entrenched gangs capture more territory and revenue and restart the cycle. Any "security-first-then-services-then-legitimacy" sequencing is therefore structurally self-defeating.

### 3. Actors & Institutions
**G9 an fanmi / Chérizier (domestic, coercive).** G9 is an armed gang federation led by Jimmy "Barbecue" Chérizier, a former Haitian National Police officer, exercising territorial control over large portions of Port-au-Prince within the broader Viv Ansanm alliance. Its leverage is territorial-military: control of movement corridors and the capacity to choke commercial, humanitarian, and state access. **U.S. stake:** by holding terrain the state cannot enter, G9 holds an effective veto over electoral logistics and over any GSF operation, and its dominance sustains the trafficking corridors and displacement that drive migration toward U.S. shores.

**Guy Philippe (domestic, insurrectionary).** Guy Philippe is a former coup/rebellion leader and U.S.-convicted felon (money laundering) who, after repatriation to Haiti, positioned himself as an insurrectionary figure opposing the transitional government, drawing on 2004 rebellion credentials and alignment with the Brigade for the Security of Protected Areas (BSAP). His leverage is legitimacy-erosion and mobilizational spoiling rather than territorial control; his operational reach over BSAP is contested (latest firm directive April 2025). **U.S. stake:** he can sap the U.S.-backed interim executive's authority and complicate command over any BSAP element folded into anti-gang operations — a spoiler risk to the stability the U.S. seeks.

**CARICOM (broker).** CARICOM convened Haitian stakeholders and produced the TPC's power-sharing structure. Its leverage is legitimacy and convening, not coercion or funding — a hard ceiling set by TPC cohesion, which eroded until the Council's February 2026 dissolution. **U.S. stake:** CARICOM channels keep the response regionally owned and U.S./partner-led rather than rival-led, but cannot compel Haitian factions.

**UN Security Council / Kenya-led MSS → GSF (authorizer, not commander).** The UNSC authorized the MSS under Chapter VII but placed it **outside UN command**: a member-state-led, voluntarily-funded coalition operating with Haitian consent — **not** a UN blue-helmet peacekeeping mission and **not** under UN operational command. Res 2793 (Oct 2025) is transitioning the MSS into a ~5,500-authorized Gang Suppression Force (GSF) with OAS/UN support and a UN-supervised voluntary trust fund; Kenya withdrew (last officers ~17 Mar 2026) and Chadian troops began arriving April 2026. Authorization provides legal cover and a funding mechanism but not guaranteed troops, funding, or unified command. **U.S. stake:** this structure multilateralizes cost and risk (a plus for burden-sharing) but leaves outcomes hostage to voluntary contributions — and U.S. credibility riding on a force Washington co-sponsored but does not command.

### 4. U.S. National Interests (the governing lens)
Every assessment and option below is scored against these, in weighted order:
1. **Migration (high):** reduce collapse-driven irregular flows and the U.S. interdiction/repatriation burden.
2. **Regional stability (high):** contain spillover onto the Dominican Republic/Hispaniola and small CARICOM states.
3. **Trafficking, two-way (high):** disrupt northbound cocaine *and* the southbound U.S.-sourced arms arming the gangs.
4. **Counter-influence (medium-high):** foreclose PRC/Russia/malign footholds; keep solutions U.S./CARICOM/OAS-led.
5. **Credibility/leadership (medium-high):** protect U.S. multilateral credibility and diaspora-facing standing.
6. **Cost/burden-sharing (gating constraint):** avoid an open-ended, unilateral, MINUSTAH-type commitment. *(Note: the drafting team treats this as a veto-capable constraint on the other five, not a co-equal interest — options that drift toward an unbounded unilateral commitment fail regardless of other merits.)*

*Load-bearing tensions:* migration-deterrence vs. humanitarian credibility; stabilization ambition vs. the burden-sharing veto; and (secondary) counter-influence urgency vs. burden-sharing.

### 5. Policy Options (exactly three; distinct mechanisms)

#### Option 1 — Enable the Gang Suppression Force *(mechanism: multilateral security-force enablement — trust-fund financing, airlift/sustainment, ISR)*
The U.S. acts as indispensable enabler — not troop contributor — of the UNSC-authorized, non-UN-commanded GSF, via cash to the trust fund, airlift, sustainment, and ISR to target Viv Ansanm/G9 chokepoints, working through the UNSC/OAS framework and Haitian consent.
**Benefits (U.S. interests):** The only lever that attacks the security-monopoly collapse gating the fiscal and legitimacy loops; re-opening ports/highways is the precondition for revenue recovery and electoral logistics. Advances **regional stability** (rolls back gang expansion), **counter-influence** (keeps the response in U.S./CARICOM/OAS channels), **credibility** (validates U.S.-co-sponsored Res 2793), and honors **burden-sharing** (no U.S. command/combat footprint); medium-term relief of displacement eases **migration**.
**Risks (U.S. interests):** The GSF is early-stage with no verified territorial results and an incomplete trust fund (~$223M pledged/~$174M cash); credibility becomes hostage to a force the U.S. funds but cannot command. Urban kinetic operations risk civilian casualties that spike **migration** and reputational exposure; folding in BSAP risks empowering Guy Philippe or blurring command; a stalled GSF invites mission creep against the **burden-sharing** veto. *High-likelihood, moderate-to-high-severity that contributor shortfalls leave the force under-strength.*

#### Option 2 — Coercive legal-financial pressure *(mechanism: OFAC sanctions, visa restrictions, DOJ prosecutions, southbound arms interdiction)*
The U.S. applies its domestic and near-unilateral toolkit against the money and matériel sustaining armed actors: OFAC designations of Viv Ansanm/G9 leaders and elite financiers, visa bans, DOJ prosecutions (leveraging the jurisdiction that convicted Guy Philippe), and intensified ATF/DHS/Coast Guard interdiction of U.S.-sourced firearms, coordinated with CARICOM and UN sanctions channels.
**Benefits (U.S. interests):** The only option centrally serving **two-way trafficking** — interdicting the southbound arms pipeline attacks the U.S.-supply side of gang lethality while degrading the narcotics economy; it strikes the fiscal-capture node (advancing **stability**), reaches elite enablers force cannot (advancing **counter-influence**), and is low-footprint (satisfies **burden-sharing**), proceeding independent of GSF timelines.
**Risks (U.S. interests):** Slow and diffuse — gangs draw revenue from extortion beyond OFAC's reach and this restores no territory, so **migration** drivers persist. Designations can harden Chérizier's nationalist-victim narrative (a **credibility** own-goal); action against elites entangled with PM Fils-Aimé's fragile executive risks destabilizing the authority the U.S. backs (**stability** cost). *High-likelihood, low-to-moderate-severity — the principal risk is under-delivery rather than escalation.*

#### Option 3 — Humanitarian-economic stabilization and migration management *(mechanism: conditioned aid, revenue/port recovery, and a Caribbean migration compact)*
The U.S. channels conditioned humanitarian/reconstruction aid to restore service delivery and customs/fuel-terminal revenue functions, provides fiscal-administration support to the interim executive, and negotiates a compact with the Dominican Republic, The Bahamas, and CARICOM to share interdiction/repatriation burden and calibrate TPS/repatriation policy — through USAID, OAS, CARICOM, and Hispaniola bilateral channels.
**Benefits (U.S. interests):** Uniquely targets **migration** (highest-weighted) by cutting push factors (53% in IPC 3+, 1.3–1.5M displaced) and pre-distributing burden; advances **regional stability** (defuses Dominican friction), **credibility** (diaspora-facing leadership), and **counter-influence** (works through U.S.-led legitimacy channels); burden-sharing built into the compact.
**Risks (U.S. interests):** Aid durability tracks the security environment — with gangs controlling ~90% of the capital's fiscal chokepoints (stale estimate), spending risks capture into the G9-dominated economy (own-goal against **stability/trafficking**). The sharp **migration-vs-credibility** tension: interdiction/expedited repatriation/TPS restriction advance **migration** but damage **credibility** and invite legal challenge. Conditioning aid on an unelected, cohesion-poor executive may stall. *Moderate-likelihood, high-severity of aid capture absent a parallel security track — this option depends on Options 1 or 2 for durability.*

### 7. Analyst's Note — Data-Currency Caveats and Points of Internal Divergence
*Surfacing these explicitly so senior readers weight the judgments correctly:*

- **Baseline updates that supersede standing assumptions.** Two developments postdate this report's founding assumptions and are load-bearing: (1) the CARICOM-brokered TPC **dissolved 7 Feb 2026 with no successor**, leaving sole executive authority with the **unelected, U.S.-backed PM Alix Didier Fils-Aimé** — Haiti still has no elected president or legislature; (2) the Kenya-led MSS is **transitioning to the ~5,500-authorized Gang Suppression Force** under UNSC Res 2793, which remains **UN-authorized/OAS- and UN-supported but NOT UN-commanded and NOT a blue-helmet mission**. Do not describe the TPC as currently sitting or the GSF as UN peacekeeping.
- **Figures to treat as estimates, not settled fact.** The "~90% of Port-au-Prince" gang-control figure is a **stale estimate** with no fresh methodological measurement; IDP totals span a **1.29M–1.5M range** across sources; **no verified IPC Phase 5 (catastrophe) figure** and **no verified 2026 cholera/GBV-incidence figures** were found; the **GSF has no assessable territorial results, casualties, or gang-loss data** yet, and its trust fund is pledged-but-incomplete. Options premised on GSF success (Option 1) should be discounted for this uncertainty.
- **The one substantive internal divergence.** The drafting team declined to include a standalone **diplomatic/electoral-brokering** option, folding legitimacy restoration into Option 1's consent framework and Option 3's CARICOM/OAS channels — a deliberate trade to preserve dedicated coverage of migration (the highest-weighted interest). A senior reader who prioritizes a near-term electoral pathway over migration management could reasonably ask for that fourth mechanism to be surfaced as its own option.
- **Interdependence, not menu-selection.** The upstream analysis is unanimous that the three collapses cannot be repaired in isolation. Consequently, **Option 3 adopted alone would contradict the assessment** (it depends on 1 or 2 for the security conditions that make aid stick), and the options' severity characterizations are **not directly comparable** without weighting by interest priority (migration/stability/trafficking highest; burden-sharing gating). Treat the three as a portfolio to be sequenced-in-parallel, not as mutually exclusive choices."
