# Ready-to-Shoot Production Outline — YouTube Short
## "Which Cup Makes the Chain Jump Higher?"

*Vertical 9:16 • Total runtime 34 seconds (well under the 3:00 Shorts cap) • Solo shoot, smartphone + free apps only*

---

## 1. THE CONCEPT AND WHY IT WORKS (content strategy)

The phenomenon is the **chain fountain**, also called the **Mould effect**. Drape one end of a beaded metal ball-chain over the lip of a cup and let that end fall to the floor. The chain doesn't just spill out — it leaps into a self-sustaining arch that rises *above the rim of the cup* before curving down to the floor. Metal appears to climb into empty air with nobody touching it. That single image is the entire reason this Short can stop a thumb mid-scroll: it directly contradicts the deepest pattern any viewer holds, "things fall DOWN."

**The one idea, stated at explainer depth:** the falling side of the chain cannot simply lift each new link by tension alone. Because the rigid links can't bend instantly as they're picked up off the heap, the stationary pile pushes back with an extra upward reaction — a "kick" — that throws the chain higher than gravity alone would allow. That is the whole physics of the video. One concept, no second thread.

**The original angle — a bet the viewer will lose.** Almost every existing chain-fountain video sells it as a "gravity-defying trick" (single cup, pure wonder) or a "how does it work" lecture, and several even reinforce the wrong intuition by calling it a *siphon*. This Short breaks from all of them by staging a **two-cup A/B prediction**: two identical cups of the same chain, one sitting low on the floor and one held up high. On-screen text asks, *"Which one makes the chain jump higher?"* Almost everyone guesses the low cup or "the same," because intuition says the effect is about being *pulled down and out*. The reveal punishes that guess: **the higher you hold the cup, the higher the fountain leaps above the rim** — the opposite of the "it's being sucked down" model.

The reframe that makes this treatment distinct is that the chain isn't pulled up from above like a siphon — it's *kicked up from below* by the pile it's leaving. The distinct question is not "how does it work?" but "what's throwing it UP if gravity only pulls DOWN, and why does dropping it *farther* make it jump *higher*?" No cataloged competitor runs a viewer-prediction bet or the height-scaling reveal, so the payoff here is "you guessed wrong," not merely "neat."

**Why it's demonstrable on consumer gear.** The only critical purchase is a rigid beaded metal ball-chain — the kind on ceiling-fan pulls, bath-plug chains, roller-blind loops, or dog-tag necklaces. Roughly 10–30 ft of #6 ball chain runs about $8–12 at any hardware store, or it's free if repurposed from around the house; the whole build is well under $20. Everything else is household: a tall cup or mug with a smooth rim, a stack of books, masking tape, and a phone. A modern iPhone or Android shoots slow-motion (240fps / 0.25×), which is all the "special equipment" the leap needs. Free editing apps (CapCut or InShot) handle the speed ramp, split-screen, freeze-frame, and captions.

## 2. ACCURACY GUARDRAILS (fact-check — read before filming)

The core spine is scientifically sound at a general-audience level, but two things must be worded carefully or a knowledgeable commenter will break the video with a single follow-up.

**Guardrail 1 — the pinch-collapse is NOT proof of the "kick."** The video includes a live proof beat: pinch the falling strand mid-run and the arch collapses instantly. This is a great *visual*, but be precise about what it proves. Pinching stops the chain from moving, so the fountain would collapse **no matter which mechanism is true** — a siphon would also stop if you halted the flow. Therefore the pinch does **not** single-handedly prove "kicked from below." It only proves the fountain exists **while the chain is actively flowing** — it isn't a rigid arch holding itself up.

- **Wrong (breaks under one question):** "Pinch it and it drops — proof it's kicked from below."
- **Correct, ready-to-speak:** "Pinch the falling side and the arch instantly drops — proof the fountain only stands *while the chain is flowing*, it's not a solid loop."

Keep the "kick from below" as the *explanation*, established separately in its own beat — never as the thing the pinch alone proves.

**Guardrail 2 — state the "kick" as the accepted model, not closed-book science.** The rise above the rim genuinely *does* require an extra upward force from the pile/container region beyond simple tension, so the model is directionally solid and correct to tell a general audience. However, the exact origin of that force is still refined in the literature. Say it confidently but don't declare it fully-settled, and don't open the academic debate on camera (that would break the single-concept constraint). Safe on-camera phrasing: *"As each bead is yanked off the heap, the pile shoves back up on it — that extra upward kick is what launches the arch over the rim."*

**Guardrail 3 — the height-scaling claim.** "Higher cup ⇒ higher fountain" is qualitatively correct and needs **no number** on camera. Do not invent or state a specific ratio or proportionality constant; if you ever want to cite one, source it first. Also be honest with yourself in production: at household drop heights (~1–2 m) the height difference is *real but modest*, so film the "high" cup from as high as you can safely reach and let the edit alignment do the heavy lifting.

**Guardrail 4 — material is make-or-break.** It **must** be a rigid-linked beaded/ball metal chain (or fine metal chain), **never** string, yarn, or rope. Smooth cord does not fountain, because the effect depends on the links' resistance to instant bending. Test-run before you commit to a filming session.

No physical constants, coefficients, or measured magnitudes are asserted anywhere in the explanation, so nothing on camera requires a citation. With the four guardrails above applied, no physicist would wince and no obvious follow-up question breaks the Short.

## 3. TITLE

**Recommended:** **"Which Cup Makes the Chain Jump Higher?"**
This poses the two-cup bet directly; the viewer has to watch to see which guess wins, and the answer stays hidden. It balances specificity (a real chain and cup) with an open curiosity gap.

**Alternatives, for A/B testing:**
- **"Bet You Pick the Wrong Cup"** — implied-bet framing that dares the viewer to guess; front-loads the challenge hardest, slightly less tied to the visual.
- **"This Chain Climbs Out of Its Cup"** — a concrete everyday object framed as impossible; sets up the "things fall DOWN" contradiction without explaining how.
- **"Why Does This Chain Leap UP?"** — a why-question aimed at the gravity intuition; teases the mystery force but never names the reveal.

All four guard the two spoilers — that the *higher* cup wins, and the *kicked-from-below* mechanism — and deliberately avoid the words "siphon," "Mould effect," "chain fountain," and "tension" so nothing primes the misconception the video busts. Every title is under 40 characters, safe for the phone feed and any on-screen caption.

## 4. OPENING HOOK (0.0s–3.0s)

**Spoken line (verbatim, ~2.2s):**
> "This chain jumps UP — and nobody's touching it."

Eight words. Deliver fast and slightly bewildered.

**Exact first frame at 0.0s:** hard cut straight into the *peak* of the effect — no walk-up. A shining beaded metal ball-chain is mid-leap: a smooth arch of beads standing in the air *above* the cup's rim, clearly rising out of the cup on one side and pouring toward the floor on the other. Shoot in slow-mo and edit so 0.0s lands on the tallest point of the arch, beads visibly in motion. Frame tight and horizontal on the rim + arch against a plain, high-contrast wall (dark chain on a light wall, or vice versa) so the hovering loop reads instantly with sound off. Metal climbing into empty air is the thumb-stopper.

**First-second caption:** **"THIS SHOULDN'T HAPPEN"** — top-center, big bold sans-serif, white with black outline so it's legible muted over any background, hard pop-in on frame one.

**Two alternate spoken variants (swap-in):**
1. **Bold-claim:** "Higher cup, higher jump — that's backwards, right?"
2. **Question:** "Guess which cup makes this leap higher."

Only one cup is visible in this hero shot; the second cup belongs to the body. Keep 0–3s to a single dramatic arch to preserve muted readability, and let no word explain the physics — the withheld "why" *is* the curiosity gap.

## 6. GLOBAL SETUP (set once, reuse across every beat)

- **Chain:** rigid beaded/ball **METAL** chain only (fan-pull, blind-cord, bath-plug, or dog-tag chain). **Never** string. Pre-pack it loose in figure-8 layers inside each cup so it feeds without tangling. Test-run before filming.
- **Cups:** two identical smooth-rim cups/mugs. **Cup A = "low"** (sits on the floor). **Cup B = "high"** (top edge of a bookshelf, tall dresser, or door-top — as high as you can safely reach). Higher = bigger visible jump, and the delta is modest at household heights, so go as high as is safe.
- **Backdrop:** one plain high-contrast wall (dark chain vs. light wall, or reverse). Shoot near a window.
- **Camera:** **rear camera** for all demo shots (sharper); front camera only for the optional Beat-3 face shot. Lock the phone off — no handheld for fountains.
- **Solo rig — the trick that makes this possible:** the fountain **self-feeds** once you release the starter length. You start it and let go, so the phone stays locked off (propped against a mug on a stack of books, or masking-taped flat to a wall/cabinet) while both hands are free. No second person, no tripod-timer.
- **Caption zone:** on-screen text lives in the lower-middle safe band; big callouts sit between the top ~12% and bottom ~18% UI margins. Compose every shot with action in the middle/lower thirds and clear headroom up top.

## 7. PER-BEAT VISUAL & SHOT DIRECTION

### BEAT 1 — HOOK | 0.0–2.5s
- **Framing:** hard cut onto the *peak* of the arch — no walk-up. Cup B (high) at frame bottom, the shining arch of beads standing in mid-air across the middle third, headroom up top for the caption. Tight enough that hovering metal fills the frame.
- **Camera:** rear camera, locked off. Prop the phone vertically against a mug on a stack of books (or tape it flat to a wall/cabinet) at rim height, ~2–3 ft from the cup. Static.
- **Props:** Cup B packed with chain, set on the shelf/door-top; a starter length already pulled over the rim toward the floor.
- **Lighting:** window daylight hitting the wall so the beads glint; keep the light *beside or behind* the phone, never behind the chain (avoid silhouette).
- **Action:** off-camera, pull the starter length down and **release** — the chain self-feeds. Film the full run in slow-mo (240fps / 0.25×).
- **Edit:** in CapCut, scrub to the single frame where the arch is **tallest** and cut the beat to open exactly there. **Save this frame as the loop anchor for the closing beat.**

### BEAT 2 — THE BET | 2.5–7.0s
- **Framing:** wide vertical establishing shot showing **both** contestants at once — Cup A on the floor (lower third), Cup B on the shelf (mid third). Both clearly the same cup and chain. Keep the top third clear.
- **Camera:** rear camera, locked off, backed up and propped on books on a chair so the tall frame catches floor-to-shelf. Static.
- **Props:** both cups in frame, chain visible in each. Optional: tape strips on the floor and shelf so viewers register "low vs. high."
- **Lighting:** same window setup; if the raised cup is lost in ceiling shadow, angle a desk lamp bounced off a sheet of white paper up toward it.
- **Action:** a hand enters and taps/points to Cup A, then up to Cup B on "one low, one held up high." No fountain running yet — this is the setup.
- **Edit:** optional 0.3s whip of the hand between the two cups for motion; no effect required.

### BEAT 3 — THE TRAP | 7.0–10.0s
- **Framing:** fast punch-in on Cup A (the "obvious wrong guess") in the lower/mid third, then a beat of nothing. Keep caption headroom.
- **Camera:** reuse Beat 2's locked-off wide frame; no re-shoot needed. *Optional personality:* cut to a 1s front-camera selfie head-shake, phone at arm's length or leaned on books at face height.
- **Action:** a hand gives a quick "no" wag over the low cup on "You're wrong," or the selfie head-shake.
- **Edit:** in CapCut, use a punch-in/zoom keyframe on the Beat 2 clip to snap toward Cup A — a cut without re-filming. Hard jump-cut on "You're wrong" for snap.

### BEAT 4 — THE REVEAL | 10.0–16.0s
- **Framing:** **split-screen side-by-side**, both fountains at peak — left = low-cup run, right = high-cup run — aligned so the viewer directly compares arch heights (high cup clearly taller). Rim of each cup on a matched baseline. Caption top-center over both.
- **Camera:** rear camera, locked off in the **same** position for both takes so the two clips line up. Film them one at a time (solo), same distance, same wall.
- **Props:** Cup A run and Cup B run — same chain re-packed between takes.
- **Lighting:** identical for both takes (same time of day/window) so the split looks like one shot.
- **Action:** each fountain self-feeds after you release the starter length; capture both in slow-mo.
- **Edit:** CapCut split-screen/overlay — place both clips side-by-side and trim so both arches hit their **peak at the same moment** for a clean height comparison. A subtle speed-ramp to slow-mo at the twin peaks sells the delta. This alignment is what makes the modest household-height difference read.

### BEAT 5 — THE WHY | 16.0–24.0s
- **Framing:** overhead macro looking straight **down** into the cup — beads visibly lifting off the heap and whipping upward over the rim. Fill the frame with the pile; keep the very top strip clear.
- **Camera:** rear camera pointing down. Rest the phone flat, lens over the cup mouth, balanced across two stacked books or a shoebox bridge straddling the cup (or tape it to the underside of a shelf; if you need it higher, tape the phone to a broom handle wedged upright). Locked off.
- **Lighting:** desk lamp aimed **into** the cup (bounced off white paper to soften) so the beads lifting off the heap are lit.
- **Action:** start the fountain and let the camera watch the pile "buck" as each bead is snatched off. Capture in 240fps to make the lift-off legible. Optionally use a shorter drop to slow the feed so the pile motion is filmable.
- **Edit:** slow to 0.25× and punch in on the exact spot where beads leave the pile, timed to the word "kicking." **Do NOT let any caption call this "proof" of the kick — it's illustration of the stated model.**

### BEAT 6 — THE PROOF | 24.0–30.0s
- **Framing:** locked-off medium shot of a running fountain (Cup B, high, for the biggest arch), arch in the mid third, the falling strand visible down to the floor, caption headroom on top.
- **Camera:** rear camera, same book/tape mount as the hook. Static — the chain self-feeds, leaving one hand free to pinch.
- **Action:** let the fountain run 1–2s, then your hand darts in and **pinches** the falling strand — the whole arch collapses instantly. Shoot in 240fps so the collapse reads.
- **Edit:** freeze-frame (~0.3s) on the last standing arch the instant before it drops, then play the collapse at slow speed — makes the "it only stands while flowing" point land as a shareable beat.

### BEAT 7 — LOOP CLOSE | 30.0–34.0s
- **Framing:** return to the **exact** hero framing of Beat 1 — same arch, same cup position, same wall, arch in mid third with top-third headroom — so the last image matches the first.
- **Camera:** rear camera, identical locked-off position/distance as Beat 1.
- **Action:** the arch stands and climbs on the closing line; end the frame on the tall arch. **No creator, hands, or props in frame** — the "nobody's touching it" claim must read literally.
- **Edit:** match-cut the final frame's brightness/scale to Beat 1's opening frame; ideally reuse the same source clip so the auto-repeat flows straight back into the hook.
