# The Chain Fountain — YouTube Short Production Outline

## The Concept

**The Chain Fountain (the "Mould Effect," or self-siphoning beads).**

Here's the one-liner you're selling: *a metal ball-chain poured out of a raised cup doesn't just fall — it first LEAPS several inches up into the air above the rim, arcing like a fountain, because the pile at the bottom of the cup actively kicks each bead upward as it's yanked into motion.*

It looks like anti-gravity — a chain visibly jumping UP out of a cup instead of pouring straight down — yet it's a genuine, peer-reviewed effect (Biggins & Warner, 2014) caused by an upward "kick" from the pile. That combination is why it's perfect for your channel: it's instantly surprising to a stranger scrolling past, and it's scientifically real, so you can actually explain it without lying.

The whole video hangs on one continuous slow-motion take of the chain fountaining upward. That unbroken shot is your proof it isn't a camera trick, and it's the visual peak of the entire piece. Everything else — the setup, the explanation beats, the payoff — is built around reusing that single clean take.

Frame the piece as **"surprising but real."** Never claim literal anti-gravity as the mechanism. Gravity is fully active the whole time, and the chain still falls; the rise is a brief upward push from the pile. Momentum and energy are conserved throughout. That honesty is what protects you against a knowledgeable viewer in the comments — and it's what makes the reveal land harder than a cheap trick ever could.

Target length: about 38 seconds, vertical 9:16.

## The Hook (first ~3 seconds)

Your hook lever is the **mid-demo reveal**: open *inside* the payoff so a stranger's brain flags "chains don't do that" in the first half-second.

**Opening visual:** Phone vertical, framed tight on the cup's RIM with generous empty headroom ABOVE it. The very first frame is ALREADY mid-payoff — the ball-chain leaping UP and arcing several inches above the rim in silky 0.125x–0.25x slow-motion against a dark backdrop, beads catching the lamp light. Do NOT open on the release, the pull, or the empty cup. Cut straight into the take at the moment the fountain is fully formed and rising. Camera locked and steady so the arc is the only motion.

**On-screen text (exact):** "wait… it goes UP?" — CENTER, just above the rising arc. Fades in at ~0.4s (after the eye has registered the motion), holds through 3.0s.

**Spoken line (exact, 9 words, under 3 seconds):** "Watch — the chain jumps UP before it ever falls."

**Alt version for A/B testing:** Same tight rim shot, chain already fountaining. On-screen text: "gravity broken?" (top, ~0.3s). Spoken: "Why is this chain flying UP out of the cup?" Keep the mid-demo reveal as your primary — letting the visual shock speak first is the strongest scroll-stop.

## The Physics You'll Explain

These are the ordered beats of your explanation. Deliver them plainly; the goal is that a curious 12-year-old and a physics teacher both walk away satisfied.

**Beat 1 — State the surprise.** Watch the very top of the cup. The metal bead-chain doesn't just pour over the edge and drop — it shoots UP first, arcing several inches above the rim before curving back down. It looks like the chain is climbing out of its own cup.

**Beat 2 — Name the wrong intuition.** Your gut says this is impossible. Nothing is pushing the chain upward — gravity only pulls DOWN — so the chain should slump straight over the lip and fall. There shouldn't be a fountain at all.

**Beat 3 — Reveal the real cause.** Here's the twist: the pile of chain sitting in the bottom of the cup pushes BACK. Every time a bead gets yanked into motion, the pile shoves it upward for a split second — a tiny kick — and it's those kicks, one after another, that launch the chain over the rim. ("The pile pushes back" means an upward reaction force from the heap still in the cup, not a mysterious new force.)

**Beat 4 — Walk through the mechanism.** Think about why. Each little bead is a stiff, solid piece — not floppy like string. When the moving chain snaps one bead into action, that bead can't just glide straight up; it tips and levers against the beads still piled below it. It presses DOWN on the pile — and by the rule that every push gets an equal push back (Newton's third law), the pile presses UP on it, flicking it into the air.

**Beat 5 — Why the material matters.** This is why it MUST be a metal bead-chain and not a rope or string. Rope is too floppy — it just bends and slides out with no push, so no fountain. The chain works because its little rigid segments can brace and shove against the pile.

**Beat 6 — Tie back to the visible moment.** So that rising arc you're watching in slow motion? That's not the chain defying gravity — that's the exact moment each bead gets its upward kick from the pile, made big enough to see because so many beads — hundreds every second — are being launched at once.

**Beat 7 — Resolve honestly.** Gravity never stopped working. The chain still falls, and the whole thing is powered by the part already dropping and pulling. The pile just adds a brief upward nudge on the way out, so the chain rises a little before gravity wins and drags it down. Real, filmable, and confirmed in a 2014 study by physicists Biggins and Warner — not a camera trick.

**Do NOT say (this keeps the science defensible):**
- "Momentum carries it up" / "the falling chain's speed throws it up" — a pure momentum picture predicts pouring, not a rise ABOVE the rim.
- "It defies/beats gravity" or "anti-gravity" — gravity is active the whole time.
- "Centrifugal force flings it up" — nothing is spinning.
- "Air pressure or suction pulls it up" — it's mechanical contact forces, not a vacuum.

## Materials & Setup

**Budget these as minimums to take seriously, not caps.**

- **Long metal BALL-CHAIN, 3–5 m or more.** Hardware/craft bulk runs ~$5–15, or link together lamp-pull / bathtub-plug / keychain chains. **NON-NEGOTIABLE** — rope, string, or a smooth necklace will NOT fountain.
- **Tall cup, jar, or beaker** to hold the loosely piled chain.
- **A height source** — chair, stair railing, ladder, or high shelf — with a clear, unobstructed drop below. It's the size of the DROP below the cup, not the chain length by itself, that makes the fountain taller. Longer chain matters because it lets you raise the cup higher and sustain the run. Raise it as high as you safely can over a clear drop.
- **Smartphone with built-in slow-motion** (240fps if available). No app, no CGI.
- **Dark backdrop** (dark cloth or wall behind the rim) plus **a lamp** aimed across it for contrast and to light the high frame rate.
- **Phone placement:** locked on a tripod OR propped on a stack of books, aimed at the cup's RIM with generous empty HEADROOM ABOVE it so the arc doesn't clip out of frame.
- **Pre-shoot prep:** pile the chain LOOSELY back-and-forth into the cup; do a TEST PULL to confirm it feeds without tangling (tangling is the #1 failure). Reshoot the fountain 3–5×; keep the highest, steadiest run as ONE continuous take.
- **Voiceover** is recorded separately after filming. Any spoken line may instead be delivered as an on-screen caption if you prefer silence — no on-camera talking-head is required.

## Demo Reliability — Get the Shot on the First Session

Preconditions for the filmed result to match the claim:
- **Use ball-chain** (rigid beaded links). Rope, string, or a smooth necklace will NOT fountain — the single make-or-break requirement.
- **Genuinely elevate the cup** so the chain falls well BELOW the cup's base. The falling side is the engine; a small drop gives a weak or invisible arc. Bigger drop (chair → stairwell) = taller, clearer fountain.
- **Pile the chain loosely**, back-and-forth, and do a test pull. Tangling/snagging is the #1 failure — a knot stalls the flow and kills the fountain mid-take.
- **Enough length** to sustain the run so the fountain holds long enough to film cleanly.
- **Adequate light** for 240fps slow-mo (the lamp), or the footage will be dark and noisy.
- **Frame with headroom** above the rim or the peak clips out of frame.

**Failure modes to avoid:** too-short a drop (no visible rise), tangling (flow stops), smooth chain/rope (no effect at all), insufficient light (unusable slow-mo).

## Verification Notes

- **Science checked and cleared.** The concept, mechanism, and attribution are correct: Biggins & Warner (2014), *Understanding the chain fountain*, Proc. R. Soc. A 470(2163):20130689. The fountain requires an anomalous upward reaction force from the pile; rigid links lever against the heap and, by Newton's third law, get pushed up. Energy comes from the already-falling chain; gravity and momentum are conserved throughout.
- **Two numbers were corrected from earlier drafts.** (1) Fountain height scales with the DROP height below the cup (h₂ ∝ h₁), not chain length by itself — a ~30 m drop gives ~2.1 m of rise, so a household 1–4 m drop yields roughly 8–30 cm; that is why the outline emphasizes drop height. (2) "Dozens of beads per second" understated by ~10×; at realistic flow speeds it is hundreds per second, which a slow-mo viewer could count — hence "hundreds" throughout.
- **One honest hedge to keep in mind:** the anomalous upward pile force is well established and experimentally verified, but its exact microscopic magnitude is still being refined in later literature (Pantaleone 2017; Flekkøy et al. 2018). Present the kick as "the accepted cause," never as "every detail fully settled" — and never let a shortening pass revert to "momentum pushes it up" or "anti-gravity," both of which are on the do-not-say list.
- **Sources:** Biggins & Warner (2014), Proc. R. Soc. A — royalsocietypublishing.org/doi/10.1098/rspa.2013.0689; open-access preprint arxiv.org/pdf/1310.4056; en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chain_fountain (reproducibility, height scaling, refining literature); University of Cambridge news summary corroborating the mechanism and attribution.
