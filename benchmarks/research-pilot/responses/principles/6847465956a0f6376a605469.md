# When the Machines No Longer Need Us: Two Futures of Full Automation

## Framing the Comparison

Imagine a future in which the machinery of production no longer requires us. Machines and artificial intelligence perform substantially all economically valuable work, and human labor is no longer a necessary input to making the things a society consumes. This essay examines what happens next — and it examines it *twice*, changing only one thing between the two examinations.

Throughout, "fully automated" means exactly that: goods and services can be produced without anyone needing to work. The definition is deliberately narrow. It removes human labor as a *necessary factor of production*. It does not, by itself, say who owns the machines, who controls them, or who receives what they produce. Those questions are left open on purpose, because they are precisely where the two futures diverge.

To reason cleanly, we hold automation constant and run a controlled experiment. Two societies, identical in productive capacity, technology, and capacity for abundance, differ on one variable only: whether the automated output is universally distributed to every person.

- **Scenario 1 — automation without universal provision:** the machines produce, but no one is guaranteed access to what they produce.
- **Scenario 2 — automation with universal provision:** the machines produce, and everyone is guaranteed a baseline of what they produce.

"Universal resources" means guaranteed access to sustenance-level food, shelter, energy, and care, provided regardless of employment, deliverable as cash (a universal basic income) or as in-kind goods and services. "Sustenance-level" is a floor — survival and basic dignity — not a promise of affluence. Because technology is identical across the two, any divergence we later observe must be attributed to the *distribution choice*, not to differing machine capability. Everything that follows is reasoned speculation grounded in economics and political theory — contingent, plausible trajectories, not certain forecasts. We analyze each society along two fixed axes: its **economic system** (how sustenance is secured and output distributed) and its **governance** (how it is politically structured and held together).

## The Economic Baseline Both Societies Inherit

Before the fork, both societies inherit the same economic starting condition, and it is worth stating plainly because it drives everything else.

In an ordinary economy, the value production creates is split between two contributors. Labor supplies effort, skill, and judgment, and is compensated with wages that roughly track its marginal contribution — roughly what an additional hour of work adds to output. Capital supplies the durable, ownable assets used in production, and is compensated with profit, rent, and returns paid to whoever owns those assets. This split matters because wages are the channel through which the great majority of people receive a share of what the economy produces. Most people own little productive capital; they participate in the economy's output chiefly by selling their labor into it. The wage is, in effect, the pipe that disperses production to the many.

Now apply the fixed premise. If machines perform substantially all economically valuable production without requiring human labor, then labor's marginal contribution collapses toward zero. When an additional human hour adds essentially nothing the automated system was not already going to produce, there is no longer a productive reason to pay wages for most work. The pipe that dispersed production to the many narrows toward closing.

Output does not stop being produced; it is produced by capital alone. So the entire residual accrues to whoever owns the productive capital. Four categories are worth naming, because each is an ownable, excludable, productive asset: the **machines and robots** that perform the work; **energy** — the generation and grid capacity that powers production, finite, sited, and metered; **land**, the original excludable asset supplying space and resources; and the **AI models** themselves — trained systems, their weights, and the data and compute behind them, increasingly ownable intellectual property.

The structural upshot is precise. Automation does not make output vanish or even shrink; it can make output vastly *larger*. What it changes is who the output flows to. Absent redistribution, returns concentrate onto capital owners rather than dispersing through wages. This concentrated-by-default flow is not a choice one society makes and another avoids — it is the common condition both inherit. They diverge on exactly one thing: whether a standing mechanism redirects that flow back to the whole population.

## Scenario 1: The Economics of Exclusion

Scenario 1 is that baseline left to run with no redistributive valve attached. The decisive economic event is the collapse of the wage as an income channel. Once labor's marginal contribution falls toward zero, that pipe closes — and, crucially, nothing in the market automatically reopens it. No invisible hand restores mass purchasing power, because purchasing power flows to those who supply something the economy needs, and the excluded majority now supplies nothing production requires. Their labor is not underpaid; it is simply unbought.

This produces the central tension: the "who buys the output?" problem. Concentrated ownership means output can be enormous while the paying market for it shrinks, because the people who would consume it have no income with which to bid. Owners plausibly respond not by re-hiring but by reorienting production — producing luxury and positional goods for one another, reinvesting returns into still more capital, exporting to whatever solvent markets remain, or simply producing less of what the excluded once consumed because that demand is no longer backed by money. None of these routes runs through wages. The economy does not need the majority as workers, and increasingly does not need them as customers either.

Stripped of wages and of any guaranteed transfer, the surplus population must improvise sustenance from whatever channels survive outside the automated core — none of them reliable. Informal and gray economies: barter, mutual aid, small-scale trade and services rendered among the propertyless. Subsistence pockets: reclaimed land and small-plot farming on terrain owners find unprofitable to contest. Charity and residual transfers: philanthropy or minimal relief, discretionary and revocable rather than guaranteed. Scavenging of surplus: living off the discards of an abundant elite economy. And debt: borrowing against a future income that automation has structurally removed, tending toward insolvency rather than recovery.

Honesty requires stating that some simply fail. Where none of these channels reaches far enough, the outcome is destitution — food insecurity, precarious shelter, no dependable income. The essay's abundance and the majority's scarcity coexist without contradiction, because abundance measures what is *produced* while scarcity measures what one can *claim*, and claiming now depends on assets the majority does not hold.

The likely structure is three near-frozen tiers. At the top, the capital owners who capture the output flow. Beneath them, a thin intermediate class the owners still find useful — specialists, security and maintenance personnel, artists, and personal-service providers whose work is valued precisely because it is scarce or status-conferring — receiving income as a discretionary spillover from elite spending, not as a broad wage. At the bottom, the excluded surplus, detached from production entirely. Mobility freezes because the classical ladder — earn wages, save, acquire assets — is severed at the first rung: one cannot accumulate savings from wages one does not earn, and without savings one cannot buy into the capital that is now the sole source of income. Ownership, once concentrated, compounds and self-perpetuates, maintained through asset title, through geography and enclave, and through the sheer capital cost of entry rising out of reach. The unresolved demand paradox resolves into one of several contingent equilibria: a decoupled elite economy trading capital-to-capital; a shrinking, neglected mass whose demand carries no monetary weight; or a *managed minimal subsistence*, where owners fund a bare floor not from obligation but as a calculation to prevent unrest. That last is where economics quietly hands off to coercive governance.

## Scenario 1: Governance Without the Governed

Politics follows the economics. Every historically durable check on rulers has run through the ruler's dependence on the ruled. The many constrained the few because the few needed them — to labor, to soldier, to pay taxes, to buy goods, and, in the modern era, to legitimate rule through votes. Each dependency was a lever: withdraw labor and production stops; refuse conscription and armies empty; deny consent and legitimacy erodes. In an economy where automated capital performs substantially all production, the first and most important of these levers simply disappears. Because the majority holds no economic leverage — no strike power, no indispensable role as producers, no consumer necessity the elite economy actually requires — the traditional mechanism that forced concessions is gone. Power gravitates to whatever is still *needed*: the automated productive stack together with the security layer that defends it. Ownership of that stack becomes, functionally, sovereignty.

Formal institutions need not vanish overnight; more likely they are hollowed. Elections and legislatures can persist as facade while decisions migrate to where the assets sit — corporate boards, capital consortia, and the private security and infrastructure entities owners control directly. When income concentrates as radically as the collapsed-wage economy implies, the conversion of money into political influence becomes near-total. The plausible endpoint is a **capital-holder oligarchy**: rule by and for the owners of automated capacity, whether it wears republican costume or drops it.

The coercive apparatus is the decisive break. Historically, the great vulnerability of any repressive regime was its army: coercion required men willing to point weapons at other men, and those men could refuse, defect, or turn. Automation dissolves precisely this dependency. The same technological base that removes human labor from production removes it from enforcement — automated surveillance (pervasive sensors, facial and gait recognition, financial and communications monitoring), predictive policing that flags dissent before it organizes, and autonomous or remotely operated security forces that execute orders without conscience, fatigue, or fraternal sympathy. The classic revolutionary hinge — the moment the troops join the people — is engineered out. Coercion becomes a capital good owners simply buy and run.

Governance over the excluded becomes a containment problem, drawing on the toolkit of extraction regimes that never depended on their subjects. *Spatial segregation* is foundational: secured enclaves for owners, assets, and specialists, walled off from excluded zones. *Selective repression* targets organizers rather than blanketing everyone, keeping suppression cheap. *Calibrated pacification* — the "managed minimal subsistence" the economics already gestures toward — supplies just enough to keep unrest below the threshold that threatens the enclaves, deliberately delivered as revocable charity rather than right, so it confers no leverage. *Narrative and information control* shapes what the excluded believe. And *co-optation and divide-and-rule* peel a thin servant-and-enforcer stratum out of the excluded population while cultivating fractures so the majority never coheres. Colonial administrations and rentier petro-states — which drew wealth from resources rather than citizens' labor — are instructive analogues: not predictions, but demonstrations that rulers *can* govern people they have no use for.

Legitimacy is the crux, and genuinely novel. An elite that needs neither the majority's labor nor its consumption may not need mass legitimacy at all. The likely reality is a spectrum: toward one end, *manufactured consent* — an ideology of meritocratic deservingness, spectacle, nationalism, or religion — pursued where it is cheaper than force; toward the other, *naked coercion*, where legitimacy is simply not sought. The regime's fragilities are real: permanent repression is a standing cost paid forever; **elite in-fighting** becomes the sharpest danger, since with the masses neutralized the only actors who matter are other owners; **defection** of indispensable specialists is a pressure point; and technical or moral failure can crack the edifice. The plausible arc runs from early capture through entrenchment toward either a stable **techno-feudal caste order** or an **unstable cycle** of repression and revolt — but both roads run through owner supremacy, because the economic foundation removed every lever the majority once held.

## Scenario 2: The Redirection Mechanism It Must Build to Exist

The mirror society does not arise from automation on its own. Guaranteed sustenance for every person is precisely a *reversal* of the default flow, and reversals require a standing apparatus to accomplish them. Scenario 2 is defined by that apparatus — and its durability, not the technology, is the fragile thing.

**Why the mechanism is necessary, not optional.** If returns concentrate on capital owners by default, universal resources can exist only if some continuous mechanism diverts a portion of that concentrated flow back to the whole population. There is no equilibrium in which abundance simply reaches everyone; abundance is produced, but it accrues to owners unless it is redirected. The redirection mechanism is therefore the load-bearing institution distinguishing the two societies. Because automation is held constant across both, Scenario 2 *is* the mechanism: remove it and the same machines keep producing the same output for the same owners — the society decays back into Scenario 1.

There appear to be two broad routes. **Collective or public ownership of automated capital** redirects at the point of ownership: change *who* owns the productive assets so the default capture works on behalf of the public. If a state, a public trust, or citizen cooperatives own (or hold large equity in) the machines, energy, land, and models, the output they capture is public revenue, distributable as a citizens' dividend or as in-kind provision. Variants span full nationalization; sovereign- or "social-wealth" funds holding diversified equity and paying out returns (an Alaska Permanent Fund logic scaled up — an illustrative analogy, not a claim it scales cleanly); worker and citizen cooperatives; and dividends specifically on the data and models automation depends on. **Heavy taxation of automated output** leaves ownership private but skims and recycles a large share — corporate and profit taxes, capital and wealth taxes, automation or compute levies, output or value-added taxes, and land-value taxes — to fund a UBI or in-kind services.

**Durability and failure modes** are the analytical core, because a mechanism that must run continuously against the economy's default pressure is only as good as its stability. *Political-economy capture* is the deepest tension: if owners hold concentrated returns, they hold concentrated means to lobby, litigate, and shape law, so the taxation route must survive the sustained incentive of its own payers to erode rates and widen exemptions, while the ownership route faces the mirror threat before it is built. *Exit, mobility, and jurisdictional competition* mean capital, compute, and firms can relocate toward friendlier jurisdictions, so a purely national mechanism may require international coordination it cannot guarantee. *Valuation and enforcement difficulty* makes "automated output" gameable through transfer pricing and accounting discretion — and valuing publicly held capital is no easier. *Concentration of control* is the public route's distinctive risk: it redirects the money but concentrates control of the means of production in the state or trust, trading an owning class for a potential governing class. And *fiscal and dynamic stability* exposes any mechanism resting on a narrow or volatile base to shocks that cut provision precisely when it is depended on. On balance the mechanism looks durable where redistribution is structural rather than discretionary — constitutionally entrenched dividends, capital transparency, international coordination — and fragile where it depends on the ongoing goodwill of concentrated owners. The honest verdict is conditional: Scenario 2 requires the mechanism to exist at all, but nothing in automation supplies it automatically.

## Scenario 2: Distribution as the Operating Principle

That mechanism is the wellspring of everything in Scenario 2's economy. The machines, energy, land, and models produce the same abundance as in Scenario 1; the only difference is that a standing institution captures a portion of the flow and converts it into resources people can actually claim.

**Sustenance and distribution.** The redirected output reaches individuals as *cash*, *in kind*, or a blend. Cash — a universal basic income — preserves individual choice and lets a residual price system route goods, but exposes the floor to inflation in genuinely scarce items: a UBI spent chasing a fixed stock of desirable land simply bids up rents, eroding the guarantee's real value. In-kind provision — housing units, staple food, metered energy, care services — guarantees the *thing* rather than the *money for the thing*, insulating sustenance from price swings, but standardizes what people receive and concentrates allocative power in whoever administers supply. The likely equilibrium is hybrid: the truly decommodified essentials supplied largely in kind so the survival floor cannot be inflated away, topped by a modest cash dividend for discretionary spending. Which mix prevails follows the mechanism's shape — public ownership lends itself to in-kind provision, a taxation route to cash transfers.

**Consumption.** Once survival is decoupled from work, the structure of demand changes even though the technology does not. Marginal desire migrates away from mere sufficiency toward things the automated system cannot mass-produce to everyone's satisfaction: *positional and status goods*, whose value derives precisely from others not having them; *experiences* — travel, events, access, presence — consumed in time and impossible to stockpile; and *artisanal, human-made goods*, valued for provenance and scarcity rather than function. The general law is that scarcity does not disappear under material abundance; it *relocates* to whatever cannot be automated to universal supply. Land, human attention, authenticity, prestige, and originality become the binding scarcities — the new hard currency of a world where calories, kilowatts, and floor space have been guaranteed away.

**Prices.** The economy splits into two tiers. The *guaranteed tier* is effectively decommodified: food, shelter, energy, and care supplied through the mechanism carry a price at or near zero to the recipient, administratively set rather than market-cleared, because what is near-costless to produce at the margin and guaranteed to everyone can no longer sustain a market price. Above it sits a *residual market tier*, where prices persist for the positionally scarce, the locationally fixed, and the authentically human. Prices in Scenario 2 thus stop measuring the cost of making things and start measuring the scarcity of things that cannot be made-to-order for everyone.

**Non-work economic activity.** Activity does not cease; it detaches from necessity and reorganizes around motive. A voluntary economy plausibly grows atop the guaranteed base: creative and artisanal making, whose human origin is itself the source of value; care and community work the guaranteed tier may under-supply in warmth even where it meets it in quantity; and entrepreneurial ventures aimed not at earning a living — already secured — but at capturing the residual scarcities of prestige, attention, and position. Whether this voluntary economy stays a modest supplement or swells into the society's main arena of striving is an open fork, turning on culture as much as economics.

## Scenario 2: Governing an Abundant Commons

Where Scenario 1's politics is about excluding a majority, Scenario 2's is about allocating a surplus. The central question stops being "how do we make and share enough to live?" and becomes "who decides how the surplus is allocated, by what authority, and how do people matter once a paycheck no longer confers rank?" Politics reorganizes around three axes: the *allocation* of what remains scarce, the *legitimacy* of whatever authority operates the automated commons, and the *meaning and status* people pursue when work is optional.

**Allocation politics.** Because the survival floor is decommodified, politics migrates to what automation cannot mass-produce for everyone at once. Land and location remain fixed — a dwelling by the water, a place in a vibrant city. Attention is finite and zero-sum. Energy and compute budgets are real physical ceilings: someone must decide whether this quarter's surplus trains a new model, terraforms a coastline, or runs everyone's private simulations. And decision-making authority and prestige are irreducibly scarce. So the new battlegrounds are concrete: how large the universal allocation should be, whether it is flat or differentiated, who gets priority access to the scarce residual, and how the commons' compute and energy are spent. Politics becomes an argument over "how much everyone receives" and "who decides" — not a fight over who eats.

**The distributing authority and its legitimacy.** Something must operate the redirect mechanism. Plausible forms span a constitutional charter entrenching the dividend as a right; a technocratic allocation bureau; an algorithmic system clearing requests against energy and compute budgets; a democratic assembly voting allocation levels; or a DAO-like arrangement where citizens hold governance tokens over the commons — most likely a hybrid of entrenched baseline rights, a technocratic operator, and periodic democratic override. The legitimacy problem is acute and novel, because the usual lever of state power weakens: when sustenance is guaranteed unconditionally, the threat of destitution that once backed obedience largely dissolves. The authority cannot easily compel — it must persuade a population that can afford to disengage or resist, so consent must be *earned continuously* through procedural fairness, delivered abundance, and participatory ownership. Its failure modes are equally clear: **capture** (whoever controls the operator captures the leverage the mechanism was meant to disperse — a governing class quietly replacing an owning one); **opacity** (an algorithm allocating scarce housing by unauditable criteria erodes legitimacy into suspicion); **entrenchment** (an unremovable board breeds resentment); and **participation collapse** (comfortable citizens stop showing up until a motivated faction captures the machinery). Sustaining mechanisms follow: transparency mandates, sortition, binding referenda, regular elections, and — crucially — **exit and forking rights**, letting communities split off a share of the commons and run it on their own rules. Exit is the abundance-world substitute for the old threat of withdrawn labor: the credible ability to leave disciplines the center.

**Meaning, status, and social purpose.** When work no longer confers income or, necessarily, status, identity must reorganize — and this becomes politically load-bearing. Humans appear to seek relative standing regardless of material sufficiency, so status competition does not end; it changes currency. Prestige flows to creativity, craft, scientific discovery, athletic achievement, care, community contribution, reputation, and influence, and new prestige economies form — reputation systems, contribution ledgers, peer recognition functioning as the real hierarchy beneath formal equality. Two political risks emerge: *anomie*, a minority unmoored from the structure work once provided drifting into purposelessness (a governance concern through mental health, disengagement, and susceptibility to demagogues promising meaning), and *neo-hierarchy*, where recognition hardens into stratified status classes even without wealth differences. Because recognition is now the scarce good, "who gets recognized" becomes genuinely political. Likely equilibria include a **chartered democratic commons** — entrenched baseline rights, a flat-plus-modest-earnable allocation, a technocratic operator bound by transparency and answerable to sortition and referenda, with forking rights as a pressure valve — and a **federated patchwork** of many local commonses coordinating shared energy and compute. Across both, the deepest fragility is constant: the authority that redistributes abundance also concentrates control over it, so the permanent task is keeping the distributor legitimate, transparent, and removable — lest the commons recentralize into the very concentration it was built to undo.

## Interrogating the "Rising Population" Premise

The question framing this essay smuggles in an assumption worth pausing on: that population keeps climbing while jobs vanish, so an ever-larger mass collides with an ever-smaller pool of work. The vanishing of jobs follows directly from full automation. But the *rising population* half is not a settled fact — it is a demographic forecast, and a contestable one.

Start with the most robust regularity development economics offers: the demographic transition. As societies grow wealthier, more urban, more educated — with the education of women and girls an especially strong lever — and more materially secure, fertility tends to fall, frequently below the roughly 2.1 births-per-woman replacement rate (an approximate figure). This is a directional pattern, not an iron law, but remarkably consistent: Japan, South Korea, Italy, Germany, and much of Europe and East Asia already sit well below replacement, and global fertility has trended downward for decades. Several mutually reinforcing mechanisms plausibly link high provision to lower fertility. In agrarian, insecure conditions, children function partly as labor and old-age insurance; as security rises, that rationale weakens. Child-rearing carries a steep opportunity and attention cost that grows as adults gain competing options. Autonomy expands, especially for women, widening the menu of paths other than early or large family formation. Urbanization and delayed family formation compress completed fertility. These forces map onto Scenario 2, whose guaranteed security and abundant alternative pursuits deepen precisely the conditions the historical record associates with *lower* fertility.

Honesty requires weighing the opposite mechanism. Much of modern fertility decline is attributed to the *financial* deterrents of parenthood — housing, childcare, career penalties, precarity. Scenario 2 removes several outright and frees adults from job constraints, which could plausibly enable earlier and larger families. So the direction is genuinely uncertain: Scenario 2 strengthens *both* the security-and-autonomy forces that suppress fertility and the removal-of-barriers forces that could raise it, and which dominates may hinge on whether secure people find meaning in family or in the wide field of other pursuits. Scenario 1 is more ambiguous still: deprivation correlates with higher fertility in some contexts yet suppresses population through elevated mortality and sheer inability to feed and shelter children.

The upshot is that the imagined collision of an ever-growing population with vanishing jobs is *least* likely precisely where one might expect the worst crunch — high-provision Scenario 2, whose conditions best match the profile of shrinking societies. If its population stabilizes, the squeeze softens, swapping one governance puzzle (mass unemployment) for another (aging, the sustainability of provision). But note a crucial reframing: under full automation, output no longer depends on human workers, so the classical "dependency ratio" anxiety — too few workers supporting too many dependents — loses much of its bite. The rising-population premise should be held loosely, as one contingent trajectory among several.

## What the Comparison Reveals

Run the two societies side by side and the lesson is stark: distribution, not technology, is the decisive variable. Identical machines produce, in one society, a frozen caste order held together by coercion an elite no longer needs the masses to supply, and in the other, a contested commons whose central problem is the legitimacy of whoever hands out the surplus. The same wage collapse that dooms the majority in Scenario 1 is, in Scenario 2, simply the backdrop against which a standing mechanism redirects the flow. The same concentrated-by-default output that becomes sovereignty in the first becomes public revenue in the second. Nothing in the machines chooses between these; the choice is institutional and political.

The parallels run deeper than the contrast, and in an inverted key. Both societies face a concentration-of-control problem. In Scenario 1 it is naked: owners capture the productive stack and the security layer, and power pools where the assets sit. In Scenario 2 it reappears one level up: the authority that redistributes abundance also concentrates control over it, so a governing class can quietly replace an owning one unless the distributor is kept transparent, accountable, and removable. Both societies also see status competition survive material change. In Scenario 1 status is a spillover of ownership; in Scenario 2, where survival is guaranteed and ordinary goods are abundant, scarcity relocates to prestige, attention, land, and the authentically human, and politics reorganizes around recognition rather than need. Automation removes old scarcities; it does not remove the human impulse to rank.

Neither outcome is guaranteed, and the branch points that would push either society toward a better or worse version of itself are recognizable. Scenario 1 could stabilize into durable techno-feudalism or fracture into cycles of repression and revolt, with elite in-fighting and specialist defection its sharpest fragilities. Scenario 2 exists only as long as its redirect mechanism survives capture, capital exit, valuation gaming, and opacity — and could decay back toward concentration if it fails, which is to say it could quietly become Scenario 1. The levers that matter across both are the same handful: how entrenched and transparent ownership or redistribution is; whether control of the automated stack stays accountable and removable; whether international coordination blunts capital's ability to exit; and how demography actually turns.

The old world tied a person's claim on output to a person's contribution of labor. Full automation cuts that cord. What replaces it is not decided by the machines — it is decided by whoever writes the rules for claiming what the machines produce. That is the one structural claim that holds across both futures: once labor is no longer required, a society's character is set less by what its machines can do than by who is permitted to claim what those machines make. The technology poses the question. The distribution choice answers it.

## Verification Notes

This essay is reasoned speculation grounded in economics and political theory, not forecasting. Every forward-looking claim is contingent on assumptions that could break, and several deserve explicit flagging. The concentration-of-returns mechanism is derived conditionally from the full-automation premise; if labor retains meaningful economic value in some sectors, the wage channel does not fully close and both pictures soften. The demographic claims rest on well-established directional patterns (the demographic transition, sub-replacement fertility across much of Europe and East Asia, declining global fertility); the ~2.1 replacement rate is approximate and no specific population figures are asserted. The Alaska Permanent Fund and colonial/rentier-state comparisons are illustrative analogies, not claims that those cases scale cleanly to a fully automated economy. The governance trajectories — techno-feudal caste order versus unstable cycles in Scenario 1; chartered commons versus federated patchwork in Scenario 2 — are plausible equilibria, not predictions, and the essay deliberately leaves several forks open: the cash-versus-in-kind mix, the size of the voluntary economy, whether provision proves pro- or anti-natal, and whether either society's control problem is solved or captured. The single controlled variable throughout is distribution; automation is held constant, so no divergence described should be read as flowing from differing machine capability.
