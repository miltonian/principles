\begin{abstract}
We give a self-contained account of a family of problems in which a circle is populated by randomly chosen points, or crossed by randomly chosen chords, and one asks for the probability of a configuration. The tour begins with a complete solution of the seven-point semicircle problem---the probability that seven points drawn independently and uniformly on a circle all lie within some semicircular arc---which we prove to be $7/64$. From two independent proofs we extract a general methodology (the \emph{leader argument} and the \emph{spacings argument}) that solves the whole class of arc-covering problems, and we place the result inside Wendel's hemisphere theorem. We then survey neighboring classics: the center-containing-triangle problem, Sylvester's four-point problem, Bertrand's paradox of the random chord, and Buffon's needle. Finally we pivot from randomness to determinism and treat the combinatorial siblings of these questions---the lazy caterer's sequence (maximal pieces of a pancake under $n$ straight cuts) and the cake number (its three-dimensional analogue)---exhibiting both as truncated rows of Pascal's triangle and unifying them through the theory of hyperplane arrangements.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}

Geometric probability is the art of putting a measure on a space of geometric configurations and then computing the size of an event. The circle is its most fertile arena: it is one-dimensional yet closed, homogeneous under rotation, and just complicated enough that naive intuition regularly fails. This survey is organized around a single, deceptively elementary question and the widening circles of ideas it touches.

\begin{problem}[The seven-point semicircle problem]\label{prob:seven}
Seven points are chosen independently and uniformly at random on the circumference of a circle. What is the probability that all seven lie within some semicircular arc---an arc spanning exactly half the circle?
\end{problem}

The answer is $7/64 \approx 0.109$, and the two ways of arriving at it are more valuable than the number itself: each generalizes, and together they cover essentially every ``do the random points fit in a small arc?'' problem. After settling Problem~\ref{prob:seven} we develop that machinery, connect it to a celebrated theorem of Wendel on points scattered over a sphere, and then follow the historical thread outward---through random chords and the paradox of Bertrand---before turning, in the second half of the paper, to the deterministic combinatorics of cutting a disk and a cake. Throughout, the recurring motif is a truncated row of Pascal's triangle: the same binomial sums that count how points fit into arcs also count how many pieces a knife can produce.

We fix conventions once. The circle has circumference $1$; ``uniform'' means the position of a point is uniform on $[0,1)$ under arclength, and points are mutually independent. Events of probability zero (two points coinciding, three points forming a configuration with an exact $180^\circ$ separation, chords in special position) are discarded without comment, as they do not affect any probability computed below.

\section{The Seven-Point Semicircle Problem}

We prove the general statement and read off the case $n=7$.

\begin{theorem}[Semicircle covering probability]\label{thm:semicircle}
Let $n \ge 1$ points be chosen independently and uniformly on a circle. The probability that all $n$ points lie within some semicircular arc is
\[
p_n \;=\; \frac{n}{2^{\,n-1}}.
\]
In particular, for $n = 7$,
\[
p_7 \;=\; \frac{7}{2^{6}} \;=\; \frac{7}{64} \;\approx\; 0.1094.
\]
\end{theorem}

\subsection{First proof: the leader argument}

Label the points $P_1,\dots,P_n$ in the order they are generated (the labels are only bookkeeping). For each $i$, let
\[
A_i \;=\; \{\text{all of the other } n-1 \text{ points lie in the half-open semicircle that begins at } P_i \text{ and proceeds clockwise}\}.
\]
Think of $A_i$ as the event ``$P_i$ is the \emph{leader}'': the clockwise-most point of a packed configuration, the point from which all others are reached by turning through less than a half-circle clockwise.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 1: $P(A_i) = (1/2)^{n-1}$.}
Condition on the position of $P_i$; by rotational symmetry we may place it at angle $0$. The clockwise semicircle from $P_i$ is a fixed arc of length $1/2$. Each of the remaining $n-1$ points is uniform and independent, so each lands in that arc with probability exactly $1/2$. Hence $P(A_i \mid P_i) = (1/2)^{n-1}$, a value independent of where $P_i$ sits, so $P(A_i) = (1/2)^{n-1}$.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 2: the events $A_1,\dots,A_n$ are pairwise disjoint (almost surely).}
Suppose $A_i$ and $A_j$ both occurred with $i \ne j$. Let $d \in (0,1)$ be the clockwise arclength from $P_i$ to $P_j$. Because $A_i$ holds, $P_j$ lies within the clockwise semicircle from $P_i$, so $d < 1/2$. Because $A_j$ holds, $P_i$ lies within the clockwise semicircle from $P_j$, so the clockwise arclength from $P_j$ to $P_i$, namely $1 - d$, satisfies $1 - d < 1/2$, i.e.\ $d > 1/2$. These contradict, so at most one $A_i$ can occur.

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{Step 3: the union of the $A_i$ is exactly the target event.}
If some $A_i$ holds, then all points lie in a semicircle by definition. Conversely, suppose all $n$ points lie in some semicircular arc $S$. Order the points by angular position; the largest gap between cyclically consecutive points is (almost surely) unique and, since the points fit in a half-circle, that gap has length $> 1/2$. The point immediately clockwise \emph{after} the largest gap is a leader: every other point is reached from it by turning clockwise through less than $1/2$. Thus some $A_i$ holds.

\medskip
Combining the three steps, the target event is the disjoint union $\bigsqcup_{i=1}^n A_i$, so
\[
p_n \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n P(A_i) \;=\; n \cdot \Bigl(\tfrac{1}{2}\Bigr)^{n-1} \;=\; \frac{n}{2^{\,n-1}}. \qquad\square
\]

\subsection{Second proof: the spacings argument}

A complementary viewpoint looks not at the points but at the \emph{gaps} they leave. The $n$ points partition the circle into $n$ arcs of lengths $S_1, \dots, S_n \ge 0$ with $\sum_i S_i = 1$. A configuration fits in a semicircle if and only if some gap is at least a half-circle, for a gap of length $\ge 1/2$ is an empty arc of length $\ge 1/2$, and its complement---an arc of length $\le 1/2$ containing every point---is the covering semicircle.

We use the standard distributional fact about uniform spacings.

\begin{lemma}[Spacings tail]\label{lem:spacings}
For $n$ independent uniform points on a circle of circumference $1$, each gap satisfies
\[
P(S_1 \ge t) \;=\; (1-t)^{\,n-1}, \qquad 0 \le t \le 1,
\]
and by symmetry the same holds for every $S_i$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
By rotational invariance we may nail one point at $0$; the other $n-1$ points, call their positions $U_1,\dots,U_{n-1}$, are i.i.d.\ uniform on $[0,1)$. Consider the gap that begins clockwise at the nailed point, i.e.\ the arc $[0, t)$ being empty of the other points. That happens precisely when every $U_k \ge t$, an event of probability $(1-t)^{n-1}$. Exchangeability of the spacings extends the identity to any $S_i$.
\end{proof}

Now set $t = 1/2$. At most one gap can be $\ge 1/2$, since two such gaps would have total length $\ge 1$, forcing every point into a single location (probability zero). Hence the events $\{S_i \ge 1/2\}$ are almost surely disjoint, and
\[
p_n \;=\; P\!\left(\bigcup_{i=1}^n \{S_i \ge \tfrac12\}\right) \;=\; \sum_{i=1}^n P\!\left(S_i \ge \tfrac12\right) \;=\; n \cdot \Bigl(\tfrac12\Bigr)^{n-1} \;=\; \frac{n}{2^{\,n-1}}. \qquad\square
\]

\begin{remark}
The two proofs are dual. The leader argument counts \emph{points} that could anchor the covering arc; the spacings argument counts \emph{gaps} large enough to be the arc's complement. In both, the crucial simplification is disjointness: the covering object is unique, so a union collapses into a sum with no inclusion--exclusion corrections. This is exactly why the answer is a clean $n\,2^{-(n-1)}$ rather than a sum of alternating binomial terms.
\end{remark}

\begin{example}[Small cases as a sanity check]
$p_1 = 1$ (one point trivially lies in a semicircle); $p_2 = 1$ (two points always lie in some semicircle); $p_3 = 3/4$; $p_4 = 1/2$; $p_5 = 5/16$; $p_6 = 3/16$; and $p_7 = 7/64$. The sequence is strictly decreasing for $n \ge 2$ and tends to $0$ geometrically, matching the intuition that packing many independent points into a half-circle becomes exponentially unlikely.
\end{example}

\section{A General Methodology}

Both proofs survive verbatim when ``semicircle'' is replaced by ``arc of any fixed length $\alpha \le 1/2$.''

\begin{theorem}[Arc-covering probability]\label{thm:arc}
Let $n \ge 1$ points be chosen independently and uniformly on a circle of circumference $1$, and fix a target arclength $\alpha$ with $0 \le \alpha \le 1/2$. The probability that all $n$ points lie within \emph{some} arc of length $\alpha$ is
\[
p_n(\alpha) \;=\; n\,\alpha^{\,n-1}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
The points fit in an arc of length $\alpha$ iff some gap has length $\ge 1 - \alpha$. By Lemma~\ref{lem:spacings}, $P(S_i \ge 1-\alpha) = \alpha^{n-1}$. Because $\alpha \le 1/2$ we have $1 - \alpha \ge 1/2$, so two gaps of length $\ge 1-\alpha$ would sum to $\ge 1$: impossible (a.s.). The $n$ events are disjoint and the probabilities add to $n\alpha^{n-1}$. Setting $\alpha = 1/2$ recovers Theorem~\ref{thm:semicircle}.
\end{proof}

\begin{remark}[Why $\alpha \le 1/2$ matters]
For $\alpha > 1/2$ disjointness fails---two gaps can each exceed $1-\alpha$---and one must correct with inclusion--exclusion. The clean monomial $n\alpha^{n-1}$ is precisely the regime in which the covering arc, when it exists, is unique.
\end{remark}

\medskip
\noindent\textbf{The methodology in four steps.} The recipe that solved every problem above, and that the reader can carry to new ones:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\arabic*)]
\item \emph{Translate to gaps or to a leader.} Restate the event as ``some spacing exceeds a threshold'' or ``some point anchors a covering arc.''
\item \emph{Compute the single-index probability} $P(A_i)$ or $P(S_i \ge t)$ using rotational invariance to fix one coordinate, reducing to independent uniforms.
\item \emph{Check disjointness.} If the covering object is forced to be unique, the union is a disjoint sum---multiply by $n$ and stop.
\item \emph{Otherwise apply inclusion--exclusion} over the $\binom{n}{k}$ ways several thresholds can be met simultaneously.
\end{enumerate}

This program situates the semicircle problem inside a far more general theorem, discovered by James G.\ Wendel in 1962, about points on spheres of any dimension.

\begin{theorem}[Wendel, 1962]\label{thm:wendel}
Let $N$ points be chosen independently from any probability distribution on the sphere $S^{\,n-1} \subset \mathbb{R}^n$ that is symmetric about the origin (and assigns no mass to any hyperplane through $0$). The probability that all $N$ points lie in some closed hemisphere is
\[
P_{N,n} \;=\; 2^{-(N-1)} \sum_{k=0}^{n-1} \binom{N-1}{k}.
\]
\end{theorem}

For the plane, $n = 2$: a ``hemisphere'' of $S^1$ is a semicircle, and
\[
P_{N,2} \;=\; 2^{-(N-1)}\!\left[\binom{N-1}{0} + \binom{N-1}{1}\right] \;=\; 2^{-(N-1)}\bigl[1 + (N-1)\bigr] \;=\; \frac{N}{2^{\,N-1}},
\]
which is exactly Theorem~\ref{thm:semicircle}. The seven-point problem is the two-dimensional shadow of a phenomenon living in every dimension---and note the first appearance of our leitmotif, a truncated binomial sum $\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\binom{N-1}{k}$.

\begin{remark}
Wendel's theorem is remarkable for what it does \emph{not} require: the distribution need not be uniform, only symmetric. For the circle this means the formula $N/2^{N-1}$ holds whenever the point law is invariant under the antipodal map $x \mapsto -x$, not merely for the uniform law. The seven-point answer $7/64$ is therefore far more robust than its elementary proof suggests.
\end{remark}

\section{Related Problems and Their Solutions}

\subsection{The center-containing triangle}

A classic in disguise: what is the probability that three random points on a circle form a triangle whose interior contains the center?

\begin{proposition}\label{prop:center}
For $n$ points chosen independently and uniformly on a circle, the probability that their convex hull contains the center is $1 - n/2^{\,n-1}$. For $n = 3$ this is $1 - 3/4 = 1/4$.
\end{proposition}

\begin{proof}
The convex hull of points on a circle fails to contain the center precisely when all the points lie strictly within some open semicircle (then a supporting diameter separates them from the center); conversely, if they straddle every diameter, the center is enclosed. Thus the hull contains the center iff the points do \emph{not} all lie in a semicircle, an event of probability $1 - p_n = 1 - n/2^{n-1}$. For $n=3$, $1 - 3/4 = 1/4$.
\end{proof}

So the answer to the celebrated ``probability the triangle contains the center'' problem is $1/4$, and Proposition~\ref{prop:center} reveals it as the complement of the $n=3$ semicircle case---one formula, two famous problems.

\subsection{Sylvester's four-point problem}

James Joseph Sylvester asked in 1864 for the probability that four points, chosen at random in a convex region of the plane, are in \emph{convex position}---that is, that no one of them lies inside the triangle formed by the other three. Unlike the circle problems above, the answer depends on the shape of the region (this shape-dependence is the crux of Sylvester's problem and a warning that ``at random in a region'' is underspecified until the region is named). For points uniform in a disk, the probability that the four are in convex position is
\[
1 - \frac{35}{12\pi^{2}} \;\approx\; 0.7045,
\]
so the probability that one of the four falls inside the triangle of the others is $35/(12\pi^2) \approx 0.2955$. The appearance of $\pi^2$ signals that this is a genuinely two-dimensional (areal) computation, not a one-dimensional arclength one; the circle's boundary problems stay rational, its interior problems need not.

\subsection{Bertrand's paradox: choosing a chord at random}

Our theme so far has been random \emph{points} on the circumference. The complementary theme---random \emph{lines} across the circle---produces the most famous cautionary tale in geometric probability.

\begin{problem}[Bertrand, 1889]
A chord of a circle is chosen ``at random.'' What is the probability that it is longer than the side of the inscribed equilateral triangle?
\end{problem}

Joseph Bertrand pointed out that three natural interpretations give three different answers:
\begin{enumerate}[label=(\alph*)]
\item \emph{Random endpoints.} Choose the two endpoints of the chord independently and uniformly on the circumference (our familiar model!). Fixing one endpoint, the chord exceeds the triangle's side iff the other endpoint lands on the far $120^\circ$ arc: probability $\boxed{1/3}$.
\item \emph{Random radius.} Choose a radius uniformly, then a point uniformly along it as the chord's midpoint. The chord beats the triangle's side iff the midpoint is nearer than the halfway mark to the center: probability $\boxed{1/2}$.
\item \emph{Random midpoint.} Choose the chord's midpoint uniformly over the disk's area. The chord is long enough iff the midpoint lies in the concentric disk of half the radius, an area fraction of $\boxed{1/4}$.
\end{enumerate}

The three answers---$1/3$, $1/2$, $1/4$---are all correct for their respective models. The paradox is resolved not by declaring a winner but by recognizing that ``at random'' is meaningless until the generating mechanism is fixed. Edwin T.\ Jaynes argued in 1973 that if one demands invariance under translation and scaling of the circle---so that the answer cannot depend on where the circle sits or how large it is---then only the second model (probability $1/2$) qualifies, a resolution he called the principle of maximum ignorance. The lesson propagates back to our point problems: the clean answer $7/64$ is clean only because ``uniform and independent on the circumference'' is an unambiguous mechanism.

\subsection{Buffon's needle: the ancestor}

The subject was born not on the circle but with a needle. Georges-Louis Leclerc, Comte de Buffon, asked (posed 1733, published 1777) for the probability that a needle of length $\ell$, dropped on a floor ruled with parallel lines a distance $d \ge \ell$ apart, crosses a line. The answer,
\[
P \;=\; \frac{2\ell}{\pi d},
\]
is historically decisive for two reasons: it is the first problem to place a continuous measure on a space of geometric positions (angle and offset of the needle), and it expresses a probability in terms of $\pi$, turning needle-tossing into an experimental estimator of $\pi$. Every problem in this survey descends from Buffon's insight that ``geometry'' and ``probability'' can share a single measure space.

\subsection{Covering the whole circle by random arcs}

A natural inversion of the arc-covering problem: instead of asking whether points fit inside one arc, place $n$ arcs of length $a$ at random and ask whether they \emph{cover} the entire circle. W.\ L.\ Stevens solved this in 1939, obtaining for the probability of complete coverage
\[
P(\text{cover}) \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{\lfloor 1/a\rfloor} (-1)^{k}\binom{n}{k}\,(1 - k a)^{\,n-1}_{+},
\]
where $x_+ = \max(x,0)$. Here disjointness fails---many arcs can overlap---so the clean monomial of Theorem~\ref{thm:arc} is replaced by a full inclusion--exclusion alternating sum, illustrating step (4) of our methodology in its most honest form.

\section{A History of Circular Geometric Probability}

It is worth pausing to lay the problems along a timeline, because the field advanced by a sequence of memorable puzzles rather than by systematic treatise.

\begin{itemize}
\item \textbf{1733/1777 --- Buffon's needle.} Buffon presents the needle problem to the Paris Academy in 1733 and publishes it in his \emph{Essai d'arithm\'etique morale} (1777). Geometric probability is founded.
\item \textbf{1864 --- Sylvester's four-point problem.} Sylvester poses the convex-position question in the \emph{Educational Times}; its shape-dependence spurs decades of work on random polytopes.
\item \textbf{1868--1885 --- Crofton's integral geometry.} Morgan W.\ Crofton develops the ``Crofton formula'' relating the length of a curve to the expected number of times a random line meets it, and writes the influential ``Probability'' article for the ninth edition of the \emph{Encyclop\ae dia Britannica} (1885). This is the machinery behind random-chord computations.
\item \textbf{1889 --- Bertrand's paradox.} In his \emph{Calcul des probabilit\'es}, Joseph Bertrand exhibits the three-answer random-chord problem, forcing the field to confront the meaning of ``at random.''
\item \textbf{1897 --- Whitworth's exercises.} William Allen Whitworth's \emph{DCC Exercises in Choice and Chance} collects and popularizes many discrete and geometric probability puzzles; the $n/2^{n-1}$ semicircle result is commonly traced to this tradition of Victorian problem-books.
\item \textbf{1939 --- Stevens on covering.} W.\ L.\ Stevens solves the random-arc covering problem in the \emph{Annals of Eugenics}.
\item \textbf{1962 --- Wendel's theorem.} James G.\ Wendel proves the hemisphere formula (Theorem~\ref{thm:wendel}) in \emph{Mathematica Scandinavica}, unifying the semicircle problem across all dimensions.
\item \textbf{1973 --- Jaynes's resolution.} Edwin T.\ Jaynes argues that invariance principles single out the answer $1/2$ in Bertrand's paradox, reframing the ``paradox'' as a demand for a well-posed mechanism.
\end{itemize}

Two morals recur. First, the innocent-looking phrase ``at random'' carries a hidden mechanism, and different mechanisms give different---but individually correct---answers (Bertrand). Second, an elementary planar puzzle is often the low-dimensional face of a general theorem (Wendel). We now cross from probability into pure combinatorics, where the same binomial sums reappear counting not configurations of random points but pieces produced by deterministic cuts.

\section{From Random Points to Deterministic Cuts: The Lazy Caterer's Sequence}

Replace the random circle with a pancake and ask a purely combinatorial question.

\begin{problem}[The lazy caterer]
What is the maximum number of pieces into which a disk (a pancake, or the top of a round cake) can be divided by $n$ straight cuts?
\end{problem}

The name comes from the image of a caterer too lazy to rearrange the pieces between cuts, seeking the most servings from the fewest strokes. The cuts are chords of the disk; to maximize pieces, each new chord should cross every existing chord at a distinct interior point.

\begin{theorem}[Lazy caterer / central polygonal numbers]\label{thm:caterer}
The maximum number of regions produced by $n$ straight cuts of a disk is
\[
p(n) \;=\; \binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} \;=\; 1 + n + \frac{n(n-1)}{2} \;=\; \frac{n^{2} + n + 2}{2}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
We show the recurrence $p(n) = p(n-1) + n$ with $p(0) = 1$. With no cuts there is one piece, so $p(0) = 1$. Now add the $n$-th chord to a configuration of $n-1$ chords already in general position. The new chord meets the previous $n-1$ chords in at most $n-1$ distinct interior points; these points split the new chord into at most $n$ segments. Each segment passes through exactly one existing region and divides it into two, so the new chord creates at most $n$ additional regions. Placing the chord in general position achieves the maximum, giving $p(n) = p(n-1) + n$. Unrolling,
\[
p(n) \;=\; 1 + \sum_{k=1}^{n} k \;=\; 1 + \frac{n(n+1)}{2} \;=\; \frac{n^2 + n + 2}{2},
\]
and this equals $\binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2}$ since $\binom{n}{2} = \tfrac{n(n-1)}{2}$.
\end{proof}

The sequence (Table~\ref{tab:seqs}) is $1, 2, 4, 7, 11, 16, 22, 29, \dots$, catalogued as OEIS A000124 and known as the \emph{central polygonal numbers}. For $n = 7$ cuts one obtains $p(7) = (49 + 7 + 2)/2 = 29$ pieces---a suggestive numerical rhyme with the seven-point probability problem, though the two sevens are unrelated.

\begin{remark}[The same count for lines in the plane]
Because a disk is convex and every chord behaves locally like a full line, the lazy caterer's formula is identical to the number of regions cut from the whole plane by $n$ lines in general position: $1 + n + \binom{n}{2}$. The pancake and the infinite plane are combinatorially the same problem.
\end{remark}

\begin{remark}[A cautionary sibling: Moser's circle problem]
A closely related but genuinely different question asks: place $n$ points on a circle and draw all $\binom{n}{2}$ chords between them; into how many regions is the disk divided (with no three chords concurrent)? The counts begin $1, 2, 4, 8, 16, \dots$ and every student's instinct screams $2^{n-1}$---but the next term is $31$, not $32$. The true formula is
\[
\binom{n}{4} + \binom{n}{2} + 1
\]
(OEIS A000127), which agrees with powers of two only through $n = 5$. This is the canonical warning that five confirming data points do not make a theorem, and it underscores why the truncated-binomial patterns in this survey deserve proof, not extrapolation.
\end{remark}

\section{The Cake Number: \texorpdfstring{$n$}{n} Cuts in Three Dimensions}

Lift the lazy caterer into three dimensions: the pancake becomes a cake, and the straight cuts become planar cuts.

\begin{problem}[The cake number]
What is the maximum number of pieces into which a solid (a cake) can be divided by $n$ planar cuts?
\end{problem}

\begin{theorem}[Cake numbers]\label{thm:cake}
The maximum number of three-dimensional regions produced by $n$ planar cuts is
\[
C(n) \;=\; \binom{n}{0} + \binom{n}{1} + \binom{n}{2} + \binom{n}{3} \;=\; \frac{n^{3} + 5n + 6}{6}.
\]
\end{theorem}

\begin{proof}
When the $n$-th plane is added, it intersects the previous $n-1$ planes in a family of lines lying \emph{within} the new plane, in general position. Those lines partition the new plane into exactly $p(n-1)$ two-dimensional regions, by Theorem~\ref{thm:caterer}. Each such planar region slices one existing solid piece in two, so the new plane adds $p(n-1)$ pieces:
\[
C(n) \;=\; C(n-1) + p(n-1).
\]
With $C(0) = 1$ and $p(k) = \binom{k}{0}+\binom{k}{1}+\binom{k}{2}$, summing and using the hockey-stick identity $\sum_{k}\binom{k}{j} = \binom{n}{j+1}$ gives
\[
C(n) \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{n} \text{(new pieces at step }k) \;=\; \binom{n}{0}+\binom{n}{1}+\binom{n}{2}+\binom{n}{3},
\]
which simplifies to $(n^3 + 5n + 6)/6$.
\end{proof}

The cake numbers (Table~\ref{tab:seqs}) run $1, 2, 4, 8, 15, 26, 42, 64, \dots$ (OEIS A000125). Note that they coincide with powers of two through $n = 3$ (giving $8$) and then diverge---$15$, not $16$---for the same structural reason as Moser's problem: the pattern is a truncated binomial sum, and the truncation bites once $n$ exceeds the number of retained columns.

\section{The Unifying Formula: Hyperplane Arrangements}

The lazy caterer and the cake are the two-- and three--dimensional cases of a single result about slicing $d$-dimensional space with flat cuts.

\begin{theorem}[Regions of a hyperplane arrangement in general position]\label{thm:schlafli}
The maximum number of regions into which $\mathbb{R}^{d}$ is divided by $n$ hyperplanes in general position is
\[
R_d(n) \;=\; \sum_{k=0}^{d} \binom{n}{k}.
\]
\end{theorem}

For $d = 1$ (points cutting a line) this is $1 + n$; for $d = 2$ it is the lazy caterer $1 + n + \binom{n}{2}$; for $d = 3$ it is the cake number. The result---that region-counts of arrangements are partial sums of a row of Pascal's triangle---goes back to Ludwig Schl\"afli's \emph{Theorie der vielfachen Kontinuit\"at} (written around 1850--1852, published posthumously in 1901), among the founding documents of higher-dimensional geometry. The proof is the natural induction: adding one hyperplane in dimension $d$ intersects the existing arrangement in $n-1$ hyperplanes \emph{within} the new copy of $\mathbb{R}^{d-1}$, and each of its $R_{d-1}(n-1)$ regions cleaves one existing solid region, yielding Pascal's own recurrence $\binom{n}{k} = \binom{n-1}{k} + \binom{n-1}{k-1}$ column by column.

We can now name the thread running through the entire paper. The semicircle probability is
\[
p_N \;=\; 2^{-(N-1)}\sum_{k=0}^{1}\binom{N-1}{k}
\]
(Wendel, dimension $2$); the lazy caterer is $\sum_{k=0}^{2}\binom{n}{k}$; the cake is $\sum_{k=0}^{3}\binom{n}{k}$; and Wendel's theorem in $\mathbb{R}^n$ is $2^{-(N-1)}\sum_{k=0}^{n-1}\binom{N-1}{k}$. In every case the answer is a \emph{truncated row of Pascal's triangle}---weighted by $2^{-(N-1)}$ in the probabilistic setting, unweighted in the combinatorial one. This is no coincidence: Wendel's own proof counts the sign patterns realizable by $N$ points against hyperplanes through the origin, precisely a hyperplane-arrangement count in disguise. The probability that random points fit in a half-space and the number of pieces a knife can make are two readings of the same binomial sum.

\section{Conclusion}

Starting from a single elementary question---seven random points and a semicircle, answer $7/64$---we found two proofs, distilled a four-step methodology, and watched the same structure widen into Wendel's sphere theorem, the center-containing triangle, Sylvester's convex-position problem, and the random-chord paradoxes of Bertrand and Buffon. Turning the crank from randomness to determinism, the lazy caterer's $\tfrac{1}{2}(n^2+n+2)$ and the cake number $\tfrac{1}{6}(n^3+5n+6)$ emerged as the low-dimensional instances of Schl\"afli's arrangement formula, itself a partial sum of Pascal's triangle. The unifying moral is twofold: geometric probability rewards the discipline of naming the sampling mechanism exactly (Bertrand's warning), and the counts it produces are, again and again, truncated binomial sums whose apparent patterns---$1,2,4,8,16,\dots$---must be proved rather than trusted (Moser's warning).
