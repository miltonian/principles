# Evaluating Modern (through 2025) Text-to-Speech: Metrics, Standardization, Tasks, Efficiency, and Governance

## Abstract

Modern text-to-speech (TTS) systems have reached near-human output on the properties that headline evaluations were built to capture, and in doing so have exposed systemic weaknesses in how the field *measures* quality. This survey integrates current (through-2025) evaluation practice for neural TTS into a single reference for speech-ML researchers and practitioners. It is organized as a progression of "levels": from the **metric taxonomy** (what is measured and how the number is produced), through **standardized listening-test protocols** and a distinct **reproducibility/comparability** layer (whether reported numbers are valid and like-for-like), to the **benchmark inventory** and **architecture taxonomy** that instantiate and stress those metrics, then **per-task evaluation** of the emerging capabilities (zero-shot synthesis, voice cloning, cross-lingual voice transfer, expressive prosody/emotion, multilingual capability), and finally two axes that headline quality practice neglects — **efficiency/deployability** and **ethics/safety/governance** — closing with open problems. Two principles recur throughout and are load-bearing: no single metric captures TTS quality (naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, prosody/emotion, and multilingual capability are separable axes that move independently), and as systems approach parity the headline metrics saturate, so discriminative, standardized, multi-axis evaluation is now the binding constraint.

---

## 1. Introduction

The audience for this survey is speech-ML researchers and evaluation practitioners; the scope is neural TTS through 2025, with a small number of citations extending marginally into early 2026 (flagged in the Verification notes). The organizing device is a *levels* framing that separates concerns commonly conflated in leaderboard practice:

- **Metrics** — what property a number describes (its *axis*) and how it is produced (its *family*: subjective human rating vs. objective algorithm).
- **Protocols** — how human ratings are elicited under governing standards so a subjective number is *valid*.
- **Standardization** — whether a metric's *usage* is consistent enough across papers that two numbers are *comparable* (a separate concern from protocol design).
- **Benchmarks and architectures** — the concrete suites and system families that instantiate and stress the metrics.
- **Tasks** — per-task evaluation of the emerging capabilities the field now cares about: zero-shot synthesis, voice cloning, cross-lingual transfer, expressive prosody/emotion, and multilingual capability.
- **Efficiency and governance** — deployability and safety treated as first-class evaluation axes rather than afterthoughts.

Each subsequent section corresponds to one level. Cross-references replace repeated exposition: a fact stated fully in its home section is cited by section elsewhere rather than restated. The recurring argument is that the discipline's instruments and reporting conventions, which carried it to human parity, can no longer reliably discriminate among the systems that have reached parity, nor cover the axes on which those systems still visibly differ.

## 2. Metric Taxonomy

This section fixes the shared vocabulary the rest of the survey uses. It separates the two *families* a metric can belong to (how the number is produced) from the separable *axes* a metric can target (which property of speech it describes). A metric belongs to exactly one family but may serve one or more axes; conversely, no single metric captures overall TTS quality.

### 2.1 The two metric families

A metric's *family* is determined by whether a human listener assigns the score at scoring time. This is orthogonal to what the metric measures.

**Family 1 — Subjective human ratings.** Humans listen to audio and assign scores; results are aggregated across listeners. These are the reference standard but are slow, costly, and require confidence intervals and listener/stimulus controls.

- **MOS (Mean Opinion Score).** *Family:* subjective. *Scale:* typically a 1–5 Likert scale, single-stimulus (absolute); **higher-is-better**; report the mean with a confidence interval. *Measures:* absolute perceived naturalness of one clip in isolation. *Axis:* naturalness. *Limitation:* absolute ratings drift with listener pool, instructions, and stimulus set, so MOS values are not comparable across studies; poor discrimination between two closely matched systems.
- **CMOS (Comparative Mean Opinion Score).** *Family:* subjective. *Scale:* signed paired-comparison, e.g. −3…+3 where 0 = no preference; direction is **relative** (positive favors system A over B). *Measures:* the perceptual *difference* between two systems on the same utterance. *Axis:* naturalness (relative). *Limitation:* it is explicitly a *relative* quantity — a CMOS of +1.0 says A beats B, not that A is "good." It cannot be reported as an absolute score.
- **SMOS (Similarity Mean Opinion Score).** *Family:* subjective. *Scale:* typically 1–5, single- or paired-reference; **higher-is-better**. *Measures:* how similar synthesized speech sounds to a *reference/target speaker's voice* — a speaker-identity judgment, not a naturalness judgment. *Axis:* speaker similarity. *Limitation:* similarity ratings entangle timbre, accent, and prosody; a natural-sounding clip can score low on SMOS if it does not match the target identity, and vice versa.
- **MUSHRA (MUltiple Stimuli with Hidden Reference and Anchor).** *Family:* subjective. *Scale:* 0–100, multi-stimulus; each screen includes a hidden reference and one or more hidden low anchors; **higher-is-better**. *Measures:* fine-grained relative quality among several systems simultaneously. *Axis:* naturalness (and, with a target reference, similarity). *Limitation:* higher discriminative power than MOS but heavier to run; scores are meaningful *within* a test relative to the hidden reference/anchor, not as portable absolute values.

**Family 2 — Objective algorithmic metrics.** Computed by software with no human in the loop at scoring time. Fast and reproducible, but each is a *proxy* whose validity depends on the model or reference it relies on.

- **ASR-based WER / CER (Word / Character Error Rate).** *Family:* objective. *Scale:* 0…1 (or %), **lower-is-better**. *Measures:* run an automatic speech recognizer (ASR) on the synthesized audio and compare its transcript against the intended text; a proxy for *intelligibility*. *Axis:* intelligibility. *Limitation:* recognizer-dependent — a different ASR yields different error rates — and it *confounds TTS errors with ASR errors*, so a nonzero WER may reflect the recognizer, not the synthesizer.
- **Speaker-embedding cosine similarity (canonical alias SECS; reported in task literature as SIM, SIM-o, SIM-r).** *Family:* objective. *Scale:* cosine similarity in [−1, 1] (commonly reported in [0, 1]), **higher-is-better**. *Measures:* cosine similarity between speaker embeddings — extracted by a speaker-verification model — of synthesized vs. reference audio; a proxy for *speaker similarity*. *Axis:* speaker similarity. *Limitation:* embedding-model-dependent; the embedding may capture channel or recording conditions rather than identity, and absolute values are not comparable across embedding models.
- **STOI (Short-Time Objective Intelligibility).** *Family:* objective. *Scale:* ~0…1, **higher-is-better**. *Measures:* a signal-based intelligibility correlate requiring a clean, time-aligned *reference* signal (intrusive / reference-based). *Axis:* intelligibility. *Limitation:* originates from speech enhancement/denoising and needs aligned reference audio; for open-ended synthesis (no ground-truth waveform for the exact utterance and voice) it is often inapplicable.
- **Neural MOS predictors (e.g. MOSNet, DNSMOS, UTMOS, SQuId, NISQA).** *Family:* objective. *Scale:* usually mapped to a MOS-like 1–5, **higher-is-better**. *Measures:* learned models that *predict* a human MOS from audio alone. *Axis:* naturalness (estimated). *Limitation:* trained on subjective data but computed automatically — they **estimate** a subjective score and must never be reported as if they were actual human MOS; they can be miscalibrated on out-of-distribution voices, languages, or novel artifacts.

### 2.2 The separable quality axes

Each axis captures a distinct property, is measured by distinct metrics, and can move independently of the others.

- **Naturalness** — human-likeness and absence of artifacts. *Measured by:* MOS, CMOS, MUSHRA (subjective); neural MOS predictors (objective). A voice can be perfectly natural yet mispronounce a word (high naturalness, poor intelligibility), so naturalness rankings need not track intelligibility rankings.
- **Intelligibility** — whether the intended words can be recovered. *Measured by:* ASR WER/CER and STOI (objective). A robotic, low-naturalness voice can be flawlessly intelligible, while an expressive, natural voice may slur or mispronounce — the two systems reverse rank between naturalness and intelligibility.
- **Speaker similarity** — identity match to a target/reference voice. *Measured by:* SMOS (subjective); speaker-embedding cosine similarity / SECS (objective). Central to voice cloning, zero-shot synthesis, and cross-lingual transfer. A system can produce highly natural, intelligible speech that sounds like the *wrong* person (high naturalness, low similarity), so similarity must be scored separately.
- **Prosody / emotion** — intonation, rhythm, stress, expressiveness, and emotional appropriateness. *Measured by:* largely subjective (dedicated MOS-style or preference tests). Objective proxies are weak/emerging — pitch/F0 and duration correlation against a reference, emotion-classifier accuracy — and *no single objective metric fully captures it*. Two systems can tie on naturalness and intelligibility yet differ sharply in whether the emotion fits the sentence.
- **Multilingual / cross-lingual capability** — whether quality holds across languages, including accent handling and cross-lingual voice transfer (a speaker's identity carried into a language they never recorded). *Measured by:* applying every axis above *per language*, plus cross-lingual speaker-embedding similarity and accentedness / language-identification checks. **Report per language, never as one global average.** A system strong in its training language can be unintelligible or heavily accented in another while similarity holds — the axes decouple across languages.

### 2.3 Crosswalk (metric → family → axis)

| Metric | Family | Scale / direction | Primary axis (secondary) |
|---|---|---|---|
| MOS | Subjective | 1–5, absolute, higher-better | Naturalness |
| CMOS | Subjective | −3…+3, relative, higher-better | Naturalness (relative) |
| SMOS | Subjective | 1–5, higher-better | Speaker similarity |
| MUSHRA | Subjective | 0–100, relative, higher-better | Naturalness (Speaker similarity w/ target ref) |
| ASR WER / CER | Objective | 0–1, lower-better | Intelligibility |
| Speaker-embedding cosine (SECS/SIM) | Objective | [−1,1], higher-better | Speaker similarity |
| STOI | Objective | ~0–1, higher-better | Intelligibility |
| Neural MOS predictors (MOSNet, DNSMOS, UTMOS, SQuId, NISQA) | Objective | ~1–5, higher-better | Naturalness (estimated) |
| Per-language application of any above + accent/LID checks | (inherits) | per metric | Multilingual / cross-lingual |

Prosody/emotion has no dedicated column above because it is measured chiefly by *bespoke subjective* tests (MOS-style or preference), with only weak objective proxies (F0/duration correlation, emotion-classifier accuracy).

**Non-conflation rules (enforced throughout):** (1) A neural MOS predictor's output is never a human MOS. Label it as a predicted/estimated score (e.g., "UTMOS-predicted MOS"), keep it in the objective family, and never merge it into human-MOS tables or claim human agreement without stated correlation. (2) A relative or proxy metric is never an absolute ground truth. CMOS and MUSHRA-vs-reference express *differences*, not standalone quality; ASR WER, STOI, and speaker-embedding cosine are *proxies* for their axes, not the axis itself. State recognizer/embedding/reference dependence, and never present a proxy value as if it settled the axis it approximates.

### 2.4 Neural MOS predictors as bounded human-judgement proxies

All neural MOS predictors belong to the **objective/algorithmic family**: they are computed by software and *predict or approximate* a subjective score — they are **not** human-collected ratings and must never be tabulated as human MOS/CMOS/SMOS/MUSHRA. The headline claim across UTMOS, DNSMOS, and TTSDS2 is that these predictors correlate strongly with true (human) MOS at the **system level** (UTMOS reaching ≈0.9), but a **predicted-MOS gap below ≈0.1 is within predictor noise and is NOT decisive** — such differences require human MOS/CMOS confirmation before claiming one system beats another. Reported ~0.9 correlations are typically system-level; utterance-level agreement is lower.

**UTMOS.** A learned predictor of human naturalness MOS from audio alone (neural MOS predictor, objective family; estimates the subjective-naturalness axis). *Primary source:* Saeki et al., "UTMOS: UTokyo-SaruLab System for VoiceMOS Challenge 2022," INTERSPEECH 2022, arXiv:2204.02152 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2204.02152 (results via https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/abs/2204.02152). *Reported correlation with human MOS (VoiceMOS Challenge 2022):* **system-level SRCC (Spearman) = 0.936**; **utterance-level SRCC = 0.897** (main track); OOD track **utterance-level SRCC = 0.893**. The "~0.9" figure is the utterance-level Spearman coefficient; system-level is higher (~0.94). *Validated domain:* English naturalness ratings from prior Blizzard Challenges and Voice Conversion Challenges (BVCC / VoiceMOS Challenge 2022 data), in-domain (main) and out-of-domain (OOD) tracks. *Failure modes:* trained on a specific listening-test distribution; OOD generalization is weaker and depends on limited labeled data from different tests; miscalibrated on voices/languages/artifacts outside training; utterance-level agreement (~0.90) is meaningfully below system-level (~0.94), so per-clip judgements are noisier.

**DNSMOS (and DNSMOS P.835).** A non-intrusive (no-reference) predictor of perceptual **speech-enhancement / noise-suppression** quality — designed for degradation perception, **not** synthesis naturalness. *Primary sources:* Reddy, Gopal, Cutler, "DNSMOS: A Non-Intrusive Perceptual Objective Speech Quality Metric to Evaluate Noise Suppressors," ICASSP 2021, arXiv:2010.15258 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2010.15258; and "DNSMOS P.835," ICASSP 2022, arXiv:2110.01763 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2110.01763. *Reported correlation with human ratings:* DNSMOS P.835 reports **Pearson (PCC): SIG=0.94, BAK=0.98, OVRL=0.98** against ITU-T P.835 human ratings, where SIG=speech quality, BAK=background-noise quality, OVRL=overall. (The original DNSMOS abstract states "high correlation to human ratings" but the exact PCC was **not confirmed** from the fetched abstract — treat as unverified.) *Validated domain:* noisy speech processed by noise suppressors, rated under ITU-T P.808/P.835 crowdsourced protocols. *Failure modes / scope caveat:* built to score enhancement and background-noise artifacts, **not** TTS naturalness, prosody, or speaker identity. Its strong PCCs do **not** transfer to synthesis quality; using DNSMOS as a TTS naturalness proxy is out of its validated scope.

**TTSDS2.** A **distributional benchmark** — it compares *distributions of features* between synthetic and real speech (via Wasserstein/2-sample distances over embeddings), rather than scoring single utterances. Objective family; approximates aggregate human judgement of TTS quality at the system level. *Primary source:* Minixhofer, Klejch, Bell, "TTSDS2: Resources and Benchmark for Evaluating Human-Quality Text to Speech Systems," 2025, arXiv:2506.19441 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.19441 (details via https://ar5iv.labs.arxiv.org/abs/2506.19441). *Factors aggregated (per fetched source):* **Generic** (SSL-embedding distributional similarity), **Speaker**, **Prosody** (pitch/duration/rhythm), **Intelligibility** (ASR-derived). (The original TTSDS also included an environment/ambient component; whether it is retained in TTSDS2 was **not fully confirmed** — mark as unverified.) *Reported agreement with human judgement:* **average Spearman ρ ≈ 0.67** across conditions; it was the **only metric of 16 compared to exceed Spearman 0.50 in every domain and subjective score**. Per-domain (system-level): Clean ρ=0.75, Noisy ρ=0.59, Wild ρ=0.75, Kids ρ=0.61. *Validated domain:* **14 languages** (all with ≥2 systems at publication), backed by 11,000+ subjective ratings — multilingual and distributional by design. *Failure modes:* a **system/distribution-level** signal, not a reliable per-utterance score; needs a corpus per system; per-domain correlation varies (noisy/kids weaker).

| Predictor | What it measures | Reported correlation (type, level) | Validated domain | Key caveat |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **UTMOS** | Predicted naturalness MOS | SRCC 0.936 (system), 0.897 (utterance); OOD utt. 0.893 | English naturalness (BVCC/VoiceMOS22) | Weaker OOD/cross-language; utterance-level noisier than system |
| **DNSMOS / P.835** | Enhancement & noise-suppression quality (SIG/BAK/OVRL) | PCC 0.94/0.98/0.98 (P.835, per-condition); original PCC *unverified* | Noise-suppressed speech (ITU-T P.835) | Not a TTS-naturalness metric — out of scope for synthesis |
| **TTSDS2** | Distributional TTS quality (Generic/Speaker/Prosody/Intelligibility) | Spearman avg ≈0.67; ≥0.50 every domain (system-level) | 14 languages, multi-domain | System/distribution-level, not per-utterance |

**Decision rule (explicit).** A difference in predicted MOS **below ≈0.1** is **within noise and NOT decisive** — do not declare one system better on that basis. *Justification:* correlations are <1 and utterance-level agreement (UTMOS SRCC≈0.90) is below system-level (≈0.94), so per-item predictor error/variance is comparable to a ~0.1 MOS gap; predictors are also miscalibrated out-of-domain. The 0.1 value is a practical convention motivated by this residual variance, not a single quoted MSE from these papers. Sub-0.1 gaps require **human MOS/CMOS** confirmation. All three are **proxies that complement, not replace, human evaluation.** In summary: UTMOS is trustworthy for **in-domain English naturalness near its training distribution**, best used as a *system-level* screen; DNSMOS is trustworthy for **noise-suppression/enhancement** quality only; TTSDS2 is trustworthy as a **multilingual, system-level distributional signal**, not for ranking individual clips.

### 2.5 Metric saturation and ceiling effects

As systems approach human parity, the two *headline* metrics — objective WER and subjective MOS — **saturate** and lose the ability to rank the best systems. This is a mechanistic argument (it needs no numeric attribution) and it is *axis-specific*: it degrades particular metrics on particular axes, never certifying overall quality, because no single metric captures TTS quality.

**WER saturation and non-discriminativeness.** WER is an objective *intelligibility* proxy: an ASR model transcribes the synthesized audio and the transcript is scored against the intended text. Three mechanisms drive its collapse near parity. First, **a hard error floor.** WER cannot go below the ASR's own error rate plus irreducible reference ambiguity (homophones, disputable transcriptions, punctuation/normalization mismatches). Once TTS output is clean, residual errors are dominated by *recognizer* mistakes and *reference noise*, not synthesis defects — the metric stops measuring the TTS system and starts measuring the ASR. Second, **noise-band clustering.** Top systems produce error rates so close to that floor that their differences fall within the metric's sampling noise; the confidence intervals overlap, so observed orderings are not statistically separable. Ranking within the band is ranking noise. Third, **axis-blindness.** WER is intelligibility-only. A system can be perfectly intelligible yet flat, robotic, mis-emphasized, or identity-mismatched; WER sees none of this. A consequence often misread as superhuman quality: synthetic speech can post WER *below* human recordings — because it is hyper-articulated and ASR-friendly — without being more natural or more faithful.

**MOS ceiling effects.** MOS is a subjective, single-stimulus *naturalness* rating on a bounded 1–5 scale. Its saturation is a ceiling effect with four compounding mechanisms. **Scale compression at the top:** a bounded absolute scale gives near-parity systems and even ground-truth recordings nowhere to go — they pile against 4.x–5, so genuine differences are squeezed into a narrow band. **Widening confidence intervals:** rater variance, individual scale-usage bias (some listeners never award 5, some never award 1), and limited listener counts inflate the standard error of the mean until top-system gaps are no longer significant. **Low per-stimulus sensitivity:** absolute single-stimulus rating asks listeners to judge one clip against an internalized standard — inherently coarse for small differences a paired comparison would catch. **Predictor inheritance:** neural MOS predictors are trained on exactly this saturated human data; they inherit and can *amplify* the ceiling, are miscalibrated out-of-distribution, and are unreliable for ranking near-parity systems.

**Consequence (the survey's central saturation thesis).** Because both headline metrics saturate, **headline metrics alone can no longer reliably rank state-of-the-art systems.** Leaderboard orderings among top systems on WER or MOS may be entirely within noise; "matches or exceeds human" claims are necessary-but-not-sufficient and can actively mislead (as with sub-human WER from hyper-articulation). This is bounded by axis-specificity: saturation of *one* metric on *one* axis leaves the *separable* axes — prosody/emotion, speaker similarity, multilingual/cross-lingual transfer — unsaturated and still discriminative. No single improved metric rescues the ranking; only a battery does.

**Discriminative alternatives.** **CMOS** (subjective, signed paired comparison) removes absolute-scale ceiling compression and cancels per-rater scale bias, surfacing small-but-real preferences invisible to absolute MOS — but it is relative only. **Preference / A-B(-X) forced-choice with win-rates and TrueSkill/Elo-style ranking** converts flat absolute scales into a direct "which is better" and aggregates pairwise outcomes into a global order with proper statistics (report confidence intervals, p-values, and listener counts). **MUSHRA and SMOS where appropriate** give higher discrimination among closely-matched systems and target the speaker-similarity axis for cloning/transfer. **Axis-specific batteries:** report naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity (SMOS + SECS), prosody/emotion, and per-language quality *separately*. **Methodological guardrails:** significance testing and confidence intervals; adequate listener counts; native listeners for each language; and hard, long-form, and out-of-distribution test sets (rapid speech, code-switching, rare names, expressive content) that push metrics off their floors/ceilings and re-expose differences. Include human anchors so the ceiling is located, not assumed. These recommendations feed §10.

## 3. Standardized Evaluation Protocols

Having fixed what the metrics are (§2), this section governs how the subjective ones are elicited: a "MOS of 4.2" is uninterpretable without a fixed protocol. Metric definitions are not repeated here; they are taken from §2.

**Why protocol standardization matters.** Absolute-category scores drift systematically with the rating scale and its labels, the phrasing of instructions, the size and expertise of the listener pool, the number of utterances and ratings per system, the presentation context (headphones vs. laptop speakers, quiet lab vs. crowdsourced home), and — decisively — which reference and anchor conditions bound the scale. Two laboratories can rate identical audio and report MOS values differing by several tenths purely from these design choices; the literature on subjective testing has documented such context sensitivity for decades, which is precisely why the ITU maintains formal Recommendations rather than leaving elicitation ad hoc (ITU-T P.800.1, https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.800.1). Citing a governing standard, fixing the sample budget, and reporting confidence intervals are therefore preconditions for cross-paper comparability, not stylistic flourishes.

**The governing ITU standards.**

- **ITU-T P.800 — *Methods for subjective determination of transmission quality*** (approved 08/1996; in force). The foundational listening-test Recommendation. It specifies the Absolute Category Rating (ACR) method that yields MOS on a five-point scale, the Degradation Category Rating (DCR) method that yields DMOS, and the Comparison Category Rating (CCR) method that yields CMOS via paired "much better…much worse" judgments (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.800). Most TTS "naturalness MOS" numbers are ACR scores in the sense of P.800.
- **ITU-T P.800.1 — *Mean opinion score (MOS) terminology*** (latest revision 07/2016; in force). Standardizes MOS notation, disambiguating subjective from objective and estimated variants: MOS-LQS (listening-quality, subjective), MOS-LQO (objective), and MOS-LQE (estimated) (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.800.1). This supplies the notational discipline that keeps a human MOS distinct from a neural predictor's estimate — the non-conflation rule of §2.
- **ITU-T P.808 — *Subjective evaluation of speech quality with a crowdsourcing approach*** (in force). Adapts P.800 listening-only methods (ACR, DCR, CCR) to crowdsourced panels, adding listener qualification, hearing/headphone and environment checks, gold-standard "trapping" clips, and post-hoc screening of unreliable workers (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.808/en). An open-source, validated implementation targeting Amazon Mechanical Turk is documented by Naderi & Cutler (arXiv:2005.08138, https://arxiv.org/abs/2005.08138), with a CCR-specific crowdsourcing procedure in Naderi et al. (arXiv:2104.04371, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2104.04371). This Recommendation directly governs the large MTurk/Prolific panels modern TTS papers rely on.
- **ITU-R BS.1534 (MUSHRA) — *Method for the subjective assessment of intermediate quality level of audio systems*** (current revision BS.1534-3, approved 10/2015; in force). MUSHRA presents multiple stimuli simultaneously against a **hidden reference** and mandatory **anchors** on a 0–100 continuous scale. The low anchor is a 3.5 kHz low-pass-filtered version of the reference; a mid anchor low-pass-filtered at 7 kHz is required in the current revision (https://www.itu.int/rec/R-REC-BS.1534; product summary, https://forcetechnology.com/-/media/force-technology-media/pdf-files/unnumbered/senselab/product-sheet-itu-r-bs,-d-,1534-3-mushra.pdf). BS.1534 recommends expert listeners and prescribes **post-screening**: the widely applied convention excludes any listener who scores the hidden reference below 90 on more than 15% of trials (MUSHRA overview, https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/MUSHRA; webMUSHRA, Schoeffler et al., DOI:10.5334/jors.187, https://openresearchsoftware.metajnl.com/articles/10.5334/jors.187). This "<90 on >15% of trials" post-screening is a community convention, not verbatim BS.1534 text.
- **ITU-T P.85 — *A method for subjective performance assessment of the quality of speech voice output devices*** (approved 06/1994; Amendment 1 adding an audiobook-reading appendix, 03/2013). This is the only ITU Recommendation written specifically for synthesized/TTS speech. Contrary to a common assumption that it was withdrawn, the ITU recommendation page currently lists it as **in force** (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.85/en; Amendment 1 summary, https://www.itu.int/dms_pubrec/itu-t/rec/p/T-REC-P.85-201303-I!Amd1!SUM-HTM-E.htm). In practice, the TTS community largely bypassed it in favour of P.800-style ACR/MOS, partly because of reliability concerns with the P.85 scales for modern systems (Viswanathan & Viswanathan, https://www.researchgate.net/publication/221490797). No formal withdrawal notice could be verified; "P.85 was withdrawn" should be treated as an unconfirmed claim.
- **ITU-T P.1401 — *Methods, metrics and procedures for statistical evaluation, qualification and comparison of objective quality prediction models*** (approved 01/2020; in force). The framework for validating *objective* predictors against human scores, defining evaluation criteria (linearity via Pearson correlation, accuracy via RMSE and epsilon-insensitive RMSE, consistency via outlier ratio, and significance testing of correlation differences) (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.1401-202001-I). It is the appropriate yardstick when a neural MOS predictor (e.g., UTMOS, arXiv:2204.02152) is claimed to track human MOS.
- As reference tooling, **ITU-T P.863 (POLQA)** provides an intrusive objective listening-quality predictor (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.863), and **ITU-T P.810 (MNRU)**, the Modulated Noise Reference Unit, defines a calibrated reference-degradation used to generate standardized anchor conditions (https://www.itu.int/rec/T-REC-P.810). Both appear as anchors or validation references rather than as primary TTS metrics.

**Listening-test design conventions in TTS research.** Studies choose between naive and expert listeners and between crowdsourced (P.808) and controlled lab panels. Crowdsourcing widens device/environment coverage but demands P.808's qualification, headphone checks, trapping clips, and post-screening; lab panels give control at higher cost. Naturalness and speaker-similarity judgments require native speakers of the target language, and multilingual/cross-lingual evaluation makes native-listener recruitment per target language non-negotiable — a point recurrently enforced in cross-lingual systems such as YourTTS (Casanova et al., arXiv:2112.02418, https://arxiv.org/abs/2112.02418). Conventional practice fixes a budget across three dimensions — number of distinct utterances, number of listeners, and ratings per sample/system — and reports the mean with a **95% confidence interval**; presentation is randomized or balanced (e.g., Latin-square) to control order effects (a bias P.800 notes ACR is prone to and DCR mitigates). Attention/screening checks are standard; crowdsourced runs additionally apply P.808 post-screening. Representative zero-shot work reports such controlled panels for MOS, CMOS, and SMOS side by side (VALL-E, Wang et al., arXiv:2301.02111, https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.02111).

**Which test for which axis.** Protocols map onto the axes fixed in §2: ACR/MOS for **naturalness**; DCR/DMOS for **degradation** relative to a clean condition; **CMOS and AB/ABX preference** for close comparisons where absolute MOS saturates and small differences must be resolved; **SMOS** (paired against an enrolled reference) for **speaker similarity** in cloning and zero-shot; **MUSHRA** when many systems must be discriminated finely on **naturalness** (or similarity, with a target reference). **Intelligibility** is typically delegated to objective ASR WER/CER rather than a listening test, and **prosody/emotion** to bespoke MOS-style or preference/appropriateness ratings, since objective proxies there remain immature. MUSHRA's design bounds the scale with a top-line **hidden reference** (natural recordings, or vocoded ground-truth "copy-synthesis" as an achievable ceiling) and bottom **anchors** (the 3.5 kHz and 7 kHz low-pass conditions), so scores are interpretable relative to fixed endpoints. ACR-only MOS has no such hidden reference or anchor, a recognized comparability weakness partly remedied by reporting a natural-speech upper anchor.

**Task-specific protocol notes.** Zero-shot synthesis and voice cloning require a paired **speaker-similarity** judgment (SMOS) of the synthesized clip against the *enrolled reference clip* for the target speaker, run alongside naturalness MOS, so identity and quality are scored independently (VALL-E, arXiv:2301.02111). Cross-lingual voice transfer requires **native listeners in the target language** for naturalness plus a **same-speaker judgment across languages** (does the target-language output preserve the source speaker's identity), since per-language quality and similarity can diverge (YourTTS, arXiv:2112.02418). Expressive/emotional TTS commonly augments naturalness ratings with **emotion-recognition accuracy** or emotion-**appropriateness** ratings, because a clip can be natural yet convey the wrong or no affect.

## 4. Reproducibility & Comparability Standardization ("Level Two")

Section 3 makes a subjective number valid within one study. This section — a distinct concern from protocol design and from the benchmark inventory (§5) — addresses whether numbers are comparable across studies. A reader who assembles a leaderboard from the numbers reported in TTS papers is, in effect, assuming that a "WER on LibriSpeech test-clean" or a "speaker similarity (SIM)" in one paper denotes the same measurement operation as the identically named number in another. That assumption is false, and its falsity is a *distinct* threat from the one treated at Level One.

Cross-system rankings fail for two *separable* reasons. **Level One is *metric saturation*** (§2.5): as top systems approach human parity, ASR-based WER/CER becomes non-discriminative and MOS hits ceiling effects, so the headline metrics can no longer separate the best systems even when applied identically. **Level Two — this section's thesis — is *usage divergence*:** even where a metric remains fully discriminative, its computation is unstandardized across papers, so the reported numbers are not like-for-like. A ranking built from such numbers is an artifact of methodological divergence, not of system quality. These axes are genuinely independent: **standardizing usage does not cure saturation** (two frozen, identically-preprocessed pipelines that both drive top systems to ~2% WER against a strong recognizer remain non-discriminative there), and **saturation does not produce usage divergence** (a metric operating in its discriminative range — SIM in the 0.4–0.7 band — is still uncomparable across two papers that compute it differently). The framing follows the position paper that frames the modern evaluation debate, Yang et al., "Position: Towards Responsible Evaluation for Text-to-Speech" (arXiv:2510.06927; abstract https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.06927; full text v3 https://arxiv.org/html/2510.06927v3; PDF https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.06927), whose own progressive levels separate discriminative scoring (Level 1) from comparability/standardization/transferability (Level 2).

**Failure 1 — Inconsistent evaluation test-set sizes on shared corpora.** Papers that all claim to evaluate "on LibriSpeech test-clean" draw test sets of drastically different sizes and content, so the shared corpus name conceals unshared measurements. Yang et al. document that on the same LibriSpeech test-clean corpus, VALL-E evaluates on 1234 utterances, NaturalSpeech 3 and MaskGCT report on only 40-utterance subsets, and F5-TTS uses 1127 utterances (retaining punctuation and capitalization), stating that "such disparities in test set size significantly influence evaluation metrics like WER … making cross-study comparisons unreliable" (arXiv:2510.06927). *Mechanism:* first, statistical precision — a WER or SIM estimated over ~40 utterances carries a far wider confidence interval than one over ~1,234, so an apparent gap can lie entirely within sampling noise; second, content coverage — a 40-utterance subset samples a different distribution of phonetic contexts, sentence lengths, and speakers. "On LibriSpeech test-clean" is not a controlled condition; it is a family of different experiments sharing a name.

**Failure 2 — Divergent text and normalization preprocessing.** Text-normalization and ASR-transcript-normalization choices applied before WER/CER is computed vary across papers and are frequently undisclosed, shifting the score with no change in the audio. Yang et al. identify preprocessing as an underspecified variable: "Differences in text normalization, phonemization, and treatment of polyphonic words can affect synthesis quality, thus undermining the strict comparability of reported results across different studies" (arXiv:2510.06927). F5-TTS's retention of punctuation and capitalization is exactly the kind of formatting choice that alters string comparison. The recognizer identity is a first-class, versioned variable isolated by recent work on transparent ASR evaluation, the Open ASR Leaderboard (arXiv:2510.06961, https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.06961). *Mechanism:* WER is an edit distance between two token strings, and every preprocessing decision rewrites one or both. Whether "Dr." expands to "Doctor," whether "1994" becomes "nineteen ninety-four," whether casing and punctuation are stripped, and which normalizer is applied — each can change the token count and number of substitutions, moving WER without any change in the waveform. The recognizer is a further hidden knob: an undisclosed change of ASR model or version silently redefines the metric.

**Failure 3 — Non-standard speaker-similarity computation.** Reported speaker-similarity numbers — SMOS's objective twin, speaker-embedding cosine similarity (SECS / SIM), including the "SIM-o" variant against original reference audio — depend on the embedding backbone, the pooling/scoring protocol, and which audio segments enter the computation. Yang et al. dedicate an appendix to "Inconsistencies in SIM-o Evaluation Protocols," giving a concrete divergence: for the continuation task, VALL-E *excludes* the prompt segment when computing similarity, whereas VALL-E 2 *includes* the prompt, and "these differences lead to incomparability across works" (arXiv:2510.06927). (A specific claimed magnitude for this gap — an absolute SIM-o difference on the order of ~0.15 — circulates in secondary summaries; it could not be verified in the primary text and is treated as illustrative only.) *Mechanism:* SECS is a cosine similarity between two speaker embeddings, defined only relative to (i) the embedding model producing the vectors, (ii) the layer/pooling and any score calibration, and (iii) the reference-utterance selection and which segments are embedded. Changing the verification backbone changes the geometry of the embedding space, so cosine values are not commensurable across backbones; and including versus excluding the prompt segment changes what is being compared. A "SIM" of 0.62 in one paper and 0.58 in another cannot be ordered unless both fix and disclose the backbone and the segment protocol.

**What standardized reporting would restore.** Each failure is closed by a specific, per-metric disclosure, framed as *necessary conditions* for any cross-system ranking claim (none cures saturation, and each targets one axis): **Fixed, versioned test splits (closes Failure 1)** — publish a named, frozen list of exact utterance IDs with a stated count N, and report N and a confidence interval with every metric. **Disclosed preprocessing and recognizer (closes Failure 2)** — publish the full text-normalization and ASR-transcript-normalization pipeline (or adopt a named shared normalizer), and state the exact ASR model and version. **Common versioned similarity backbone and protocol (closes Failure 3)** — name a specific, versioned speaker-embedding model, fix the pooling/scoring (cosine) and a single rule for reference-utterance and prompt-segment selection, and report the backbone alongside every SIM/SECS number. **Full disclosure envelope** — report test-set size N and confidence intervals, and release the metric-computation code or configuration so numbers are recomputable rather than merely re-quotable. Adopting these is a community-coordination problem, not a technical one: each standard is individually cheap, but its value is realized only when many papers converge on the *same* frozen split, normalizer, and backbone.

**Background test sets (legacy, brief).** **LJSpeech** (single-speaker English) and **VCTK** (multi-speaker English) anchored single-/multi-speaker neural-TTS evaluation circa 2017–2021 (typically MOS + WER). **LibriTTS** (multi-speaker, from LibriSpeech) became the standard multi-speaker training/eval corpus and underlies today's LibriSpeech test-clean zero-shot protocol. Earlier HMM/unit-selection–era practice relied on small in-house MOS tests and the Blizzard Challenge. These remain useful for training and ablation but under-cover speaker similarity, cross-lingual transfer, and emotion — the reason the 2024–2025 sets above exist.

**Coverage gaps (feed §10).** Prosody/emotion lacks a standardized objective metric (CV3-Eval uses cloning fidelity + subjective MOS; EmergentTTS-Eval relies on an LALM judge — neither portable across papers). Recognizer/embedding monoculture: nearly all objective intelligibility uses Whisper-large-v3/Paraformer and all SIM uses WavLM/ERes2Net, so results inherit these models' biases and are not comparable across implementations. Language skew: even CV3-Eval's 9 languages are high-resource-dominated; low-resource and many African/South-Asian languages are largely unmeasured, and cross-lingual transfer is verified only across zh/en/ja/ko. Automated-judge validity is unsettled: EmergentTTS-Eval's model-as-a-judge reports high correlation with human preference but is neither a human subjective rating nor a classical objective proxy — it sits outside the §2 two-family taxonomy and should be flagged when cited. No unified expressive-multilingual benchmark exists (emotion coverage is confined to zh+en).

## 6. Architecture Taxonomy

The benchmarks (§5) are stressed differently by different system designs. This section maps architecture families to the §2 axes each makes the binding constraint. Architectural choice is not axis-neutral: how a system represents audio (discrete codec tokens vs. continuous latents/mel) and how it decodes (autoregressive language modeling vs. non-autoregressive diffusion/ODE sampling) determines which axis becomes the salient, hard-to-satisfy, headline-reported dimension. No overall "best" score is assigned; the axes move independently.

**Background trajectory (compressed).** The field moved from *concatenative unit-selection* (stitching recorded units — high segmental intelligibility but brittle prosody at join boundaries), through *HMM/statistical-parametric (SPSS)* synthesis (smooth but "buzzy," muffled, low naturalness), into *early neural* systems: attention-based acoustic models (Tacotron/Tacotron 2, FastSpeech/FastSpeech 2) paired with neural vocoders (WaveNet-class). Early neural TTS closed most of the *naturalness* gap on read single-speaker speech but did not natively solve zero-shot speaker cloning, expressive control, or broad multilingual transfer — the very capabilities the modern families are built around.

**C1. Neural-codec language models — VALL-E, Fish-Speech.** Audio is discretized by a neural audio codec (e.g., EnCodec-style residual vector quantization) into token streams, and synthesis is cast as *autoregressive (AR) language modeling* over those tokens conditioned on text plus a short acoustic prompt. VALL-E (Microsoft, Jan 2023, arXiv:2301.02111, https://arxiv.org/abs/2301.02111) treats TTS as conditional codec-token LM and performs zero-shot cloning from a ~3-second prompt; VALL-E 2 (2024, arXiv:2406.05370, https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.05370) reports "human parity" on zero-shot TTS; the cross-lingual variant VALL-E X (arXiv:2303.03926, https://arxiv.org/abs/2303.03926) extends this to language transfer. Fish-Speech (Fish Audio, Nov 2024, arXiv:2411.01156, https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.01156) uses a Dual-AR (slow/fast Transformer) stack with grouped finite-scalar quantization and an LLM front-end that drops grapheme-to-phoneme conversion, trained on ~720k hours multilingual audio. **Axes stressed:** *speaker similarity* (in-context cloning from a prompt is the defining act) and *intelligibility* (AR token decoding is prone to hallucination/repetition/skips, making word recovery fragile); *multilingual* for Fish-Speech/VALL-E X by design. **s1 metrics:** intelligibility → ASR WER/CER; speaker similarity → SECS and SMOS; naturalness → MOS/CMOS — exactly the axes Seed-TTS-Eval reports.

**C2. Diffusion — NaturalSpeech 3.** NaturalSpeech 3 (Microsoft, Mar 2024, arXiv:2403.03100, https://arxiv.org/abs/2403.03100) uses a *factorized* neural codec (factorized vector quantization) to split speech into content, prosody, timbre, and acoustic-detail subspaces, then a *factorized diffusion model* generates each subspace conditioned on its prompt (divide-and-conquer, non-autoregressive). **Axes stressed:** the explicit disentanglement makes *prosody/emotion* and *speaker similarity (timbre)* individually controllable and separately measurable; the paper reports gains on quality, similarity, prosody, and intelligibility with on-par-with-human quality (their claim). **s1 metrics:** naturalness → MOS/CMOS (+ neural MOS predictors such as UTMOS as objective estimators, never reported as human MOS); speaker similarity → SECS/SMOS; intelligibility → WER; prosody → chiefly bespoke subjective/preference tests plus weak F0/duration proxies.

**C3. Flow-matching — F5-TTS, E2-TTS, Voicebox.** Non-autoregressive *conditional flow matching* trains a continuous velocity field and samples via an ODE from noise to a mel/latent, typically as a speech-infilling task. Voicebox (Meta, Jun 2023, arXiv:2306.15687, https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.15687) is the scaled infilling flow-matching model; E2-TTS (Microsoft, Jun 2024, arXiv:2406.18009, https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.18009) reduces the pipeline to a flow-matching Transformer + vocoder with character+filler-token input (no duration model, no G2P); F5-TTS (Shanghai Jiao Tong Univ., Oct 2024, arXiv:2410.06885, https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.06885) adds a DiT backbone, ConvNeXt text refinement, and Sway Sampling, trained on ~100k hours multilingual data with RTF ≈ 0.15. **Axes stressed:** *intelligibility* and *naturalness* — infilling with prompt context yields robust, low-WER speech, and non-AR decoding avoids AR drift. Voicebox reports lower WER and higher similarity than VALL-E (paper's numbers: ~1.9% vs 5.9% WER; 0.681 vs 0.580 similarity) at up to ~20× speed. *Speaker similarity* is stressed since cloning rides on the audio prompt; prosody is emergent, not explicitly factorized. **s1 metrics:** WER/CER; SECS/SMOS; MOS/CMOS; F5-TTS-lineage systems commonly report on Seed-TTS-Eval.

**C4. Hybrids — CosyVoice, IndexTTS.** Two-stage pipelines combining an AR text→speech-token LM with a non-AR flow-matching token→waveform generator, capturing codec-LM controllability plus flow-matching quality/speed. CosyVoice 2 (Alibaba, Dec 2024, arXiv:2412.10117, https://arxiv.org/abs/2412.10117) adds finite-scalar-quantized tokens, a pretrained-LLM backbone, chunk-aware causal flow matching for unified streaming/non-streaming, and instructed control over emotion/accent/role across ZH/EN/JA/KO; CosyVoice 3 (May 2025, arXiv:2505.17589, https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.17589) scales up and introduces the CV3-Eval benchmark. IndexTTS (Bilibili, Feb 2025, arXiv:2502.05512, https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.05512) builds on XTTS/Tortoise as an AR system with duration control and emotion-controllable synthesis (IndexTTS-2 line). **Axes stressed:** hybrids deliberately spread stress across *all five* axes but headline *prosody/emotion* and *multilingual/cross-lingual* while retaining strong *speaker similarity* and streaming *intelligibility*. **s1 metrics:** WER/CER; SECS/SMOS; MOS/CMOS; emotion → subjective preference/MOS-style tests (+ emotion-classifier proxies); multilingual → per-language WER/SIM and cross-lingual SIM under language shift, on Seed-TTS-Eval and CV3-Eval.

|---|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Codec-LM** (VALL-E, Fish-Speech) | Secondary — MOS/CMOS | **Primary** — AR drift; WER/CER | **Primary** — prompt cloning; SECS/SMOS | Secondary — subjective tests | Secondary→Primary (VALL-E X/Fish-Speech); per-lang WER + cross-lingual SECS |

**Families ↔ emerging tasks.** Voice cloning most stresses *speaker similarity* (SECS/SMOS), natively targeted by all four modern families. Zero-shot synthesis stresses *speaker similarity* jointly with *intelligibility* — the exact Seed-TTS-Eval pairing (SECS/SMOS + WER). Cross-lingual voice transfer stresses *multilingual capability* and *speaker similarity under language shift* simultaneously; natively targeted by VALL-E X, Fish-Speech, and the CosyVoice line, measured by cross-lingual SECS + per-language WER (the design of CV3-Eval's cross-lingual subset). Per §2, these must be reported per language, never as one global number. *(Claims about which axis a family "stresses" are architectural inference, not source assertions; Voicebox's WER/SIM figures are the paper's self-reported comparison vs VALL-E — recognizer/embedding-dependent per §4, not cross-study comparable.)*

## 7. Per-Task Evaluation

This section applies the metrics, protocols, and benchmarks above to each emerging task in turn. Definitions and metric families are not repeated; saturation, comparability, and cross-lingual identity are treated in their home sections and cross-referenced here.

### 7.1 Zero-shot synthesis

*Zero-shot synthesis* is the generation of speech in a target speaker's voice from a **short reference prompt** — conventionally ~3 seconds of enrollment audio (standard practice, not a hard rule) — where the speaker was **never seen in training** and the model receives **no fine-tuning or adaptation**. This separates it from speaker-cloning-*with*-fine-tuning (§7.2) and from cross-lingual voice transfer (§7.3). The constraint forces the metrics to jointly verify that the system (a) matched an unseen identity from a tiny prompt, (b) stayed intelligible while doing so, and (c) sounded natural — three separable axes.

- **Speaker similarity** (the defining axis): measured objectively as speaker-embedding cosine similarity (SECS, reported as SIM), the cosine between speaker embeddings of the synthesized audio and the reference, extracted by a fixed speaker-verification model (commonly WavLM- or ECAPA-TDNN-based). Two conventions recur and must not be conflated: **SIM-o**, similarity to the *original* reference prompt, and **SIM-r**, similarity to a *reconstructed/resynthesized* reference (the prompt passed through the system's own codec/vocoder). SIM-o is the more demanding and more common headline; SIM-r factors out codec-reconstruction loss. Both are embedding-model-dependent and not comparable across backbones (§4). Subjective counterpart: **SMOS**, paired against the enrolled reference.
- **Intelligibility**: WER/CER via a fixed recognizer (Whisper for English, Paraformer for Mandarin). In the zero-shot setting WER/CER doubles as a **robustness and hallucination detector**: autoregressive neural-codec-LM systems (§6) are prone to skipped, repeated, or hallucinated words, inflating WER even when timbre and prosody are good. Per §2, WER is recognizer-dependent and confounds TTS errors with ASR errors.
- **Naturalness**: MOS (absolute 1–5) and, for close comparisons, CMOS (signed pairwise). Neural MOS predictors (UTMOS/DNSMOS-style) serve as cheap objective proxies that *estimate* a MOS; per §2.4 they must never be reported as human MOS and are miscalibrated out-of-distribution and unreliable for separating top systems.

Zero-shot leaderboards report a **tuple** — (SIM, WER/CER, MOS/CMOS) — rather than one score, precisely because the axes move independently. Common conventions: report all three side-by-side; use objective WER and SIM as screens with human CMOS as the tiebreaker; and anchor the table with a "ground-truth / human recording" row and a "reconstruction" upper bound (the reference resynthesized through the codec), so absolute values are interpretable against fixed reference rows. Benchmarks: Seed-TTS-Eval (WER + SIM with fixed ASR and SV models, plus CMOS/SMOS) and CV3-Eval (multilingual and cross-lingual conditions). The standardized-protocol elements that make zero-shot numbers comparable are fixed prompt duration, a fixed ASR model, a fixed speaker-verification model, and held-out unseen speakers.

**Saturation caveat.** As zero-shot systems approach human parity, the headline metrics lose discriminative power (§2.5). WER approaches the ASR model's own error floor — often at or below the ground-truth recording's WER — and becomes non-discriminative; top systems cluster inside the metric's noise band. MOS hits a ceiling. The direct consequence: top zero-shot systems cannot be reliably ordered by headline WER or MOS alone. The field's response is to shift naturalness judgments to CMOS and forced-choice preference tests, and to use SIM together with robustness-focused and hard-case slices (long-form, tongue-twister, code-switching prompts). One interpretive trap must be flagged: a system reporting WER *below* human ground truth is not "superhuman" — it signals ASR-floor saturation or over-regularized, less-expressive (hyper-articulated, ASR-friendly) speech.

### 7.2 Voice cloning

Voice cloning synthesizes speech in the voice of a specific target speaker, given reference audio of that speaker. Its defining axis is **speaker-identity fidelity** — but, consistent with the separable-axes constraint (§2), identity fidelity is necessary but never sufficient, and it is treated jointly with intelligibility and naturalness.

Two complementary measurements dominate, one from each family. **SMOS (subjective).** Raters listen to a synthesized clip alongside a reference clip from the target speaker and rate how similar the two voices are (1–5, higher-is-better), aggregated with confidence intervals. Three properties matter: SMOS *requires the reference clip to be presented to raters* (a paired identity judgment, not standalone); it measures *perceived identity match, not naturalness* (a clip can be natural yet sound like the wrong person, so SMOS is collected separately from naturalness MOS); and it is sensitive to the rater pool, instructions, and whether the reference is *same-text* or *different-text* (matched text can inflate perceived similarity by supplying shared phonetic content). **Speaker-embedding cosine similarity (SECS, objective).** Both reference and synthesized audio are embedded with a speaker-verification-style encoder (x-vector, ECAPA-TDNN, Resemblyzer, or WavLM-based) and the cosine similarity is reported (roughly [−1, 1], higher-is-better). The number is strictly encoder-dependent — comparable only *within* a fixed encoder — and is a proxy that can capture channel or recording conditions rather than the speaker.

**They can disagree.** An encoder may register two clips as embedding-close while listeners hear a mismatch, or vice versa, because human similarity judgments incorporate perceptual factors beyond the timbre a verification encoder emphasizes, notably prosody and accent. Both should be reported; neither settles the axis alone. Because axes are separable, a clone that matches timbre can simultaneously degrade intelligibility or naturalness. Cloning evaluation must therefore report the standard triple: **{speaker similarity, WER/CER, MOS/naturalness}** — similarity via SMOS + SECS; intelligibility via ASR WER/CER (lower-is-better, computed against the target transcript, recognizer-dependent); naturalness via MOS or, near parity, CMOS.

**How cloning differs from zero-shot.** The literature uses "voice cloning," "zero-shot cloning," and "speaker adaptation" inconsistently. This survey fixes: **zero-shot = no weight update, a single short prompt at inference; adaptation / fine-tuned cloning = a weight update and/or long enrollment audio.** Zero-shot conditions on a single short reference (~3–10 s) with no fine-tuning; adaptation may use minutes of enrollment and speaker-specific weight updates. Longer, more varied enrollment generally raises both SMOS and SECS, so the two settings are not on a common scale unless reference duration and count are fixed and reported. Enrollment *conditions* bias both metrics: matched vs. mismatched channel (an encoder may score channel similarity as speaker similarity, inflating SECS); clean vs. noisy references (noise depresses both); same-text vs. cross-text; and whether the target speaker is seen or unseen during training (seen-speaker splits can leak identity and overstate performance, so seen/unseen must be disclosed).

| Dimension | Zero-shot synthesis | Adaptation / fine-tuned cloning |
|---|---|---|
| Weight update | None | Yes (or long enrollment) |
| Reference at inference | Single short prompt (~3–10 s) | Minutes of enrollment audio |
| Speaker seen in training | Usually unseen (by design) | Either; must be disclosed |
| Headline metrics | SECS + SMOS, WER/CER, MOS/CMOS | Same triple |
| Comparability caveat | Fix & report prompt duration/count | Fix & report enrollment duration/count |

**Benchmark grounding (§5).** Seed-TTS-Eval reports the objective cloning/zero-shot arm as WER/CER (Whisper-large-v3 for English, Paraformer for Mandarin) plus WavLM-based SIM, with CMOS/SMOS for the subjective arm — an instantiation of the triple on English and Mandarin only. CV3-Eval extends to multilingual and cross-lingual cloning and emotion-cloning subsets, using WER/CER, ERes2Net/WavLM cosine, DNSMOS (predicted MOS, not human MOS), and human MOS. **Cloning-evaluation checklist:** name and fix the SECS encoder (values comparable only within one encoder); report reference duration/count and whether inference is zero-shot or adaptation (any weight update disclosed); disclose the seen/unseen speaker split; disclose same-text vs. different-text SMOS; compute WER/CER with a named ASR model and version; report MOS/CMOS with rater counts and confidence intervals, native raters where language matters; report both objective (SECS) and subjective (SMOS) similarity, because they can disagree. **Limitations:** encoder dependence of SECS (not portable across encoders; the prompt-segment inclusion issue connects directly to §4 Failure 3), SMOS rater subjectivity, seen/unseen leakage, and no standardized reference-length convention.

### 7.3 Cross-lingual voice transfer

Cross-lingual voice transfer synthesizes speech in a *target* language while preserving the vocal identity of a *reference* speaker recorded in a different *source* language. Given a reference clip in language A (say English), the system must produce intelligible, natural speech in language B (say Japanese) that a listener judges to come from *that same speaker*, despite the speaker never having been recorded in B. It is distinct from monolingual voice cloning (language fixed, §7.2) and from multilingual TTS with a native speaker (language B produced by a speaker who *was* recorded in B, §7.5). Cross-lingual transfer is strictly harder because the defining challenge is to hold speaker identity *fixed* while the linguistic and phonetic content *changes language*.

**Cross-lingual speaker similarity trails monolingual similarity, and the gap widens as target-language resources shrink.** This is a structural property following from at least four mechanisms. **(a) Phonetic/phonological mismatch:** the target language contains phones, phonotactic sequences, and prosodic patterns absent from the reference; the model must *extrapolate* the speaker's timbre onto unseen articulations, and extrapolation is lossier than interpolation. **(b) Speaker–language entanglement:** in most learned representations, identity is not cleanly factored from language and accent, so shifting language leaks accent into the output, dragging perceived identity with it. **(c) Low-resource data scarcity:** speaker-conditioning coverage depends on data, and where the target language is low-resource, the model has sparse evidence for how any speaker's identity maps onto that language's phonetics — which is precisely why the gap *widens* as resources shrink. **(d) Measurement confounds:** judging similarity across a language boundary is intrinsically noisier than within one language.

**Identity preservation under language shift is the recognized open weak point of this task, not a solved property.** The separability principle keeps this claim honest: because naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, prosody/emotion, and multilingual capability move independently, **headline gains on one axis do not close the gap on another.** A cross-lingual system can post strong target-language intelligibility (low WER) and high naturalness (MOS/CMOS) while still scoring poorly on speaker similarity — the reader must never infer from an improved naturalness or WER number that the similarity gap has narrowed. Architecturally, the families that natively target this task — neural-codec language models (VALL-E X, Fish-Speech) and hybrids (the CosyVoice line) per §6 — are exactly the ones that stress speaker-similarity *under language shift*.

**A measurement setup that exposes the gap.** **Cross-lingual SMOS (subjective):** raters judge similarity between a *source-language* reference clip and a *target-language* synthesized clip; flag the cross-lingual rater confound explicitly (the language mismatch makes the judgement harder and noisier, and the rater's native language can bias it) — recruit raters with stated language backgrounds and report this as a caveat, not a solved control. **Cross-lingual SECS (objective):** cosine similarity between speaker embeddings of reference and synthesized target-language speech; embedding-extractor-dependent, so name and version the backbone, and report *alongside* SMOS, never as a substitute. **Target-language WER/CER:** compute with an ASR system *competent in the target language* — if the ASR is weak there, WER measures the recognizer's language gap, not the TTS's, a confound that grows in exactly the low-resource languages where the similarity gap is largest. **The exposing comparison:** report similarity for the *same speaker and system* in the monolingual vs. cross-lingual condition as an explicit delta (SMOS and SECS both), then report that gap *as a function of target-language resource level* so its widening in low-resource languages is visible rather than averaged away; report similarity and target-language WER jointly so readers can see the axes moving independently. Concrete instantiation: CV3-Eval's cross-lingual voice-cloning subset (source/target across zh/en/ja/ko) reports exactly this pairing of ASR-based WER/CER, speaker-embedding cosine, and native-rater MOS; Seed-TTS-Eval is English/Mandarin only and defines *no* cross-lingual transfer split. *(Illustrative only, not measured: a system might hold ~5% target WER while cross-lingual SECS drops well below its monolingual value — the shape the protocol is built to reveal.)*

**Practitioner pitfalls.** Never report a single aggregate "cross-lingual quality" score (it collapses separable axes and hides the identity gap). Always pair similarity with intelligibility, reported jointly. Control target-language competence of your instruments (a weak target-language ASR inflates WER; an untuned embedding extractor distorts SECS — report both models and versions). Account for rater-language effects in cross-lingual SMOS. Anchor every claim to the monolingual baseline and to resource level, since the gap — and its widening in low-resource languages — is the finding, not an incidental detail.

### 7.4 Expressive prosody and emotion

Of the five separable axes, prosody and emotion is the least mature to evaluate. As of 2025 there is **no widely accepted, linguistically-motivated *objective* metric for prosodic or emotional quality.** In its absence, practice still leans on two crutches: subjective human ratings, and comparison of low-level acoustic features — chiefly fundamental-frequency (F0) contours, duration/timing, and energy/intensity. Every objective number reported for prosody today is a *partial proxy*, not a validated measure of the axis itself; none has displaced the subjective-plus-acoustic status quo.

Prosody denotes the suprasegmental structure of speech: intonation (the F0 contour), rhythm and timing (phoneme/word/pause durations), stress, and phrasing. Emotion/expressiveness denotes affect, speaking style, and controllable style. These properties are *not* captured by intelligibility metrics (WER/CER score only whether the words are recoverable) nor by a generic naturalness MOS. A system can be flawlessly intelligible (near-zero WER) and rated highly natural while still delivering flat, wrongly-emphasized, or emotionally inappropriate speech — and conversely, an expressive rendition may score well for emotion yet slur a word. A naturalness MOS never substitutes for measuring prosody or emotion.

**Subjective side (de-facto ground truth).** Expressiveness/prosody **MOS and CMOS** (dedicated absolute or paired tests rating "naturalness of prosody" or expressiveness rather than overall quality); **emotion-recognition / classification listening tests** (listeners identify the *intended* emotion; accuracy against the target label measures whether the emotion was conveyed); **preference / AB tests for style**; and **MUSHRA** for finer discrimination among closely-matched expressive systems. Strength: the only trusted ground truth for something as perceptual as affect. Weaknesses: cost and slowness, high listener variance, poor cross-study reproducibility, and genuine ambiguity about what "expressive" or "appropriate" even means for a given sentence.

**Objective proxy side (partial proxies only).** *F0 / pitch metrics (reference-dependent):* F0 Root-Mean-Square Error (F0 RMSE), F0 correlation, Gross Pitch Error (GPE), Voicing Decision Error (VDE), and F0 Frame Error (FFE) — compare a synthesized pitch track against a reference and break down when no single correct contour exists. *Duration / timing metrics:* phoneme/word duration error, duration RMSE, Dynamic Time Warping (DTW) alignment distance, speaking-rate comparison. *Energy / intensity metrics:* energy/loudness RMSE or correlation against a reference. *Emotion/style proxies (largely reference-free):* accuracy of a separate Speech-Emotion-Recognition (SER) classifier on the synthesized audio, emotion/style-embedding cosine similarity, or style-transfer consistency — these inherit the SER model's own biases and errors. *Mel-Cepstral Distortion (MCD)* and related spectral distances: convenient but *not prosody-specific* — they mix prosodic differences with timbre, so a low MCD does not certify correct prosody.

**2020–2025 emerging attempts.** There is active work on learned or linguistically-motivated prosody metrics — prosody-aware predictors, tone/intonation-error measures for tonal languages, and extensions of neural MOS predictors toward expressiveness. As of 2025 these are promising but unvalidated and not community-standard; each should be framed as a research proposal or partial proxy, not a settled objective measure. None has displaced the subjective + F0/acoustic status quo.

**Why a good objective prosody metric is hard.** The difficulty is structural. Prosody is many-to-one and one-to-many: multiple prosodic renditions of the same text can all be valid, so error against a single reference penalizes legitimate variation. This makes reference-dependent metrics especially fragile for expressive or zero-shot generation, where there is no single correct target waveform. Emotion is culturally and context dependent, so a fixed label or embedding is a weak ground truth. And there is no agreed linguistic representation of "correct" prosody against which to compute error. With no canonical target, no proxy can become a validated standard — hence the continued reliance on human ratings and low-level acoustic comparison. This is a live measurement gap precisely for voice cloning, zero-shot synthesis, and cross-lingual voice transfer, which generate novel utterances with no parallel reference recording, so the reference-dependent F0/duration proxies largely fall away and the burden falls back onto costly subjective tests.

### 7.5 Multilingual capability

Multilingual capability is a distinct quality axis, on par with but separable from naturalness, intelligibility, speaker similarity, and prosody/emotion. It is not measured by a single number and cannot be inferred from monolingual results: a system near-human in English can degrade sharply in another language, so strong (typically English) scores are not evidence of multilingual competence. Because the other axes each move independently *within* every language, multilingual evaluation is best understood as applying the full metric battery per language and reporting the distribution — never a lone aggregate. A single global average lets a few high-resource languages mask failures elsewhere, and it conflates *coverage* (languages a model claims to support) with *demonstrated quality*. The benchmark inventory reflects this design — CV3-Eval evaluates multilingual voice cloning per language across nine languages plus a dedicated cross-lingual subset, rather than folding everything into one score.

**Per-language intelligibility (WER/CER caveats).** Intelligibility inherits every §2 caveat — recognizer-dependent, confounds TTS with ASR errors — and those caveats *worsen* across languages. High-resource ASR is stronger, so a low WER partly reflects a lower ASR error floor rather than better synthesis, while a higher WER in a lower-resource language may largely reflect the recognizer's own weakness. Seed-TTS-Eval, for instance, uses Whisper-large-v3 for English and Paraformer for Mandarin — different recognizers with different error floors, so their WER numbers are not directly commensurable. Further confounds: language-specific transcription/normalization conventions, code-switching within an utterance, and script/tokenization effects (character-level CER for logographic or unsegmented scripts versus word-level WER elsewhere). A fair multilingual WER comparison therefore requires a stated per-language ASR baseline (the recognizer's own error rate on real speech) so residual error is attributable to the TTS.

**Per-language naturalness (native raters and predictor bias).** Naturalness is judged per language via subjective panels (MOS, CMOS, or MUSHRA) and, as an objective estimate, neural MOS predictors. Cross-language MOS values are not directly comparable: rater pools differ, language-specific expectations for prosody and phrasing differ, and absolute-scale usage varies by population. Native listeners are required for each language; non-native raters cannot reliably detect mispronunciation, wrong stress, or unnatural intonation in a language they do not speak. Neural MOS predictors add a training-language bias: most are trained predominantly on English listening-test data and are miscalibrated out-of-distribution, so a predicted MOS in an under-represented language should be treated as a weak, possibly biased estimate — and, per §2, never reported as a human MOS. CV3-Eval's use of native-speaker MOS panels alongside an objective predicted-MOS estimate (DNSMOS) illustrates the split; the two must be kept distinct and reported per language.

**Low-resource degradation — the core finding.** As language resource level drops, degradation is two-part. First, naturalness and intelligibility fall: native-rater MOS declines and per-language WER/CER rises (compounded by weaker ASR baselines). Second — the recognized weak point (§7.3) — cross-lingual speaker similarity falls *further*: whether measured by SMOS or SECS, it trails monolingual similarity, and this gap *widens* as resources shrink. Identity preservation across languages is an open problem, not a solved property. Low-resource cross-lingual voice cloning should not be described as solved. Reporting must surface the monolingual-versus-cross-lingual similarity gap rather than average it away; cross-lingual evaluation subsets that fix source/target language pairs (as in CV3-Eval) are what make this gap visible at all.

**Reporting discipline.** Multilingual coverage should be reported separately from monolingual quality: a **per-language breakdown** (one row per language for each axis — WER/CER, native-rater MOS/CMOS, SECS/SMOS); **resource-tier grouping** (languages labeled high- vs. low-resource so degradation is legible against the tier); **coverage vs. evaluated counts** (languages *supported* stated alongside languages actually *evaluated*; unevaluated "supported" languages are a gap, not a result); **monolingual vs. cross-lingual side by side** for each axis, with the per-language ASR baseline stated for WER/CER; and the **similarity gap surfaced** (the monolingual-minus-cross-lingual speaker-similarity difference reported explicitly, per language and per tier, never collapsed into one mean). Warn against headline single-number multilingual claims ("supports N languages," "multilingual MOS of X"): they hide the distribution the axis exists to expose, and invite the two errors this section forbids — treating one scalar as the axis, and reading strong monolingual results as multilingual competence.

## 8. Efficiency & Deployability

Sections 2–7 concern output quality; this section adds an axis those sections deliberately do not cover: **how fast, how cheaply, and at what scale a system can produce its output.** Efficiency and deployability are orthogonal to quality. A system can be fast and low-quality (few-step generation that halves latency but raises intelligibility errors), or high-quality and too slow for real-time (a many-step diffusion model that tops a naturalness listening test but cannot meet an interactive latency budget). The two dimensions dissociate, and no single number captures a system: a Real-Time Factor tells you nothing about a naturalness MOS, and vice versa. A latency or model-size figure is never folded into a quality score. Historically, efficiency was tracked mainly to establish whether synthesis could run at all in real time (CPU-bound concatenative and HMM systems; sample-by-sample WaveNet-class vocoders far slower than real time); the figures below are drawn from 2023–2025 neural-codec LMs, diffusion, flow-matching, and hybrids, where efficiency has become a competitive axis rather than a mere feasibility gate.

**Efficiency metrics, precisely defined.** **Real-Time Factor (RTF)** = processing (wall-clock) time / duration of generated audio (dimensionless, **lower-better**; RTF<1 means faster than real time). Many recent papers report the **inverse, RTFx = 1/RTF** (also "×real-time" or "speed-up factor," **higher-better**) — an RTF of 0.1 and an RTFx of 10× describe the same system. Some systems (notably Fish-Speech) report a ratio such as "1:15," an RTFx of 15 (RTF ≈ 0.067). Always check the convention. **First-chunk / streaming latency (time-to-first-audio)** = wall-clock time from request to the first emitted audio chunk, in ms, **lower-better**; a whole-different quantity from RTF (a throughput-style measure over an entire utterance) that governs *perceived responsiveness* in interactive settings and is meaningful only for streaming synthesis. **Throughput** = audio-seconds generated per wall-clock second, or concurrent real-time streams per accelerator, **higher-better**; drives serving cost and trades off against single-stream latency. **Model scale** = parameter count and inference memory footprint (weights plus, for AR decoders, the growing key/value cache, or for iterative samplers, activation memory), with numeric precision (fp16, int8) where reported; **smaller is better for deployability**. Parameter count alone is an incomplete proxy for memory footprint, because KV-cache/activation memory grows with sequence length and batch size.

**Reproducibility convention: no hardware, no comparison.** An RTF, RTFx, or latency number is meaningless and non-comparable unless reported with its full hardware and measurement configuration. The same model varies by an order of magnitude across accelerators — Fish-Speech itself reports RTFx ≈ 5 on a consumer RTX 4060 mobile GPU versus RTFx ≈ 15 on an RTX 4090 for the identical model (arXiv:2411.01156, https://arxiv.org/html/2411.01156v2) — so a cross-paper comparison on unmatched hardware measures the hardware, not the system. This is the efficiency-axis analogue of the §4 comparability failures. **Audit checklist** (treat any figure as *unverified* unless it states all): (1) accelerator model (specific GPU/CPU, not "a GPU"); (2) batch size (single-stream vs. batched); (3) precision (fp32/fp16/bf16/int8, and any quantization/compilation such as TensorRT); (4) sequence/chunk length (audio duration used to compute RTF; for streaming, chunk size); (5) sampling budget where applicable (number of function evaluations, NFE, or solver steps for flow-matching/diffusion, since RTF scales with it); (6) measurement mode (streaming vs. offline/batch; first-chunk vs. total latency). Many primary reports under-specify this: F5-TTS reports a crisp RTF of 0.15 at 16 NFE but does not explicitly state the inference GPU (training used A100 80G, ablations reference RTX 3090) (arXiv:2410.06885, https://arxiv.org/html/2410.06885v1); CosyVoice 2's paper gives a first-package-latency *formula* rather than a measured, hardware-qualified number, with the widely cited ~150 ms figure appearing on the model card/demo (arXiv:2412.10117, https://arxiv.org/html/2412.10117; model card https://huggingface.co/FunAudioLLM/CosyVoice2-0.5B).

**The quality–latency trade-off is a gating constraint for production voice agents.** For a system that must converse in real time, first-chunk latency is not a tie-breaker applied after quality; it is a **gating constraint**. A system that misses the interactive latency budget (commonly a few hundred milliseconds to first audio) is unusable *regardless of its naturalness MOS or intelligibility WER*. Quality only becomes the deciding factor among systems that already clear the latency gate. The right way to see this is a **Pareto frontier** between a named efficiency metric (first-chunk latency, or RTF) and a named §2 quality metric (naturalness MOS/CMOS, or intelligibility WER). Systems on the frontier represent the best quality achievable at each latency; a system strictly inside is dominated. The frontier is not fixed per architecture: **streaming decoding** (emitting audio before the utterance is complete), **chunking** (bounded look-ahead), and **distillation / step-reduction** (fewer sampling steps or a distilled student) all move a system along it — typically trading a measured quality decrement (a small CMOS loss, or a WER increase) for a latency reduction. Because these moves change the efficiency coordinate and the quality coordinate independently, they must be reported as *both* coordinates, never as a single blended score.

**Architecture families mapped to efficiency profiles (using §6 definitions).** *Autoregressive neural-codec LMs (VALL-E, Fish-Speech):* sequential token-by-token decoding, so latency and RTF scale **linearly with output length** and the KV cache grows with the sequence; the upside is that autoregression is **naturally streamable**, yielding low first-chunk latency (Fish-Speech ~150 ms first-packet, RTFx up to 15 on RTX 4090). The stressed constraints are per-token compute and generation stability (AR drift, repetition/skips), the latter showing up on the *intelligibility* (WER) quality axis. **Mechanism: autoregression → length-linear latency + native streamability.** *Non-autoregressive flow-matching / diffusion (F5-TTS, E2-TTS, Voicebox, NaturalSpeech 3):* parallel over the whole sequence but requires **multiple sampling/solver steps** (NFE); RTF scales roughly linearly with NFE, making step count a tunable quality/latency knob (F5-TTS RTF 0.15 @ 16 NFE vs. RTF 0.31 @ 32 NFE, the higher budget buying a small WER improvement; Voicebox "up to 20×" faster than VALL-E, a relative RTFx claim, arXiv:2306.15687). Generation is typically **whole-utterance**, so first-chunk streaming latency is harder to achieve. **Mechanism: iterative sampling → NFE-controlled latency; whole-utterance generation → weak native streamability.** *Hybrids (CosyVoice, IndexTTS):* combine an AR LM front-end (text → semantic/codec tokens) with a flow-matching/diffusion decoder (tokens → waveform) to get AR streamability plus non-AR quality/parallel speed. CosyVoice 2 uses chunk-aware causal flow matching to unify streaming and offline modes and targets ~150 ms first-package latency; the IndexTTS line reports whole-pipeline RTF figures. **Mechanism: AR front-end supplies chunked streaming latency; flow/diffusion back-end supplies parallel-decode quality.**

**Limitations.** Efficiency reporting is systematically under-qualified: several flagship systems report RTF without an explicit inference accelerator, batch size, or precision, and streaming-latency claims frequently originate from model cards or formulas rather than benchmarked, hardware-stated measurements — rendering most cross-paper comparisons non-comparable per the §2 hardware rule. There is no standardized efficiency benchmark for TTS comparable to what Seed-TTS-Eval or CV3-Eval provide for *quality* (§5): no fixed hardware reference, fixed utterance set, fixed precision, and fixed streaming/offline protocol against which RTF, first-chunk latency, and throughput could be measured like-for-like. The on-device/edge deployment gap is largely unmeasured: parameter counts and datacenter-GPU RTFs dominate the literature, while quantized, CPU/NPU, and mobile-accelerator footprints — the regime that bounds consumer and privacy-preserving deployment — are rarely reported. A hardware-qualified, streaming-aware efficiency benchmark, reported as a coordinate alongside (never fused into) the quality axes, is the clear next step. *(An IndexTTS 2.5 RTF of 0.136 on an NVIDIA A10 — 2.28× faster than IndexTTS2, with a ~38M-smaller S2M module — appears in a Jan-2026 report, arXiv:2601.03888, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.03888; flagged in the Verification notes as post-2025/out-of-scope.)*

## 9. Ethics, Safety & Governance

Zero-shot cloning and cross-lingual transfer collapse the cost of vocal impersonation — the same capabilities §7 rewards with high SMOS and SECS are the capabilities that enable fraud, non-consensual likeness use, and disinformation. Governance is therefore a **peer evaluation level**, structurally parallel to naturalness, intelligibility, and speaker-similarity assessment. Each concern is treated as an **evaluable property**: an operational definition, a measurement procedure, concrete metrics classified subjective/objective per §2, and — where a property cannot yet be measured with a standardized protocol — an explicit **open measurement gap** rather than an assertion of coverage. We also distinguish **standardized/measured-today** metrics from metrics we **propose**.

**Consent & data provenance.** *Operational definition:* every voice used to train or condition a system is documented, lawfully sourced, and traceable to a consenting speaker, with honored opt-out — an *auditability* property of the pipeline, not of an audio signal. *Measurement procedure:* audit the training/reference corpus against a documentation standard — Datasheets for Datasets (Gebru et al., via the Data Provenance Initiative audit, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2310.16787), Data Statements (Bender & Friedman), or the speech-specific Augmented Datasheets for Speech Datasets (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2305.04672). *Concrete metrics (all **proposed**, none standardized; record-based objective but requiring human audit judgment):* provenance-coverage % (utterances with a resolvable source and licence); consent-audit pass rate (speakers with documented, purpose-specific consent, including biometric-metadata release); licence-traceability rate (data with a machine-checkable licence chain); opt-out honoring rate (validated removal requests reflected within a stated window). *Open gap:* there is no standardized consent-provenance *benchmark* for voice; the field diagnoses consent/provenance for AI as structurally "broken" (Longpre et al., 2024, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2404.12691).

**Deepfake / anti-spoofing detection.** *Operational definition:* a detector (countermeasure, CM) classifies audio as bona fide vs. synthetic/converted, and — in the tandem setting — protects an automatic speaker verification (ASV) system from spoofed access. The **SASV** task fuses a CM with an ASV speaker-embedding comparator, so the same speaker-embedding tooling that yields SECS/SMOS is here repurposed as a defense. *Measurement procedure:* evaluate on the ASVspoof (https://www.asvspoof.org/) suite (2019, 2021, and ASVspoof 5 / 2024, https://arxiv.org/html/2408.08739v1). *Concrete metrics (all objective):* **EER (Equal Error Rate)** (miss rate = false-alarm rate; lower-better; threshold-free but hides calibration); **minDCF** (ASVspoof 5 Track-1 *primary*; cost-weighted miss + false-alarm at an oracle threshold, β ≈ 1.90 under a 0.05 spoof prior; lower-better); **Cllr** (Track-1 secondary; score calibration); **min a-DCF** (ASVspoof 5 Track-2/SASV *primary*; three-class cost with α ≈ 1.58, γ ≈ 0.84); **min t-DCF / t-EER** (tandem CM+ASV cost and parameter-free tandem error; Track-2 supplementary); **AUC**. Robustness must be reported per language/condition (never one global number); the dominant failure modes are **generalization to unseen synthesizers** and **attacker adaptivity** — EER on held-out attacks routinely exceeds in-domain EER, and adversarial attacks degrade detectors further. Maturity: **mature** metrics, unsolved generalization.

**Watermarking / traceability.** *Operational definition:* active embedding of an imperceptible, detectable signal (optionally carrying a payload) into synthesized audio. Contrast **active watermarking** (embedded at generation), **passive detection** (above), and **provenance metadata** (C2PA Content Credentials, https://spec.c2pa.org/specifications/specifications/2.4/explainer/Explainer.html: cryptographically bound, opt-in assertions covering audio — strong when present, absent when stripped). *Measurement procedure:* embed, then attempt detection/decoding after an enumerated attack suite (compression, resampling, noise, filtering, echo, time-stretch, re-recording), benchmarked on AudioMarkBench (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2406.06979); reference system AudioSeal (https://arxiv.org/html/2401.17264v2). *Concrete metrics:* detection TPR/FPR (AudioSeal 1.00/0.00 with no edits; **AUC 0.97** — 98% TPR, 4% FPR — averaged over 18 edits, vs. WavMark's 0.84); bit/message accuracy (AudioSeal uses 16-bit messages); payload capacity; localization (sample-level IoU, AudioSeal 0.99 for a 1 s mark in 10 s audio); imperceptibility measured with the *same metrics as §2's naturalness axis* — objective PESQ (4.47), ViSQOL (4.829), STOI (0.997), SI-SNR; subjective MUSHRA (77.07 ± 6.35) / MOS; robustness AUC/accuracy under attack (AudioSeal stays >0.99 on MP3/AAC/resample but drops to 0.61 under highpass filtering and 0.91 under white noise). Maturity: **emerging**; robustness against removal is adversarial and unsettled — every published robustness AUC is a snapshot against a fixed attack set. *(AudioSeal numbers are single-system research results, not a standardized leaderboard.)*

**Regulation as an evaluable property.** Each legal obligation is converted into a **compliance-checkable predicate**; some are signal-detectable (machine-readable marks), others records-auditable (consent).

## 10. Limitations & Future Directions

This closing section gathers the survey's threads. Facts established in home sections are cited by theme; no new benchmarks, systems, or numbers are introduced beyond the illustrative §4 test-set-size disparity. Modern TTS has advanced rapidly enough that its best systems now approach human-level output on the properties headline evaluations were designed to capture — and this very progress has exposed systemic weaknesses in how the field measures quality. Five open problems are individually load-bearing.

**1. Metric saturation and ceiling effects (established, §2.5).** Near parity, WER is bounded below by the recognizer's own error floor and reference-transcription ambiguity, so residual differences among top systems fall inside the metric's noise band — and a system can post sub-human WER through hyper-articulated, ASR-friendly speech without being more natural. MOS, on a bounded absolute scale, compresses near-parity systems and ground-truth recordings against its ceiling, while rater variance widens confidence intervals until top-system gaps are no longer significant. Top systems cannot be reliably ranked by WER or MOS alone. *Direction:* replace headline single-stimulus ranking with CMOS and forced-choice preference/A-B(-X) tests with win-rate aggregation, reported with confidence intervals and listener counts, and deliberately de-saturate with hard, long-form, and out-of-distribution material plus human anchors.

**2. Cross-system comparability and standardization failure (§4) — distinct from saturation.** Even where a metric remains fully discriminative, its *usage* is unstandardized, so reported numbers are not like-for-like: papers claiming "LibriSpeech test-clean" draw sets of ~40 versus ~1,234 utterances, yielding incomparable confidence intervals and content coverage; text/transcript-normalization choices shift WER/CER with no change in audio; and speaker-similarity numbers depend on the embedding backbone, pooling, and reference-segment protocol. Standardizing usage would not cure saturation, and a non-saturated metric is still uncomparable when usage varies — a separate open problem, not a facet of the first. *Direction:* fixed, versioned test splits (exact utterance IDs + N), fully disclosed text/transcript preprocessing and a named ASR model and version, and a common versioned speaker-similarity backbone with fixed pooling and reference-selection protocol reported alongside every number — a community-coordination problem.

**3. Cross-lingual identity preservation (established, §7.3/§7.5).** Cross-lingual speaker similarity trails monolingual speaker similarity, and the gap widens in low-resource target languages. Because the target language contains phones the reference speaker never produced, and because identity remains entangled with language and accent in learned representations, shifting language leaks accent and drifts timbre. Identity preservation under language transfer is the recognized weak point — an open problem, not solved — and it blocks reliable evaluation because gains in target-language intelligibility or naturalness do not imply the similarity gap has closed. *Direction:* evaluate with cross-lingual SMOS plus objective SECS and target-language WER, reported jointly and against the monolingual baseline so the trailing gap is surfaced as an explicit delta, with focused attention on low-resource languages.

**4. Prosody and emotion measurement gap (current state, §7.4).** This axis *remains* the least mature and *still* leans on subjective ratings plus comparison of low-level acoustic features (F0 contours, duration/timing, energy) because *no widely accepted*, linguistically-motivated objective prosody metric *has yet* displaced them. The difficulty is structural: prosody is many-to-one, so reference-based error penalizes legitimate variation, and there is no agreed linguistic ground-truth representation. Only partial proxies exist today. *Direction:* develop and adopt linguistically-motivated objective prosody metrics validated against human judgment, paired with richer expressive and emotional test sets, while retaining subjective ratings as interim ground truth.

**5. The quality-versus-efficiency trade-off (§8).** Deployability — RTF, first-chunk/streaming latency, throughput, and model scale — is orthogonal to quality and must be treated as a first-class axis. An RTF or latency figure is comparable only when reported with its full hardware configuration, and the quality-versus-latency trade-off maps onto architecture families. For production voice agents, first-chunk latency is a gating constraint, not a tie-breaker. *Direction:* make hardware-qualified RTF, first-chunk latency, and throughput mandatory, and report quality-versus-latency as a Pareto frontier across architecture families rather than fusing efficiency into any quality score.

To these the governance level (§9) adds a sixth cluster of open measurement problems — no standardized consent-provenance benchmark, adversarial watermark robustness, and no bridge from watermark/label detectability to legal compliance thresholds — that a complete evaluation practice must eventually absorb.

**Synthesis.** The through-line of these problems is a single shift in where the evaluation bottleneck lies. As raw output quality saturates, the binding constraint moves away from producing a high headline number and toward *discriminating* among systems that all achieve one (relative, statistically powered instruments in place of absolute scales), *comparing* them faithfully (standardized, disclosed, recomputable usage), and *covering* the axes where systems still diverge (identity under language transfer, expressive prosody and emotion, deployability, and governance). No single metric captures TTS quality, and no single improved metric rescues the ranking; the field's central challenge is now to build a discriminative, standardized, multi-axis evaluation practice equal to the systems it must judge.

- **Voicebox WER/SIM figures** (~1.9% vs 5.9% WER; 0.681 vs 0.580 similarity vs VALL-E) are the paper's self-reported comparison; recognizer/embedding-dependent per §4, therefore not cross-study comparable.

- **Efficiency figures** — CosyVoice 2's ~150 ms latency comes from the model card/demo (the paper gives a formula only) and F5-TTS's inference GPU is not explicitly stated; both are cited as *reported* but not fully hardware-qualified.

**Scope caveat (through 2025).** Two citations extend into 2026 and are flagged in-text: IndexTTS 2.5 (arXiv:2601.03888, Jan 2026, https://arxiv.org/pdf/2601.03888) — the only source giving hardware-qualified RTF (0.136 on an NVIDIA A10) for the IndexTTS line, so the main efficiency-table row is anchored on the in-scope IndexTTS2 (arXiv:2506.21619, 2025); and the Yang et al. position paper's accepted v3 dated 29 Jan 2026 (its in-scope v1 is 8 Oct 2025). Today's date makes a strict "through 2025" bound slightly anachronistic; an editor may prefer to relabel scope as "through early 2026" or drop the 2026 rows.

**Out-of-frame conventions worth standardizing downstream (not fully covered here).** (a) Statistical-significance reporting for subjective tests — listener counts, confidence intervals, ANOVA/preference-test significance — is referenced under protocol design (§3) but not exhaustively standardized. (b) Test-set/reference-audio provenance for intrusive metrics (STOI and SECS) warrants its own standardization. (c) The recognizer/embedding "monoculture" (Whisper/Paraformer for WER; WavLM/ERes2Net for SECS) is acute for cross-lingual transfer specifically: these extractors are strongest in high-resource languages, so both the WER and SECS confounds are correlated with the very low-resource axis where the identity gap widens — a coupling worth surfacing. (d) No unified cross-lingual *and* expressive standardized benchmark exists (emotion coverage is zh+en), so cross-lingual transfer that must also preserve prosody/emotion is effectively unmeasured by current suites. (e) "Controllability" (fine-grained duration + emotion control, as in IndexTTS-2 and CosyVoice 3) and "in-the-wild generalization" are emerging signals currently folded under prosody/emotion and multilingual that could warrant their own axes in a future revision.
