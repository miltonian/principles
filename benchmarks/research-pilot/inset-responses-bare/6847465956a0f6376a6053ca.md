# Instance Segmentation of Overlapping Cells in Noisy Fluorescence Microscopy: A Modified U-Net Design

## 1. Problem framing

Fluorescence microscopy instance segmentation is hard for three compounding reasons:

1. **Low SNR / structured noise.** Poisson (shot) noise dominates at low photon counts, plus Gaussian read noise, uneven illumination, and out-of-focus background. Semantic boundaries are faint.
2. **Touching and overlapping objects.** Cells and organelles (mitochondria, nuclei in dense tissue) abut or overlap. A plain semantic foreground mask merges them into one blob — the central failure of vanilla U-Net for instance tasks.
3. **Scale and morphology variability.** Objects range from small round nuclei to elongated, branched mitochondria; densities vary by orders of magnitude.

A U-Net produces a dense per-pixel prediction but has **no notion of instances**. The whole design problem is therefore: *what should the network predict per pixel so that a cheap postprocessing step can recover instances?* This "representation choice" matters more than the exact backbone.

> `★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
> - Instance segmentation from a U-Net is really **representation engineering**: the network stays fully-convolutional and dense; the *instance* information is encoded in auxiliary regression channels (distance transforms, gradient flows, boundaries) that a watershed-style decoder turns into labels.
> - Detection-based methods (Mask R-CNN) instead put the instance logic *inside* the network via region proposals — powerful for sparse scenes, brittle for hundreds of tightly-packed nuclei.
> - Choosing the **target encoding** and the **loss** is where most of the accuracy comes from; the backbone (attention/residual/transformer) is a secondary, though meaningful, gain.
> `─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## 2. Proposed architecture: **Residual-Attention U-Net with a lightweight transformer bottleneck (RAT-UNet)**

### 2.1 Overview

I combine three well-motivated modifications on top of the U-Net skeleton, each targeting a specific failure mode:

| Modification | Failure mode it addresses | Mechanism |
|---|---|---|
| **Residual encoder/decoder blocks** (pre-activation) | Vanishing gradients + noise sensitivity in deep nets | Identity shortcuts stabilize optimization and let early layers act as denoisers |
| **Attention gates on skip connections** | Skip connections leak noisy, irrelevant background features into the decoder | Gates suppress background activations, sharpening faint boundaries |
| **Transformer (ViT-style) bottleneck** | Limited receptive field — a CNN can't reason about *whole-cell* context or long, branched organelles | Global self-attention at the coarsest resolution captures long-range dependencies for consistent instance interiors |

### 2.2 Multi-task output head (the instance representation)

The decoder produces **three heads** at full resolution — this is the core of the instance recovery:

1. **Semantic foreground logits** (1 channel, `sigmoid`) — is this pixel part of *any* object?
2. **Normalized distance-to-boundary regression** (1 channel, `[0,1]`) — the interior "energy" that peaks at object centers and dips to 0 at boundaries. This is what lets watershed split touching objects.
3. **Boundary / overlap map** (1 channel) — explicit inter-object contour, weighted heavily to carve seams between adjacent instances. A separate channel handles *overlap* (pixels belonging to ≥2 objects), which distance transforms alone cannot represent.

Optionally a **2-channel gradient-flow field** (à la Cellpose) can replace or augment the distance head for highly non-convex organelles — discussed in §5.

### 2.3 Why these choices (justification)

- **Residual over plain conv:** microscopy images are noisy; residual identity paths mean a block only has to learn a *correction* to its input, which empirically denoises and greatly improves gradient flow in the 4–5 level U-Net depth we need for large receptive fields.
- **Attention gates rather than a heavier backbone:** the dominant error source is background leakage through skip connections (bright debris, uneven illumination). Additive attention gates (Oktay et al., 2018) are cheap (~1–2% params) and directly gate skip features by decoder context, so faint true boundaries survive while background is suppressed.
- **Transformer bottleneck, not a full transformer (e.g., TransUNet-style):** a full transformer U-Net is data-hungry and slow. Annotated microscopy data is scarce. Restricting self-attention to the low-resolution bottleneck (e.g., 16×16 tokens) gives global context for *whole-object consistency* at modest cost, keeping the model trainable on hundreds — not millions — of labeled crops.
- **Distance + boundary + semantic multi-task:** the distance map is the single most important instance cue for round/blobby nuclei (Stardist and many "deep watershed" methods rely on it); the explicit boundary/overlap head is what pushes performance on *touching* and *overlapping* objects specifically.

## 3. Loss functions

We optimize a weighted sum matched to each head:

$$\mathcal{L} = \lambda_{\text{seg}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{seg}} + \lambda_{\text{dist}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{dist}} + \lambda_{\text{bnd}}\,\mathcal{L}_{\text{bnd}}$$

- **Semantic head — Dice + boundary-weighted BCE.** Dice handles heavy foreground/background imbalance (organelles are sparse). The BCE term is spatially weighted with the classic U-Net separation map $w(x)=w_c(x)+w_0\exp(-\tfrac{(d_1+d_2)^2}{2\sigma^2})$ that *upweights the thin gaps between touching cells*, forcing the net to keep them separate.
- **Distance head — MSE (or Smooth-L1) on the normalized distance transform**, masked to foreground so background zeros don't dominate.
- **Boundary/overlap head — Tversky loss** (Dice generalization) with $\alpha<\beta$ to penalize false negatives more, because *missing* a seam merges two instances — the costliest error for this task.

> `★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
> - **Dice/Tversky vs BCE:** BCE optimizes per-pixel likelihood and gets swamped when 95% of pixels are background; Dice optimizes *overlap* directly and is class-imbalance robust. Tversky's asymmetric α/β lets you say "false negatives on seams hurt more than false positives" — a domain decision encoded numerically.
> - The **exponential separation weight** from the original 2015 U-Net paper is still the cheapest, most effective trick for touching objects: it literally pays the network more attention for getting the 1–2 pixel gap between neighbors right.
> `─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## 4. Postprocessing: turning dense maps into instances

The network never outputs instance IDs — postprocessing does. Pipeline:

1. **Foreground mask** = `sigmoid(seg) > τ_fg` (τ≈0.5), cleaned with small-object removal and morphological opening to kill noise speckles.
2. **Seed detection** = local maxima of the predicted distance map (`peak_local_max`, min-distance ≈ expected object radius). Peaks are robust object *centers* because the distance map is smooth and denoised by the net.
3. **Marker-controlled watershed** on the *negative* distance map, constrained to the foreground mask, flooding from the seeds. This splits touching objects along the distance ridges.
4. **Boundary subtraction / overlap handling:** subtract the predicted boundary map before watershed to deepen seams; for genuine overlaps, pixels flagged by the overlap head can be assigned to *both* adjacent labels (soft/multi-label output) rather than forced to one.
5. **Post-filters:** remove instances below a size threshold or with low mean foreground probability (removes noise-induced false positives).

## 5. Key Python components

> `★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
> - Note the **pre-activation residual block** (BN→ReLU→Conv, He & Zhang 2016): normalizing *before* the conv gives cleaner gradient flow than the original post-activation ordering and is the standard for U-Net encoders today.
> - The attention gate computes a *gating signal* from the coarser decoder feature and uses it to reweight the finer skip feature — this is additive attention, cheaper than the multiplicative dot-product attention used in transformers.
> `─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

### 5.1 Building blocks — residual conv + attention gate

```python
import torch
import torch.nn as nn
import torch.nn.functional as F


class ResidualBlock(nn.Module):
    """Pre-activation residual block: BN -> ReLU -> Conv, twice, plus identity."""
    def __init__(self, in_ch, out_ch):
        super().__init__()
        self.bn1 = nn.BatchNorm2d(in_ch)
        self.conv1 = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, out_ch, 3, padding=1, bias=False)
        self.bn2 = nn.BatchNorm2d(out_ch)
        self.conv2 = nn.Conv2d(out_ch, out_ch, 3, padding=1, bias=False)
        # 1x1 projection so the identity path matches channel count
        self.proj = nn.Conv2d(in_ch, out_ch, 1, bias=False) if in_ch != out_ch else nn.Identity()

    def forward(self, x):
        identity = self.proj(x)
        out = self.conv1(F.relu(self.bn1(x)))
        out = self.conv2(F.relu(self.bn2(out)))
        return out + identity


class AttentionGate(nn.Module):
    """Additive attention gate (Oktay et al. 2018).
    g: gating signal from coarser decoder level; x: skip feature to be filtered."""
    def __init__(self, x_ch, g_ch, inter_ch):
        super().__init__()
        self.theta_x = nn.Conv2d(x_ch, inter_ch, 1, bias=False)
        self.phi_g = nn.Conv2d(g_ch, inter_ch, 1, bias=True)
        self.psi = nn.Conv2d(inter_ch, 1, 1, bias=True)

    def forward(self, x, g):
        # g is upsampled to x's spatial size before gating
        g = F.interpolate(g, size=x.shape[-2:], mode="bilinear", align_corners=False)
        attn = torch.sigmoid(self.psi(F.relu(self.theta_x(x) + self.phi_g(g))))
        return x * attn  # suppress background, keep salient boundaries
```

### 5.2 Transformer bottleneck

```python
class TransformerBottleneck(nn.Module):
    """Flatten the coarsest feature map into tokens, apply global self-attention."""
    def __init__(self, ch, num_heads=8, depth=2, mlp_ratio=4):
        super().__init__()
        layer = nn.TransformerEncoderLayer(
            d_model=ch, nhead=num_heads, dim_feedforward=ch * mlp_ratio,
            activation="gelu", batch_first=True, norm_first=True)
        self.encoder = nn.TransformerEncoder(layer, num_layers=depth)

    def forward(self, x):
        B, C, H, W = x.shape
        # add fixed 2D sinusoidal pos-embedding in practice; omitted for brevity
        tokens = x.flatten(2).transpose(1, 2)      # B, H*W, C
        tokens = self.encoder(tokens)
        return tokens.transpose(1, 2).reshape(B, C, H, W)
```

### 5.3 The RAT-UNet model

```python
class RATUNet(nn.Module):
    def __init__(self, in_ch=1, base=32, depth=4):
        super().__init__()
        chs = [base * 2 ** i for i in range(depth + 1)]  # e.g. [32,64,128,256,512]

        # Encoder
        self.enc = nn.ModuleList()
        prev = in_ch
        for c in chs[:-1]:
            self.enc.append(ResidualBlock(prev, c)); prev = c
        self.pool = nn.MaxPool2d(2)

        # Bottleneck: residual + transformer for global context
        self.bottleneck = nn.Sequential(ResidualBlock(chs[-2], chs[-1]))
        self.transformer = TransformerBottleneck(chs[-1])

        # Decoder with attention-gated skips
        self.ups, self.gates, self.dec = nn.ModuleList(), nn.ModuleList(), nn.ModuleList()
        for i in reversed(range(depth)):
            self.ups.append(nn.ConvTranspose2d(chs[i + 1], chs[i], 2, stride=2))
            self.gates.append(AttentionGate(x_ch=chs[i], g_ch=chs[i + 1], inter_ch=chs[i] // 2))
            self.dec.append(ResidualBlock(chs[i] * 2, chs[i]))

        # Three-task output heads
        self.head_seg = nn.Conv2d(base, 1, 1)   # foreground logits
        self.head_dist = nn.Conv2d(base, 1, 1)  # distance regression
        self.head_bnd = nn.Conv2d(base, 1, 1)   # boundary / overlap logits

    def forward(self, x):
        skips = []
        for enc in self.enc:
            x = enc(x); skips.append(x); x = self.pool(x)
        x = self.transformer(self.bottleneck(x))
        for up, gate, dec, skip in zip(self.ups, self.gates, self.dec, reversed(skips)):
            x = up(x)
            skip = gate(skip, x)                       # filter the skip feature
            x = dec(torch.cat([x, skip], dim=1))
        return {
            "seg":  self.head_seg(x),
            "dist": torch.sigmoid(self.head_dist(x)),  # normalized [0,1]
            "bnd":  self.head_bnd(x),
        }
```

### 5.4 Composite loss

```python
def dice_loss(logits, target, eps=1.):
    p = torch.sigmoid(logits)
    num = 2 * (p * target).sum(dim=(2, 3)) + eps
    den = (p + target).sum(dim=(2, 3)) + eps
    return (1 - num / den).mean()


def tversky_loss(logits, target, alpha=0.3, beta=0.7, eps=1.):
    """alpha<beta penalizes false negatives more -> protects thin seams."""
    p = torch.sigmoid(logits)
    tp = (p * target).sum(dim=(2, 3))
    fp = (p * (1 - target)).sum(dim=(2, 3))
    fn = ((1 - p) * target).sum(dim=(2, 3))
    return (1 - (tp + eps) / (tp + alpha * fp + beta * fn + eps)).mean()


def instance_loss(out, tgt, w_map, lambdas=(1., 1., 1.)):
    """
    out:   dict with 'seg','dist','bnd' (network output)
    tgt:   dict with 'seg','dist','bnd','fg_mask' (precomputed targets)
    w_map: per-pixel separation weight map (upweights gaps between touching cells)
    """
    l_seg, l_dist, l_bnd = lambdas
    # Weighted BCE + Dice for semantic foreground
    bce = F.binary_cross_entropy_with_logits(out["seg"], tgt["seg"], reduction="none")
    seg = (w_map * bce).mean() + dice_loss(out["seg"], tgt["seg"])
    # Masked MSE for the distance transform (ignore background)
    m = tgt["fg_mask"]
    dist = (m * (out["dist"] - tgt["dist"]) ** 2).sum() / (m.sum() + 1e-6)
    # Tversky for the boundary / overlap seams
    bnd = tversky_loss(out["bnd"], tgt["bnd"])
    return l_seg * seg + l_dist * dist + l_bnd * bnd
```

### 5.5 Watershed postprocessing

```python
import numpy as np
from scipy import ndimage as ndi
from skimage.feature import peak_local_max
from skimage.segmentation import watershed
from skimage.morphology import remove_small_objects


def to_instances(seg_prob, dist_map, bnd_prob,
                 fg_thr=0.5, bnd_thr=0.4, min_size=25, min_dist=6):
    fg = (seg_prob > fg_thr) & (bnd_prob < bnd_thr)   # carve seams out of foreground
    fg = remove_small_objects(fg, min_size)

    # Seeds = local maxima of the (denoised) distance map -> one per object center
    coords = peak_local_max(dist_map, min_distance=min_dist, labels=fg)
    markers = np.zeros_like(fg, dtype=np.int32)
    for i, (r, c) in enumerate(coords, start=1):
        markers[r, c] = i

    # Flood the negative distance surface from the markers, bounded by fg
    labels = watershed(-dist_map, markers, mask=fg)
    return labels
```

## 6. Comparison to existing models

| Aspect | **RAT-UNet (proposed)** | **Cellpose** | **Stardist** | **Mask R-CNN** |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Paradigm | Dense multi-task U-Net + watershed | Dense U-Net predicting **gradient flow fields** → gradient tracking | Dense U-Net predicting **star-convex polygon** distances → NMS | Detection: RPN + RoIAlign + per-RoI mask |
| Touching-object separation | Distance ridges + explicit boundary/overlap head + watershed | Flow-field vector clustering (basins of attraction) | Radial polygon per pixel + NMS | Separate RoI per object; masks can overlap |
| Overlapping objects | **Yes** — dedicated overlap channel allows multi-label pixels | No (each pixel one flow) | No (star-convex, no overlap) | **Yes** — independent RoI masks |
| Non-convex / branched shapes (mitochondria) | Good (distance + flow option) | **Best** (flow fields handle arbitrary shapes) | **Weak** (star-convex assumption) | Moderate |
| Round/blobby nuclei | Very good | Very good | **Excellent & fast** | Good |
| Noise robustness | High (residual denoising + attention gating) | High (large training corpus, generalist) | High | Moderate (proposals sensitive to background) |
| Dense scenes (100s of cells) | Scales well (fully conv) | Scales well | Scales well | **Struggles** — NMS/proposal cost, RoI limits |
| Data efficiency | Good (transformer only at bottleneck) | Needs large/diverse data (mitigated by pretrained generalist) | Good | Data hungry |
| Global context | **Yes** (transformer bottleneck) | Limited (CNN receptive field) | Limited | Limited (per-RoI) |
| Main weakness | More heads/hyperparameters to tune; watershed seed sensitivity | Struggles at very high overlap; flow-tracking hyperparams | Star-convex assumption fails on concave/branched objects | Poor for crowded, similar-sized objects; heavy |

### Where each wins

- **Stardist** is the go-to when objects are convex, roughly star-shaped nuclei — its polygon representation is elegant and needs no watershed. It *cannot* represent our branched organelles or overlaps.
- **Cellpose** is the strongest general-purpose baseline: gradient flow fields handle arbitrary, non-convex shapes and it ships robust pretrained generalist models. Our design borrows its flow-field idea as an optional head. Its weakness is genuinely *overlapping* objects (one flow vector per pixel).
- **Mask R-CNN** shines when instances are sparse and can genuinely overlap (its masks are independent), but its proposal/NMS machinery degrades badly in the crowded, hundreds-of-similar-objects regime typical of microscopy.
- **RAT-UNet's niche:** noisy images with *both* touching *and* overlapping objects of *variable morphology*, where (a) the attention gates + residual denoising handle low SNR, (b) the transformer bottleneck gives whole-object consistency, and (c) the explicit overlap channel does what distance-only and flow-only methods cannot.

> `★ Insight ─────────────────────────────────────`
> - The deep lesson: **Stardist encodes a shape prior (star-convex), Cellpose encodes a topological prior (gradient flow to a center), and deep-watershed/RAT-UNet encodes a geometric prior (distance ridges).** Each prior is a bet about object morphology. Pick the method whose prior matches your objects, or — as here — combine cues and let a flexible postprocessor arbitrate.
> - No architecture "solves" instance segmentation; the win is aligning the **predicted intermediate representation** with the **postprocessing decoder** and the **object statistics** of your data.
> `─────────────────────────────────────────────────`

## 7. Practical training recommendations

- **Preprocessing:** percentile normalization (1st/99th) per image; optional self-supervised denoising (Noise2Void) pretraining on unlabeled stacks, since fluorescence noise is pixel-independent given the signal.
- **Augmentation:** elastic deformations (critical for cell morphology), random 90° rotations/flips, intensity/gamma jitter, simulated Poisson-Gaussian noise, and out-of-focus blur to match acquisition variance.
- **Targets:** precompute the normalized per-instance distance transform, the boundary map (morphological gradient of the label image), the overlap map (pixels in ≥2 dilated instances), and the U-Net separation weight map once, offline.
- **Loss weighting:** start `λ = (1, 1, 1)`; if instances merge, raise the boundary/distance weights; if noise creates false positives, raise the semantic Dice weight. Consider uncertainty-based automatic task weighting (Kendall et al.) to avoid manual tuning.
- **Evaluation:** report **Average Precision at multiple IoU thresholds (AP@[0.5:0.9])** and **Panoptic/Aggregated Jaccard Index (AJI)** — pixel Dice alone hides merge/split errors, which are exactly what this task is about.

## 8. Summary

The proposed **RAT-UNet** keeps the fully-convolutional U-Net efficiency but adds (1) **residual blocks** for noise-robust optimization, (2) **attention-gated skips** to suppress background leakage, and (3) a **transformer bottleneck** for global object context. Crucially, it predicts a **multi-task instance representation** — semantic mask, normalized distance transform, and a boundary/overlap map — trained with **Dice + boundary-weighted BCE + masked MSE + Tversky** losses, then decoded into instances via **marker-controlled watershed**. Relative to Stardist (convex prior), Cellpose (flow prior), and Mask R-CNN (detection), its differentiator is jointly handling **low SNR, touching, and genuinely overlapping objects of variable morphology** in a single dense, scalable model.
