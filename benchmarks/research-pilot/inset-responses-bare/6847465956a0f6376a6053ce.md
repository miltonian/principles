# Sunlight, the Outdoors, and Vitamin D: A Public Health Research Report

**Prepared for the general public by the Office of Population Health Sciences**
**Date: 3 July 2026**

---

## 1. Executive Summary — Key Findings & Recommendations

Being outdoors is good for you, and vitamin D is good for you — but they are **not the same thing**, and conflating them has muddied public understanding for years. This report separates the two and translates recent peer-reviewed evidence into plain, actionable guidance.

**Key findings:**

1. **Two distinct benefit streams.** Sunlight produces vitamin D in your skin (a *chemical* benefit you can also get from a pill), **and** it triggers effects that a pill cannot replicate — notably the release of nitric oxide that lowers blood pressure, plus circadian, mood, and sleep benefits from bright light and time in nature ([UK Health Security Agency](https://research.ukhsa.gov.uk/our-research/sunlight-cardio/); [American Heart Association](https://www.heart.org/en/news/2020/02/28/could-sunshine-lower-blood-pressure-study-offers-enlightenment)).

2. **The "more is better" era for vitamin D pills is over.** The 2024 Endocrine Society guideline concluded that **most healthy adults under 75 do not benefit from taking vitamin D above the standard Recommended Daily Allowance (600 IU)** ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2024/endocrine-society-recommends-healthy-adults-take-the-recommended-daily-allowance-of-vitamin-d); [Healio](https://www.healio.com/news/endocrinology/20240604/endocrine-society-guideline-most-adults-do-not-benefit-from-additional-daily-vitamin-d)). A large 2026 BMJ meta-analysis of ~154,000 people similarly found little fracture/fall benefit from routine supplementation in independent-living older adults — **except in defined higher-risk groups** ([BMJ meta-analysis summary](https://www.medicaldaily.com/calcium-vitamin-d-supplements-fractures-bmj-meta-analysis-153000-people-2026-475677)).

3. **Targeted groups still benefit clearly:** children, pregnant people, adults ≥75, people with prediabetes, and anyone with confirmed deficiency (blood 25-OH-D < 20 ng/mL), osteoporosis, malabsorption, or minimal sun exposure ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease)).

4. **Form matters:** Vitamin **D3 (cholecalciferol)** raises and maintains blood levels more effectively than **D2 (ergocalciferol)**, especially as a single/weekly dose ([Advances in Nutrition, 2023](https://advances.nutrition.org/article/S2161-8313(23)01394-7/fulltext)).

5. **Both extremes carry risk.** *Underexposure* → deficiency, weak bones, muscle weakness, low mood. *Overexposure* → skin cancer (from UV) and, from excessive supplements, hypercalcemia. Both are avoidable with simple strategies (Section 8).

**Bottom-line recommendation:** Aim for **short, regular outdoor time** for the non-vitamin-D benefits (mood, blood pressure, circadian rhythm), get vitamin D **primarily from safe casual sun and diet**, and **supplement only if you fall into a higher-risk group** or have low blood levels — using **D3** at the doses in Section 6.

## 2. The Central Distinction: Vitamin D vs. Being Outdoors

A recurring error in health messaging is treating "sunshine" and "vitamin D" as interchangeable. They are interconnected but scientifically separable.

- **Vitamin D** is a hormone precursor. You can obtain it from **sunlight, food, or a supplement** — the molecule is identical regardless of source. Its benefits are therefore *portable*: a pill in winter can substitute for summer sun.
- **Being outdoors** delivers vitamin D **plus** a bundle of effects that **cannot be bottled**. This is the crucial insight from the last decade of research.

| Benefit | Driven by Vitamin D? | Driven by being outdoors (independent of Vitamin D)? |
|---|---|---|
| Calcium absorption / bone mineralization | ✅ Yes (primary) | ❌ No |
| Muscle strength / fall prevention | ✅ Yes | Partly (activity) |
| **Lower blood pressure via skin nitric oxide** | ❌ No | ✅ **Yes — independent of vitamin D** |
| Circadian rhythm / better sleep | ❌ No | ✅ Yes (bright light) |
| Mood elevation / reduced depression | Partly | ✅ Yes (light + nature + activity) |
| Physical activity, social contact, "green space" effects | ❌ No | ✅ Yes |

### The clearest proof they are separate: the nitric oxide finding
When skin is exposed to UVA, it releases **nitric oxide (NO)** into the bloodstream, which widens blood vessels and **lowers blood pressure**. Critically, in controlled human experiments these cardiovascular changes occurred **without any rise in vitamin D levels** and were not explained by skin warming ([ScienceDirect: UVA-induced NO release](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S0022202X15368974); [UKHSA](https://research.ukhsa.gov.uk/our-research/sunlight-cardio/)). As researchers summarized, "there is something about sunlight that can't be replaced by popping vitamin D pills" ([Outside](https://www.outsideonline.com/health/wellness/sun-exposure-blood-pressure-research/)).

### The independent mental-health stream
Time in outdoor light is linked to better mood, sleep, and circadian outcomes. A UK Biobank analysis of **>400,000 people** found more daylight time predicted better mood and sleep ([ScienceDirect / J. Affective Disorders](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0165032721008612)), and a 2023 study in *Translational Psychiatry* linked more outdoor light to **lower incidence of depression** ([Nature](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02338-0)). Randomized trials of nature-based activity show improvements in depressive mood and anxiety ([systematic review/meta-analysis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC8498096/)). These effects flow from **bright light, physical activity, and nature contact** — not from vitamin D.

> **Practical implication:** A vitamin D supplement in December does **not** replace a winter walk. Keep going outside even when your vitamin D needs are met by pills or diet.

## 3. Physiological Benefits Specifically from Vitamin D

Vitamin D's best-established role is in the **calcium–bone axis**:

- It enables the gut to **absorb calcium and phosphate**, maintaining the blood calcium needed for **bone mineralization**. Deficiency causes **rickets** (children) and **osteomalacia**/accelerated **osteoporosis** (adults) ([ScienceDirect review](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1530891X25009656)).
- It supports **muscle function**; deficiency correlates with reduced muscle strength and higher **fall risk**, compounding fracture risk in older people ([PMC muscle-mediation study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12626798/)).
- Emerging, more tentative roles include **immune modulation** (e.g., reduced respiratory infections in children) and **metabolic** effects (progression from prediabetes to diabetes) — reflected in the 2024 guideline's targeted recommendations ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease)).

**Important nuance from 2024–2026 evidence:** For *most* healthy, sufficient adults, adding *more* vitamin D beyond the RDA does **not** meaningfully cut fractures or falls ([BMJ, 2026](https://www.medicaldaily.com/calcium-vitamin-d-supplements-fractures-bmj-meta-analysis-153000-people-2026-475677)). The benefit is concentrated in the **deficient and the high-risk**. Vitamin D corrects a deficiency; it is not a general-purpose tonic.

## 4. Benefits Independent of Vitamin D (from Being Outdoors)

1. **Cardiovascular:** UV-driven skin **nitric oxide** release lowers blood pressure — relevant because hypertension affects >1 billion people worldwide ([AHA](https://www.heart.org/en/news/2020/02/28/could-sunshine-lower-blood-pressure-study-offers-enlightenment)).
2. **Circadian & sleep:** Morning/daytime bright light anchors the body clock, improving sleep quality ([UK Biobank study](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0165032721008612)).
3. **Mental health:** Reduced depressive symptoms and improved mood from light, nature, and activity ([Nature, 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02338-0)).
4. **Physical activity & social connection:** Outdoor settings encourage movement and social contact — themselves major determinants of cardiometabolic and mental health.

## 5. Benefits and Considerations by Demographic (each with ≥1 unique point)

> These build on general guidance; each group has a **distinctive** benefit or risk.

### Infants (0–12 months)
- **Unique risk:** Breast milk is low in vitamin D, so exclusively/partially breastfed infants are at **specific risk of rickets**. Standard pediatric practice is **400 IU/day** from birth. Infants should also be **kept out of direct strong sun** (burn risk, thin skin).

### Children & Adolescents
- **Unique benefit:** The 2024 guideline **supports supplementation above the RDA in children**, partly for **reduced respiratory tract infections**; trial doses averaged ~**1,200 IU/day** ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease)). Adequate vitamin D during growth also builds **peak bone mass**, a lifelong buffer against osteoporosis. (Note: some experts argue the guideline understates pediatric benefit — see the [rebuttal in PMC](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12158689/).)

### Adults 19–70 (general)
- **Unique point:** For most healthy adults in this band, **routine high-dose supplementation is not recommended** — follow the **600 IU RDA** and get benefits mainly from safe sun and diet ([Endocrine Society, 2024](https://www.endocrine.org/news-and-advocacy/news-room/2024/endocrine-society-recommends-healthy-adults-take-the-recommended-daily-allowance-of-vitamin-d)).

### Pregnant People
- **Unique benefit:** A 2024 systematic review/meta-analysis found supplementation reduced **preeclampsia risk by ~45%** and preterm labor by ~30% ([BMC Pregnancy & Childbirth, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-024-07081-y)). The Endocrine Society **suggests empiric supplementation in pregnancy** while **advising against routine 25(OH)D testing** ([guideline](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease)).

### Women — Postmenopausal
- **Unique risk:** Estrogen loss accelerates bone turnover, making **vitamin D insufficiency especially costly for bone mineral density (BMD)**. **500–800 IU/day (± calcium)** has been shown to increase BMD around age ~63, and **exercise + calcium + vitamin D** improves spine and femoral-neck BMD ([exercise + supplement meta-analysis](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12735737/)).

### Men
- **Unique point:** Men have **lower average lifetime osteoporosis risk** than postmenopausal women but tend toward **higher UV/occupational sun exposure and higher melanoma mortality**, so the **overexposure/skin-cancer side of the ledger is comparatively more salient** for many men — making sun-protection habits especially important.

### Older Adults (≥75)
- **Unique benefit:** This is the group the 2024 guideline **most clearly endorses for empiric supplementation**, associated with **lower mortality** (trial-weighted average ~**900 IU/day**) ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease); [Healio](https://www.healio.com/news/endocrinology/20240604/endocrine-society-guideline-most-adults-do-not-benefit-from-additional-daily-vitamin-d)). Aging skin synthesizes less vitamin D, and **~800–1,000 IU/day** is associated with fewer fractures/falls in higher-risk elders ([e-ENM meta-analysis](https://www.e-enm.org/journal/view.php?number=2294)).

### People with darker skin
- **Unique risk:** Higher melanin **filters UVB**, so **more sun time is needed** to make the same vitamin D — at high latitudes, skin type VI may need **over an hour** vs. minutes for lighter skin, raising deficiency risk ([Nutrients / MDPI, 2024](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/10/1489); [Colour Counts study](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC5946242/)).

## 6. Quantifiable Recommendations

### A. Sun exposure duration (for vitamin D)
Skin synthesis is **self-limiting** — it plateaus after ~**5–10 minutes** and then stops, so long exposures add cancer risk *without* adding vitamin D ([Benefits & Risks review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10239563/)).

- **General guidance:** **10–30 minutes of midday sun** on bare skin (arms/legs/face), several times per week ([synthesis overview](https://lifestyle.sustainability-directory.com/learn/what-is-the-recommended-daily-duration-of-sun-exposure-for-adequate-vitamin-d-synthesis/)).
- **By skin type & latitude (midday):**
  - Equator: **~3–15 min** (lighter → darker skin).
  - Every 10° of higher latitude adds time; **skin type VI** may need **>1 hour** at high latitudes ([MDPI, 2024](https://www.mdpi.com/2072-6643/16/10/1489)).
  - Example — **Boston (~42°N), type III skin, midday Apr–Oct: ~3–8 min** yields ~400 IU ([JAAD equivalency study](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/20363523/)).
- **Winter caveat:** Above ~**37–40° latitude**, UVB is largely filtered out in winter; at ~42°N vitamin D synthesis is **essentially zero from November to early March** — rely on **diet/supplements** in those months.
- **Variables that increase needed time:** darker skin, higher latitude, winter/morning/evening (low sun angle), cloud/pollution, older age, sunscreen, glass (blocks UVB), more clothing coverage.

### B. Supplemental intake (consensus, IU) — includes diet + supplements
| Group | Recommended intake |
|---|---|
| Infants 0–12 mo | **400 IU/day** (standard pediatric practice) |
| Children/adolescents | **600 IU RDA**; guideline supports higher (trials avg ~1,200 IU) in some cases |
| Adults 19–70 | **600 IU/day** |
| Adults >70–74 | **~800 IU/day** |
| Adults ≥75 | **Empiric supplementation suggested** (~800–1,000 IU typical; trial avg ~900 IU) |
| Pregnancy | **Empiric supplementation suggested** (commonly ~600 IU, often more) |
| Postmenopausal (bone) | **500–800 IU/day (± calcium)** |

Sources: [Endocrine Society 2024](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease); [Harvard Health](https://www.health.harvard.edu/healthy-aging-and-longevity/new-guidelines-released-for-vitamin-d-testing-and-supplementation); [Patient Care Online](https://www.patientcareonline.com/view/endocrine-society-releases-new-clinical-guideline-on-vitamin-d-supplementation-for-prevention-of-disease). *These are total-intake targets, not "extra on top of everything."*

### C. Vitamin D2 vs D3 — which to take
- **D3 (cholecalciferol):** made in skin and found in animal foods; **more effective at raising and maintaining blood 25(OH)D**, with a longer half-life (~3–4 weeks). A 2023 meta-analysis found D3 markedly more potent, especially with **bolus/weekly dosing** ([Advances in Nutrition, 2023](https://advances.nutrition.org/article/S2161-8313(23)01394-7/fulltext); [British Journal of Nutrition](https://www.cambridge.org/core/journals/british-journal-of-nutrition/article/longterm-vitamin-d3-supplementation-is-more-effective-than-vitamin-d2-in-maintaining-serum-25hydroxyvitamin-d-status-over-the-winter-months/A6CD4FE5C649E0FAC9BCBAFB23538707)).
- **D2 (ergocalciferol):** plant/fungal-derived (suitable for strict vegans); **less potent per dose**, half-life ~2–3 weeks. The gap **narrows with daily dosing** and in people with overweight/obesity ([PubMed meta-analysis](https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37865222/)).
- **Recommendation:** **Choose D3** unless you require a plant-based product; if using D2, prefer **daily** dosing.

## 7. Interconnected Relationships (How It All Fits Together)

- Sun → **UVB** makes **vitamin D3** (chemical benefit) **and** **UVA** releases **nitric oxide** (cardiovascular benefit) — same source, two independent pathways.
- Latitude/season **gates** the vitamin D pathway (winter shutdown) but the **light/mood/circadian** benefits of being outdoors persist year-round.
- Skin pigment simultaneously **protects against UV cancer risk** and **slows vitamin D production** — the same trait sits on both sides of the risk ledger, which is why guidance must be **personalized by skin type and latitude**.

## 8. Primary Risks — Underexposure vs. Overexposure — and Mitigation

### Risks of UNDER-exposure / deficiency
- **Bone disease:** rickets (children), osteomalacia and worsened **osteoporosis/fractures** (adults) ([ScienceDirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/pii/S1530891X25009656)).
- **Muscle weakness & falls** in older adults ([PMC](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/pmc/articles/PMC12626798/)).
- **Pregnancy:** higher preeclampsia/preterm risk when deficient ([BMC, 2024](https://link.springer.com/article/10.1186/s12884-024-07081-y)).
- **Mood/sleep:** low outdoor light associated with more depressive symptoms and poorer sleep ([Nature, 2023](https://www.nature.com/articles/s41398-023-02338-0)).

### Risks of OVER-exposure
- **UV (sun) overexposure → skin cancer.** UV is the leading environmental cause of skin cancers and **melanoma**; UVB damages DNA directly ([UV & skin cancer review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4579804/)). Because skin vitamin D synthesis **maxes out in minutes**, prolonged tanning adds cancer risk with **no extra vitamin D**.
- **Supplement overdose → vitamin D toxicity (hypervitaminosis D).** Rare, but causes **hypercalcemia, hypercalciuria, and suppressed PTH**; defined as blood 25(OH)D **>100 ng/mL**. Notably, **you cannot overdose from sunlight** — skin synthesis is self-regulating; toxicity comes from **excessive pills** ([Benefits & Risks review, PMC](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10239563/)).

### Mitigation — avoiding BOTH extremes
1. **"Short and regular, not long and rare."** Get vitamin D from **brief midday exposures (≈5–15 min for lighter skin; longer for darker skin)**, then **cover up or apply sunscreen** for the rest of your time outside.
2. **Protect high-cancer-risk skin:** routine sunscreen on face/hands; expose less-often-exposed areas briefly instead of burning cumulative-exposure zones ([UV/vitamin D balance review](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC4579804/)).
3. **Never burn.** Sunburn signals DNA damage without vitamin D payoff.
4. **Bridge winter/high-latitude gaps with diet or a supplement**, not longer sunbathing.
5. **Supplement within recommended IU ranges (Section 6); avoid mega-doses** unless prescribed and monitored — this prevents hypercalcemia. Aim to keep blood 25(OH)D in the **sufficient range (>20 ng/mL / >50 nmol/L)**, not sky-high.
6. **Keep going outside for the non-vitamin-D benefits** (blood pressure, mood, sleep) even when your vitamin D is handled by diet/pills.
7. **Test only when indicated** (deficiency risk, symptoms); the 2024 guideline **advises against broad routine testing**, including in pregnancy ([Endocrine Society](https://www.endocrine.org/clinical-practice-guidelines/vitamin-d-for-prevention-of-disease)).

---

## 9. Summary Recommendations (Actionable)

- **Everyone:** Spend **short, regular time outdoors** — the mood/sleep/blood-pressure benefits are real and **cannot be replaced by a pill**.
- **For vitamin D specifically:** brief midday sun matched to your skin type/latitude; **D3** supplement if you're in a **high-risk group** (infants, children as advised, pregnancy, ≥75, dark skin at high latitude, housebound, malabsorption, or confirmed deficiency).
- **Doses:** 400 IU (infants) · 600 IU (most adults) · ~800–1,000 IU (≥75 / higher-risk elders); stay within recommended ranges.
- **Avoid both extremes:** don't burn (cancer), don't mega-dose (hypercalcemia), don't neglect winter intake (deficiency).
